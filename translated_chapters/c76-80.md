# Chapter 76: The Face

Elrouga finished his meal and returned to his tent.

Krische likewise, after taking a bath.

Granmeld, drunk on wine, had remained at the estate, and Roland had provided him with a woman.

It was meant to extract information, but he did not particularly expect results.

He had already identified the important information he needed.

In the room, Elvena stood with proper posture, eyes downcast, while Roland worked at his writing desk.

And one more person.

A middle-aged man knelt in silence, waiting for his master.

His features were indistinct — a face without much definition.

Neither tall nor short, his build was unremarkable.

He wore simple trousers and a shirt of the kind worn by townspeople, with a single shoulder bag.

One might say he was a man whose appearance left no impression.

The sort of man who, if you tried to recall him later, would blur into vagueness.

Roland's spy — his name was Daglis.

Originally an assassin, his abilities had earned him twenty years in Roland's employ.

Turning sixty this year, even as a mana-user he had begun to feel the decline of his body, but the experience and techniques he had cultivated allowed him to blend into crowds, race through the darkness of night, traverse forests, and slip past the most stringent surveillance.

Being summoned like this meant it must be a matter of considerable gravity, Daglis surmised.

Having spent a long career in this line of work, one could not survive without a sharp mind.

Even without knowing the specifics, he could roughly grasp the nature of the task.

"You understand, Daglis."

"Yes. Slipping past military patrols is a simple matter. To Margrave Hilkintos?"

The state of commerce, reputations, the position of armies.

By gathering up each scrap of rumor and binding such information together, one could make predictions about the course of events.

And about what one's master desired.

Roland nodded with satisfaction at Daglis's words.

"Indeed. It seems they have no intention of heading to the Dragon's Maw. I wish to sell as much favor to His Highness the Prince as possible."

Alongside his legitimate business, Roland dealt in the slave trade.

Naturally, he never dirtied his own hands. "Directed" would be more accurate.

He had planted deep roots in the kingdom's center, building his own empire.

Which side winning the civil war would be better for him — that required no deliberation.

Gildanstein was one of Roland's most important clients.

The prince's downfall would deal a devastating blow to Roland's business as well.

In contrast, Kreschenta was a frail, pitiable princess who valued peace.

Surrounding her were fools who prized honor, great causes, and pride not worth feeding to dogs.

Under such a queen's reign, business would become far more difficult — the scales were not even worth balancing.

He was aware of the Markelus army's defeat, but did not regard it as a serious problem.

Christand without their hero.

His daughter commanding, with only Nozan Velraigh as their lifeline.

Roland was quite sure that Gildanstein would emerge victorious.

All he had to do was give them a push.

Once they marched on the capital, hiring brigands to strike at their rear would not be a bad idea.

That alone would see them drown within the kingdom, and Roland could further thicken his connections to the kingdom's center.

Smirking at such a future, Roland ran his pen across the paper and asked:

"Elvena, did you learn anything from that cursed child?"

"……No."

"Useless wretch. ……I never expected much to begin with, but when I can't even see the effort, that's a different matter. Have you ever managed to extract anything useful? Is opening your legs and moaning the only thing you're good for?"

"……I'm terribly sorry."

To the trembling Elvena, Roland showed a sadistic face.

It was a vulgar smile.

"Well, if that's all you're good for, so be it, Elvena. In that case, I'm a merciful man. I'll find you a master who'd be delighted to make use of a whore like you. It wouldn't be any fun unless it's one who'd enjoy cutting off your limbs."

"P-please, have mercy, Roland-sama……"

"How many times have I heard those words. Hmph, fine. Whether I remain a kind master depends on you……you understand, Elvena?"

"……Yes. Please, let me stay here."

Daglis watched the usual exchange with exasperation.

No matter the occasion, the man could not bed a woman without first threatening her.

His complex about his own appearance and power was seeping through.

Daglis found Roland repulsive, but it was likely that very complex that had made this man one of the kingdom's foremost merchants.

Because he was a coward, he was thorough; to obtain power, he did not balk at licking others' boots.

He was scum who had sacrificed every shred of human dignity for the power called money.

That said, receiving money from such a person made Daglis little different, he thought with an inward laugh.

This scene stirred no righteous indignation in him — only pity.

Glancing at Elvena with something like contempt, thinking he never wanted to end up like that, Daglis melted wax at the fireplace and brought it to Roland.

Roland rolled the parchment, set it down, and Daglis dripped wax onto the seal.

The stamp pressed bore a winged scales emblem.

Scales symbolized fairness and equality.

Where was the fairness in this rotten merchant? Daglis thought with exasperation at the emblem, but accepted the document.

"Then, Roland-sama."

"Yes. Mind you don't blunder. ……Elvena, I'll give you a chance to make up for your failures. Come here."

"……Yes, thank you."

Daglis left the room without watching that, and headed to the second floor.

He passed a few slaves along the way, but their indecent attire and beautiful features stirred not a single emotion or flicker of interest in him.

While on assignment, Daglis operated like some kind of mechanism, acting solely for the objective.

Ignoring a woman who bowed politely, he proceeded down the corridor to its end.

From his bag he drew a dark blue-gray cloak, threw it over himself, and leapt from the window into the night.

Dissolving his form into the darkness — and in that very instant, he noticed.

"……Right from the start."

Multiple lines of sight.

He could not see the watchers. But from the arrangement of the surrounding buildings, he estimated where the hidden observers lurked.

Amateurs? But the gazes came from rooftops and towers. From elevated positions.

Mana-users. Several of them.

He judged that forcing his way through would be too dangerous.

A body that had survived countless life-or-death situations. Daglis had no intention of falling behind one or two skilled fighters, and rough work was more his domain than anyone's.

Still, considering the feel and number of the gazes, cutting his way through would be reckless.

It seemed Roland had been targeted from the very beginning.

There was no surprise in this. If anything, it was business as usual.

A job that went as planned was the rarity for Daglis.

If it were an easy task anyone could manage, Daglis would never have been summoned in the first place.

The watchers were likely soldiers.

Even if they were professionals like himself, escaping them with Daglis's skills would be simple enough.

He leapt over a wall, onto a rooftop.

Then immediately down into an alley.

This city was like his own garden.

From the alley, through an unguarded house's window, and out from another location before anyone noticed.

Doing this twice was enough to completely lose the watchers' eyes.

Daglis had already identified the direction he needed to go.

As if his vision could perceive gazes that should have been invisible.

He raced through the darkness, threading the gaps between them.

Movements with shifting tempo — even the cloak dancing in the wind bore his consciousness.

No sound of running, no rustle of cloth.

Daglis passed over the wall dividing the city center and headed outside.

Slipping through the gaps between houses, keeping low.

The techniques ingrained in his body — once they lost sight of him, it was over.

After that, no one in this world could follow him.

Pursuers and watchers had completely vanished.

Even after confirming this, he remained alert, soundlessly leaping from a rooftop and —

"Stop, please."

— and found a blade pressed to his throat.

"——!?"

His body moved before thought could form.

The heel of his shoe — a spring-loaded knife — toward the figure behind him.

No contact. No presence, no sound.

At the edge of his vision, a faint glimpse of swaying silver hair — that alone he barely caught.

He twisted his waist, then his arm.

He swung his concealed blade to sweep behind him — and Daglis's world spun.

Without pain, he found himself staring at the sky.

His right arm was pinned underfoot, and the tip of a blade was wedged between his teeth.

Behind the crescent moon, what filled his vision was a silver silhouette.

A dark cloak wavered like flame.

— Only purple eyes shone colder than the moon.

"The demand is 'don't move.' If you resist further, first Krische will shatter your teeth so you can't bite your tongue."

On the girl's lips floated a smile.

"After that, Krische will cut your fingers off from the tips, then peel the skin. Don't worry, Krische is experienced with butchering game, so Krische can do it without killing you."

Beautiful, bewitching — yet like a flower concealing deadly poison.

It was a spine-chilling smile.

"If you understand, blink twice. If you don't understand, close your eyes."

Why, how, me.

What filled Daglis's mind was bewilderment and a fear he had long forgotten.

His position had been taken from behind without a trace of presence, and in an instant he had been forced into a state where resistance was impossible.

His teeth trembled against the blade, and Daglis closed his eyelids twice.

"……Good. It's all right. Roland will die soon. If you wish for your personal safety, Krische will guarantee it. Just listen to what Krische has to say, make a promise, and work for Krische — if you do that much, Krische will never do anything cruel."

*It's all rather troublesome*, the girl said, looking bothered.

He knew the name Krische.

The Christand First Corps, its current commander — the rumored cursed child.

Head-Hunter of Bernaich, Krische the cursed child.

The battles at the Dragon's Maw had reached even here.

Not only the outcomes, but her name as well.

A number of soldiers who had gone to the battlefield had returned to this city as well.

Some of them, he had heard, had gone mad, unable even to function in daily life.

It was common enough for those who went to war to return shattered by the reality.

He had not regarded such rumors with particular weight.

She was probably a noblewoman with some skill at the sword, he had thought.

He had not believed the words calling her a cursed child, a monster.

Those who wielded mana were, to a greater or lesser degree, viewed that way by ordinary people.

From an ordinary person's perspective, Daglis too could easily be called a monster.

She was likely a skilled mana-user accustomed to fighting in the enclosed space of the forest, and the fear of that situation combined with the devastating casualties had led them to describe her as a monster.

A common enough story, he had thought — but.

"It's true, you know. Krische doesn't really lie."

Gazing into those purple eyes, feeling his body tremble, he thought:

— Yes, indeed.

This was nothing less than a cursed child.

Daglis did not underestimate or overestimate his own abilities.

He was no longer at an age for overconfidence or arrogance.

And yet, in this city — no, in the entire kingdom — those who could match him could be counted on one hand.

Because he held that confidence, he could understand that she was a monster beyond reason.

The dubious rumors he had heard. The figure described in them.

The girl before his eyes was precisely as the rumors told —

"……Do you belong to the Night's Claw guild? Or perhaps Dark Wing? If the former, blink once, if the latter……ah, no, it doesn't matter either way."

The girl's mouth spun words.

A voice sweet enough to melt the ears, and yet,

"Krische doesn't really understand what you work for, but Krische thinks that ultimately you work for some kind of profit."

Somewhere, it was distorted.

"If you cooperate, Krische will arrange various conveniences for your benefit. But if you refuse —"

— *then it will be a thorough elimination.*

So said those eyes.

Like looking at a pebble on the roadside — the way a person looks at an insect, and a god at a person.

They were the eyes of an absolute being.

"Krische will kill Roland and you without much thought, and wipe clean all the existing structures in this city. ……You see, if that happened, whatever your work is, whatever your wishes are, it wouldn't be a pleasant outcome. So, nodding here is really the best choice."

Nodding to herself, the girl narrowed her eyes.

"Krische could use rough methods too, but it would be very inefficient and troublesome. So Krische simply wants your cooperation as well. Krische wants as peaceful a resolution as possible. Of course, Krische will be generous with the compensation. What do you say?"

Slowly she withdrew the curved sword from between his teeth.

But there was not the slightest opening to bite his tongue. His teeth would be knocked out first.

"Y-you said compensation?"

Daglis had completely given in, and asked.

As one who lived in the underworld, he had a measure of pride.

But he was neither young enough nor senile enough to cling to pride that yielded no profit.

Obey, or die. Everything around you included.

The demand was that simple, and she harbored not a shred of hesitation.

And he had no means to resist.

From the moment those eyes had been turned on him, it had already been over.

"Yes. What is it you want?"

"If, for example, I said……I wanted this city — would you agree to that?"

"Let's see. So long as you maintained order as before and didn't do anything too conspicuous, that would be fine. Krische isn't really interested in your people's business."

Just as her words stated — she was likely equally 'uninterested' in Daglis's life.

He could not fathom the purpose of the monster before him.

But there were things he could understand.

"Understood……very well. Making an enemy of you seems decidedly unprofitable."

Roland had bowed his head to the fatally wrong person.

"So……what do you want from me?"

Whether it was experience or inborn instinct.

A body that had survived countless mortal situations was telling Daglis.

The one who would win this war was not Prince Gildanstein.

"He's running on schedule. Replacing the head of Roland shouldn't be a problem."

After dealing with a few loose ends, the place she headed was Elrouga's tent.

As Krische approached him seated in a chair and reported, Elrouga nodded with a wicked smile.

"Most satisfactory. ……No concerns at our backs, then."

The main problem the other day when she had encountered Roland was that there had been no justification to kill him.

But this time was different.

This city would henceforth be treated as an important logistics hub.

Its ruler naturally had to be cooperative with their side, which meant Roland was unsuitable.

In that case, the only conclusion was to kill him.

Leaving sources of uncertainty behind them was the thing most to be avoided in their current situation.

Any factor that obstructed the army's operations should be eliminated as far as possible, and when Krische had proposed disposing of Roland the other day, no one had objected.

That meant she could dispose of an unpleasant man in the name of justice.

Krische was in high spirits.

There was some groundwork to be done for the future, but Krische did not find it burdensome.

"However, Corps Commander Varkus has been quite a problem. Taking advantage of the situation to get carried away……"

"Krische thought Corps Commander Varkus was doing a good job with his acting, though……"

"Oh, no, I mean what happened afterward……"

"……?"

"……Apologies, never mind. Please forget about it."

It had been necessary to let Roland know that their objective was not the Dragon's Maw but Hilkintos.

Granmeld's role was to feign drunkenness and manipulate Roland into action. That was fine.

But Granmeld had taken the opportunity to stay behind at the estate and spend the night with the woman Roland had provided.

Elrouga was angry about his lack of seriousness in this regard, but he did not wish to go on at length explaining such matters in front of a young maiden.

That said, Krische was a country-bred girl.

She did not understand human matters of that sort, nor was she interested — though utterly unguarded and defenseless as a hen dancing beside a stew pot, Krische at least possessed a level of knowledge comparable to beasts mating, and she understood well enough that a fair number of men would pay money to mate.

Naturally, she possessed no sensibility that would cause her to blush at such conversation.

There was truly nothing for Elrouga to worry about regarding the distorted girl that was Krische, but perhaps it was simply the nature of an old man. He wished Krische, whom he doted on like a real granddaughter, to remain untouched by such vulgar topics.

"Setting aside Roland's execution at a later date……what will happen to the servants?"

"Well now. Given the state of things, they all have debts imposed on them. They'll likely drift off elsewhere."

"……I see."

"Is something on your mind?"

Elrouga asked, noticing Krische's contemplative expression.

"Um, the person Krische spoke with was a kind person, so Krische would like to do something for her if possible."

"Hm……well, if Roland holds the debt, we can apply the law to treat them as de facto slaves and free them easily enough. But if the debts are held elsewhere, it becomes rather troublesome. It's difficult to prosecute those involved in such slave operations."

"……I see."

These were unexpected words from Krische, Elrouga thought.

But he knew well that her heart was kind.

He spoke in a deliberately gentle, instructive tone.

"Such people exist everywhere, after all. It's pitiable, but one cannot save everyone. Sometimes one must pretend not to see —"

Mid-sentence, Elrouga shook his head.

"No, something like that can hardly be called sage advice. Merely the idle words of a jaded old man, nothing but imposition. It would be improper for someone with Krische-sama's talent and future."

Where there are those who prosper, there are those who are poor.

Those who are taken from continue to be taken from.

Such was the way of the world.

Overturning what has been established is no ordinary feat — it is beyond the hands of common people.

— But that is only if one is a common person.

The girl before him was not.

"If you try to pick up everything, someday your hands will overflow and you'll be crushed by the burden. Krische-sama's sentiments are worthy of respect, but you should know that many who gave too generously to the poor ruined themselves."

"Mm……true. That is a fair point."

If it were merely paying off one person's debts, it would be a trivial matter.

From the previous war against the Holy Elsren Empire — Krische had taken the deputy commander's head, captured a general's aide, and combined with various other exploits, she had received rewards even greater than Selene's.

She had thrown it all into the Christand household, but under an allowance system where she could withdraw it whenever she wished. If she wanted to help that woman, it would likely suffice.

But if it became a matter of helping person after person in the same way going forward, that would be difficult.

"Power, violence, and wealth. Those who possess strength are expected to use it properly. Consider that carefully, and then decide what you want. What you wish for, what you choose. There is no single right answer, and it is an extremely difficult question, but……"

"……Bery said the same thing long ago."

Krische let her gaze wander as if troubled, then bowed her head.

"Thank you. ……Krische will think about it a bit more."

"Yes, that would be wise. ……That said, if after careful thought it is your conclusion, whatever it may be, that is Krische-sama's freedom. I intend to assist with whatever conclusion you reach."

Krische gazed at Elrouga happily.

Elrouga likewise looked pleased — floating a smile that resembled a ghoul rising from the grave — and gently stroked Krische's head.

Krische, cheeks faintly colored, gazed at the hand stroking her head and smiled.

"Ehehe, Skeleton has been doing so much for Krische. Krische has to give a lot back."

"There's no need for that. I'm simply helping because I want to."

"Then Krische will help Skeleton lots too. You can ask Krische for anything, okay?"

"Well now, that does put me in a spot……"

Adjutant Kyunez, who had been secretly watching this exchange alone, rubbed the corners of his eyes thinking that if you only heard the voices, it was a heartwarming exchange.

A kindly old man and his granddaughter — but the sight before his eyes was evil through and through.

*Standing, a reaper; seated, a demon lord; and their smiles — beyond words.*

Elrouga's sinister visage, already feared by his subordinates, only grew more terrifying when he smiled.

His companion may have had the appearance of a lovely young girl, but she was the monster whom the enemy had dubbed "Head-Hunter."

There had been a number of soldiers among the Markelus army who had fought at the Dragon's Maw, and every last one of them feared Krische. He had heard there were even prisoners who had fled out of sheer terror of her.

What exactly did one have to do to be feared that much?

There were many armies famed for their valor, but surely none so steeped in malevolence as this one.

"……Kyunez, you were listening?"

"Y-yes, sir!"

"Make arrangements so we can respond however necessary."

"Yes, sir! Understood!"

To Krische his voice was the complete opposite — a curse-like voice reverberating from the depths of the earth.

Kyunez performed a regulation salute.

"I will have various matters investigated on our end. Though of course, the battle ahead of us comes first."

"Yes, thank you. Krische will do her best."

"Indeed, indeed. So that you need not overexert yourself, this untalented body will do everything in its power to assist."

"Fufu, Skeleton is a corps commander who does his job properly, so Krische feels very reassured. Well then, let's do our best together."

"Of course."

His voice was once again gentle toward Krische.

Watching his corps commander, Kyunez couldn't help but feel a certain indescribable mood — wishing Elrouga would show him just a bit of that gentleness too — but then again, being smiled at by Elrouga was its own kind of punishment.

In the end, the best course was to quietly watch from a distance, and Kyunez let out a silent sigh.

When Krische finished her talk with Elrouga and stepped out of the tent, First Squad was outside as always.

*The job is done*, Krische told the five of them in a single statement.

"Man, our bunny's amazing. Mia lost sight of you right away."

"……That goes for you too, Kalua."

"I was properly tracking and following you, wasn't I?"

Mia puffed her cheeks and complained, and Kalua laughed.

The ones who had been securing the perimeter were the black century.

Naturally, chasing down a single fleeing spy was labor-intensive even for Krische.

Extremely efficient mana usage, body control — Krische's physical abilities were extraordinary even among mana-users.

But when factoring in raw build difference, stride length, the fundamental amount of mana within her body, and various other conditions, there were many areas where she fell short compared to others. For instance, chasing down an opponent fleeing in a straight line inevitably required considerable effort.

Acceleration and turning via walls, ceilings, and trees as footholds, leaps.

Though Krische possessed such high mobility, in terms of raw speed she could not gain that much advantage over other mana-users.

Therefore, this time the century was deployed solely to narrow the escape routes.

She predicted the path the man would flee — distracted by the watchers — concealed her presence, and lay in ambush.

With Krische's skills, that alone was enough.

As long as she knew where the target was heading, ambushing and disabling them was child's play for her.

Daglis had been caught in that trap.

"Adol, report to Vulture. After that, full rest. Tomorrow is a bit early."

"Yes, ma'am!"

"Mia and the others can dismiss as well. Krische is going to rest now."

"Understood."

"It's a tent tonight, so, wanna sleep together, bunny?"

Kalua said it teasingly.

Until now there had been no rain, and they had been camping entirely under the open sky, so she had been sleeping alone, but today proper tents had been erected.

Krische was slightly surprised by the offer, thought for a moment, then nodded with some embarrassment.

"Um, then that would be……ah, Mia, please sleep next to Kalua."

"Ugh……y-yes……or rather, me too……?"

"……? You don't want to sleep together, Mia?"

"N-no……"

Krische fundamentally harbored no reservations about sleeping together with others.

There was the question of propriety for a corps commander, but given Krische's age and appearance, no one outwardly complained.

The three remaining men looked at the two with a hint of envy.

Then Bagu said jokingly:

"Commander, shall I keep you company too?"

"Men aren't allowed. Honestly. The only person of the opposite sex you can share a blanket with is family."

"Wow, enemy of women. The worst……though I'm a tiny bit relieved bunny has at least that much wariness."

"……Kaa-sama taught Krische that much properly. Krische is a lady."

"Mm-hmm, that's good. Men are beasts, after all. Between girls it's perfectly safe……oh, I know, tonight I could teach you all sorts of things — owdeh!?"

"Kalua, don't say stupid things."

Mia sighed, pulling Kalua's ponytail.

"What if my lovely hair falls out, huh."

"That's what you get for always saying things like that. From now on I'm pulling it every time you say something stupid."

Krische gazed at Kalua's sulking profile and tilted her head slightly.

Comparing it to a face in her memory. Overlapping them.

"Hm, what is it? Bunny?"

"Nothing, well, let's go rest."

"Yeah. Heheh, sleepy?"

A gentle smile. The atmosphere was completely different.

Yet those almond-shaped eyes and slender features were strikingly similar to that servant.

"Since Krische took a bath after a long time, maybe Krische's body has gone all lazy."

"Mmm, so jealous, I wanted a bath too."

*These things happen*, Krische thought, stifling a yawn, and walked toward her tent.

"Nn, then Krische will wipe Kalua down tonight. Krische is actually quite good at it."

"Ooh……feels like a bonus. Good thing I asked."

"Krische always washes with Bery, but today it was only Krische getting washed. Shall Krische wipe you too, Mia?"

"Huh? N-no……I-I'll do it myself……"

When Mia shook her head shyly, Kalua said with exasperation:

"……Country girl, yet so bashful."

"Because Kalua makes fun of me, calling me a cutting board."

"Pfft, you're still hung up on that? All I said was 'what modest breasts you have.'"

"You……!"

"……True, Mia might be a bit smaller than Krische."

Krische looked at her own chest, then tilted her head at the frozen Mia.

Snickering, Kalua took Krische's hand, and with that, Krische headed for the tent.

# Chapter 77: Hilkintos of the West

During the march — Krische's forces marched from dawn to dusk.

They finished meals in the darkness before dawn, and began setting camp after sunset.

Not quite a forced march, but a swift-paced one.

The time spent on making and breaking camp increased, but it gained them roughly two koku more in distance than usual. (T/N: One koku ≈ approximately 2 hours.)

However, to keep fatigue to a minimum, the marching speed itself was unchanged.

The important thing was to make it appear they were in a hurry.

Once they had gained a certain distance, the enemy scouts would calculate from the daily march distance and conclude that they were force-marching on their own.

Having crossed westward through the plains of the kingdom's center and entered the complex forested zone, Krische looked up at the sky and spoke to Dagra.

"……The look of the sky. Rain's coming, Vulture."

"So it seems."

The shape of the clouds, their position, changes in wind and humidity.

To her words, drawn from vast knowledge, Dagra agreed based on his long years of experience.

Predicting the weather was an extremely important skill for a soldier.

"What shall we do?"

Asked by Dagra, Krische thought for a moment.

She wanted to keep the soldiers' fatigue to a minimum, but the proper balance was difficult for Krische.

"Rain wears soldiers out more than you'd imagine. ……We've covered a decent distance so far. Perhaps it would be best to let them rest early today and have them cook."

Dagra immediately picked up on this and spoke, and Krische nodded.

The words of an experienced, excellent centurion were closer to reality than anyone else's.

Because he was in a position of constant contact with the soldiers, he was intimately familiar with how marching, rest, and small things affected them.

That was the primary reason Krische trusted Dagra and kept him at her side.

Years of experience were one reliable basis for judgment.

Krische did not take them lightly.

She understood the limits of knowledge gained from books, and did not think she could understand how soldiers felt.

"In about one koku, we should come to a somewhat open area. Let's make that today's rest stop."

"Yes, ma'am. Tagel, send out two fast runners. Dispatch messengers to Corps Commander Faren and Corps Commander Varkus. And one to Battalion Commander Kiltins as well. Anyone will do for that one."

Captain Tagel immediately raised his voice and called two names.

Inside the forest, using them was faster and less trouble than horses for relaying messages.

From Krische's group at the head of the column to the rear was a considerable distance, and driving horses along the poor-footed edge of the road caused significant fatigue.

Messages within the First Corps were relayed through Third Battalion Commander Keith Kiltins, who served as de facto adjutant.

That could go to anyone conveniently nearby, just behind them.

Krische had been making Keith handle even trivial matters, entrusting him with command relay throughout the corps.

By having Keith routinely issue instructions to the entire First Corps, the coordination between him and the other battalion commanders was strengthened, and the relationship of commander and commanded was established.

This would preemptively resolve the organizational and psychological problems that could arise when Keith actually took command on the battlefield — such was the intent of this arrangement, proposed by Elrouga.

Keith's control of the corps was essential for Krische to move freely, and Krische had been happily dumping various troublesome tasks onto Keith using Elrouga's proposal as justification.

Keith, for his part, regarded it as an honor in itself, so no particular problems had arisen thus far.

"But rain, you say. That's good."

"……?"

"Krische had several candidate locations for the place to lure them into, but this settles it."

Krische said it with something like enjoyment.

Aulgorn Sakizren Nilkrinea Hilkintos.

He had single-handedly borne the defense against invasions from the Elderant Kingdom to the kingdom's west for many years, and in the recent war, had liberated the occupied Wulfenite.

In his battles, there were no dramatic reversals from disadvantage.

Neither bold offensives nor brilliant tactics — he always built superiority in terrain and troop strength, and brought about the outcome with that advantage intact.

Steady and orthodox: he believed that this was what brought victory, and his unbroken record of success came from executing it with absolute thoroughness.

His strength lay in a cold-bloodedness that would discard sentiment and commit everything for the sake of victory.

After confirming that Aulgorn had withdrawn troops from the forest side, they had emerged.

To decide the outcome.

With war cries they bore down on the center — and by the time the vanguard cavalry crashed into the enemy line, black-armored soldiers who had cut through from the south struck the center as well.

15,000 of Christand against 27,000 of Hilkintos — such were the numbers when this battle began.

The Hilkintos army held an overwhelming numerical advantage, and yet — the result was the utter and complete defeat of the Hilkintos army.

# Chapter 80: The Dictates of the Battlefield

Dead: Christand 2,300. Hilkintos 10,200.

A battle begun with Christand at a numerical disadvantage ended, however, in their overwhelming victory.

The final troop count slightly favored Christand, and for a battle begun in such disparity, the result could only be called overwhelming.

But the Christand right wing had suffered heavily. The Fourth Battalion under Barga had sustained over sixty percent casualties and been effectively annihilated. Barga himself was lightly wounded, and seven of his centurions were lost.

Maintaining the line while taking enemy arrows, with no archer support of their own.

If anything, the fact that thirty percent survived could be considered a fine result.

The Fourth Battalion required reorganization, but many of the soldiers had been insufficiently trained to begin with.

The thirty percent who survived could be regarded as excellent soldiers who had returned from hell, and would serve as the pillars of the Fourth Battalion going forward. Viewed as rough-hewn live training, the outcome was not bad.

Krische, as in the previous battle, busied herself with prisoner handling, reorganization arrangements, and miscellaneous duties, and come evening.

She visited a certain tent.

In the center — lying on the bed was Dagra.

His brow was furrowed, groaning with closed eyes, sleeping with a pained expression.

Around the bed, soldiers in black-painted armor stood in a ring.

Krische stared at him, furrowed her brow, and glanced around.

"……Unclean air is said to be bad for wounds. Outside."

"Yes. ……Everyone, as you heard. I'll stay by his——"

"Krische will be here for a while. Everyone, outside."

The tone was cold, permitting no argument.

Mia flinched for an instant at Krische's demeanor, then lowered her eyes sadly and saluted.

One by one they filed out of the tent.

After they left, Krische slowly traced her fingers along Dagra's flank.

A stab wound through the belly. Not shallow by any means.

The field surgeon had said whether he survived the night would be the deciding factor.

"Vulture is a fool. Since it was Krische they were aiming at, you should have just left it alone."

She said it like a sigh, and closed her eyes.

Surrounded, Aulgorn had been helpless.

Aulgorn's individual skill was not particularly low, but the situation had already passed the point where one act of reckless courage could reverse anything.

Begil, commanding cavalry and light infantry, had struck the Hilkintos center from behind with over a thousand men, every one of them crack troops of the First Corps — Aulgorn, for all his ability, could see their quality at a glance.

A unit that maintained discipline and coordination even in disordered lines was, by that fact alone, a sign of high caliber.

Before First Battalion Commander Begil, who pressed a sword to him and demanded surrender, Aulgorn ground his teeth.

Fighting still continued across the field.

To minimize damage, either a surrender or his head was needed.

Further resistance was meaningless — the only question was whether his head would be raised or not.

It was a total defeat.

Aulgorn dismounted bitterly, set down his spear, and knelt.

Seeing this, the standard-bearers around him lowered their banners — and the others followed suit.

"I swear upon the name of Begil Rinea Sandika, First Battalion Commander of the Christand army, and upon the name of the Kingdom — Her Highness the Princess — to uphold the Sacred Convention. That was a valiant fight, worthy of honor, General Hilkintos."

Aulgorn did not answer.

From just beyond the line of soldiers, a silver-haired girl appeared with black-armored troops in tow.

Krische — the defeated general received a single glance from her purple eyes, and their gazes met.

"……Under the Sacred Convention, you and yours are taken as prisoners. Naturally, Krische swears upon this name to protect your rights as a margrave of the kingdom. ……Krische asks that you accompany us for a time. As you know, we must head to the Dragon's Maw."

That was all she said to Aulgorn.

The bare minimum of words.

Showing not a fragment of interest, Krische turned her gaze to Begil.

"Begil, dispatch cavalry to announce the outcome to all units."

"Yes, ma'am!"

Even after the outcome was transmitted by mana-amplified voice, small skirmishes continued ahead.

Ordering Christand banners raised, Krische issued instructions one after another.

Without pause, as though everything had been predetermined.

She had utterly defeated the Western Hilkintos — the unbeatable general — and yet showed not a trace of lingering in victory.

The girl had dealt Aulgorn a total defeat and likely considered it nothing more than the natural outcome.

Like an insect, like a roadside pebble — what she had given him was a cold, indifferent glance.

Aulgorn watched her, and dark things settled into his eyes.

"Ha ha ha, a perfect victory, Krische-sama. ……My, with victories this thorough coming one after another, I'm afraid it might breed overconfidence."

Granmeld, who had finished his portion of the cleanup faster than the rest owing to the situation, approached Krische with a laugh.

"Yes, it went well. Woof-woof did a very good job too."

The black century, and the soldiers nearby, all jerked and stared at Krische.

*Woof-woof*.

The atmosphere froze for an instant, and all eyes turned to the speaker.

"Since Woof-woof did such a good job splitting them, it was so easy for Krische's group to……ah."

Krische tilted her head at the stares.

Only then did she realize her slip, hastily covering her mouth and darting her eyes about.

Then she stared up at Granmeld.

Granmeld, stared up at, was so bewildered he had frozen mid-smile.

"Um, so, Krische has been thinking she'd like to give Corps Commander Varkus a nickname too, and, well, it just slipped out before Krische asked permission……"

She pressed both hands to her cheeks and wiggled shyly, as if embarrassed by her own breach of etiquette.

Her silver hair swayed like a tail — an utterly out-of-place, adorable sight for a battlefield.

"I-I, uh, I see……that is, so……woof-woof is?"

Recalling how he had laughed at the name "Skeleton" the other day, Granmeld broke into a cold sweat.

He was a warrior. Granmeld Varkus was one of the kingdom's foremost warriors.

He took pride in that.

Anyone who denied it, anyone who mocked it — he had silenced them with fist and war mace.

Proving his strength, surviving death, smashing countless enemies——

"Yes, a nickname!"

——And it was *woof-woof.*

Utterly terrible.

"See, your grin is kind of dog-like, ehehe, and when Krische saw it, it just went *zing*. There were lots of candidates — like wufwuf, or grrr — but Krische really thinks woof-woof fits best."

In high spirits, Krische sidled up and gazed happily up at Granmeld.

Unlike the look she had given Aulgorn — it was a heated, upward gaze.

This was tough.

Even the stalwart Granmeld was powerless against the sparkling purple eyes of this girl gazing up at him.

Before this slender girl whose head barely reached his chest, Granmeld inched backward.

"Come on, can Krische call you Woof-woof? Ehehe, you can call Krische whatever you like too. Selene told Krische to get along with all sorts of people——"

Sensing pity in that scene, Dagra sighed and approached the enemy general, Aulgorn Hilkintos, surrounded by soldiers.

"……I'm sorry, General, but I must bind you."

The seated Aulgorn turned his protruding eyes from beneath his goddess-faced visor.

Eyes filled with rage and hatred.

The defeated general, ensnared by strategy — understandable, Dagra thought.

He continued in as soft a tone as possible, trying not to provoke.

"It will be an inconvenience for a time, but please bear with it. We will confiscate the sword at your hip, but in accordance with the Sacred Convention, it will be returned through proper procedure in due course."

He signaled with his eyes to the two subordinates beside the general.

Two black-armored soldiers approached Aulgorn with rope.

And Aulgorn's bloodshot eyes fixed on something behind Dagra.

— What he felt in that instant was unmistakable killing intent.

"——Restrain him!!"

Dagra shouted and leapt back.

But his voice was too late. Aulgorn surged to his feet and drew the sword at his hip.

He beheaded one of the two who had been about to bind him.

Without losing momentum, he charged past Dagra — toward Krische.

— *Why should I endure the humiliation of captivity?*

Unforgivable. Why?

Unbearable humiliation and rage. These tangled emotions were directed at a single girl.

*"……Under the Sacred Convention, you and yours are taken as prisoners. Naturally, Krische swears upon this name to protect your rights as a kingdom noble. ……Krische asks that you accompany us for a time. As you know, we must head to the Dragon's Maw."*

Having stated that one-sidedly, she had shown not a fragment of interest in Aulgorn.

Like looking at livestock, at vermin, at a roadside pebble — Krische Christand harbored no feeling whatsoever toward Aulgorn Hilkintos.

She had looked down on him as worthless.

Not Elrouga Faren. Not Granmeld Varkus.

It was this girl who had orchestrated this entire battle.

He did not consider himself incompetent. But the fact was, the girl before him had surpassed him.

That she was called a genius was no lie — fine, he could accept that.

But the fact that she, Aulgorn Hilkintos — a name known to every soul in the kingdom, victor of countless battles — had been looked at with *those* eyes. That alone was unforgivable.

An unbearable disgrace.

And on top of that, she intended to parade his captive self at the Dragon's Maw and humiliate him.

As a pathetically defeated, incompetent general.

She would smear everything — his reputation, his legacy — in filth and leave it to rot.

Without so much as changing expression, she would strip away every shred of pride Aulgorn possessed.

— That alone he could not tolerate.

He would not permit this slip of a girl to negate everything these decades had stood for.

It was not reason.

Rejecting the defilement of his name more than death itself, Aulgorn pressed forward.

The girl, who had been smiling at Granmeld, turned her eyes back to Aulgorn.

Even seeing him charging toward her, she showed no surprise.

She merely gazed at Aulgorn, coldly, as if looking down.

Fury enough to crack the teeth he was grinding.

He directed all of it at the girl — and was knocked aside from the flank.

"Halt!! Don't disgrace yourself!!"

A man in black-painted armor, a hook-nosed man — Dagra.

The target of his rage shifted.

To the man who had interfered.

"Do you know whose hand you raise against!!"

"Guh——!?"

Even in white-hot fury, his sword was sure.

Against the veteran centurion, the blade feinted and plunged — into the flank.

But his opponent was veteran too.

As Aulgorn tried to wrench the deeply embedded blade free, Centurion Dagra seized his arm with brute force and locked it.

"Damn you——ugh, gh!?"

And then — an instant.

The arm gripping the blade — its sensation was suddenly, entirely lost.

He felt no pain.

His right arm — upper arm and all — had been severed clean through the steel gauntlet.

He lost his balance and toppled — and his neck was seized in the girl's slender, gauntleted right hand, lifted into the air.

— What Aulgorn's eyes found was an inorganic purple glow.

A darkness visible behind the cold gleam.

What Aulgorn felt peering into it was fear.

"……Mia, treat Vulture immediately."

The voice was the polar opposite of the tone she had used moments ago with Granmeld — cold.

Kalua caught the collapsing Dagra, and Mia scrambled to issue orders.

"Y-yes! Bilza, emergency treatment!"

"Y-yes!"

"Adol, Bagu, help Kalua! Kels, bring a field surgeon, as fast as possible! Priority!"

Krische's eyes did not waver from Aulgorn for an instant.

Her expression was like a doll's — utterly blank.

"Krische-sama, I understand how you feel, but if you keep that up, he'll die. We need to stop the bleeding first. After all the trouble of capturing him, causing more casualties and then just killing him would be completely pointless."

Granmeld was the only one who could speak to the girl in this state.

He traced the scar on his cheek with displeasure as he looked at Aulgorn.

"……You're right."

"Gah, aggh……!?"

The pale-faced Aulgorn was flung aside, and then his remaining left arm was shattered by the reinforced heel of her boot.

His ear-splitting shriek drew not a flicker of emotion from that endlessly cold face.

Granmeld grimaced at the treatment, then used a knife to cut Aulgorn's mantle, tying off the severed right arm over the mail.

A rough tourniquet with no thought for later.

Then he examined the cross-section of the arm.

Elegantly engraved armor.

The steel that had encased the arm was flawlessly bisected.

As if it had been made that way from the start.

And Krische's curved sword bore not a single nick despite cutting through steel — Granmeld looked again at her ability and felt a chill.

"Mia, assume temporary command in Vulture's place."

"……Yes."

"Bind that one and gag him. Cauterize the stump to stop the blood. Someone take care of it."

"……I will."

It was Bagu.

The usually grinning, lighthearted face was suffused with fury.

Having no parents, he had respected Dagra as a true father.

"……Killing is not permitted. Nor is abuse. This one will be executed in due course, but until then, regardless of anything, a prisoner's treatment applies. Krische does not permit violations of the Sacred Convention. Punishment for the crime must be imposed through sentencing, at least in form."

"……Yes, ma'am."

"That is all. Take a full rest. We run from tomorrow morning."

She looked at the prone Dagra.

The embedded sword could not yet be removed. It could only be held in place while he lay still.

Sufficient salt and alcohol had to be prepared, and the field surgeon awaited.

Watching this, Krische sighed quietly.

She was concerned, but there were things that needed doing.

"Mia, I leave this to you. Krische still has skirmishes to settle."

"Yes, please be careful."

* * *

— Inside the tent, Krische quietly breathed as she stroked Dagra's forehead.

Dagra should have simply left it alone when Aulgorn had charged toward her.

But it had been a reflexive reaction, she supposed.

It was the same as Grace.

If he had just stayed still, it would have ended without incident.

But that simply could not be helped.

Krische was Krische, and she could not freely control their wills.

If everyone could act on reason alone, Krische would have already ended the war.

*Humans are like that*, she understood in a vague way.

Knowing that, she couldn't blame him. But she wasn't in the mood to praise him either.

If anything, she wanted to scold him.

For that, he needed to be alive.

"……Bery probably wouldn't be angry."

She murmured, and closed her eyes.

She extended the mana that clung to her, reaching toward the bandaged area.

A faint — pale blue light filled the tent.

Countless geometric patterns floated within it.

She perceived the inner wound through mana, grasping the torn flesh inside.

The surface had been cleaned with alcohol and salt, sewn and pressure-bandaged, but the inside was different.

Krische perceived it through mana and narrowed her eyes slightly.

There was a small tear in the intestine.

Mana-users converted food into mana before it was excreted.

Dagra too wielded mana, and so no waste was leaking from the wound.

That was fortunate amid misfortune.

She was not as ignorant as she had once been.

Bogan's death, and the recent experience of badly damaging both her hands, feet, and body during the rest period, had prompted her to read some medical texts and study.

Wounds had to be cleaned with alcohol or salt and kept sterile; lacerations had to be sealed.

Otherwise they would fester, become inflamed, and through such areas new *jaki* — harmful agents — would invade and ravage the body. (T/N: 邪気 (jaki) is a traditional concept referring to malign or infectious agents — roughly equivalent to infection/contamination in modern terms.)

A healthy body could resist such jaki, but a wounded body could not.

She had not tested it herself, but sensing the amount of blood pooling inside, she could not leave it alone.

This was her first time actually applying such knowledge, but it was better than doing nothing.

The principle was the same as operating imaginary muscles.

She carefully manipulated the torn tissue inside Dagra's abdomen, reshaping it to what it should have been.

Carefully, slowly.

*If there's a tear, simply press it shut physically* — that was the idea.

It was no different in principle from suturing a wound.

"Ugh……"

Dagra groaned faintly in pain, and Krische took care not to burden him.

When that was done, she next wove a formula inside Dagra's body.

Heating, cooling, emitting light.

The same principle as the spells carved into magic crystals.

She inscribed a formula with the mana she had threaded into Dagra's interior.

To maintain the sealed state of the wound.

The geometric patterns that had been spread around the tent seemed to be drawn inward, flowing into Dagra, and when it was done, Krische felt somewhat unsteady.

Constructing and operating imaginary muscles did not consume much energy, but manipulating mana externally like this was far more draining.

The more complex the formula, the greater the mana consumption.

For Krische, who constantly moved her body through mana, it was not negligible. She leaned against the edge of the bed, settling her listless body.

"……Perhaps Krische should properly practice this sort of thing."

Just as Vulture had been wounded, Selene could be wounded the same way.

A moment's carelessness could easily result in injury.

Bery and Kreschenta were not safe either.

— It was very different from when Grace had died.

*A shame. It can't be helped.*

To think that way and discard it was easy.

But if she had reflected properly back then and studied, she might have been able to provide this kind of treatment now.

Perhaps she could even have saved Bogan.

But by discarding it as 'it can't be helped,' she felt she had also discarded the very opportunity to reflect.

She decided to discuss it properly with Bery next time they met.

These past few months had produced nothing but things she ought to reflect on.

She should have thought of solutions for each and every one.

— Whose fault was this? The general's.

But she could not say none of the cause lay with her.

The root cause was allowing him the delusion that 'a surprise attack could kill Krische.'

She had allowed him the arrogance of thinking he could kill Krische.

As a result, Vulture's belly had been stabbed for nothing.

She thought she should have simply slaughtered everyone present on the spot.

This time it was Vulture. But Krische had many precious things.

And perhaps more would come.

Mia, Kalua, Skeleton — gradually.

Physically silencing the enemy, rendering them unable to grip a sword, was not a bad method.

But killing them all that way was difficult.

There were limits to what Krische could do alone.

And using others inevitably meant casualties.

She needed to make them more afraid.

Afraid enough that they would not even conceive of resistance. She should make examples of every person who defied her, killing them for all to see.

Teach them thoroughly what happened to those who disobeyed, and make it known far and wide.

Someday, those who would raise a sword against Krische would disappear, and casualties would decrease greatly.

That was what armies existed for in the first place.

That was what armies were permitted to do.

"Bunny, Skeleton corps commander is calling for you."

"……Yes. Coming."

A voice from outside, and Krische stood.

Outside, black-armored soldiers were watching the tent and Krische with concern.

Dagra was strict, yet kind.

He tightened discipline where it was needed, but always considered his men's feelings and guided them.

There was not a single soldier in this unit who did not respect him.

And it was not only Dagra — one soldier had died a meaningless death as well.

In the middle of battle, it could be accepted as unavoidable, but after the victory, it was a different story.

The soldiers' faces were dark.

"Regardless, Vulture will rest for a while. Even if he becomes able to move, further participation in this campaign is impossible. Temporary commanding officer will be Adjutant Mia. Considering her record, she is adequate if not ideal. Is that acceptable?"

Mia thought for a moment, then looked at the two captains, Korintz and Tagel.

Both nodded gravely, and she turned to face Krische.

"……Yes. Then, as acting commander for Captain Dagra, I will take charge of this unit."

"Adjutant equivalents will be the captains Korintz and Tagel, as with a normal century. Unlike Vulture, Mia can be a bit dim, so please support her."

"Yes, ma'am."

A faint laugh at Krische's words, and Mia flushed with embarrassment.

"For Vulture……Bilza's Seventeenth Squad is best suited. You'll handle his care until he's sent to the rear."

"Yes, ma'am."

Slim young Bilza came from a family of herbalists, and while not a doctor, had done something close to doctoring in his village.

Tending to the wounded was usually Bilza's role.

Perhaps because of that experience, the Seventeenth Squad was accustomed to emergency treatment.

"That is all. Dismissed. Starting tomorrow, everyone except the Seventeenth runs. Get to bed early tonight."

Krische exhaled, and Kalua handed her a bag of nirkana.

"Thought you might be a little hungry."

"……Thank you."

She thanked her, and Kalua asked curiously.

"Bunny, were you doing something in the tent earlier?"

"No. What is it?"

A fluctuation of mana — that was probably what she had sensed.

Krische shook her head calmly.

She had never felt guilt about lying.

"Hm……maybe I imagined it. Nah, never mind. It's a bit far — wanna borrow a horse?"

"……Krische will walk."

"Horse-hater……really such a brat."

Kalua sighed with exasperation, then stroked Krische's head.

"Well, don't worry about Eagle Captain. He's stubborn enough."

"Yes. ……Krische hopes so."

"He adores bunny, after all. He won't die without seeing your face."

"Kalua, that's just……"

"I'd prefer 'kind,' thanks."

*Probably fine.*

But there was no certainty.

Because she was fond of Dagra, the thought of losing him was unwelcome.

She hoped what she had done would work.

Something swirled in her chest — her emotions were unsettled, most likely.

And so, the sensation of being stroked was comforting.

Krische quietly leaned into her.

Kalua smiled, said *it'll be fine,* and put an arm around her shoulders.
