# Chapter 296: A Sigh — II

『Hm hm, it looks better the more I look at it. Shall we hold a banquet here tonight, to mark the occasion?』

『Yes. Ufufu, I thought you would say that, my lady, so I prepared accordingly.』

『……You are, as always, infuriatingly prepared.』

The hut built beside the hollow of the holy spirit was quite something.

Wooden stakes driven firmly into the earth, clay walls and sturdy pillars.

Large enough for seven to sleep and live, and she was in high spirits about making it her residence from now on.

The commemorative banquet was truly delightful — however.

『What is it, Lila?』

『Ah, um…… Kushenalars-sama came, and the wing-beats upon landing…… the, the hut……』

One year's effort — gone, one week after its completion.

The handmade furniture vanished along with the hut, swept away by a dragon's wingbeats.

Selene spent three days sulking indoors.

『Hm hm, this one is sturdier than the last. Surely even your needlessly troublesome wing-beats won't bring it down……』

『Oh? Shall I test that?』

『Wha—? Wait just a moment! It isn't finished yet——』

A year and a half spent, a second and sturdier hut built upon the ruins.

Kushenalars blew it away again, with completion almost in reach.

Selene spent three days sulking indoors.

Then a third, a fourth, a fifth, a sixth — all destroyed by a dragon's wingbeats.

The seventh hollow-hut abandoned any attachment to wood and was built entirely of refined stone, and this one proved far more durable. It lacked visual charm, but it was solid — it survived for some two hundred years, until seven dragons gathered at once, and one of them stepped on it without noticing.

Selene, who had been mid-renovation, spent three days sulking indoors, as she always did.

The eighth was set close against the cliff face to avoid being trodden on, likewise in refined stone.

It aimed to be less a hut and more a country cottage, and this too was coming along quite nicely — until the tremors from a landing shook the cliff and brought it down.

Undeterred, Selene spent thirty years excavating a hollow in the cliffside, widening and reinforcing it — building out the interior framework to rival the great temple of Arna.

The exterior entrance kept the look of a hut, but the interior had been expanded into something resembling a small manor, and the expansion was still ongoing.

A disaster struck when flying debris from a wing-beat landed squarely on the entrance and brought it down — but it was repaired, and it lived on, intact, to this day.

And it was not only the hut.

『Hm hm, what do you think? I rather think this turned out well.』

『My, my. It is lovely. Then…… let us see, shall we put it by the entrance?』

Selene made vases and ornaments and all manner of things, and

『S-Selene-sama……』

『What is it, Anne?』

『I am so, so sorry……! I was polishing Selene-sama's vase and my hand slipped and…… it went with the horse figurine beside it……!』

— and Anne destroyed them.

『……My lady, about the Miilshu villa……』

『Miilshu? Ah…… that one. Something the matter?』

『I went to check on it after some time, and, well, mana crystals appeared to have grown up through the floor from the earth below…… from underneath the floorboards…… I am so sorry. I have already gone to confirm the floors in the other villas and removed the crystals that had grown in the meantime.』

The other villas too collapsed of their own accord.

Selene had by now lived through any number of moments when the fruits of her labour crumbled in an instant.

Each time she had gotten back up, rallied herself, and continued.

A few setbacks — she was well past the point of being troubled by such things——

"Aaah……"

——Or so one might expect.

A misfired blow had sent a deep crack running through the nearly finished stone statue.

Selene pressed both hands and both knees to the floor and let out a plaintive groan.

"Selene-sama is as clumsy as ever, I see. You cannot even carve a stone that cannot move properly."

"I *was* carving it! You shouted at the worst possible moment and this happened!"

"That is an excuse. Neither onee-sama nor I would commit such a blunder."

"You……!"

She shot Kreschenta a glare, but the girl took not the slightest notice. She surveyed the crack in the statue with mild interest, then turned a disinterested gaze on Selene.

"Never mind that — do listen to this."

"Never mind that? Of course I mind! Why should I have to sit here listening to your tiresome complaints while I am in *this* state? It is going to be something like Krische and Bery snubbing you while they pack the wagon, is it not."

"It is not tiresome!"

"Looking at it from any angle, it is entirely tiresome……"

Tomorrow they were going into town.

The two were packing the wagon for that reason today.

Anne was probably helping.

Depending on which district they were selling in, they prepared around five large carts, sorting through wine casks and produce, the swords and silver-worked pieces Selene had forged, and especially the finer wines which needed to be portioned into individual bottles and labelled — there was no small amount of work involved.

Kreschenta's version of helping was mostly *Please help me, Krische, please help me, Bery, please help me Krische-Bery, please help me Bery-Bery-Krische* — the time spent pestering people and getting in the way consistently outnumbered the time actually helping, and whenever things came to that, when Kreschenta stopped being fussed over, she would pout and drift over to Selene.

The usual sequence of events.

For something that tiresome to cause three months of effort to crumble so casually — it was truly something.

And yet she found she could not even be bothered to complain.

Kreschenta was on the level of Gururun — a somewhat cleverer animal.

Getting properly upset at everything the girl did would be exhausting.

Selene let out a long sigh and kneaded the bridge of her nose.

She had no will left for another attempt at the statue, not right now. She made her way to the workshop.

"Wait! Do not ignore me!"

Kreschenta pattered along behind her, grumbling all the while.

She was less a person than, truly, a pet.

They apparently still spoke of her as the greatest ruler in history over there — and every time Selene caught word of it, she could only marvel at the gap between the legend and the reality before her.

That this little child had governed a kingdom — Selene could not quite believe it.

Though when she thought back, the girl had managed to conceal it remarkably well.

Of course, like Krische, Kreschenta's mind was something inhuman, so far beyond Bery as to put her at the bottom of that measure — but she was catastrophically foolish and childish. Entirely self-centred from first to last, interested in nothing beyond her own desires, and with the sensibility of a five-year-old.

She had slightly more cunning than Krische, which meant she was better at keeping up appearances — but now that she no longer had any need for appearances, all that remained was the sensibility of a foolish five-year-old.

Back in the other world she had seemed at least marginally more adult than Krische — since coming here that had reversed.

Selene entered the workshop and settled onto the half-finished sofa. Kreschenta climbed sideways into her lap.

Selene sighed again and stroked her head.

Kreschenta's cheeks relaxed in evident satisfaction, as if to say, *this is how it should be.*

Selene sprawled lazily back across the sofa, and Kreschenta nestled into her like a second layer.

She cupped the girl's cheeks in her palms and kissed her slowly.

"……Hmph, very well. If you must, I suppose I can keep you company while you nap, you sulky little princess."

"Do not treat me as though I am sulking. Though, since Selene-sama wants me to stay and sleep beside her, I shall oblige."

Kreschenta answered with a cheerful little smile and pressed her face into Selene's chest.

The sight of it was oddly endearing. In the dim light of the workshop, beautiful golden hair glimmered faintly red, and Selene pressed her lips into it.

"All right, all right. Kreschenta-sama, would you be so kind as to stay and sleep with me?"

"Ufufu, if you insist."

She answered with satisfaction and closed her eyes. Selene put her left hand behind her head as a pillow.

Then she reached with one hand for the blanket draped over the back of the sofa, pulled it down and spread it over them, and sighed.

She found herself wondering how many sighs she had let out since coming here, and patted Kreschenta's back gently.

The sound of breathing grew drowsy and then became the sound of sleep in an instant — it was about the right time for a nap, it seemed.

Kreschenta slept splendidly. She went to bed before anyone else, woke up after everyone else, and once the sun had passed its peak she would be due for another nap.

Why she had come here — most likely Krische had put her out for pestering her in her sleep, or something like that.

Setting aside the morning's second sleep, Kreschenta always wanted to sleep beside someone.

Selene poked her plump little cheek and the girl wrinkled her brow. She watched that and smiled faintly.

Looking at her now and recalling the first unsettling impression — she had changed quite a lot.

Selene had seldom asked about who Kreschenta had been before they met.

It would hardly have been pleasant for her to recall.

Apart from talk of the servant called Nora, Kreschenta never brought up those days herself. Perhaps this was a kind of rebound from a childhood like that.

She grew younger as the years passed — and now she had arrived at a permanent childhood.

Every day, as free as could be, without a single trace of restraint. If anyone was making the most of eternity, it was surely Kreschenta.

Selene stroked her hair and closed her own eyes.

She hoped that when she woke, the shock of earlier would have faded from her mind.

* * *

A room — inside her mother's lap.

Her mother appeared mid-lesson and said, *The studying is over for today, Selene*, then lifted her from the chair by the waist and sat on the sofa with Selene in her arms.

As usual, Bery spoke a word of mild protest. As usual, her mother paid it no mind and banged the table with her palm, demanding cookies, demanding tea, making herself at home.

And then, as always, she said the same thing to Selene.

"Selene, you must not become the sort of person who thinks of nothing but difficult things the way Bery does. If you turn out like her, all you will ever be able to do is complain."

"……Nee-sama."

Bery let out an exasperated sigh, but prepared the tea and cookies for her mother all the same.

In those days, Bery must have been sighing every single day.

And yet for some reason — being imposed upon by this woman, subjected to her selfishness at every turn — Bery always looked strangely happy. Selene had wondered about that, even then.

On days when her mother did not come, there were cookies for two.

On days when her mother came, cookies for three, without fail.

Bery was always perfect, and never made a mistake.

The complete opposite of her casual, careless mother — whatever she set her hand to was faultless — and yet her mother spoke of her as something to guard against.

From any angle, Bery was clearly the more admirable one, but her mother always had an inexplicable confidence about herself.

"The only question worth asking in life is whether you are enjoying yourself or not. What matters is that you sometimes throw your studies aside, take a day off, and do as you please. Selene takes after me; she is clever. Even with only so-much studying, once you are grown you will manage well enough, just like your okaa-sama."

"So-much……"

"Haa, nee-sama. When ojou-sama is finally in a studious mood, why would you say something to undermine it……"

"Your job may be to raise Selene into a proper Christand daughter, but my job is to spoil her and dote on her. This much more than balances us out."

*That seems rather unfair*, Bery sighed and settled beside them, shoulders drooping.

"Selene takes after Bogan in stubborn places. I worry she might decide she wants to be like you. You try too hard to be a good example for her at every turn — she cannot afford to think you are normal. Extraordinary as you are, if you become what she sees as ordinary, that will be nothing short of a tragedy."

"……I believe, in its own way, taking after nee-sama's sloppiness as one's standard for ordinary would also be something of a tragedy……"

"How rude. What kind of way is that to speak to your onee-sama."

"……I do not believe nee-sama has ever once demonstrated what propriety looks like to me."

"That is only natural, Bery. An elder sister is the one to whom her younger sister offers propriety from one side only. It does not go the other way."

Her mother's reasoning was always outrageous.

In any argument, Bery would invariably say the sensible thing — but she could never get the better of her mother, who would hold forth with self-assured nonsense, heedless of logic, and simply refuse to hear otherwise.

"A question for Selene. Between a person with great knowledge and a person with little, which is the more admirable?"

"Umm…… the one with more……"

"And what about between a person who calculates quickly and a person who is slow?"

"……? The quick one……"

"That is fundamentally wrong. Neither is correct. Whether one's knowledge is great or small, whether one calculates quickly or slowly — none of that has anything to do with being admirable."

Bery pressed a hand to her forehead in exasperation and sighed again.

Her mother continued.

"If sheer ability were what made a person admirable, Bery would have become queen by now. Such things are trivial and unimportant — of course it is better to have them than not, but you can be perfectly admirable without them. Just like this okaa-sama of yours."

"Ye, yes……"

"If you lack knowledge, you ask someone who has it; if you are slow at calculating, you leave the calculations to someone who is good at them. You do not need to become perfectly capable yourself — you simply ask various people for help."

Her mother said this while squeezing Selene's waist tight and nuzzling her hair with her cheek.

"In turn, Selene, you will help those people when they are struggling. If you can do that with everyone in the world, then no matter what problem stands before you, you can solve it. The world's most clever person, the world's strongest person, the world's wealthiest person — they would be on your side. Any problem would be easy with friends like that, would it not?"

"That…… might be so……"

"The most admirable person in the world, if there is such a one, is exactly that kind of person. Your beloved, admirable father, whom you respect so much — the reason he manages such difficult work is that he found so many people he could rely on. It is not about being clever, or being good with a sword."

*Do you understand?* her mother asked. Selene nodded, half-comprehending.

Her mother smiled warmly and hugged Selene like a stuffed toy.

"Good. Then the first thing you must do is start by being more spoiled with me and Bery, and getting closer to us. Difficult studies and the like are fine to do only so-much."

"So-much……"

"For anything troublesome, just ask Bery and she will handle it. I do the same. Bery will happily do whatever I ask. She will happily do whatever you ask too."

"……You know, nee-sama——"

"Do not try to interrupt and throw me off while I am giving Selene such very good maternal advice."

And to Bery, who was sighing and trying to edge in a word: "You. You just nod and say *yes, quite right* and that is all I need from you," her mother commanded, as a matter of course.

"Well, Selene is a hard worker, and that is one of her good qualities — but the world is wide. No matter how hard you try, there will always be someone somewhere you cannot surpass. And there will be problems in your future that no amount of effort alone can solve. ……The important thing is that when that time comes, you have found many people you can rely upon."

"……Yes."

"In that respect, you are fortunate. You have an okaa-sama as admirable as me, a Bery who can resolve difficult things effortlessly, and the kingdom's finest general as your otou-sama. From the moment you were born you have been blessed with more people to rely upon than anyone else."

*Okaa-sama's precious lesson is over*, her mother said, and ate a cookie.

What her mother told Selene again and again, pressing it on her insistently — was things like that.

She had thought she understood. But probably she had not.

The true understanding of her mother's words came to her decades and decades later.

Her mother had been a very clever person, she thought.

Not in Bery's way — without the breadth of knowledge, the effortless facility for everything, the quick mind. She was lazy and slapdash, rough in everything she did.

But she was always herself, and she saw a great many things.

If Selene had properly understood her mother's lessons, she would not have been carried away by her emotions, and most of her worries would have dissolved with startling ease.

But Selene chased after Bery's back, not quite reaching it. Chased after Krische's back. Chased after Kreschenta's back. She simply ran and ran.

She had only needed to take the hand held out to her, and instead she was stubborn and pushed herself too hard.

Her mother would have scolded her for that, she was certain. *You silly thing.* Those children don't want anything like that from you.

And indeed it proved to be exactly as her mother said.

When Selene left herself in the hands of the world's most clever person, and the world's strongest person, and the world's wealthiest person — all the problems before her were resolved with astonishing ease.

Selene became the greatest Grand Marshal in the world, and for a time war vanished from the continent, and now she was living happily like this with the people she loved.

What she had worried about had been so simple it seemed almost foolish in retrospect.

She did not think she had surpassed her father. She did not think she had surpassed her mother.

She had not become as clever as Bery, had not become like her.

But such things were trivial, and very unimportant.

Selene had only ever been Selene, and whether she loved or hated herself, Selene could be no more and no less than Selene.

That was a given, and it was surely the same for Bery.

Bery must have known she could never become like her mother, even as she admired her from the bottom of her heart — and perhaps that was precisely why she loved her the way she did.

Krische and Kreschenta and everyone else — it was the same. No person anywhere had everything, and perhaps that was why they were drawn to one another and lived to fill in each other's gaps.

The only question worth asking in life is whether you are enjoying yourself or not.

Her mother's words had been extreme and rather self-serving — but they were surely an answer she had found after looking squarely at her own lack, too.

You can only reach that answer once you have stopped wishing for things you were never going to have.

"I should say this to you too, Bery, not just Selene. Try just once leaving your work and spending a whole day lying around doing nothing."

"……If I actually did that, the cookies you are eating at this moment would not appear, nee-sama, and neither would the tea."

"How foolish. Do you think service to your onee-sama is your work? That is a younger sister's duty."

"……Haa."

The image came to mind — Bery, sighing again and again at her self-indulgent, demanding sister.

She had felt sorry for her, she supposed. And yet Bery had always looked strangely happy. And Selene believed, looking back, that it truly was a happiness.

A single sigh breathed out because of a beloved person's self-indulgence.

Exasperated, weary to the bone — and yet the everyday of being made to worry over such things was something to be cherished.

Now, Selene knew this too.

# Chapter 297: A Sigh — III

『Fufu, I win. Care for another round?』

『……No. ……Thank you very much.』

『……?』

Childhood practice bouts.

She had grown able to hold her own against adults. She began winning against fresh recruits, and then against soldiers who were not fresh recruits.

Most reacted with surprise, or with a laughing *well done*, and praised her: *Selene-sama is a natural genius.*

But sometimes there were other reactions.

Some wore an expression as though the world were ending — as though they would weep, so bitter was their defeat.

Seeing them laugh after a loss, she wondered: why do they not feel frustrated? Or, alternatively: how could a single point in a bout make them so miserable?

The Selene of that age could not understand their feelings.

She herself had been blessed with many things.

She was beautiful, with hair that fell in smooth, gleaming gold.

At banquets, eyes gathered on her, and her sense for choosing dresses was excellent.

Her frame was well-made; she could wear anything with ease.

Her appearance — that was a matter of personal preference, and there would be varying opinions, but at least there was no one who would call Selene ugly.

Intellectually, too, she was above the common run.

Among her peers she was a cut above, and she worked hard.

She could follow the political discussions of adults, and everyone said she was a gifted young woman.

No doubt some of that was flattery, but objectively she was in the upper portion.

No genius, perhaps, but capable — her education had given her a wealth of knowledge.

She was blessed with sword talent too; since childhood she had been able to hold her own against grown soldiers.

The more she swung it, the more her sword improved visibly, and effort translated directly into results.

And her father was the celebrated Northern General, a border lord — a house that deserved the name great nobility.

Evaluated overall, Selene Christand was not ordinary in the slightest.

She was undoubtedly among those envied by others — objectively, she had been blessed with many things, and there would have been people who wished they could be like her.

But Selene, too, was a person who could not understand the feelings of the defeated.

Everyone has their pride, and pride, when wounded, hurts like an injury to a vital point.

For example, those who praised Selene as a genius and laughed off their defeat to her were, in that way, protecting their own pride. They defined Selene as someone different from themselves — a general's daughter overflowing with talent. And by telling themselves that was why they lost, they avoided the pain.

To be defeated by a child barely reaching your waist — that would naturally be unpleasant, naturally feel frustrating, and all the more so if you had been putting in effort.

Each of them had been putting in their own effort, and each had, to a greater or lesser degree, the pride of having trained hard.

The Selene of those days thought of their effort as merely a stepping-stone, and could not even grasp the simple fact that these men had lives of their own, had pride of their own, had things they were aiming for.

And yet, strangely — once she understood the feelings of those who were defeated, she felt as though she had forgotten what it was to be the victor.

Bery, for instance, was the 'victor' closest at hand.

She had never once seen Bery practice with a sword, and yet in bouts Selene could not touch her.

At least in those days — if Krische had never appeared, if she had gone on sparring with Bery across the years, perhaps Bery would have remained above her on the strength of that casual 'practice' alone.

Bery's mind was incomparably sharp, and she was gifted in every sense.

As her mother had said, Bery was a genius, and Selene could never surpass her.

Working hard at nothing in particular, yet having everything in reach — Selene had always envied her.

It was not the same as being beaten by trained warriors like her father and his comrades.

A servant who did the housework every day and never swung a sword.

And yet no matter how hard she trained, to never be able to touch her — it was absolutely galling, and she always wondered why she herself had not been given that kind of talent.

Yet it was the same thing. Surely the many soldiers defeated by the young Selene had felt the same.

Just as Selene worked in her own way and had things she had gained from it, they too had each worked in their own ways and naturally had things they had gained.

Just as Selene had thought *it is not fair* of Bery, they must have thought *it is not fair* of Selene.

Selene could reach most things by extending her hand.

But there were those who could not reach them even so, and equally, those for whom things were within reach without even trying.

And as a rule, people cannot find value in anything other than what they obtain through effort.

The talent that Selene could not match Bery with was worthless to Selene — but for the soldiers who lost to Selene, it was something they would have given anything for.

Bery, in the same way, likely thought her own talent worthless — but for Selene, it was something she would have given anything for.

Wishing for what you cannot have — that is human nature. People burn for what they cannot reach.

And even that Bery — who surpassed even her by such a distance that she seemed to exist in another world — was longing to be like someone, wasting her talent in service of that hazy goal of *making someone happy*, suffering over the fact that she could not be the person she wanted to be.

Selene had her own suffering. Bery had Bery's. Krische had Krische's. Kreschenta had Kreschenta's.

The same was true of everyone else — her father had his own, her mother had her own.

To be well-endowed does not mean to be happy.

Even if someone born into poverty were handed the rich life of royalty, it would not mean freedom from all suffering.

Without knowing when enough is enough, all one can do is keep wanting.

The important thing is to accept and receive oneself.

To stop wishing for what you cannot have.

To treasure small happiness.

Whether royalty or noble, craftsman or hunter, or someone living day to day with nothing to spare — if one has in one's heart the ability to accept oneself and take joy in small things, that alone is enough to be happy.

All the more so if one finds someone to share it with.

If the everyday can be filled with the kind of sighs one breathes out over something silly — nothing more is needed.

* * *

A market stall — atop a long bench at a shop.

"Ehehe, it is delicious, is it not."

"……You really do think about nothing but eating."

They had put up a stall and were selling sundry goods and wine, and Krische was biting into a skewer bought from a nearby food stand — brainlessly serene, as usual.

They had come to a mountain town out in the western reaches called Linareseria.

It had apparently been settled about a thousand years ago after a World Tree took root there and drew attention.

A mana crystal seam ran beneath it, and naturally mana was abundant — there was even a small Academy of Magic, and despite being rugged terrain, the flow of travellers was not nothing.

The World Tree to the south, with steep mountains in all four directions.

Like the Dragon's Maw, only a single road wound through the gap between peaks.

It should have been a dark, inconvenient place to live, but the leaves of the World Tree scattered the sunlight and let it filter down through the shade.

A curious thing — the branches and leaves of the World Tree, once rooted, were outwardly no different from ordinary trees; but at some point over the years they had begun to shine with sunlight.

*The people living here wished for it, so that is how it became*, Krische and the others said, and each World Tree had its own particular character.

The World Tree in Albernaria scattered rainbow-coloured petals once every several decades.

In Albyagel, sky-coloured flowers bloomed.

Here, at Dogaruaz, where the dragon renowned as Kushenalars had made its lair — at the topmost branches, a single great indigo flower, and a vermilion fruit.

They had picked one once, long ago, and eaten it — it had been exceptionally good.

The legendary fruit of immortality, brimming with mana as dense as a dragon's blood.

In some places, quite literally 'flying lizards' soared, and in others, the spirits one saw frequently over there danced — each place had its own character.

Legends, myths, the faiths of each land.

These things intermingled, and World Trees in places where no people gathered tended to grow more mysterious — while, conversely, those in populated lands were often more brilliant and ornamental.

Neither Krische nor Kreschenta had apparently given any specific character to theirs, it seemed; things had simply become the way they were on their own, and every time they saw it they made the same vacuous face: *Oh, whaaat?*

Living over there, surrounded by wandering legend, you stopped feeling awe at anything. But seeing things like this here — there was something freshly surprising about it.

Humans were remarkably adaptable creatures.

Bery and Kreschenta were on the eastern end.

Anne and the others were at Naut Arna.

Sometimes they would all visit the same town; other times they would split up and trade in different regions like today.

It was mostly down to whim — Kreschenta was probably grumbling to herself while letting Bery spoil her, and Elvena was probably trying to tease Lila while Anne foiled her.

Just imagining it was enough to be entertaining, and Selene let her shoulders shake slightly.

"……?"

"Nothing. I was just thinking about something silly."

Krische made a puzzled face, put down the skewer she had finished, and gave a small yawn.

Selene smiled faintly, then patted her own knee. Krische stretched out happily on the bench and laid her head there.

In the old days she would have thought, *how unmannerly in public* — but they had been to so many places and seen so many customs.

Even a single bench at a stall said as much.

In dry regions, only a carpet spread on the ground — the shopkeeper sat directly on it.

Goods displayed casually on the carpet as well.

In humid regions it was standard to set up proper stands and sit on chairs.

This town was the same — and long benches in particular were preferred here.

The travelling merchants kept the benches spread with blankets and cushions.

Lounging slovenly while receiving customers was the normal style for travelling merchants here. It seemed there was some kind of etiquette to showing you were not desperate: *my goods are good enough that I do not need to work hard to sell them.*

Apparently, travelling merchants who did not know the custom and shouted themselves hoarse bringing in customers were seen by locals as embarrassing — as having no confidence in their goods.

If a customer picked up an item and looked it over, speaking to them right away was considered presumptuous and uncouth.

The custom here was to wait a moment, or only speak once the customer opened their mouth.

No doubt it had something to do with the peaceful nature of the place and its good public order.

Travelling from place to place and going to so many different towns exposed you to so many different cultures and customs.

What she had thought of as common sense was really only the common sense of a very small world, Selene had come to feel.

What a man ought to be, what a woman ought to be; how a noble should conduct themselves, how a merchant should conduct themselves, how a craftsman should be, how a hunter; what constitutes propriety.

The common sense of Alberan, where she had been born and raised, remained Selene's foundation — there were certainly moments of surprise — but to come into contact with worlds so varied was pleasant.

The faces of Bery and Elvena when they visited lands like Kreisharana, where adorning yourself was considered shameful and wearing the minimum was the virtue, had been truly something to behold.

Was that two hundred years ago, or three hundred?

Looking back on that time, she had fought a rare combined front with Elvena against Bery to keep her there — she had forgotten that had ever happened.

Krische was already letting out gentle sounds of sleep.

Selene stroked her head, watching her sleeping face.

This town's travelling merchants valued sloppiness as a virtue — this convenient truth had been extracted and deployed in its most useful form: Krische, free and easy, doing exactly as she pleased.

When she slept she slept, when she ate she ate, when she played she played, leaving the work to Selene.

She had taken an unexpected liking to running a stall, but still — eat, sleep, play, that was Krische. Left to herself, she did nothing but what she liked.

"May I look at a few of your goods?"

"Of course."

It looked like someone connected to the Academy of Magic.

"……What sort. Being asked what sort, I am rather at a loss…… "

Fiiri thought with a tilt of her head, and Reine tilted her head too.

"Are you painting it without knowing what sort of painting it is?"

"That is right…… fufu, I rather often just paint as things occur to me."

"As things occur……"

"Yes. Drawing lines any which way, and then shaping whatever vaguely comes to mind…… thinking about what kind of picture I want to see, painting like that."

Fiiri was mysterious.

Sometimes she thought she could not understand her.

She wondered whether this was what her mother meant by *cannot understand* — but the *cannot understand* she felt for Fiiri did not seem like that sort of thing.

She was sure there were things Fiiri could see that Reine could not, and that was why Reine could not understand — it was not that Fiiri was inferior to Reine.

Reine was certainly better than Fiiri in many respects, but she did not think that meant she was superior to Fiiri.

Reine was probably less mature than Fiiri in many places.

Reine could take 1 and capture it as 1, but Fiiri could take 1 and capture it as 10 or 100, and Reine thought that must surely be something remarkable.

The difference between Fiiri's paintings and her own was exactly like that, and Fiiri's paintings had something in them that Reine's did not.

Fiiri always had something that Reine did not have.

"That is right. If I were to give what I am painting now a title…… *small paradise* — Fiiri, let me see…… how about *Elsren*?"

"Elsren……"

"Yes. A world without conflict, endlessly peaceful. Where everyone — without starving, without suffering — can repeat a happy everyday life together. Such a world."

Fiiri laughed softly, with what seemed like pleasure.

Reine thought she remembered her mother saying something like that too, and mulled it over, tilting her head.

Economic, cultural, individual gains and losses — at least the world involved many complicated circumstances intertwined, and a world without conflict was something she could not imagine.

Even her mother had said that even unifying the world would not guarantee safety.

The world was full of worthless people, and they started foolish conflicts.

"That is…… um, very difficult, I think."

"Yes. But it is free to paint a world like that…… as I said before, painting is free to paint what you want. Seeing what is there and painting that is also a painting, but painting an imaginary happy world like this is also within painting's domain."

"An imaginary world……"

"Yes. Dreams, ideals, desires, things like that are included."

At Fiiri's words Reine tilted her head, and she laughed softly, shoulders shaking.

*It was a little difficult, wasn't it,* she said and stroked her head.

"Is a world like the one in that painting Fiiri's, um, something of that sort?"

"That is right. I sometimes think: wouldn't it be nice to live in a world like this."

Fiiri smiled faintly and looked at Reine.

"But then, it depends how you look at it. Perhaps it is already within reach."

"……?"

"……In a sense, this place right now is my *Elsren.*"

Reine made a difficult face and thought hard, and Fiiri grew even more cheerful.

*Kusu, kusu, kusu, kusu* — a restrained, happy-sounding laugh echoed in the room, and Reine looked at her.

Fiiri was mysterious.

Sometimes she could not understand Fiiri.

She did not understand why such a Fiiri was laughing.

And yet watching her like that, Reine found herself strangely glad too.

Why does Fiiri laugh?

Why does she seem to be having fun, seem to be happy?

Reine wants to know Fiiri much more, to understand her.

So that perhaps she can make Fiiri enjoy herself more, make her feel even happier.

That was the only thing Reine thought about.
