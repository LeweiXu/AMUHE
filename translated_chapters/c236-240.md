# Chapter 236: Antinomy

Before the night had given way to dawn.

It was not even a manor — a cramped house with no partitions between rooms.

Not a place where someone holding the rank of warrior chief should be living, at the very least.

The man stroked the cheek of his sleeping infant and rose to his feet.

Careful not to make a sound, he draped clothes over his bare body, narrowed his eyes at the great sword propped against the wall, and stood there for a time.

He reached for the hilt——

"……Red-sama."

——A voice called out to him.

Red froze, and from behind came a soft warmth.

His wife's arms squeezed tightly around his waist.

"You would leave baby Zren behind…… leave Feni?"

Her slender body — thin arms filled with strength.

Filled with the will to never let go.

"Please, don't go. ……This is the one and only plea I will ever make in this life."

Red opened his mouth to say something, but no words came.

He simply stood there in a daze, and lowered his eyes.

"If there is anything about me that falls short, I will surely correct it. ……I swore upon my name to spend the rest of my life at your side. ……Please, please do not abandon Feni."

Her voice trembled, as did her body.

Perceptive, gentle, deeply compassionate, and beautiful.

——She was a woman far too good for the likes of him.

"What good could vengeance possibly do? Even Feni, a mere woman, understands — it would bring no one joy. Not those still living, not the dead…… not even you, my lord."

Perhaps, at that time.

*'Die here, or accept the terms. That's all I'm asking.'*

Perhaps he should have chosen death.

If the alternative was making this gentle woman weep.

"The year and more I spent with you…… it was a fulfilling time."

A mere year — he had never imagined such a brief span could produce this much hesitation.

Feni had heard everything from her brother Toba, and yet she had never once spoken of it.

She had simply lived at Red's side as a good wife.

Red, who had been on guard, came to realize over the course of living with her that something within him was quietly withering away.

"……To the point where there were even moments I forgot everything."

That was surely a happy thing.

Blessed with a good wife and child, nurturing them, leaving the wars of old in the past, and spinning a future from what lay ahead.

"……I would never ask you to forget Shelna-sama. Feni loves Red-sama, accepting even those feelings. ……It does not matter if you never love Feni in return. So please——"

"Feni."

Abandoning friends, abandoning his wife, abandoning his child.

Measured against that, he could see clearly just how foolish his own choice was.

"……I love you. The time I spent with you was happiness."

"Then——"

"But the more I taste that happiness, the more it flickers across my mind."

——His master's head, displayed in the forest.

The heads of his comrades-in-arms, and then — Shelna's head.

"……I am running away. And having run, I alone savor happiness. ……I cannot forgive myself, no matter what."

He looked up at the dark ceiling.

Moonlight barely filtered in, illuminating the tiny room.

So small a house that he could feel the warmth of her skin.

——At first it had been almost a form of harassment.

He had thought a woman of Feni's good breeding would give up at once.

*'Fufu, cleaning first, then. Let us gather things little by little.'*

But she had simply loved this humble house and Red, looking happy all the while.

The cold, barren interior was now steeped in her warmth and her scent.

Hair he had let grow out carelessly had been groomed by Feni's hands; the beard he had left to grow had been carefully shaved away.

Each time he remembered how to live as a human being, memories of the past came flooding back.

"It is not pride. Nor, anymore, is it a thirst for revenge. ……But I do not wish to run from the man I once was."

He grasped her arms, pried them loose, and turned to face her.

No cosmetics, her beautiful red hair simply tied and flowing — and still she was beautiful.

The eyes that looked up at him shed only tears.

Surely those were droplets more beautiful than any jewel in this world.

"……I am a weak man. Forget me. You deserve far better."

He kissed her, and drew away.

Feni's beautiful face crumpled, and she covered it with both hands.

She shook her head, pressed herself against him — and he stroked her hair.

For a time they remained like that, until the infant, roused by its mother's weeping, began to cry.

When she turned her attention there as a mother, he seized the sword and opened the door.

"……I will await your return. For however many years, however many decades……"

Red did not answer her trembling voice. He closed the door with his back turned.

His vision blurred; he pressed the heels of his hands against his eyes and squeezed them shut.

He walked a short distance east, into the forest — to the graves.

"……Bad feelings always turn out to be right."

Sitting before the grave was a red-haired man.

Toba Akaz, spear resting across his shoulders, did not look at him.

He sighed as if exasperated.

"……If you walk out, I'll kill Feni and the kid."

His voice was cold.

"Family of a traitor. Makes a fine example for discipline, too."

Hearing this, Red shook his head.

"……You're not a man cold-blooded enough to go through with that."

Toba clicked his tongue and grimaced.

"Ha, so you'll just sit on that kindness and take it for granted, Red? ……You've got some nerve."

"……I know I'm being faithless. I'm leaving them in your hands."

Toba lowered his eyes, sighed again.

Then, after a brief pause, he spoke.

"If things go south, I'll come kill you myself. Even if you win, I won't lift a finger to help. I'll be wagging my tail for your beloved Alberan, kissing up for all I'm worth. That's my final answer, in the end…… the moment you walk east from here, you're a traitor to Elderant——"

This is the last time, Toba said.

"Go home. Just like this. ……Pretend nothing happened. Then show up at the meetings like usual, and report every agitator you find to me. Cry and tell Feni you'll never do it again — that'll be enough."

He said it lightly, laughing.

Then narrowed his eyes.

"Things are irreversible. River water only flows one way. ……But as it flows, what clings to it thins out and fades."

——Good memories and bad alike.

He added those words, stood, and turned toward Red.

And yet, even so, Toba did not look at him.

"Running isn't wrong, and being weak isn't wrong either. Nobody lives without regrets. ……Isn't what matters living from now on so you have fewer?"

That's true, Red said.

But I've already decided, he added.

"……That so."

Toba, still not looking at Red, passed by him as if they were strangers.

"Well, honestly…… I don't dislike that side of you."

"……I see."

——In an instant Red twisted his hips, dodging the butt-end of the spear that had been thrust at him.

He seized Toba's arm and slammed his body into the earth.

The ground shook and the trees rustled.

Red looked down at Toba and spoke.

"I don't dislike that side of you, either."

"……You son of a bitch."

The rounded butt-cap of the spear.

He understood the reason, and the meaning.

"……Forgive me, my friend."

Red knew no greater fool than himself.

* * *

The royal estate, Krische's room.

Bery was seated in a chair, running a finger along the ledger as she explained.

"——And that's the gist. These numbers here are for the reasons I mentioned."

Anne and Elvena listened with serious faces and studied the ledger.

The ledger detailed every aspect of the Christand household's income and expenditure — ordinarily Bery's responsibility, but at Anne's request to lighten her burden, half had been delegated to her.

Anne was not unintelligent to begin with, and Elvena was in fact skilled at arithmetic.

They were quick learners, and with practice, more than sufficient.

*That should do for now*, Bery thought, and nodded before glancing at the bed.

Kreschenta was on the bed, breathing softly in sleep.

Thanks in part to Bery's care, she spent most of her free hours napping.

Standing, Bery drew up the blanket that had slipped and tucked it over her.

Spring still lingered with snow in the north — Bery stroked her forehead and smiled.

A knock, and the one who entered was Selene.

"Welcome home, ojou-sama."

"I'm back, Bery."

She glanced at the ledgers spread across the desk and narrowed her eyes.

Then turned her gaze to the two.

As if reading the room, Anne nodded and exchanged a look with Elvena.

"Elvena-sama, shall we go over it a bit in our room?"

"Yes."

They gathered the ledgers from the desk, returned them to the shelf, and excused themselves from the room.

Bery moved to make tea, was told *sit down* in an exasperated tone, and reluctantly returned to her chair.

Selene poured hot water into the pot, deftly prepared the tea, and set two cups on the table.

"They're both quick learners. Most of it is already——"

"……When you say that, it just sounds like condescension, so stop."

Bery praised people often, but the bar was usually low.

If someone took months to accomplish what she herself had cleared in a day or two, she would still praise them with a cheerful *well done*.

A personality issue, probably.

It was not malice or intentional condescension, but to those who knew her, it came close.

She was excessively strict with herself, and excessively lenient with everyone else.

"……Are you alright today?"

"Yes. I've been feeling quite well so far today. ……Last night was a bit painful, but thanks to Kreschenta-sama I managed."

"……I see."

*A bit*, or *somewhat*.

It could not have been merely that kind of pain, yet Bery always kept such words close and offered them.

Selene wanted to object, but pointing it out would change nothing.

The pain would be no easier, nor would it disappear.

"……Krische didn't come home today either?"

"No, she's in the royal castle basement again."

Bery smiled with a touch of loneliness.

"……Is that all right?"

When she asked, *it's fine*, and Bery shook her head.

"She is working so hard for my sake. ……I cannot say a thing."

She raised her cup of tea to her lips, then gazed at it.

Bery said quietly.

"……Please look after Krische-sama."

"Didn't I tell you not to talk like that? It's bad luck."

"You did."

She laughed, amused for reasons beyond Selene.

Selene lowered her eyes.

"Perhaps there is still plenty of time…… and Krische-sama will manage somehow. ……But I might be gone tomorrow."

*That is why I want to ask now*, Bery continued.

With her usual smile, showing no sign of anguish.

Which only made it more painful.

"……That girl adores you. You know that. I can't take your place."

"Take my place — please. Ojou-sama is ojou-sama, and I am I. ……Didn't you say that to me once?"

"All the more reason. ……I could never fill the hole you'd leave behind."

Don't you dare underestimate me, Selene said.

"……I love that girl as much as you do. She loves me too. But it's different from what she feels for you."

"Well, yes. ……I am quite confident I am the most beloved by Krische-sama in all the world, so if we're keeping score, I win."

"……You're unbelievable."

Selene glared, and Bery looked amused.

She chuckled softly and spoke.

"But it is a trivial thing, a trivial difference. If the two of us were drowning at the same time, perhaps Krische-sama would save me first. ……But if it had been ojou-sama who was stabbed back then, Krische-sama would still have gone to the ancient dragon."

Staking her life on it.

"……Would she?"

"Have you forgotten the time ojou-sama struck Krische-sama? Krische-sama would not listen to my pleas at all."

She spoke as if fondly reminiscing, and closed her eyes.

"……Affection may have degrees, but love has no such measure. What exists is at most an order, and even that is a trivial thing."

"……You can only say that because you're at the top of that trivial order."

"Fufu, that's true. It would be vexing, and I wouldn't say it. ……But if I were in ojou-sama's position and told the same thing, I would answer *of course*."

Selene furrowed her brow, and Bery smiled.

"Even if Krische-sama were to say ojou-sama is her number one, it would not make my love any lighter. ……If, on top of that, she still loves me, then that is more than enough."

"……You're telling me to spend my whole life as the runner-up?"

"Is there truly any winning or losing? It is like arguing whether the lawful wife or the concubine stands higher. Both are wives, and that should suffice."

She said it, and then fixed Selene with a gaze that held a competitive edge.

"Obsessing over who wins and who loses, and then calling yourself a runner-up on top of it — that is not like you, ojou-sama. ……It takes all the fun out of teasing you, and frankly, that is a shame."

"……I'm not rising to the bait."

Bery laughed again at Selene's words.

Merrily, and then after a moment, she narrowed her eyes.

"It is a silly thing. ……As I told you before, Krische-sama is simply not that sort of person…… and that is what I love most about her."

She reached out and stroked Selene's cheek.

"Krische-sama cannot choose just one person. She is clumsy, hopelessly so — she scatters her affection to the very limits of what she can give, at bargain prices. ……Without even realizing she is shortchanging herself, saying silly things about repaying what she owes."

She tilted her head slightly, and her grown-out red hair flowed smoothly.

The arms glimpsed from her sleeves were unhealthily thin, visibly thinner than before.

And still, she was beautiful.

"Krische-sama falls head over heels for even a wretch like me. ……If ojou-sama were to approach her seriously——*guh*"

Unable to hold back, Selene pulled her into an embrace and felt the reality of her.

Her body was one, two sizes thinner, and Selene's face twisted.

"……Why are you always like this? Always, always worrying about everyone else…… worry about yourself for once."

Bery stared blankly for a moment, then embraced her in return.

"I would rather you wailed and threw tantrums. Things like *it hurts*, or *it's hard*, or *it's painful*, or *I'm scared*…… you must have plenty."

And then Bery simply smiled, stroking the trembling back, combing through her hair.

"This is the fourth time, after all. ……Childhood, the time nee-sama stopped me, when I was stabbed, and now this makes four. I suppose one does become accustomed to bracing oneself……"

"……As if you could ever get used to it, you fool. ……You're a hopeless liar."

Bery laughed sheepishly and pressed her cheek against Selene's, as though she cherished her.

"Since ojou-sama is honest to a fault, I suppose it all balances out."

And she whispered.

"If anything happens to me, please look after Krische-sama. ……No answer needed."

"……You're the worst."

"Fufu, I am aware. ……Please consider it in lieu of wailing unbecomingly."

"Truly…… the worst."

She squeezed the slender body tight.

Bery's body was as hot as if she had a fever, and that was her normal.

As if burning through her life, her body repeated the cycle of destruction and regeneration.

If there were anything to make her even a little more comfortable, Selene wanted to do it.

If she could take half the pain, she wanted to.

But Selene was no genius — she was ordinary — and there was nothing she could do.

She could not tell her to *hang in there*.

She could not tell her *that's enough*.

She could not even offer empty comfort, and that made her unbearably frustrated and wretched.

After a while, a knock.

Selene wiped her eyes and asked; Elvena informed her that a messenger had arrived from the western army.

*I'll be right there*, Selene said, steadied her breathing, and pinched Bery's cheek.

"……I'm not making any promises."

To Bery, who insisted she was fine, those were the only words she could offer.

Bery smiled happily and said *yes*, then produced a handkerchief and dabbed Selene's eyes clean.

Selene accepted it without resistance, kissed Bery's forehead, and left the room.

Bery watched her go with a quiet smile, then rose and returned to sit on the edge of the bed.

When she reached to stroke the sleeping girl's forehead, her hand was slapped away.

"I'm sorry. I woke you."

"……It was so noisy I'm wide awake now."

Saying so, Kreschenta sat up and pressed herself against Bery's back.

She wrapped her arms around Bery's waist and asked, *You won't ask me?*

"With Kreschenta-sama, I can already guess the answer without asking."

"Is that so? I might throw a tantrum like Selene-sama, you know. You might get a surprising answer if you tried."

"I see…… Please look after Krische-sama."

"As if I need the likes of you to tell me. Who do you think you are?"

At that, Bery laughed softly.

She grasped the hand that had been wrapped around her waist, amused.

Blue mana dissipated from the palm that had been checking the state of her body.

Bery gazed at it, and quietly asked.

"……How long, would you say?"

"Who knows. Today or tomorrow, perhaps — or maybe a year, three years from now…… if you stop, you could die today."

She said it, and after a brief pause.

Kreschenta simply tightened the arms that embraced her thin waist.

"What is certain is that it will only become more painful from here. Onee-sama's research advances only to retreat, or starts over from scratch."

She pressed her cheek against Bery's back and asked.

"……Supposing it holds — how many years do you intend to wait?"

At those words, Bery chuckled softly and answered.

"Why, as many decades as Krische-sama wishes."

"Even if conditions worse than this continue?"

"There is nothing to consider. ……I have sworn."

Kreschenta fell silent for a time.

"You're out of your mind."

Then she said that, and——

"Perhaps so."

Bery returned it cheerfully.

She pried Kreschenta's arms from around her waist.

And as always, settled her on her lap.

"……No amount of thanks could ever be enough for you, Kreschenta-sama."

"After causing this much trouble, do you really think mere thanks will suffice?"

She grasped the thin wrist that held her waist, and lowered her eyes.

"……I should have left onee-sama here and done the research myself. That would have been far better."

Then she pulled the hand she held against her chest.

Bery nestled her left cheek against the girl's head and whispered *I'm sorry* with a smile.

"Apologies are not enough either. It is all your fault. You make onee-sama and me — the two people who deserve to be treasured most in this world — lose sleep over you. You are the worst scoundrel alive."

"……Yes."

"Insolent on top of it, and dragging our feet — who do you think you are? Do you fancy yourself above the queen? You are obstructing what should have been an era of unprecedented prosperity for Alberan. What an unforgivable criminal."

"Fufu, yes."

Bery's shoulders shook as she received the abuse with evident enjoyment.

She tilted her head slightly back, and Kreschenta rubbed her right cheek against Bery's.

"After turning my onee-sama into such a fool, how many more irreparable things will you pile up? My suffering, my displeasure — all of it, every bit of it is your fault."

And she raised her arm, touching Bery's cheek with her palm.

Tracing along the contours, as if confirming the shape and temperature.

"……It is all your fault."

She murmured, and lowered her eyes.

"I despise you."

"……Yes. Fufu, is it as I said before?"

Holding her gently, Bery said with deep affection.

"I adore Kreschenta-sama, and your *I despise you* is automatically replaced by *I love you* in my head. ……So please, say it as many times as you like."

"You are sick. ……You are truly hopeless."

Kreschenta leaned her weight against Bery.

Feeling the warmth and the presence against her back.

After a pause, she spoke.

"Say, Argan-sama."

"……Yes?"

She opened her mouth to ask, but the words never became sound.

*It's nothing*, she said, and shook her head.

# Chapter 237: Those Who Are Compelled

Distrust toward King Akaz, who had raised the banner of cooperation and reconciliation with Alberan.

Those who deemed his stance weak formed what was called the Anti-Akaz Alliance.

The great tribes of Veze and Mekrea, which had suffered enormous losses in the previous war, formed the core — they rebelled against the Akaz king's decisions and rose in arms.

In the blink of an eye, they seized control of the entire eastern region.

Leading them was Red Lani — a man who had been warrior chief of the Lani, a tribe under Veze's banner.

After the great war, he had fought alongside the Akaz king through the Elderant civil war, but a clash of opinions regarding the new direction led to a parting of ways.

At least from the information that reached Alberan, there was nothing suspicious about this — a man who had once fought under Veze's banner had clear enough reasons.

Vengeance was the most transparent of motives.

The three great tribes had always been on bad terms.

Veze and Mekrea found common cause against Akaz — the shared enemy who had escaped harm and usurped royal power — and joined hands, producing a force of forty thousand.

The objective their commander Red had proclaimed was the subjugation of western Alberan and the establishment of a new nation, Vezrea, to replace the feeble Elderant under Akaz.

Inevitably, having seized eastern Elderant, they marched further east.

They launched the invasion of western Alberan.

Breaking through the border with breathtaking speed, they captured cities one after another and chose as their first major objective Michelsave — the great city that served as the western army's stronghold — situated just before the great western forest.

Lightning-fast would not be an exaggeration.

Politically, too, the timing was immediately after King Akaz had announced his reconciliation policy — it was inevitable that Alberan was caught off guard.

Envoys had been traveling back and forth between the two nations to negotiate terms, including reparations for the protracted great war.

No one could have dreamed that a force of this scale would launch an invasion.

War was, after all, merely one tool of diplomacy, not simple violence.

Such choices were always rational, never impulsive, and international relations operated upon the unspoken understanding that everything was grounded in reasoned calculation.

Naturally, no countermeasure existed even in Alberan against an invasion that disregarded all of that.

The mob that appeared swept through the west with its momentum intact, and by the time the euphoria of victory had swelled, the force besieging Michelsave had ballooned from forty thousand to sixty thousand.

The Western General, Tolka Karnarius, issued emergency conscription and brought his army to strength, but could muster a mere twenty thousand.

Arming a portion of Michelsave's citizens as militia brought the total to thirty thousand.

The numerical disparity was clear — and Michelsave was by no means a formidable walled city.

Since the era of Aulgorn Hilkintos and his predecessor, the western army had valued mobility above all else.

Rather than concentrating on the defense of a single stronghold, their strategy was to build small forts across the region, stage a gradual withdrawal to draw the enemy in, exhaust them, and then deliver the killing blow.

This likely owed much to the fact that Elderant's threats typically came as scattered raids by individual tribes.

Decisive engagements were deemphasized in favor of attrition and mobile piecemeal destruction; in fortifications, they prioritized quantity over quality.

Michelsave was no exception — it was not a city capable of withstanding a large-scale siege like Wulfenite.

Red, flying the banner of the new Vezrea, exploited his numerical superiority with relentless day-and-night assaults.

He personally led a small elite force of mana wielders on sabotage operations, took enemy wall commanders' heads with his own hands — and when Karnarius judged that holding out was no longer possible, he ordered a withdrawal.

The rearguard that opened a hole in the eastern encirclement and allowed the retreat was led by none other than the renowned old general.

——Felwors Keesliton.

"……That's a face I've seen before. To think we'd meet again like this — call it fate."

On the grassland, surrounded by soldiers of both armies, clad in silver armor sculpted in the likeness of a dragon and shouldering a great lance.

The old general wore a smile upon his deeply wrinkled face, dismounted from his horse, and toyed with his long-grown beard.

"It seems I can no longer handle you from horseback, Red of Lani."

"……I heard you were on death's door."

Red drove a greatsword nearly as tall as himself into the earth.

Steel breastplate, gauntlets, greaves.

An alarmingly light loadout for an army's commanding officer — he did not even wear a helm.

Yet despite being drenched in the blood of others, not a single wound marked his body.

"Since you've come to the battlefield, I won't hold back. This is unfinished business from that war. ……Sorry, but I'll be taking your life, Keesliton."

Red said it with no trace of amusement.

The timing of the invasion of Alberan had been chosen based on intelligence from his spies — the report that Keesliton had collapsed.

Over a hundred and thirty years old.

Rumors of declining health had circulated for some time, and he had already ceded the generalship to his adjutant, Tolka Karnarius.

It was certain he would die of old age before long.

He had planned to strike during the ensuing chaos — and yet, even now, the figure clad in armor and shouldering the great lance radiated the aura of a battle-hardened warrior.

That fighting spirit filled the field, betraying no hint of age's erosion.

Red, who had known the great sage Jiegret — a man of a hundred and fifty years — harbored no contempt for age.

Even so, inwardly, he felt an honest admiration.

That this man had collapsed and been bedridden was fact.

He had simply willed his way through it and chosen this place as his final ground.

"Any further fighting would be futile. If you agree to surrender, I'll consider it…… though you don't look like the sort of man who'd say that."

"……Indeed."

Those who remained as the rearguard numbered ten thousand regular soldiers.

Those who had withdrawn were primarily the wounded and the ten thousand militia.

Exact numbers aside, the retreat was motivated by purely humanitarian concerns.

The soldiers' morale was high; they could have fought to the last here.

The sole purpose of this arrangement was to spare the militia from that — it was not an action taken for strategic advantage.

Having ensured the militia's successful withdrawal, they had already fulfilled their purpose.

"As you surmise. If it can be hoped for, I wish for a warrior's death."

"A clean resolve. Both you and I have achieved our objectives. As generals, this war is a draw — if what remains is to be settled, it can only be by the blade. ……What say you?"

And Red likewise.

He had accomplished his objective of seizing western Alberan.

This was merely cleanup.

Felwors Keesliton, even in his twilight, was a general who lived up to his renown.

But numerical superiority was absolute — though Red had taken some casualties, his advance to the enemy's main formation meant the outcome was all but decided.

The question was unnecessary. He could simply ride the momentum and grind them down.

And yet he made such a proposal — because something wavered within his heart.

"A man of arms, Red. ……If you swear upon that sword to abide by the Sacred Convention, I shall accept. These are good soldiers here — better they fall as warriors at my side than be taken prisoner and tortured to death."

Felwors laughed merrily, and Red placed his hand upon the greatsword's hilt.

"Very well. ——On this sword and my name, Red of Lani agrees."

And without the slightest hesitation, he answered.

"Haha, to think such joy awaits at this age. ……I had resigned myself to dying of old age, but this is an honor."

Felwors shifted his great lance into a half-guard.

Serenity itself — an endlessly beautiful stance.

Neither hostility nor killing intent, and yet something overwhelming was there.

Like an ancient tree that had taken root in the earth since time immemorial — a force that simply was.

Technique and spirit, accumulated and tempered through a century of experience from sapling to hoary giant, fused without the faintest seam and sublimated into a single beauty.

The old general Felwors Keesliton was not a man blessed with talent.

Yet from his mediocre roots, he had charged through hundreds of battles great and small.

A formless tree rooted on the formless battlefield, garbed over long years in a flawless beauty.

His prime lay in the distant past — he stood now at the edge of withering.

And yet the old general was, at this very moment, at his zenith as a man of arms.

Like a great tree at the end of its days, bursting with countless blossoms.

"Come, Red. ……Old though I am, the warrior in me has not yet died."

Red narrowed his eyes and noticed that his hands had grown hot.

His blood was boiling.

At the prospect of facing a man of arms worthy of respect.

——Something lost long ago, taken from him in that war.

The radiance had faded from the battlefield; fighting had become mere labor.

And yet everything he thought he had abandoned was still here.

Perhaps it was to reclaim it that he stood in this place.

Perhaps that was precisely why he had cast aside everything he had gained anew, to be here.

He closed his eyes for an instant and pulled the greatsword from the earth.

Left foot forward, half-guard — the greatsword's tip dragging to the right.

"Under the direct command of the war princess Shelna Veze — captain of the Hunting Party of Veze. ……Red of Lani will take your life."

Red broke into a bestial sprint.

* * *

"……What will you do?"

"I'll go myself. Leading Krische's army."

In Selene's room — Selene answered Kreschenta's question.

Elvena watched them anxiously as she poured tea.

"Onee-sama could——"

"……Kreschenta. Krische worries me more than I do right now. Do you really think she's in any state to take command on a battlefield?"

An insurrection within Elderant and an invasion of western Alberan.

It had come as a bolt from the blue.

Diplomatic negotiations with Elderant had only just begun — Alberan had been caught completely off guard.

*Don't worry, it'll be fine*, Selene told Kreschenta.

"The north and south can operate without issues, and the Alberinea army is the kingdom's finest even without Krische. He did not want to kill a warrior of that caliber through such base means.

At the very least, the conclusion was born of calculation and emotion, reason and feeling, blended together — of that there was no doubt.

Perhaps that was his own weakness.

He had come here resolved to discard all emotion, prepared to commit any cruelty necessary.

And yet, there would have been countless other means.

He could have spent years building a network in the capital and pursuing an assassination, without anything this elaborate.

He chose war — chose the battlefield — because whatever still lingered within Red existed here.

Comrades' smiles, Shelna's voice.

Everything burned into his memory belonged to the battlefield, and the path that led him here was, from beginning to end, nothing more than emotion.

In his duel with Keesliton, something within him had found a measure of peace.

The battlefield, no matter how it was adorned, was nothing but unreason.

The strong took the lives of the weak, unilaterally — a single law governed it, a world of cruelty.

The moment one set foot upon it, excuses ceased to exist.

Those who died, died because they were weak.

The answer was neither more nor less, and by that law, Shelna and Red had killed many in the name of justice.

Having killed so many, to resent being killed in return was unreasonable.

He had never once apologized to those they had slain.

In the end, his vendetta against Alberinea was not logic. It was emotion's answer, nothing more.

The sense of righteousness he had felt at the time had thinned and vanished with the years.

And yet here he stood. Why?

"Something I left behind."

"……?"

"However I answer, whatever reason I attach — in the end, I'm simply here because I want to take back what I left behind that day."

After the civil war ended, he had gone to Shelna and the others' graves every day.

At some point, his wife had started coming along without a word, offering something at each visit.

There was a boundless, gentle happiness in it, and everything had been on the verge of becoming memory.

"Nothing but emotion. ……I am a fool, through and through. I can't move forward until I've reclaimed it. ……That's why I am truly grateful — from the bottom of my heart — that you followed me."

The man stared, then laughed — *What are you saying, captain?*

More than half of the surviving Hunting Party was still here, at Red's side.

To reclaim something they, too, had forgotten.

"The Sword Princess's hounds — and you are their master. Same as back then. We'll swing our swords at your side until we die."

The words seeped in, flowing through Red's interior.

He closed his eyes for a brief moment, then opened them.

"……Thank you."

He said it, and realized it was a word he had not spoken in a very long time.

Not to Toba, not to Feni, not to these men.

The most ordinary word a human being could say.

That, too, was surely something Red had left behind.

"We kill Alberinea. ……I need your help."

At those words, the men smiled and nodded.

* * *

The Alberinea army placed the Second Corps under Kokys at the center, and Aleha's Fourth Corps on the right wing.

Keith's First Corps held the rear-left with the militia, constructing withdrawal works, while the Third Corps under Begil guarded the left wing from the central headquarters.

The left was kept thin because the main road at center and the southern flank were separated by a river.

By coincidence, the ground Selene's force had advanced into was the same clearing that had been the stage for the decisive battle against Aulgorn Hilkintos.

Forest to the right — the north.

A swift-flowing river to the left — the south. Despite being deep in the great forest, the space was remarkably open.

The bridge to the rear, which Krische had once destroyed, had been rebuilt sturdily enough that retreat would pose no great difficulty.

Aleha would handle any northern flanking; if the enemy attempted a wide detour south across the river, Keith could buy time — and that would suffice.

"General Remin's corps has detected the enemy flanking force. She is conducting delay operations."

"Tell Aleha to continue the delay as long as possible. We begin the main body's withdrawal once the Second Corps links up."

Selene relayed orders to the messenger from horseback.

The Second Corps had already made contact with the enemy column's vanguard.

She watched the withdrawing troops returning safely and exhaled with relief.

Apparently the enemy supreme commander, Red Lani, had personally led the flanking ambush.

His aim had been to split the Second Corps.

At first glance, an almost recklessly aggressive commander.

Despite having numerical superiority, he led his own men forward to whittle down enemy strength.

He clearly had immense confidence in his own prowess.

But he was no reckless fool.

Even with the advantage of this blitz invasion and superior numbers, he was not the slightest bit complacent.

Even from a position of strength, he sought to further reduce the enemy and create overwhelming conditions.

Rather than reckless, he was cautious.

Judging by the northern flanking maneuver, he had entered this engagement assuming that the outnumbered Alberinea army would advance into the forest.

"A dangerous opponent."

"Indeed. In a normal situation, one would not expect us to push in…… the enemy is focused on a specific target."

Begil, who stood nearby, spoke, and Selene nodded.

"So their objective is to crush this army?"

"That is how it looks to me."

An ordinary army, under an ordinary general, would not have entered.

Advancing into the enemy's forest while outnumbered and without intelligence was folly.

Selene had done so only because this was *the kingdom's strongest army*.

Had it not been, she would never have set foot in this forest regardless of what anyone said.

And the fact that the enemy had anticipated that move meant he had assumed from the start that the Alberinea army would come.

——Defeat the kingdom's strongest army, and seize total initiative.

That was the enemy's objective, most likely.

"A bit early, but we withdraw as planned. The enemy chose this as his battlefield, after all — staying in the forest is far too dangerous."

"No disagreement. What was chosen for us should be avoided."

Begil answered, and asked:

"After the withdrawal — as planned?"

"Link up with General Remin, gather forces from the rear, and seal off the forest on the capital side. If the enemy really was targeting this army, then the likelihood of them having already split their forces is low. We may be able to aim for a victory without damage to the capital region."

If the enemy had not assumed this army would enter the forest, then staying outside was the dangerous option.

If the enemy intended to rampage through the capital region, the response would inevitably lag.

But if the enemy had been fixated solely on this army, the calculus changed.

The enemy, too, would need some delay to adjust their plans.

Even if they chose to bypass and advance on the capital, it would not happen immediately.

Selene could use that window to consolidate the situation outside, alongside Teknrea.

"If they press the pursuit too hard, we hit them with the Jaleia Gashea after all. It'll make things trickier later, but——"

"No need to hesitate. Loosing countless jade tigers into the forest — there is no finer delaying tool."

The Jaleia Gashea were held in the rear with Keith's First Corps, along with the engineering team.

If the pursuit was fierce, they would scatter the golems and annihilate the pursuit force.

The problem was that in the poor visibility of the forest, recovering the scattered units would be impossible, but that could be dealt with after the war.

——*Not bad*, she thought, and nodded.

She had worn herself out thinking this through to reach her conclusions.

Selene did not believe her own abilities were exceptional.

Her inexperienced mind lacked snap judgment, and she had no guarantee of producing sound answers on the fly.

But by methodically examining every possibility and building her conclusions in advance, she could issue orders fit for a general.

No matter what the situation, if she had already envisioned and decided it all beforehand, she could select the answer without needing to think.

That was the one method by which the unfinished Selene could fight on equal terms.

At the very least, with an army like this, she could wage a battle she would not lose.

And in this war, so long as Selene did not lose, that directly equated to victory.

Up ahead, where the forest narrowed, Tolka appeared from the column alongside the Second Corps — and after a short interval, the silver-tiger armor of Kokys.

"Begil, let the Second Corps through first. You take the rear."

"Right, as planned——"

——And just as Begil was about to nod, a voice like a cry came from behind them.

She spun around on reflex, but her eyes could not find the source.

"Selene."

It came from above, landing behind Selene and the others as they turned.

"……Krische."

"What's the situation?"

An eight-shaku, two-jou jade tiger — two large bags hanging from its flanks, its massive frame radiating intimidation.

Perched atop it was a girl with silver hair.

Her long hair swayed in two tails, a cloak over a one-piece dress.

Gauntlets and greaves, and two curved swords at her waist.

Her fairy-like beauty was utterly cold, and her purple eyes held not a shred of emotion.

"……Why are you here?"

"Krische heard from Kreschenta that the enemy might be too much for Selene. ……Krische will end this quickly. The situation."

Selene's eyes widened for an instant; she bit her lip and clenched her fists.

Then she crushed a host of emotions, lowered her gaze, and spoke.

"The enemy is between forty and sixty thousand. We've just struck the enemy vanguard inside the forest. We were about to begin the withdrawal."

"I see. The enemy is that direction?"

"Yes. Kokys reported sighting the enemy commander."

At those words, Krische gazed ahead.

Kokys, who had quickened his pace upon recognizing Krische, was already close.

"……Krische-sama."

"The commanding general approached from that direction?"

"Most likely."

"Then, change of plans. Selene, deploy here."

"Wait——!"

Selene shook her head and looked up.

"We have no preparations for a defensive stand here. No matter how you look at it, this is reckless——"

"——Selene."

Krische spoke, leaped down from the jade tiger.

She walked to Selene and stroked her cheek.

"Krische wants to get this trivial business over with and go home. Preparations and all that — it doesn't matter. Krische has already prepared, and Krische will finish it all."

Something in those purple eyes brooked no argument.

And yet the words were like a busy mother soothing a fussing child.

Had it been anyone but Selene, her tone would likely have been far more imperious.

She had left behind Bery — more precious to her than anything — to come here.

No one understood her feelings better than Selene.

"……Selene doesn't need to worry about anything. Just leave it all to Krische, like always."

And precisely because she understood, those words cut Selene to the core.

Her body went rigid, her vision blurred, and she closed her eyes and looked down to keep it from spilling over.

"……Understood."

At those words, Krische — seemingly unaware of her sister's state — turned to Kokys.

Kokys had been watching Selene and Krische with the look of a man witnessing something painful.

"The Second and Third Corps will form a line here. This is just mopping up, so it won't be much work."

"……Mopping up?"

"Yes."

Krische touched the bags hanging from the jade tiger's flanks.

When it had landed, the sound that rang out had been hard and high-pitched.

"Krische will kill most of them, so you just deal with whatever's left."

——It was the sound of mana crystals, packed into the bags by the hundreds.
