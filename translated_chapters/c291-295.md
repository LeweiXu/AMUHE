# Chapter 291: The Priestess and the Cage of Desire — Part One

A single campfire and a single tent in a nighttime cave.

The red-haired girl had draped a blanket over her apron dress and was wrapping two other girls inside it.

Curled up within the cloak were locks of silver hair and locks of gold hair shimmering with red.

"Onee-sama, I simply cannot go on any longer……"

"Now, now, Kreschenta. ……Falling asleep in a snowy mountain means dying."

"Uuu……"

The silver-haired girl pinched the cheek of her sister, who kept trying to doze off, while clinging to the arm of the red-haired girl.

Her own eyes were drowsy and narrowed, yet her cheeks softened into something that looked unmistakably like happiness.

The red-haired girl being clung to by both of them — the servant Bery — settled Kreschenta onto her lap and rubbed her cheek against the top of her head with a quiet laugh.

Kreschenta, adrift in that drowsy in-between place, surrendered to the pleasure of warmth, her eyelids fluttering open and closed.

Across from them, the enormous jade tiger let out a yawn, and the golden-haired girl leaning against it — Selene — replied with a yawn of her own: "You two never get tired of this, do you."

It was, at a glance, a rather arduous holiday: two full weeks spent enjoying the descent of the world's highest mountain on sleds and boards, sleeping in tents and caves.

That said, while they played, Krische was keeping the wind lifted away from the mountainside, so the climate was mild even while snow raged around them in appearance.

The north face of this mountain saw frequent avalanches, but each one carved out a fresh course, which meant the novelty never faded.

The sea would have been ideal, if she were being greedy, but the snowy mountain wasn't bad at all in its own right.

There was something indescribable about the rush of riding down a mountain alongside an avalanche, and Selene had grown rather fond of the snow-descent herself.

Each night they would choose a suitable spot to eat and sleep.

Krische and Kreschenta had apparently taken a liking to this arrangement and were thoroughly enjoying what they called their "stranded-in-the-mountains game," pretending to be lost.

Both of them hated the cold, yet using the cold as an excuse to press up against someone and stay warm was very much to their liking.

Once they had pressed themselves close, neither of them would budge a single inch — the degree to which they demanded to be coddled was without limit.

By roughly the third day, Selene would hand them off to Bery, but Bery showed no sign of minding the two clingy creatures at all, which meant she was, as ever, quite thoroughly out of her mind.

"……Lila, I'm cold just watching you. Are you really all right?"

"A, a little cold, yes……f-ふふ, but you know, I do think it's nice to come somewhere like this once in a while. Snowy mountains are fun……"

*My body certainly feels braced*, she said, shivering, trembling, as she gave her answer.

She had at least put boots on, but her outfit was otherwise the same as always, with nothing but a fur pelt thrown over it.

Lila was busily producing snowmen one after another, while Elvena watched the proceedings with apparent amusement and ferried snow in from outside.

Though the temperature was modestly adjusted, it was still fairly cold, and she was in her usual midriff-baring look.

They knew she was the sort whose body never fell ill (though there was one particular fool who occasionally pretended to run a fever), but even so, it was cold just to look at.

It seemed she intended to fashion a thousand snowmen as a means of disciplining her lax self, but her way of thinking was sometimes quite beyond comprehension.

Anne, who was gazing at the scenery outside with a serious expression and jotting things down in a sheaf of paper, looked more presentable by comparison.

What she was writing appeared to be a rather unwholesome story, but at the very least it seemed more reasonable than producing snowmen by the hundreds in the name of training.

She was, in her own way, also a little strange.

Nothing but strange people — surely they would remain incomprehensible no matter how many years passed.

She yawned again and looked out.

Beyond the mouth of the cave, a roaring wind sent white snow swirling to fill the world.

* * *

*The Fifty-Seventh Christand Snowy Mountain Stranding Tour*

*The paradise phantasm — Crisnate. The manor.*

It was the conference room that had been set up in the manor since their arrival here.

Shelves lined the walls, displaying horse sculptures, vases, swords, and suits of armour — and at the centre stood a large table.

A single-piece table cut from a branch of the World Tree.

At the short end of the rectangle, at the head, sat one person.

The manor's mistress: Selene Christand.

The princess of the Christand name, whose fame was woven into history, sat with her golden hair gleaming and her arms folded — a figure of commanding presence.

Young though her form appeared, she possessed an air of authority no ordinary person could emit.

To her right sat the great continental unifier, Kreschenta Alberan, arms folded in like fashion, and beside her sat the hero without peer in all of history, Alberinea — Krische Christand — who was watching the proceedings with a serious face, one hand resting on her stomach.

Next to her sat the divine-being's priestess, Lila Sharana, with a look of helpless resignation, and across from them, Head Servant Bery Argan and two other servants took their seats.

Before Selene Christand and Bery Argan alike, ten face-down cards had been lined up. Selene pushed one forward, and in response, Bery pushed forward one of her own.

Both flipped their cards without a word — and it was Selene whose brow furrowed.

Selene's card was cavalry.

Bery's card was a long spear.

Cavalry being what it was, defeat was unavoidable.

Selene's card was placed in the graveyard, and Bery drew her own card back to her side face-up.

A faint smile curved the lips of Bery Argan's childlike face.

"It's only just started, Bery."

"My apologies. ……I was thinking about what to bring to the mountains, and I'm afraid I got a bit carried away."

"……Whatever do you mean. This time it's the sea."

What they were playing was a game called Kessen-fuda.

It was something along the lines of a moderately sophisticated rock-paper-scissors, using picture cards comprising three types of infantry, cavalry, archers, weapons, and a general — the outcome determined purely by card matchups.

Drawn or losing cards went to the graveyard; winning cards stayed in hand.

A game that grew more disadvantageous the more you lost — this single defeat carried substantial weight, but it was still only one loss out of the whole.

The situation was still entirely recoverable.

There was no need to panic yet, Selene told herself, bringing her tea to her lips and fixing Bery with a stare.

"Play your card."

"Yes, then — two cards."

"——!?"

Bery pushed forward two face-down cards.

Exceedingly bold. Bery meant to settle things.

A two-card play — a gamble of sorts.

It could only be executed by combining two cards of the same type, or pairing any card with the general, and if one responded to it with a single card and won, both of Bery's cards went to the graveyard.

Even if it resulted in a draw, Bery would send those two cards to the graveyard and Selene would gain one card's worth of advantage.

But if Selene lost, she would lose the card she had played and additionally have two cards of the opponent's choosing sent to the graveyard.

Two out of eight was painful enough, but the greater problem was that even by chance, if the general went to the graveyard it meant defeat — which is to say, a one-in-four chance of losing outright.

If she responded with two cards of her own, that risk could be avoided — but the question was what she should do.

The opponent had opened with a spear.

Including three spears in a deck was difficult to imagine, but the opponent was Bery.

The possibility that Bery had anticipated Selene pressing boldly forward again with cavalry was entirely plausible.

Given Bery's character, a gambit-style play of two cards in the second round was better read as a carefully laid trap.

The old Selene would have judged that three spears was unlikely and played cavalry.

She glanced at the ornaments along the wall.

For some reason her eyes kept catching on the horse figurine Selene herself had made — an unconscious imprinting.

Bery Argan was a woman who chose no means if they served her ends. Using the room's furnishings to steer Selene's thinking was the least of her tricks. She was an utterly incorrigible woman, villainous and full of cunning.

Playing cavalry on the opening turn had been intuition, but that itself had in all probability been guided by Bery's hand.

Led by Bery's manipulation, Selene had unconsciously built her deck with four cavalry cards.

Without question, the row of horse figurines along the wall was there to make Selene think of horses.

No — more than that, the very fact that it was Selene who, after their argument, had proposed playing Kessen-fuda was because she had seen the swords and armour arranged in this room.

Different furnishings from usual — from the very start, Selene had been inside this servant's trap.

Under the guise of coincidence, Bery had created a battlefield favourable to herself, in order to make Selene choose Kessen-fuda and play cavalry on the opening move.

And then a two-card play, pressing against the shock of losing on the very first round.

Selene had nearly been misled by Bery's misdirection.

The countless defeats she had suffered to Bery Argan.

The cause could be attributed to a simple difference in pure thinking ability — but it was never only that.

In her hunger for victory, Selene had been losing.

Exceptional talent in hand, never relaxing her effort, and on top of that, the brazen nerve to justify even means called underhanded as long as they served the purpose of victory.

Bery was strong. How many times had she been brought to her knees before her — there was no counting it.

But if there was a day to surpass her, it could only be today.

"You carefully prepared the battlefield, and guided even my unconscious mind. Your hunger for victory is truly admirable. ……This is where the old me would have played cavalry."

Cavalry was a powerful card on the offensive, with an advantage against heavy infantry, weapons, and archers — but on the defensive it lost those advantages and became merely an ordinary card with a disadvantage against long spears.

Even so, bringing it to a draw would allow exchanging one card for two — the old Selene would have judged that the opponent would never include three spears in her deck, and would have steadily played cavalry again to even out the disadvantage she had just conceded.

One card exchanged for two wasn't bad, she might have thought — and taken the bait of Bery's cunning lure.

"But today is different."

She slid one card forward and flipped it.

"You played a long spear, didn't you?"

The red-haired servant stared hard at the card, and then breathed a sigh.

"Haah……. As expected of you, ojou-sama."

The card she had played was the archer. Strong against long spears and light infantry, weak against cavalry.

On the offensive it was weak against light infantry, but on the defensive it was an excellent card: two advantages to one disadvantage.

"……You have grown since the last time we played."

But Bery looked at it and smiled.

"I'm glad you've grown."

"……What?"

When they were flipped over, cavalry and general were revealed, and Selene's eyes went wide.

The general could not fight alone, but could be paired with any card.

It turned draws into victories, and when winning with an advantageous card, allowed one additional card to be sent to the graveyard.

"In that case, that archer and……please send three cards from your right hand to the graveyard."

By giving Selene the information of the general's position in advance, Bery had seized an overwhelming advantage.

Whether the play had been cavalry or the archer that guessed the counter — whichever option Selene chose, Bery had made a gamble designed to win.

Selene's general had barely survived, but what remained was ten cards versus five.

——In a mere two rounds, the outcome had already been decided.

"……Oh my."

Watching Selene lock up as usual, Lila looked at Bery with something like resignation.

Bery had told Lila beforehand that this year it would be a mountain banquet, as if the destination had already been settled — and indeed, it seemed it had been settled in Bery's mind alone.

Selene had apparently been hoping for the sea, and this "Christand council" had begun, as usual, with an argument between Bery — who tried to steer the conversation toward going to the mountains from the start — and Selene.

All Lila had felt upon hearing they were going to a snowy mountain was confusion.

The outcome was as good as decided.

It seemed they would end up going to the snowy mountain just as Bery had declared, but Lila found it baffling where Bery's confidence came from.

Even in games that appeared to be decided by luck, Bery won with mysterious ease the majority of the time.

Of course, it wasn't as though she always won without fail — but in these thousand-odd years, Lila had not once seen her lose a battle that seemed important (though personally, Lila tended to think most of them were just as well either way).

From the moment she declared the snowy mountain, victory had already been a foregone conclusion in Bery's mind.

Selene looking vexed and Bery smiling warmly — most likely, even this situation had been calculated from the start.

What appeared to be a fair, equal contest, yet was at the same time a battle arranged with victory as its premise.

A servant who lured her own mistress into a trap without a second thought.

The power dynamics of this manor were complex beyond comprehension — a mystery after a thousand years.

The day she understood them would probably never come.

*I wonder if divine beings are lonely*, she thought, idly.

When Krische came to visit and spoke with the divine being, the air shifted just slightly — there was something different about those moments.

Since Krische had begun visiting, it seemed to Lila that the divine being had changed somewhat, and now more often asked Lila things, making her mana tremble as if in laughter.

That impression must have been behind the thought.

The people of Kreisharana spoke of the divine being who kept silent and merely slept as an ultimate existence.

Though she occasionally cast that red-violet gaze at one person or another, almost no one had ever heard it speak.

Seeing the divine being exist there as a pure individual, seeking no one, the people of Kreisharana had turned toward it with reverence, calling it the *strong way of being* that all should aspire toward.

A person is born into the world as an incomplete infant with nothing.

That is why people are driven by many emotions and desires.

Everyone begins as an infant born of a mother, born into society, and however harsh a life they lived, none became adults without any protection whatsoever.

Seeking connection with others is something like an instinct for humans, and emotions and desires are nurtured within that interior.

No matter how much training one undergoes, it is impossible to discard all one's emotions and desires; no matter how much one affects solitary pride, one cannot cut it off completely.

For the people of Kreisharana who honed themselves as individuals and forged themselves as individuals, the feeling they had once directed toward the divine being was surely a longing, born of knowing there was a place they could never reach.

They looked at the divine being with longing eyes because they had come to know there was a place that could not be reached even by forsaking desire, enduring solitude, and tormenting body and mind.

Even so, perhaps someday they might reach it — using the divine being as a model, generation upon generation.

Lila had grown up receiving those teachings of Kreisharana as natural, without any particular doubt.

The divine being was, from birth, a complete existence.

Unlike imperfect humans, it was untouched by any emotion or desire — with no waste in it, and beautiful.

All humans should follow that example, aspire to it, and make the effort to draw closer to it.

If that were achieved, all seeds of conflict would disappear from the world — so went the teaching.

Lila had considered it a fine teaching, certainly, but hearing words from the divine being that were unexpectedly soft, seeing it make its mana tremble as if laughing, she was left with a vague question.

*Why*, she wondered, *had the divine being been sleeping all this time?*

She had begun to feel that she might have been making a fundamental error in her thinking.

What humans called the divine being's completeness was, in the end, no more than human logic.

Seeing the rich land of Alberan, and seeing "the divine being's friend" laugh so innocently and happily in the midst of people — Lila felt confused all over again.

Though she was far from what Kreisharana called an "admirable person," that figure looked, at the very least, profoundly happy, and her natural, unstudied manner looked more like the divine being than anyone else.

It was not a bad thing to hold up the divine being as an ideal to aspire toward.

But that was not the same as assuming the divine being was a being of perfect, flawless completeness.

Leaning on someone feels pleasant; exchanging words feels enjoyable.

These things were simply natural, and there was nothing wrong with them.

That the divine being delighted in conversation was not strange in itself, and in the first place, calling the divine being perfect was merely a human assumption.

She began to think that admiration had, without anyone noticing, pushed the truth further away.

What she had been looking at until then seemed to have been a "divine being as humans wished it to be" — an idol formed inside the human mind — and as the words accumulated, different aspects came into view.

*"Rigaleiwe-san just doesn't know what loneliness is. Even coming to talk with Krische like this — it must be because she's lonely."*

*Ah*, she had thought.

In the first place, if the divine being were a complete existence from birth, needing no one else, it would never speak any words at all.

*"……If I might wish for it — could I not be taken along as well?"*

She had asked this of Krische with the thought that she might be some small help in easing the divine being's long solitude.

She had hesitated, and been opposed by her father and brother, and arrived at the decision after worrying and worrying in her own way — but since coming here, other divine beings had visited frequently, and the surroundings had become quite lively.

In the face of the resolve she had arrived at after all that agonizing, the divine being was having a thoroughly good time with games and small talk.

*——Had I been making a staggeringly foolish, presumptuous fuss?*

Every time she recalled her past anguish she felt a certain indescribable feeling, but she tried not to think about it too much.

When she remembered the grim determination she had felt then, something like embarrassment washed through her, but considering that embarrassing memories would pile up endlessly from here on, choosing to look the other way was probably the right call for now.

Eternity looked harsh but was in truth gentle.

There was no need anywhere to rush to a conclusion, and if she wished, she could put it off indefinitely.

And at the very least, the divine being did not rush to conclusions.

That was one of the things she had learned from the divine being since coming here.

She had braced herself at first, wondering what eternity would be like, but there was nothing dramatically different about it.

Or rather — was it precisely because there was nothing dramatically different that it was fit to be called eternity?

Wake each morning, spend a day, sleep each night, repeated — nothing had changed, as such.

She had had a vague understanding that an extraordinary series of days would repeat themselves, and it had seemed somehow frightening in a sense, but nothing in particular came of it.

Whether it was eternity or not, what one lived was still the single day called today, the single moment called now.

The past simply accumulated there, and whether one lived a hundred years or a thousand, one could not know even something as small as what tomorrow's meal might be.

No enlightenment had opened up; when all was said and done, Lila Sharana was Lila Sharana.

One might imagine that a being who had lived a thousand years would be a sage with divine-flash-like insight and a spirit as unshakeable as an old tree — yet Lila did not feel that she herself had changed particularly much.

She was still keeping company with that weak-willed woman who regularly fell into indulgence while swearing *never again*, and writhed in the memory of past moments.

She probably wouldn't change a hundred years from now, or a thousand.

*Today's hardship is tomorrow's fruit* — those had been words her father and brother liked, but Alberan apparently had a saying: *The same flower from the same root.*

The idea being that one's fundamental nature never changes, and if anything, those words felt more apt for herself.

As she spent her days, the lack of progress in herself — no, it was this very thought that was the problem, she shook her head, and rolled the snow again.

She piled two more snow-balls on top of each other and, shivering, made a new snowman.

She looked up — nothing but magnificent blue and white, as far as she could see.

It was the vista of Greybyalbe, the highest place in the world.

Tall, steep mountain ranges spread out below, seemingly surrounding this one peak, and far in the distance one could probably see the sea — the blue of the sky and the blue of the ocean mingled and dissolved into each other, and their boundary shimmered vaguely.

Looking straight ahead was nothing but perfect silence, yet below, great avalanches were repeating one after another, sending up an enormous roar and a trembling of the earth.

People stood on boards and rode the surface of the snow down alongside the avalanches — a thoroughly exhilarating mountain descent.

Happy voices rang out between the thunder, and Lila watched Anne get swallowed by an avalanche before looking away and concentrating on her snowman-making.

This time she had come to discipline her endlessly indulgent self and train her mind and body.

Even with the temperature somewhat adjusted, it was genuinely cold out here, and continuing to make snowmen in this chill was quite a form of ascetic practice — which was precisely why it was good for forging herself anew.

For the past several days she had been staying at the manor preparing for the trip, drifting along and delighting in fine food, having her body washed in the bath, and sleeping soundly and warmly in a fluffy bed.

This very morning she had been unable to escape from the blankets in that drowsy half-waking state and had ended up being dressed by Elvena.

Her indulgence had reached its peak.

Her father and brother would surely grieve greatly if they saw her current state.

*"It is not that I feared death, nor that I was captivated by the temptation of eternal life. ……I simply wish to serve at the divine being's side as Kreisharana's final priestess."*

*"But——"*

*"……From this point forward I will live alone in the forest, meeting no one and asking no one's help. Until I reach the end of my natural life. Would you accept that as proof of my resolve?"*

Before coming here, to show her resolve to her opposing father and brother, she had spent several decades alone in the forest.

Seeing the rich land of Alberan, it had seemed certain that its interaction with Kreisharana would in time change Kreisharana, and that this was the natural form of things.

Kreisharana's way — rigorously forging mind and body — was beautiful, but she did not think it was a happy way of life for everyone.

The physically frail were looked down upon, and those who did not fit Kreisharana's idea of "beauty" fled to the flatlands or lived quietly at the outskirts of the settlement.

Though being a divine-being's priestess was said to be an honour, there was not a single woman in Lila's generation who wished to become one.

Even her brother had repeatedly worried over whether she was truly all right with it, and she had heard that the previous priestess had been a fine person, though even she had not wished for it from the bottom of her heart.

*Adorn thyself not — be as the divine being.*

Those born in Kreisharana were raised being taught the "correct way of being."

Lila had thought that way beautiful, yet understood that even within Kreisharana she had probably been an unusual person.

She had known that she was regarded as an eccentric who wanted to become a divine-being's priestess, and everyone said Kreisharana's teachings were right and admirable — but what was right and admirable was not necessarily the same as what was happy.

Being right and admirable was a difficult thing, and there were more hardships than not.

It was precisely because it was hard, precisely because one was imperfect, that those teachings existed.

The reason she had wanted to end the tradition of the divine-being's priestess with herself was that she felt it no longer suited the coming age.

There was no guarantee that there would always be an unusual person like herself.

Surely, as things grew more prosperous, such people would become even fewer.

It would surely be a sad thing to be compelled into the priestess's way of life without choosing it, unable to choose otherwise, and however right and admirable it might be, it would not be a happy way of life for that person — such had been the noble ideals on account of which she had imposed simplicity and abstinence upon herself. That was a long time ago now.

When she saw herself on an unrelenting path of indulgence, the past swiftly turned to embarrassment.

Patting the snow flat, recalling things from long ago——

"Hya——"

Something warm was pressed against her cheek.

She tumbled backwards and nearly fell over, and then saw Elvena laughing with her shoulders shaking.

Her hair, cut to chin length, swayed as a mischievous smile spread across her face, and in both her hands were two cups trailing wisps of steam.

"ふふ, I thought you might be cold, so I brought some tea."

"Ah, th-thank you……"

She handed over the tea, and then wrapped Lila inside the thick, open front of her outer cloak.

She pressed close and sat down beside her. They were very near.

The body that had been shivering in the name of training was warm in an instant.

"You've made quite a lot of them."

"……Not nearly enough. I've been on a path of pure indulgence lately, so this amount is……"

Lila spoke while watching Elvena begin to comb her hair.

She was generally close, with more physical affection than one might expect — something that seemed to be the manor's particular style — and though Lila had ceased to feel any awkwardness about it following the adage of *when in Rome*, in Elvena's case there was something at once artless and somehow deliberate about it.

Something was subtly different from the others, and there was a certain inexplicable quality to it.

Elvena, apparently noticing Lila's gaze, tilted her beautiful face in curious inquiry and smiled.

This person was also shrewd, and seemed perceptive of other people's subtleties — which was perhaps precisely why such gestures seemed somehow calculated and put Lila on her guard.

*She's going to interfere with my training again* — no, that was rude of her.

To someone who had been so consistently kind to her, to harbour doubt was unacceptable in a person.

Certainly she did have a somewhat teasing, mischievous quality, but interpreting everything in that light was not good.

Rather, she ought to be grateful that someone who had witnessed her go through any number of embarrassing moments continued to treat her exactly as before.

Just this morning she had been dragged out of bed looking like a fool and to top it off been dressed like a child — and who had that been, exactly?

"A-as for this morning……"

"Please don't trouble yourself over it. Last night I did press quite a lot of wine on you……I knew you didn't have much of a head for it, but I'm afraid I got carried away."

"No, no……I'm just lightweight, the wine itself I……"

The wine Elvena had brought to her room last night had been truly excellent.

Honestly, in the days when she had been in Kreisharana, she had never found alcohol pleasant, but the preferences of Krische and Kreschenta must have had a great deal of influence.

Their main-product fine wine aside, the wine made specifically for the manor was refreshingly fruity — a delicious drink that even Lila with her childlike palate could enjoy.

Midway through she had been offered a cocktail of fruit and wine, and had drunk it down happily thinking how good it was, until her memory grew hazy — she could not clearly recall what they had been talking about.

"But, l-last night too, I……I feel like I might have said something rude in my cups……"

"My goodness, rude? ……But it does seem you were quite exhausted. Perhaps the strain of your abstinence and self-discipline had been building up……"

"Ah, um, I……did I say something strange……?"

All that remained in her memory, hazily, was Lila leaning against Elvena and lecturing her.

Recalling it, she felt her ears grow hot.

"Well……you said that the people of this manor ought to learn a bit more self-restraint. That having something dangled in front of you was unbearable, and that someone should have some consideration for you for holding yourself back……"

"Ugh……"

"That was, ふふ, quite the picture — like Kreschenta-sama in one of her sulking moods……I personally found it quite charming."

What a pitiful thing to have said.

Recalling Kreschenta in her sulking mode (which amounted to three states: sulking, demanding to be coddled, or acting imperious), a twofold embarrassment washed through her.

The great queen, freed from the weight of great responsibilities, was quite literally as her appearance suggested — identical to her sister.

She had no memory of seeing her speak in real earnest (as opposed to merely appearing to) not once in these thousand years.

The embodiment of desire faithful to the pleasure of each moment in each place — she was a child.

"L-like Kreschenta-sama sulking……"

Abstinence and self-restraint — Kreschenta was the person furthest in the world from those words.

They say alcohol reveals one's true nature, and if she was told that the same true nature as Kreschenta's dwelt within her, she could not deny it, and Lila locked up.

Elvena smiled pleasantly and said:

"Why not let yourself go a little, Lila-sama? Going to all this trouble to train somewhere like this……riding the sleds is fun too, you know."

"N-no, if I let myself be swept along like that, in my case I feel like I'll become a hopeless person without realising it……well, I can't exactly call my current state admirable either, but I already feel like I've let myself go too far, as it were……"

"You are very serious, Lila-sama. I do think it would be all right to relax a little more, like Krische-sama and Kreschenta-sama……"

*No one here will laugh at you*, Elvena said, gesturing at the sky with one finger.

"Every person has desires. Even if they expose them, there is no one here who will laugh — that is simply normal. Even if someone were to call it decadence, eternity itself is, in the first place, the very extremity of decadence. It is a trifling matter."

"D-decadence, its very extremity……"

"Indeed."

She leaned closer, and brought her face near enough that her breath could be felt.

Her dark brown eyes held a colour that seemed, in some way, to draw one in.

"Things have a way of settling themselves, and immersing yourself for a time need not be a problem when time is infinite. I do think that sinking all the way to the bottom, just once, might be one worthwhile experience……"

"E-experience……"

"There is no need to worry — people are creatures who take steps to avoid ruining themselves. Just as a society and rules have naturally arisen even in this manor, which should in principle have none."

*A cycle, shall we call it*, Elvena said, and gestured toward Krische and the others enjoying the snow-descent.

Selene was riding a board down the snow-slope alongside an avalanche, scattering a vivid spray of white, and far below, Bery and the others were in the middle of rescuing Anne, who had just been swallowed by an avalanche — Anne's figure was in the process of rising up out of the snow.

How peaceful. How cheerful.

"They have the power to permit themselves anything, and what they do with it is playing in the snowy mountains — admirable, really. If you indulge in decadence and luxury to excess, you grow tired of it……which is probably why they sometimes naturally want to enjoy something as childlike as this. Selene-sama is quite easy to read in that way."

"Selene-sama……?"

"Yes. She is, as you know, a very serious person, but roughly once every thirty years she gets worn out and spends time idling about……that kind of breathing space is important too. Including the part where she then recovers and announces she is going to reform the manor's discipline — it has become one of the manor's cycles."

*Even a person like Selene-sama is like that*, Elvena said, and stroked Lila's cheek.

"There is no such thing as a perfect person who can continue living up to an ideal version of themselves. *"It looks wonderful on you, Lila-sama. ……ふふ, Krische-sama, would you like to try one too? It's easy to move in and very comfortable."*
*"I see……I like it. It might be perfect for the warm season."*
*"Oh yes, yes. Shall I sew something for everyone as well? I've gotten used to the sewing by now——"*

Elvena's plan was as legible as if it were written out for her.

Something must be done before it went into circulation, but the delicate matter of culture.

She would need to proceed with the greatest care in how she handled it.

Watching Anne clearly eavesdropping as she ate, Selene sighed.

* * *

Beside the workshop — which was really too large to call merely a cabin.

Striking chisels with the hammer, carving a large rock.

For Selene, who was always carrying worries, the time spent in the workshop was precious time when she could make her mind blank.

She had originally started it thinking she needed to find a hobby she could enjoy for all of eternity, but that was a long time ago.

Making things was good.

While making something, every part of herself focused solely on that.

The touch of both hands, everything outside her field of vision disappeared as she observed closely, and she projected the ideal finished form into her mind.

Sometimes from a small failure, something better than what she had imagined would occur to her.

At those times she would follow that feeling, and work slowly to make that vague image into something certain.

Everything was probably the same.

Forging steel, kneading clay, cutting and assembling wood — she had made many different things.

None of it could she say she had truly mastered — yet she could now see things she had not seen back when she was forging sashimi knives.

What mattered was observation and imagination.

Everything begins with imitation through observation.

And in the process of imitation, learning what made the bad things bad, examining them carefully, and reducing, one by one, the parts that lacked quality — that was all.

If there were no missing parts, it would be perfect; the finished form would become visible.

To vaguely chase an ideal would only upset the balance.

Like climbing a mountain one step at a time, advancing one thing at a time with certainty.

If she did that, without realising it, the image within her would emerge in definite form.

After that, she only needed to make it appear, not a hairsbreadth off.

The sashimi knife asleep in the workshop — bought a good while ago in the east of the continent, but that delicate art had communicated the depth of craftsmanship to Selene.

A blade she could never quite replicate, no matter how many times she tried.

After agonising over why she could not produce anything of that calibre herself, she had taken detours as breaks, but now she finally understood.

To put it by analogy, it was like a child challenging Kokys to a duel.

Stripping away all that was unnecessary, refining it — and that was only the minimum first step.

Selene had not been growing; she had only been trying to imitate the appearance of that art.

That had been the reason for her ceiling.

Two people had been the catalyst for her realisation.

What was outstanding in Krische and Kreschenta above all was the sharpness of their observation.

When the two looked at something, they could read clearly what purpose it served, what use it had, what it prioritised.

And from imitation, they could precisely strip away only the irrational parts.

She did not think she could do it as easily as they could, but she had all the time she needed.

If ten years were not enough, a hundred; if needed, she could use a thousand years.

Adding value to it — that could come later.

Up until now she had been seeking too much of a plus-alpha.

Like chasing a bird and stepping off a cliff.

First taking one step at a time was what mattered.

——When this is finished, let me challenge myself once more to making a knife that surpasses that one.

With that thought, she brought the hammer down.

*Kat, kok* — a clean sound rang out, and she focused her nerves on that sound too.

Brittle parts and hard parts, changing what needed to change based on the sound — the angle of entry and the degree of force.

These past thirty years or so had been spent on the stone sculpture, but she felt more response from it than from the various things she had made before.

She was aware of being clumsy and rough-handed, but she was not particularly poor at making something fairly large — a house, a stone sculpture, something of some scale.

What was being carved from the great block of stone was a matched pair of small angels, leaning close together.

Each with only one wing — the half-wing on each side — and the image she was projecting was Krische and Kreschenta.

Pouring into her craft what she had learned from them, careful and deliberate.

Sending her awareness all the way to the tip of the hammer she brought down, cutting deeper.

At this rate, she might be able to finish in one more week.

She paused to breathe and readied herself again:

"——Selene-sama! Please listen!"

*Gonk*, an unpleasant sound rang out from the stone sculpture.
