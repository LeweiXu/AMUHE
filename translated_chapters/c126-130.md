# Chapter 126: Her Crime and Punishment, Incomplete

Keil and Katias.

These two boys could be described as Krische's childhood acquaintances.

To sum them up in a word: nuisances.

When Krische was small, they would often drag her along under the pretense of playing.

They made her participate in incomprehensible make-believe battles, dragged her all over the village under the pretense of "exploring."

After the one time they entered the forest and Krische was scolded by her father Gorka, she stopped associating with the two of them.

They seemed to find it fun to break rules, and for Krische, who sought the approval of others, associating with them brought nothing but harm.

*'Keil and his friend say they want to play. Don't worry about helping me — go play with them.'*

*'Those two are bad kids, so Krische doesn't enjoy playing with them. Krische much prefers helping Kaa-sama with things, so……'*

*'W-well, I know they can be mischievous, and I understand you're angry about what happened before, but……ignoring them isn't good either. Whether you say yes or no, you should at least talk to them properly.'*

At the very least, they were inviting her because they thought it was fun — so Grace told her.

Krische thought Grace might be right and went to them, honestly explaining that playing with them wasn't fun so she wouldn't play — simply stating her position.

If they were inviting her out of goodwill, it would be bad to refuse them every time.

So it was better to make it clear that they needn't ever invite her again — that was her conclusion.

*'Ah……th-that's not quite what I meant, Krische.'*

*'……?'*

*'Um, hah……how should I put this……'*

*'……Um, more importantly, Kaa-sama, the pot is boiling over — is that alright?'*

*'Ah……'*

Her somewhat clumsy mother made many mistakes, and Krische wanted to help cover for them, and she liked spending time with Grace.

If Krische set her mind to helping her mother, there was always work to be done, and it was far more educational than spending time with those two childish boys.

*'Kaa-sama, um……the soup, too — there was too much salt even before the ingredients went in……just a little, really only a tiny bit, but……it's too salty for Krische.'*

*'I-is that so……I'm sorry……'*

*'Ah, no, it's fine……Kaa-sama is tired from work, so please sit down and rest. Krische will make tonight's dinner.'*

All the more so after she began cooking.

From then on, the two stopped inviting her to play, but in return they began flipping her skirt, calling her names, and harassing her.

It was unpleasant, but Krische was the one who had refused their goodwill.

She decided to endure it to a degree, accepting it as somewhat unavoidable.

The harassment at that time was trivial, and everyone understood that the two were in the wrong.

She figured leaving it alone would pose no problems, and the days passed.

What changed things was probably when she began visiting the training ground.

*——At any time, calamity may befall us. When that happens, only your own sword can protect you, and if you build that strength, you can protect the people precious to you as well. What you are learning is not violence, but the power to protect someone in their time of need. Know that.*

One day, she happened to pass by the training ground just as the militia captain Zaal was saying something like that to the children.

She had long thought that learning the sword for self-defense was a good thing, and she had interest, but it was hard to bring up with Grace, who disliked violence.

Yet those words seemed quite fine.

Not just for herself, but for someone else's sake.

Learning violence to protect someone in a time of need — it served well as a first excuse for learning the sword, and while the skilled hunters Gorka and Gallen aside, it would at least benefit her mother.

If her mother ever found herself in danger, the one closest to her side who could help would be Krische.

Krische told Grace as much and asked if she might participate in the training.

That evening, thanks to Gallen and Gorka nodding in agreement with Krische's words, she was permitted to join the training — though Grace repeated over and over that she must take great care not to get hurt.

For girls, participation was voluntary; for boys, it was mandatory once they were old enough to hold a sword.

Naturally, Keil and Katias were there too, and when Krische visited the training ground, the usual harassment. It had been going on ever since she refused their invitation, and she was thoroughly fed up — having them disrupt her training was a genuine nuisance.

*——If Krische shows them she's much stronger, surely this will stop too.*

Thinking simply along those lines, she lightly learned the sword basics and on the second day decided to make the two of them understand. The two were persistent and challenged her again and again, so she beat them down every time until they gave up — their stubbornness was not bad as far as sparring partners went.

She varied her methods each time, knocking their swords away and tripping them, continuing until the two finally cried and went home.

*'Grace! What kind of upbringing are you giving that child!? Training or not……Katias came home crying, you know!?'*

*'……I'm sorry.'*

*'Cut it out, Kanya. It's just a children's quarrel, honestly. Both your boy Keil and your Katias have been picking on Krische-chan plenty. Serves them right — maybe they'll settle down now. They've always had too much energy anyway.'*

The next day, Katias's mother came storming over.

Galla defused the situation, telling Krische with a smile that she wasn't in the wrong.

Krische thought so too. She hadn't struck them with the wooden sword, and she had taken some care when tripping them. She couldn't understand why she was being scolded.

*'……Krische didn't cause any injuries. The two of them kept saying they wanted to spar, so Krische just obliged them.'*

*'You were angry at those two, Krische, so you wanted to make them cry — isn't that right?'*

*'……Krische didn't want to make them cry. ……At first, Krische thought that if they realized Krische was much stronger, they might stop the harassment, but——'*

After Galla and Katias's mother left, Grace sighed, looking troubled.

*'……I see. I think both sides were wrong in this case. Of course the two of them were wrong for all the harassment, and Krische was also a little wrong for trying to solve it with force.'*

*'Krische was wrong too?'*

*'Yes. We have mouths that can speak properly, so if you want someone to stop something, you should first try to talk it out. Using force might make the other person listen, it's true, but……if that were the right answer, then the strongest person would be the most important, able to make anyone do what they wanted.'*

A gentle embrace, a hand stroking her head.

The sensation Grace gave her was always pleasant.

*'There may be situations where you truly have no choice and force is needed, but……that should only ever be a last resort. I want Krische to become someone who can solve all sorts of things properly, through words. ……I know talking isn't your strong point, but I believe Krische is a good girl who can do that sort of thing properly.'*

*'……A good girl.'*

*'Yes. You're my pride and joy, after all. ……If there's something hard to say, or something you can't solve on your own, I'll always help. I might not be the most reliable, but once in a while, lean on me. I am your mother.'*

Krische thought about Grace's words in many ways.

But the idea that the strongest person was the most important didn't seem wrong either.

If she became the strongest, and that solved all her problems — wouldn't that be fine?

To Krische, that thought was more appealing than anything.

Children were a nuisance when they cried.

By contrast, adults above a certain age wouldn't cry and go tattling.

She threw herself into training against adults, and within a week, she lost less often than she won; within a month, no one but Zaal could beat her.

Around the time people started avoiding sparring with her, what she began to hear were whispers behind her back.

Creepy. Something's wrong with her head.

She had often heard such things when she was small, silently following behind Grace — words she hadn't heard in a long time.

Strange tales also seemed to be circulating — that the chickens they kept had died because Krische killed them, that she had been pulling out rabbit innards and eating them. Things she had no memory of doing. She found it puzzling.

Eventually, she realized that it was Keil and Katias who were spreading such stories.

Not everything was their doing, probably.

By that point, she had at last begun to understand that beating sparring opponents too harshly left a bad impression, and she realized she had been unknowingly damaging her own reputation considerably.

But having more slander spread was also a problem.

The overt harassment, such as her laundry being found on the ground, had disappeared — but they were still secretly continuing their harassment behind her back. In accordance with what her mother had told her, she considered making an opportunity to speak with them.

However — even if she were to ask them to stop the insults and harassment, Krische had no reason to bow her head to them. Being subjected to all this simply for refusing their invitation long ago clearly exceeded 'a little unpleasantness.'

Even if she bowed and asked them to stop, she thought they might only grow bolder.

If they learned that harassment would make Krische yield and grovel, they might repeat the same thing whenever something came up.

Krische said nothing to Grace.

Relying on Grace and causing her trouble was something unforgivable within Krische's mind.

Yet Grace apparently tried to speak with the two on Krische's behalf, and when they ran from her, she apologized with a troubled face.

Seeing that face, Krische thought a little more.

*'……Why's she here, Mom?'*

*'What a way to talk. ……Today is for your counting-ten feast, you know. Krische-chan said she'd help me with the preparations.'*

*'……I'm not eating any pie that freak made. Disgusting.'*

*'Keil!'*

Boys who turned ten that year were permitted to apprentice with the hunters, and as a milestone, they became the stars of the spring festival.

Galla had always been considerate of Krische and suggested that perhaps she could try offering pie at the festival. Maybe that would resolve this silly business, she said.

Krische, who had been wondering what to do, agreed.

She thought she should at least make an effort herself, for the sake of Grace, who had gone out of her way for her.

*'I'm sorry, that idiot son of mine just won't listen no matter how many times I tell him……I'm truly sorry to you, Krische-chan.'*

*'No, Krische doesn't mind at all.'*

*'……Thank you. I really am sorry.'*

She truly did not mind.

For Krische, this was meant as a final effort, and if he refused to cooperate, that was perfectly fine.

Beings whose mere existence made Krische uncomfortable and caused her harm.

They were no different from dirt and dust in the house.

Simply put, if they caught her eye, she need only clean them away.

At the very least, they were existences she had no need for in her surroundings, and at the very least, there was nothing Krische would be troubled by if those two disappeared.

The prohibition on killing — such rules were merely rules.

Krische obeyed them purely for the sake of her reputation within this community.

If obeying those rules became a detriment to her, there was no need to follow them at all, and if she killed those two and it never came to light, there wouldn't be a single problem.

Krische had the power to do so, and if she killed them, there would be no need to make them listen to reason, no need for anything.

They would have no mouths to speak with, no hands or feet to harass her.

She began preparations that day, and within a few days, she had dealt with the two of them.

Now those two would never make her uncomfortable again.

It took less than a quarter-hour that day to finish the job, and with a refreshed feeling she helped her mother with her work.

Galla grieved when the two bodies were found, but Krische thought nothing of it in particular.

She had been using the oven at Galla's house for some time, and Keil had always gotten in the way, so if anything, this was more convenient.

She visited the now-solitary woman's house every day to bake pies.

*'……Krische-chan. The oven again?'*

*'Yes. And Kaa-sama said, if you'd like, would you join us for supper?'*

*'……If that's the case, let me join you. Sorry for the trouble.'*

*'No, Krische gets to use the oven, and you've been so kind to Krische — Krische is really, really grateful to oba-san and——?'*

Galla clung to Krische, her shoulders trembling.

*'I'm sorry……can I just stay like this, for a little while?'*

*'Yes, that's fine. Krische likes it when oba-san hugs Krische like this.'*

*'……Thank you.'*

Krische smiled at the sensation.

After that, Galla became much kinder to Krische than before.

Katias's mother had never been kind to Krische to begin with — she was more the spiteful sort — and hearing that she had vanished during a foraging trip shortly after was welcome news.

Several such unpleasantries disappeared from the village.

She truly, from the bottom of her heart, thought she had done the right thing in killing those two.

She sometimes had a faint regret that the two might have been somewhat useful as future members of the militia, but more than anything, the refreshing feeling was overwhelming.

At the time, she had no questions about any of it, and she hardly ever thought about it afterward.

And yet——

——Bery listened in silence as the halting tale of the past was told.

"Why would you tell me something like that?"

"……Krische didn't want to……have secrets from Bery, so, um, Krische thought Krische should tell Bery properly."

Krische continued.

As if gauging Bery's expression.

"……After that, before the bandits came, Krische also killed an old man who had been touching Krische's body, and……but there were proper reasons for both, and honestly, even now, Krische thinks Krische wasn't wrong."

Krische waited as if expecting Bery to speak.

Bery saw this and answered.

"……I see. If that is what you truly believe, Krische-sama, then I believe the same."

"Eh……?"

Bery smiled at the bewildered Krische.

"I am always on Krische-sama's side, so of course. If Krische-sama tells me from the heart that it was right, then I too shall believe in the same rightness as Krische-sama."

And she drew close.

She leaned in, gazed straight into her eyes, and placed a hand on her cheek.

"Please be at ease. There is nothing that could make me dislike Krische-sama. Even if every person in the world said they hated Krische-sama, I alone would still love you. I have sworn it, and I have decided it. ……So there is no need to be frightened that I might come to dislike you."

"Um……that's——"

"Whether it be sin or anything else, my first priority is Krische-sama. If anyone were to condemn Krische-sama for this, I would hold my head high and proudly tell them that Krische-sama is right."

"W-wait, wait……please, that's not——Krische didn't mean——"

Krische said in a fluster, and seeing her like that, Bery chuckled softly and stroked her head.

Then she embraced her and asked.

"……Then what did you mean by telling me such a thing?"

"The truth is……Krische knows it was wrong, and Krische knows it's something Bery wouldn't like, but Krische said it anyway."

"You wanted me to scold you — is that it?"

"……Krische doesn't know."

She stroked the bewildered Krische's hair, waiting patiently.

This was not something where she could simply teach 'do this, do that' — there was no correct answer.

If Krische genuinely believed the earlier answer was sufficient, Bery would accept that.

She only felt that it must not be words spoken without hesitation, without anguish.

"What Krische said earlier wasn't a lie — Krische did think all of that. Even now, Krische thinks it was unavoidable back then, a little, but……making oba-san sad was really, really wrong, Krische thinks……but it was such a long time ago……so Krische doesn't know what Krische should do."

"……What Krische-sama wishes to know is whether you should apologize to Galla-sama, who knows nothing, or whether you should keep it a secret?"

"……Yes."

If there had been some brilliant answer, she might have wavered over whether to give it.

But no such answer existed anywhere.

"Krische likes oba-san. Coming back after so long, Krische wanted to make oba-san happy……but when Krische remembered that Krische made oba-san really, really sad, Krische wasn't sure if that was truly alright, or what to think……"

It was not the killing itself.

What Krische felt guilt over was having saddened Galla through it.

"Krische knows it was wrong, and Krische did it anyway. Krische thinks Krische should properly apologize. But Krische has been keeping it a secret……and oba-san doesn't know. ……If she found out, she would surely be very, very sad — and when Krische thought about that, um, Krische didn't know what to do."

She cannot feel that killing people is wrong.

If necessary, she kills.

She was always the sort of person who felt no doubt about that.

She learned rules to fit into society, judged good and evil by comparing things to those rules' definition of 'right,' and strived to be a good girl.

Even as she said it was unavoidable, even as she said she knew it was wrong.

What she regretted was not killing people — it was breaking the rules.

And she regretted having saddened Galla through it.

That she brought this up at all was surely because of Bery's presence.

Bery knew how dependent Krische was on her.

Bery knew how desperately she tried to be a good girl whom Bery would love.

That was why, to deal with her guilt, she seemed to want Bery to scold her.

She cannot understand what is normal for people.

Not what is good, not what is bad, not what is right, not what is wrong.

She believes this is because she is abnormal, and she has resigned herself to it.

That is why she wants to be guided.

Because she dislikes the self that cannot feel bad things are bad.

*'——Krische is strange, so even if Krische kills people, Krische really doesn't feel sad or hurt or anything like that. Krische is fine with it. ……But when Bery sees that side of Krische, it makes Bery feel hurt, feel sad — isn't that right?'*

What this girl longed for was trivial, unremarkable — simply 'normal.'

But because she knew her own values were out of alignment with normal, she entrusted this to Bery. She was asking because she wanted to feel what Bery considered right — to feel normal guilt for normal sins, and accept punishment.

To draw even a little closer to normal.

And the reason for that was, of course, to live happily with Bery and others——

"……Krische-sama, you are beautiful."

"Bery……?"

Past sins — she needn't have brought them up at all.

In fact, if she were truly 'normal,' she would have kept quiet and pretended it never happened.

That way, no one would be hurt, no one inconvenienced, and the truth could be buried forever.

Yet her clumsy self could not make that choice.

Because she wished, more than anyone, to become an ordinary normal.

"I intend to answer whatever Krische-sama may ask me, as best I am able."

What is happiness for her? — Bery wondered from time to time.

Bery intended to teach and give Krische many things so she might find happiness, yet what troubled Krische most was probably the very 'ordinary happiness' Bery offered.

Was wielding righteous principles truly what led to her happiness?

If she had never known, never been taught, she might have lived free of doubt and been happy.

She was stronger than anyone, more complete than anyone, possessed of all the power and everything she could need.

Bery was sure she made Krische happier than anyone could.

And yet — perhaps she was also making Krische weaker, more unhappy, more than anyone. The thought surfaced from time to time, and so she could not find an answer within herself either.

"But I'm afraid I cannot answer that question."

It would be easy just to say something. If she casually offered an answer, Krische's present worry would be resolved. But that would only be resolution — a mere stopgap that would not address the fundamental problem.

She was surely a cowardly villain.

Pushing self-serving kindness onto her, always pulling her this way and that.

Aware of all this, and yet——

"As I said before……I believe Krische-sama should think, struggle, and make what you believe to be right into your answer."

That was what Bery told her.

# Chapter 127: The Crescent Moon's Arc

The sky was remarkably clear that night.

Stars glittered, and a cold wind flowed quietly.

Rising into the heavens was a beautiful crescent moon, waning, tracing its arc.

Fragile in the mid-sky, it shone silver-white and illuminated the surroundings.

Slowly warming her hands with her breath, Bery gazed up at the sky in a daze.

Its great arc was imperfect, yet boundlessly beautiful.

Waxing and waning, endlessly repeating, far from the perfection of a full circle, carrying a precariousness as if it might suddenly vanish from the sky.

——Bery loved the moon in that form.

The crisp sound of footsteps in snow came from behind her.

The gait was slightly slow, irregular and unsteady.

Bery turned toward the approaching footsteps.

"Welcome back, Krische-sama."

"……Yes."

She embraced the small body that came to her side, combing through that silver hair that resembled the moonlight.

And gently stroked it.

"……Krische couldn't say it."

"I see."

Bery said, inviting her inside the warmth of her cloak.

"Look — you can see a beautiful moon tonight."

——A lovely crescent, just like Krische-sama's name, she said.

*'——Confessing in order to atone for a sin could generally be called the right thing to do. But it cannot necessarily be called a good thing. It could destroy every happiness you've built up until now. It could be called nothing more than self-satisfaction.'*

That was what Bery had told Krische.

*'……With that in mind, what Krische-sama wishes to do, what Krische-sama believes she should do — that alone is what matters. It is an issue within Krische-sama's own heart. Only……'*

Without telling her what to do,

*'I believe that Krische-sama thinking this way is the right thing, and whatever conclusion you reach, I believe there is no wrong in it. I'm sure of that, no matter what it may be — at least, that is what I think.'*

She merely entrusted the choice to Krische.

Through the night Krische kept thinking about what would be best, but the answer would not come easily.

The old man who touched her body — the same as Garo.

She had thought he deserved to die, and she felt refreshed when she killed him.

Even now she did not believe it was entirely wrong, but she now thought that saddening Galla had been a bad thing.

Apologizing for that was probably the right thing to do.

But Galla didn't know. She hadn't even noticed.

And she appeared to have already accepted her son's death; she and Krische had had a good relationship all along, and she loved Krische.

If Galla learned that Krische had killed her child, she would surely grieve and rage.

She might realize that everything until now had been a sham and become miserable.

She might think it an unbearable tragedy that she, the abnormal Krische, had been the one she loved.

To confess was right, but not necessarily good.

That was surely what Bery was trying to say, and it seemed perhaps she should not apologize.

Feeling bad toward someone she had killed.

She had never felt such a thing to begin with.

"What's wrong, Krische-chan? So formal all of a sudden."

Dinner at Galla's house ended, and Krische had asked Bery to step out ahead.

Krische was the one who had done wrong — she didn't want Bery to be subjected to the same verbal abuse.

Bery would surely accept any abuse in silence, without protest. She would not get angry at the injustice; she would listen as if she herself had committed the crime. Even if struck, she might not resist.

Bery always worked hard for Krische's sake.

For Krische, she would endure any hardship.

That was why Krische didn't want her here.

The one who was wrong was the messed-up Krische — never, ever Bery.

She wanted to become a Krische whom Bery could love from the heart.

She wanted to see the same things Bery saw and rejoice, to feel the same things Bery felt and live together, to think that what Bery thought was good was good, and what Bery thought was bad was bad — to become that kind of Krische.

"……Krische-chan?"

It all started because of Bery.

Because she had seen Bery speaking happily with Galla, probably.

Because she had seen Galla wiping tears before the grave, and Bery had looked sorrowful, probably.

So she suddenly recalled things from long ago that she no longer even thought about.

She could have just kept it secret from Bery and that would have been the end of it, but her chest felt tangled, and she felt she had to tell Bery properly.

If she asked herself why, only one thing came to mind.

She hadn't wanted to create a lie in her relationship with Bery.

Krische cannot understand other people well.

Everyone is vague, everyone has facades, and their words are full of lies.

Krische's 'normal' was never the same as theirs to begin with, so she couldn't understand them all the more.

That was why Krische valued appearances to fit in, worked hard to look like a good girl, and spent her days worrying about how she was perceived.

*'——Please show me all these different parts of Krische-sama. Whatever others may think, at the very least, I wish to understand Krische-sama. When common ground grows between us, all sorts of things will become more enjoyable, more happy. ……For me, and for Krische-sama too.'*

That was why being told those words had made her so happy.

Bery accepted and tried to understand even the strange parts of Krische — and with Bery alone, Krische didn't want to tell lies.

She didn't want to bring such deception into their relationship.

"Um, there's something Krische wants to talk to oba-san about……"

Krische slowly began.

*'No matter what sins Krische-sama may commit in the future, I will continue to love you. But it is still very painful when someone you love commits a sin without knowing it, and that is why I have taught Krische-sama so many things. ……Because I wish for you to live happily, without committing any sins.'*

Sins should be atoned for.

At the very least, if one knows it is a sin.

If Krische felt it was a sin, then she should do so.

Bery had taught Krische many things for the sake of her happiness.

Empathy, affection, cooking, everything — Bery had always taught her about what you gain by being with others.

At the very least, it was not for the sake of the false relationships Krische had been building.

What Bery always offered was something to build relationships that were not false.

She thought it might be self-satisfaction, and she didn't think it was a good thing.

Galla liked Krische, and Krische liked Galla.

It seemed like a good relationship — but it was built on a lie.

There was nothing but loss in breaking it, and yet Krische felt she should.

Krische wanted to become like Bery.

To see the same things and rejoice, to see the same things and grieve, to feel the same things.

Krische didn't know what was right or what was good.

But she felt that if Bery were here in her place, she would probably do this — and so Krische wove her words.

"It's about……oba-san's child."

"……Keil?"

Galla's eyes widened, staring at Krische as she hung her head.

"……Yes. The truth is……even though Krische said Krische didn't mind, Krische really did mind the insults from Keil and Katias."

Galla furrowed her brow as if thinking.

"They spread bad rumors about Krische to all sorts of people, and Krische thought it was really troublesome……and wished they'd just disappear."

Hearing Krische's words, she closed her eyes.

"So, Krische——"

Krische tried to continue, but——

"Krische-chan, let's stop there."

"……Eh?"

Galla stopped her.

Seeing Krische's surprise, Galla let out a sigh.

"I could tell something was off during cooking and dinner. That food-loving glutton Krische-chan had a dark expression, after all."

"……Oba-san."

And then, with a faint smile, she continued.

"What I've just heard makes sense to me. But I don't want to hear any more than that. So even if you want to keep talking, Krische-chan, please stop."

"But——"

"It's fine."

Galla looked up at the ceiling, and for a while, she remained silent.

She closed her eyes and steadied her breathing.

And then she looked at Krische.

"……It's something I accepted long ago, and today, Keil has finally departed on his journey. Regardless of what truly happened, regardless of how things were……it's over. That is a story oba-san does not want to hear."

Galla reached out to Krische's head.

Krische flinched for an instant but held still.

Galla simply, gently, stroked her head.

"Oba-san loves you dearly, Krische-chan, and by now, you're something like my reason for living. Whatever the truth of those days may be……I've spent enough time to feel that way, and I've thought about many things and made up my mind."

Still stroking,

"……No matter what anyone tells me now, I can't bring myself to resent you, Krische-chan."

Galla said it with slightly lonely, gentle eyes.

Long ago — she had suspected it. Wondered if Krische might have been involved.

Her son had done terrible things to Krische, and she knew that the girl Grace had taken in was a little different from normal.

She couldn't say she had never thought — maybe she'd done something to her son.

But Krische never showed the slightest sign of it, kept coming to borrow the oven, kept cooking together.

As those days became routine, those suspicions faded away before she knew it.

She came to see more and more of the girl's good side, and after that——

"……So that story ends here."

The Krische who cut down bandits to protect the village.

The Krische who clung to Grace and to her.

The hardworking, well-mannered, but gluttonous, stubborn Krische.

Even now, all those images of Krische from their village days floated behind her eyelids.

Whatever truth the girl had been about to speak, Galla had found happiness again by loving her.

Nothing more was needed.

"But, oba-san……"

"If Krische-chan isn't satisfied with that, then keeping it to yourself is the punishment oba-san gives you. Understood?"

"But……"

Krische still looked unconvinced.

But she looked at Galla's face and, as if giving in, nodded.

"……Yes."

"……Good girl. Come here."

Galla embraced the downcast Krische and pressed her cheek against the top of her head.

She stroked the silky silver hair with tender love, and smiled.

"……Oba-san has decided to love Krische-chan enough for Grace's share too. You've come all the way home, so don't make a face like that."

Krische couldn't say anything and simply nodded.

Galla went on.

"We say goodbye again tomorrow, and that makes me sad. Oba-san wants Krische-chan to be smiling. Grace wants the same."

"……Kaa-sama does?"

"Yes, Grace too."

Galla patted Krische's back lightly and stood, then opened the closed wooden window.

No matter what Krische did, no matter what was said about her, no matter how many mistakes she made — always.

A dark indigo sky scattered with stars, and in the center, a crescent moon tracing its arc.

So that she might gaze upon that beautiful moon with someone beside her.

Her mother's wish would probably never come true — she had vaguely felt that, and yet.

Red hair, scattered and tangled in the snow.

Silver hair mingled with red, and the moonlight reflected off the snowy earth, glittering.

The eyes that narrowed gently, happily — the heat, the sensation, all of it.

Without tiring of it, as if conveying something that couldn't be put into words.

It was upon the snow of her homeland.

——On a night when a crescent moon hung in the sky.

*'I understand the feeling. She's a good girl. There are plenty of paths to happiness without a battlefield. When the time comes, tell Krische-sama I authorized it on my responsibility. ……Krische-sama, too, will surely understand once she hears the reason.'*

*Losing her as a fighting force would be a shame,* Dagra continued.

*'How one lives is a matter of freedom. I have no right to bind her to this. ……Were it not for the coincidence of Krische-sama discovering her, she would likely have been deemed unfit and sent home. A chain of coincidences led her here……but well, I don't think it's right to devote one's life to the military simply by inertia. Perhaps this coincidence means the time has come for her to reconsider.'*

*'Yes. That's more or less what I was thinking, too.'*

*'In any case, it is Mia's choice. You're her closest friend — watch over her.'*

——Recalling the conversation she'd had with Dagra the other day, Kalua watched Mia crouching in the snow from the shadow of a tree.

She nodded, with a touch of sadness.

"……This is for the best."

Scatterbrained, clumsy.

But caring and kind.

Mia was too good a person for the battlefield.

In a nice village like this, making a family and living happily.

If such a future existed, nothing could be better — Kalua thought.

——*Mia will surely find happiness.*

She said it in her heart, and returned to Mia's house.

*This is for the best,* she told herself.

# Chapter 130: Stubborn

Milna was preparing dinner. Kalua was beset by children.

*I'm home,* Mia said curtly, and nothing more, as she removed her cloak and propped her sword against the wall.

*So how'd the confession go?* Kalua asked, and Mia pouted.

"……How did you know it was a confession?"

"……Hah, normally you can tell at a glance. Mia's just *ultra-*dense, so maybe you didn't pick up on it, but he had 'I love you' written all over his face."

Mia glared at Kalua. Milna hummed cheerfully — *Sounds like a celebration tonight* — and laughed.

"Don't. I'm turning him down."

"Huh? What are you talking about, girl?"

Milna frowned at Mia.

"You saying you don't like Bazel at all? He's not exactly dashing, I'll give you that, but he's a kind, decent man. I've been watching him since he was a boy, and I'm fond of him."

"That's true, but……it's not about that. I told you I'm the unit adjutant — I have responsibilities. I can't just quit that easily, and besides……I like it there."

"Hah……don't be ridiculous."

Milna pressed a hand to her forehead in exasperation.

"Throwing away a chance like this for duty. What do you plan to do with the rest of your life?"

"I'll figure it out."

"Listen to yourself……no marriage, no family, throwing away every happiness a woman could have — is that your plan, devoting your life to the military?"

"And what if it is?"

The two stared each other down, and the children read the tension, conspiring wordlessly to retreat to their room.

Though they were curious enough to crack the door open and eavesdrop — rather conspicuously.

"……I respect you, Mom. You're amazing at weaving, you made a home, you raised us……my sisters too. I really think it's wonderful, and maybe that's the greatest happiness a woman can have, but……"

With a level gaze, she spoke.

"But that's not the only version of a happy future for me. At the very least, I think I'm really happy right now, and even with everything, I feel satisfied."

"Hah……honestly. So what — you'll go off to battle for your country and be fine with getting killed, or something horrible happening to you? You could say you lived a satisfying life with a straight face?"

"I think you fundamentally misunderstand what soldiers do, Mom."

Mia shook her head.

"I fight as a soldier so that I don't get killed, so that Kalua and the others don't get killed. ……Obviously I wouldn't be satisfied getting killed, and if you ask whether I truly have the resolve to die — honestly……I'm not sure."

She remembered well what it was like to nearly be killed.

It had been terrifyingly, impossibly frightening, and if Krische hadn't arrived, she would have died.

Yet even after experiencing that, she still wanted to work in that unit.

Because she felt there was value enough there to warrant it.

"But my fighting like that has real meaning, and I feel like there's something there that only I can do. ……Of course, maybe in five or ten years I'll feel differently, but at least right now, that's how I feel."

Milna looked into Mia's eyes and sighed.

She rubbed her temples, leaned against the wall, and lowered her gaze.

"……I hear what you're saying. I lose. It seems you've found your own resolve."

"Mom……"

"But even so, I worry about you."

Milna touched her own belly.

As if reminiscing. As if cherishing it.

"You were in here for nearly a year. As you got bigger, you got heavier, I had to be careful about everything, and when it came time to give birth — don't even get me started on the pain. Having two before you didn't make it any easier. I screamed and cried for you to hurry up and come out."

"…………"

"……But once you were born, you were so precious. Seeing you cry with all your might, I felt relieved from the bottom of my heart. And I've watched you grow, little by little, ever since. Sure, you were clumsy and a handful compared to your easygoing sisters, but you're still my darling child."

Milna looked up.

The face of a mother, pure and simple, gazing at Mia.

"Serving your country as a soldier is a fine thing. But risking your life in some dangerous place just to earn that 'fine' ……I'd rather you not be fine at all. I'd rather you find a man who loves you, make a family, and be happy."

Mia's gaze wavered at those eyes.

She couldn't claim to understand what a mother felt, but she could believe that the words spoken were true.

Her mother, who scolded and shouted yet always, always worried about her.

There was an undeniable weight to her earnest words.

And yet.

"……I'm sorry. But I——"

"Mia."

The one who called her name was Kalua, who had been silent.

"I know it's not really my place to butt in, but……I think you should give it serious thought."

"……Why would *you* say that, Kalua?"

"Because I think this is that kind of opportunity."

Kalua pointed to the swords propped in the corner of the room.

Mia's and Kalua's, side by side.

"I can't put down the sword — I owe Bunny too much. But Mia doesn't have that. You don't have to do anything dangerous. You can stay in this village and choose a peaceful life. ……That's a happy thing, and if you can choose it, I think you should."

Kalua looked at her own hands.

Her eyes were distant.

"I've done plenty of dirty things — killed plenty of people off the battlefield too. I can't go home anymore. I just drifted until I ended up with Bunny. I'm satisfied with that. But Mia has so many roads open to her — you don't have to force yourself into finding a home in a dangerous place."

"……You're saying I should quit?"

Kalua hesitated briefly, lowered her eyes, and nodded.

"Yeah. This time, I'm on Milna-san's side. ……Because I think you really do have a place here, Mia."

"——"

Mia slammed the table and shot to her feet.

"Kalua, you idiot!!"

She grabbed her cloak and the sword propped against the wall and stormed out of the house.

Kalua sighed quietly and scratched her head.

"……I'm sorry about that."

"No. ……I was thinking the same thing."

She picked up her own sword and buckled the belt around her waist.

She noted with a slight smile that Mia had grabbed her sword on the way out — old battlefield habits ran deep.

"I'll step out too."

"Yeah, if you would. You'd be better than me at this point. ……Stubborn girl, getting it from me in the worst places. She doesn't need to take after that."

"Hehe, well……the stubborn side of her is pretty cute, so I like it."

She smiled cheerfully.

Then, for just a moment, a wistful expression crossed her face.

"……She's a good girl. Unusually good for a place like that……and that's why I want Mia to be happy. This really is a wonderful village."

"……Making you do the hard part."

"No. From the start, I came with this in mind, if the chance arose."

Milna stood and bowed, and Kalua nodded.

She pulled on her cloak and went outside.

The sky was a sunset vermilion.

The thick clouds had strangely cleared, and the sky was transparent.

The snow dyed in twilight was beautiful beyond measure, yet there was something lonely in it.

Kalua followed Mia's footprints.

A mana-user's wide-spaced stride — they led straight into the forest.

There, she had leapt to a branch, and the footprints vanished, but the snow shaken from branches and leaves left a trail.

She estimated the direction and pressed on — probably the same spot as the first day.

She made for it,

"——Mia."

And found her sitting on the rock.

Mia ignored the voice and turned her face away. Kalua smiled wryly, scooped up snow as she approached, packed it by hand,

And placed the snowball on top of Mia's head.

Mia seemed about to say something and glared, but she was committed to the silent treatment and quickly averted her eyes.

Kalua stacked another snowball on top of the first, drew a face on it with a fallen stick, and stuck twigs out from the sides.

"……Excuse me, I'm *angry* here."

Finally unable to take any more, Mia spoke.

"Hehe, I know."

Glaring at Kalua, Mia took the snowman off her head and examined it.

The craft was poor, the thing unspeakably ugly. She wavered between throwing it and keeping it, and finally set it beside her.

"……Mia was never meant to swing a sword on a battlefield. You're too good for that."

"If you want to say that, the same goes for you, Kalua. Beautiful, a proper lady, someone who threw everything away for her sister's sake——……what's the difference between me and you?"

"Refinement and bust size, I'd say?"

"……Kalua, I'm being serious."

*I know,* she repeated.

Kalua smiled, looking troubled, then smiled gently and stroked Mia's head.

Even so — when she smiled like that, Kalua really was beautiful.

Even when she acted tomboyish, that smile was elegant.

Every little gesture was strangely lovely, captivating.

Dark, lustrous hair gleaming in the sunset, cat-like eyes wrapped in lashes — mischievous, but kind.

"You have a place to come home to, Mia. Without making the battlefield your home. This is a good village, your family are good people and good kids, and from the looks of it, Bazel-kun would make you happy. ……It's a more fortunate thing than you realize."

"So you're telling me to stay?"

"Yeah. ……This village — you don't hate it?"

Mia shook her head.

Kalua went on.

"I talked to the captain. He said that if it makes you happy, that's fine. Bunny's a good kid too — she'll understand. So will everyone in the unit."

"What does *Kalua* think?"

"I just told you."

"That's different. ……I want to know how *you* feel, Kalua."

That troubled look again.

Kalua toyed with the hair she wore loose like a horse's tail, thought for a moment,

"……I'd like Mia to find happiness in this peaceful village."

And undid her hair tie.

Long hair swayed in the wind, scattering, glittering.

"A little lonely, but I think it's for the best."

She leaned close, gathered Mia's hair at the back, and tied it up carefully.

She laughed softly and patted the top of her head.

Mia lowered her eyes.

"Adol and Kels got all fired up for nothing — 'leave this to us and go on ahead.' Even Bagu, who seemed like he'd refuse to die no matter what, went just like that. I was reminded — that's what the battlefield is. I'm not all that strong, and I don't have the power. ……I can't say with confidence that I'll protect you. So——……yeah, I'm scared."

Kalua said it and let out a quiet breath.

"That atmosphere is something else. It makes you throw away things you'd normally treasure, just like that."

For comrades, for honor, for pride.

The battlefield was full of reasons to throw one's life away.

A madness that beckoned — and everyone offered up their lives.

In a world that was thoroughly mad, you couldn't survive without going mad yourself.

"That's why I don't want Mia there."

Someone like her had no place in such a world.

Someone cherished by this many people — her life was not something to be treated that way.

"……So, you want me to be happy."

Mia sighed and stood.

Then she drew her sword and leveled its point at Kalua.

"Fight me, Kalua."

"……Mia?"

Mia lowered her sword, walked slowly to open distance between them, and smiled.

"Until I win. If I give up standing, you win. ……And I'll do as you say and stay here. Simple, right?"

Kalua stared at Mia, then sighed.

She drew the sword from her hip, rested it on her shoulder, and spoke.

"Fine. I'll keep you company until you're satisfied. ……If that's what it takes."

"I'll be satisfied — I'm going to win for sure."

"……Just so you know, I won't go easy on you like usual."

"I'm saying I'll still win."

Mia smiled with confidence.

She set her stance and charged.

A simple wooden-walled and earthen structure — more of a hut than a house.

But the furnishings said *house,* and inside were two young men and a woman.

"Well, all things considered, you did well for yourself. Be proud."

"Thank you — it's all thanks to your encouragement, Teks-san. ……Though whether I'll get a good answer is still up in the air."

This was the home of the eldest sister Mees and her husband Teks.

Bazel sighed, and Teks laughed, clapping his shoulder.

"That's up to Mia-chan, but I think it'll be fine."

"You think?"

"Yeah……you're not a bad man. I've watched you long enough to believe that, and Mia-chan knows it too."

"……Thank you."

*No need for thanks,* Teks laughed as Bazel bowed.

Mees, who had been watching them while tending to the children, cocked her head and hummed.

"You two are so mean. If you'd told me sooner that Bazel-kun was in love with Mia, I'd have helped properly……"

"If you got involved it'd just get more complicated……"

Teks sighed.

True, they had kept it from her, but Teks had been counseling Bazel for nearly two years.

From Mees's perspective, she should have been able to figure it out — and indeed——

*'Huh? Bazel-kun confessed to Mia? Um, wait……? He was in love with Mia?'*

——She hadn't noticed in the slightest. A frightening level of obliviousness.

Teks had spent ten years winning her hand.

The reason Bazel had sought Teks's counsel was precisely that: to learn from a veteran who had successfully wooed an equally devastatingly dense woman.

They had been strangers before, but by now Teks doted on him like a younger brother.

"Well, what matters most is that you got through to her. I'm relieved."

"Yes, truly. Thank you for everything. I should be going."

"Sure. I'll be waiting for the good news."

"Yes."

Bazel bowed deeply and left.

He had stayed for dinner, and the sun had long since set.

A crescent moon hung in the clear sky, as if blessing the day.

*The time is right,* Bazel thought, and headed for Mia's house.

"Oh, Nii-chan!"

"Hey, Miria. What are you doing standing outside? Did Milna-san scold you?"

"No, geez……I don't do anything to get scolded for."

Standing in front of the house was Mia's younger sister Miria.

She clung to Bazel and looked around.

"Mia-nee-chan and Kalua-san haven't come home even though dinner's over, so I was keeping an eye out."

"Mia hasn't?"

"Mm-hm……Nii-chan, did you confess to Mia-nee-chan?"

"Hm? Yeah……did something happen because of that?"

Miria nodded and gave him a summary of Mia and Milna's exchange.

Bazel frowned, sighed.

And patted Miria's head.

"I see……that girl never backs down once she's decided."

"Nii-chan, want me to cheer you up?"

"Don't be silly — I'm not giving up that easy. I've spent years working up to this……I'll go see her and say it again, properly."

Miria stared up at Bazel, and he tilted his head.

She reached only to his chest. She nodded sagely and smiled.

"Then, if Mia-nee-chan turns you down, I'll be your bride."

"Haha……don't jinx it. But, well, thanks."

"……I'm serious."

He patted the head of the pouting girl, gave a wry smile, and headed for the forest.

She was probably there.

He moved at a near-jog through the snow, trying not to slip.

——And then he heard the clash of steel.

Two women with swords drawn.

The black-haired beauty swirled her long hair, freely wielding a heavy curved blade, parrying strikes.

The chestnut-haired woman held a straight sword, kicking up snow as she dashed circles around her opponent, slashing out.

The trees became footholds — up was down and down was up.

Leaps beyond comprehension, impossibly high.

She kicked off branches without hesitation, diving headfirst into the snow.

Black cloaks on both — the crescent-skull emblem on each chest, the mark of the famed black century.

This was a completely different dimension from the sparring at the village militia grounds.

He knew — was *certain* — it was Mia, yet for an instant he couldn't recognize her.

And Kalua, the black-haired beauty, caught Mia's blade effortlessly and hurled her aside.

The snow erupted as Mia crashed down — from the force of it, this was no pulled punch.

"……That's fifty-four. Still going?"

But Mia, her entire body caked in snow, breathing hard, got up.

Her sweat rose like steam.

The snow clinging to her melted instantly, soaking her.

Her opponent, her best friend, was not even winded.

The great curved sword rested on her shoulder.

Her stance looked full of openings, yet radiated a strange pressure.

Even to an untrained eye, the gulf between them was enormous, even as Mia's inhuman movements amazed him.

"*Cough*……still going."

"You could do this a thousand times and not win."

"Maybe the thousand-and-first, you don't know."

"……So stubborn."

"——!?"

Kalua closed the distance.

A charge like some great beast, blowing every flake of snow from underfoot.

The snow was nothing to her.

From within a spray of snow-mist, she twisted and swung — the impact cracked the air.

Mia barely dodged — the tree behind her snapped — Kalua spun and lashed out with a kick.

Without a shred of hesitation or restraint.

Mia barely caught it on the flat of her blade and went flying, tumbling again.

"Fifty-five. Next is fifty-six. ……Not getting up?"

Kalua said, looking displeased, and approached Mia, who couldn't rise from the impact.

And there Bazel broke free of his paralysis and threw himself between them.

"W-wait! I don't know what's going on, but calm down."

"I'm calm. At least, I am."

Kalua sighed and shouldered her sword.

Coughing, Mia rose behind Bazel.

"Bazel, get out of the way."

"Like hell I will. What are you thinking, doing this……just stop. What if you get hurt?"

"This matters to me."

Kalua looked exasperated, leapt lightly, and landed in front of Mia.

She shoved her down and held the blade to her throat.

"Fifty-six. Think you can really win if we keep going? The experience gap between us — effort alone can't bridge it. You know that."

"I don't know that. All I know is that Kalua will keep going along with me."

"Gah——"

Mia tried to throw snow, and Kalua jumped back to dodge.

Mia hauled herself up, panting, brushed off the snow, and took her stance.

"Kalua, you could knock me out or whatever and win any time you wanted. The fact that you're not means I've already won."

"That's not fair, Mia……"

"Because I won't give up until I can't move."

Kalua breathed a deep sigh and nodded — *Fine.*

"Then I'll do what you say. That'll be the end of it, right?"

"You're going to force it?"

"……If you won't stop otherwise, then yes."

Bazel stepped between them again, raising his voice.

"Wait! Just hold on, both of you……Kalua-san, what is this about?"

"Hah……if I win, Mia stays in the village. That's the wager. This girl is as stubborn as they come."

"Ah……"

Bazel made a complicated face and clutched his head.

Then he looked at Mia.

"Mia, do you hate me that much?"

"……That's not it. That's not what this is about."

"Then what? What's wrong? ……Like I said, I'll absolutely make you happy. It's not just talk — I mean it with everything I have. I——"

"That's the thing."

Mia gripped her sword, looking at both Kalua and Bazel.

"I was surprised when you said all that, Bazel, but……of course I was happy. But that's not it."

She shook her head and smiled.

"Be happy, make her happy — Mom, Bazel, Kalua, everyone says that, but……I'm already happier than I have any right to be."

"……Mia."

"Joining the military was a coincidence. It was really hard at first, but I kept at it, and then I was recognized, and given responsibility……I was so happy. Clumsy, scatterbrained, useless me — for the first time, it felt like I'd earned something like that with my own hands."

Mia looked straight at Kalua.

"Meeting Krische-sama, the captain, everyone……meeting Kalua — I'm so glad. It's just a place I fell into by pure coincidence, but it's precious to me. I want to keep doing this, together with everyone there. ……That's why I'm being this stubborn."

She stepped forward, drawing closer to Kalua.

"I said it once before, remember? I said I'd fight for Krische-sama……and for Kalua. I don't know what you thought about it, but I was serious. I thought — I'd love to become someone like that."

Kalua met Mia's eyes, then looked away.

Mia came closer still.

Right up to Kalua.

"If you think that's a nuisance, do what you want. ……If I lose here, I'll give up."

That was what Mia said.

Standing before Kalua, she was wide open, defenseless — winning would be effortless, easier than taking candy from a baby.

And yet, harder than anything.

"……That's really not fair."

Kalua said nothing further, her shoulders slumping.

"I know Kalua is kinder than anyone. ……Understanding your opponent and striking where they're weak is the foundation of tactics — that's what I was taught."

Mia pressed the tip of her straight sword to the center of Kalua's chest — her heart.

And she smiled like a child, beaming.

"That's my win. See? Told you so."

"……Yes, yes, you win, Mia-sama."

Kalua gave up, wiped her curved sword on her cloak, and sheathed it.

Mia hugged her happily, laughing even as she sagged with exhaustion.

"Sorry. ……I really do love that about you, Kalua."

"I don't even know what to say anymore."

Kalua sighed and pinched Mia's cheek in exasperation.

Even so, Mia grinned and turned to face Bazel.

"……It's not that I dislike you, Bazel. I was genuinely happy, really……but there's something I want to do right now. So——"

"Don't apologize. It'll just make it worse."

Bazel dropped to the ground and, like Kalua, slumped his shoulders with a sigh.

"If you hadn't left the village — would I have had a chance?"

"……Maybe."

"……That's all I need to hear."

He closed his eyes, then stood.

Determined to leave no lingering regrets, he said it with a smile.

"I'll take it as a lesson. Next time, I'll go straight at it from the start."

"Hmm……no, honestly, it was so sudden I was really shocked, and I think normally you should go through more stages first——"

"……Seriously?"

Bazel scratched his head in exasperation, then looked at Kalua.

"……Please look after Mia. I know it's absurd — me, who couldn't even come close to her in a fight, worrying about her……but I wanted to say it."

"Mm, I will. ……Hehe, you're a good man, Bazel-kun."

She giggled, then swatted Mia's head.

"If it weren't Mia, Bazel-kun, I bet you'd find a great girl in no time."

"Haha……there's hardly anyone my age left in the village."

"Ahh……that's a shame."

Kalua smiled wryly, and Bazel laughed too.

Then he looked up at the sky, gave a single nod.

"Well then, see you. Departure's the morning after tomorrow, right?"

"Yeah."

"I'll come see you off."

Bazel said this, turned his back, and walked away.

Kalua gave Mia an exasperated look.

"……What a waste. A fine man, and your chance might never come again."

"Shut up. ……And you'd be lonely too if I left, admit it."

"Hmm……yeah. Well, I won't deny it. Lonely."

"I-I see……"

"……You say it yourself and then get embarrassed?"

"Sh-shut up!"

Mia's face reddened. Kalua tapped her head lightly and offered her hand.

"Let's head back."

"……Yeah."

Mia took it and smiled.

Kalua sighed — she'd lost count how many times today.

"You handle Milna-san. I'll pass."

"Why!? Help me convince her!"

"The way you were back there, you'll be fine. ……I'm tired, and I'm an outsider."

"Only pulling the outsider card when it suits you — that's not fair!"

"Look who's talking about fair……"

And so the two walked on.

A road shining under the crescent moon.

Through the grey forest, only the path ahead seemed to glow.
