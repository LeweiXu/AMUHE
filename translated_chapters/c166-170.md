# Chapter 166: Hospitality

The room chosen for the dinner party was not the dining hall but the reception room.

The sofas and other furnishings had been temporarily moved to another room, and the cold floorboards were covered wall to wall with carpeting. Today, they would be dining seated directly on the floor, in accordance with Kreisharana custom.

If the intent was to show welcome, one ought to begin by accommodating the other party's culture — those had been Bery's words. Kreschenta said anything was fine, so her suggestion was adopted in full. The dishes, too, leaned more toward hearty country fare than anything delicate.

Pumpkin soup was a given — that was non-negotiable. A stew of beef simmered until meltingly tender. A light, salt-based seafood soup made with fish and shellfish procured from Arna.

Three whole roast chickens, no less.

They had been brined overnight in herbs and salt, then basted carefully with butter as they roasted. The chickens gleamed with a caramel gloss like jewels — like amber, even. Their cavities were stuffed with vivid, colorful ingredients, and a gorgeous aroma wafted from within.

Glistening bone-in drumsticks were arranged in a ring around the platter like garnish. Cheese, bacon, and other fixings were heaped on flat plates for sandwiching with bread, and skewers of chicken, pork, lamb, and beef bristled like a pincushion.

— A warrior's feast, truly.

So thoroughly devoted to meat it was almost primitive. Vegetables were to be had in the soups, it seemed to say, and the salad served only as a token garnish. Meat was the undisputed star of this table.

"Wow…… amazing……"

Lila, guided in by Elvena, let out an involuntary exclamation and bashfully covered her mouth. Vinsreel and the warriors who followed wore the same surprise in their eyes.

A dinner at the queen's invitation — and the queen herself had taken up the knife, they were told. They had wondered just how dainty and refined the dishes would be — but that worry vanished in an instant, and the heat of the meat filling the room made their mouths water and their stomachs growl.

The gap in culinary skill was staggering, but the dishes themselves were close to what one might eat at a Kreisharana feast. Carpet had been laid across the entire floor, and the tables were low — no doubt another consideration for guests who sat directly on the ground rather than using chairs.

Even the queen herself was already seated on the floor.

They were well aware that the people of the kingdom used chairs, and they understood that sitting directly on the floor was considered "uncouth" in their culture. That the ruler of a great nation would accommodate them in this way — as a gesture of welcome, it was entirely unlike anything they had encountered from any of the nobles they had visited along the way. This was, in the truest sense, heartfelt hospitality.

The journey here had not been a bad one, thanks to the efforts of their guide, but it had not been entirely free of unpleasantness either.

Their appearance was likely part of the issue. Few looked kindly upon the people of Kreisharana walking about clad in furs, and some nobles, while professing welcome with their mouths, had directed looks of contempt at them as though dealing with something unclean.

To the kingdom, Kreisharana was simply a tribe of barbarians. Mountain-dwelling country bumpkins and savages, nothing more.

Of course, they had never expected to be genuinely welcomed, and had even been prepared to accept that. Kreisharana's loyalty was directed at the one who had forged a pact with the divine being — Krische — not the kingdom. They had come to end the state of non-contact only because she lived in this country; whether to forge friendship with the kingdom itself was not strictly necessary.

If this turned out to be a relationship lasting only Krische's lifetime, that was fine. Many held that view, and naturally, some among those present here shared it.

Even so — after what they had witnessed in the audience chamber, and now this hospitality, their impressions had begun to shift somewhat. At the very least, the consideration and care here proved that the words the queen had spoken in the audience chamber were no lie.

This nation's queen was different from the likes of them, it seemed.

Kreschenta sat nestled close to Krische on her left, looking nothing like the figure from the audience chamber — just an utterly innocent, lovely girl.

That softness of atmosphere was surely deliberate. She was silently communicating that this was simply an informal meal, nothing stiff or formal about it.

The person seated to her right also appeared to be a girl — radiant golden hair and striking beauty. She was conversing with a ghoulish-faced old man and another, long-haired old man, and upon noticing the guests, she gave a light bow.

Further to Krische's left sat a large-framed man — clearly a seasoned warrior. A face like a crag, his uniform straining against his bulk; his mere presence while seated exuded an oppressive weight. No ordinary fighter, that much was certain.

The man studied them with a gaze that was more glare than observation, and—

"Nyan-nyan, you're glaring. Why are you glaring at our guests?"

"I-I wouldn't say that…… a warrior's instincts, you might call it."

— Krische was scolding him.

The warriors were guided to their seats one by one, filling in from the back, with Lila and Vinsreel at the head. Once everyone had taken their places, Kreschenta clapped her hands together.

She stood and began with a brief toast. After confirming that drinks had been poured for each person, she kept the formalities short, then raised her cup high.

The customs of toasting differed in small ways, but the fundamentals were the same. They performed the gesture of exchanging cups, and the queen drained the drink — which, filled as it was with pressed fruit juice, could hardly be called wine — and if the host of the feast did so, there was no reason the others wouldn't follow.

The warriors drank heartily, while Krische alone sipped her drink little by little, watching the scene.

As expected, the beginning of the feast had the air of everyone feeling each other out. They shared impressions of the food while recounting tales of the journey to the capital.

Surrounded by warriors and seated at the far end, their guide Argoush Giterns looked thoroughly uncomfortable. Inwardly trembling at the conversation, he drank his tasteless wine.

He was their guide and Anne's father. Krische had suggested they might as well invite him, which led to his being asked to attend the queen's dinner. Outwardly he wore a noble's composed smile, but cold sweat ran down his back.

He was, after all, a marquis. His rank among the nobility was by no means low, but in practical terms he was merely a provincial lord who administered a rural territory — essentially the head of a local bureaucracy. He was fundamentally different from the capital's nobles who steered the nation and its politics, and if given the choice, he would have preferred to live quietly in his assigned territory.

The reason the Giterns line had peacefully carried on its bloodline for so long was precisely because they had kept their distance from the royal court and remained uninvolved in politics.

For such a man, the mere fact of being invited to the queen's dinner was enough to make his stomach ache.

"The climate and way of life are different, and it would be a lie to say there were no inconveniences…… However, thanks to Giterns-dono, we wanted for nothing. He arranged blankets for us when we underestimated the cold, provided thick tents…… he looked after everything, from meals to daily necessities. Without him, the journey would never have been so pleasant."

Lila and Vinsreel heaped praise upon Argoush.

That they were grateful was surely genuine. They were repeatedly conveying his merits to his liege, Queen Kreschenta, as if to say — please, reward him.

Kreschenta tilted her head, her golden hair gleaming red in the light, and turned her violet eyes toward Argoush.

"Is that so. It seems he performed admirably despite not being an official guide — I'm glad to hear it, Marquis Giterns. I shall have to give you a reward later."

"Y-yes, Your Majesty! I merely did what was natural as a subject of Your Majesty the Queen. I merely fulfilled my duties — any special consideration would be far more than I deserve."

"The people of Kreisharana are to become friends of the kingdom, alongside Arna. If I were to give nothing to one so praised by such people, my character would be called into question. Please accept graciously."

What a beautiful smile it was. Something that could only be called a monarch's force of will emanated from that small frame.

Or perhaps that was what one called regal bearing — that formless something possessed only by those born to rule over others. Her appearance was beautiful but still that of a child — yet no one who heard she was the queen who governed the kingdom would doubt it. She wore that strange air about her.

"The northwest may lack glamour, but I am aware that you have been diligently watching over your territory. Tax revenues have increased slightly year by year since your generation took over — things have been going quite well since you completed the road improvements, haven't they? ……I understand you're now looking into developing livestock, but how is that progressing?"

Argoush froze.

The queen spoke as though reading from a document. The northwest was considered rural even by the kingdom's standards, with no notable specialty products. He had indeed been exploring livestock as a new industry, but they had only just finished surveying suitable land with an expert — in practical terms, nothing had actually begun.

He submitted reports to the nation each season, but they were mostly columns of figures. They were certainly not the sort of thing a queen would pore over, and unless one analyzed those numbers with that specific intent, there was no way to guess what Argoush was planning.

It was also hard to imagine she had gone to the trouble of investigating just because she knew Argoush would be at the dinner. His attendance had only just been decided.

Argoush glanced at the person beside the queen — a silver-haired girl gnawing on a bone-in drumstick while watching him.

The one who had forged a pact with the divine being. The hero of the civil war. What they shared in common was ability far beyond their years, and jewel-like violet eyes.

*— Could it be that this queen has a complete grasp of the fiscal situation and every minor shift across the entire kingdom?*

His suspicion was now close to certainty.

"S-so far, things are progressing smoothly…… We've identified several suitable candidate sites and are working to attract livestock farmers. The plan is to start from there and expand gradually."

As he answered, he frantically searched his memory for anything problematic in the reports sent to the kingdom. He was fairly sure there was nothing, yet with this particular person, his stomach simply ached.

"I see. I hope it goes well."

She was Queen Kreschenta, the ruler of the kingdom — a being from another world entirely. When he considered that his head could roll on a whim, there was nothing but tension.

"Going forward, the northwest will also serve as a point of contact with Kreisharana, so shall I send some nobles to assist you? Given what you're trying to accomplish, it seems like you're short on personnel…… Please don't overwork yourself."

"Y-yes! I am deeply grateful for your consideration, Your Majesty……"

"It would be troublesome if a talented person like you collapsed from overwork. That would be a loss for the kingdom."

Argoush bowed deeply from his seated position in thanks, and Anne, who had been behind Kreschenta, begged her permission before trotting over, moved by the sight of her father being so highly praised.

She positioned herself beside her father with a wine pitcher at the ready.

"Here you are, Marquis Giterns."

"……You, honestly."

Sighing at his daughter beaming with delight, he accepted the poured wine. He had a mountain of things he wanted to say to her, but it seemed she was doing well here. Reassured by her healthy appearance, Argoush smiled.

Though the dinner party was heavily laden with diplomatic intent, neither side broached the real subject for some time. The exceptional quality of the food was likely part of the reason. Once a little wine had been drunk and everyone's tongues had loosened — under the gazes of Gallen and Elrouga, it was Kokys, who had been talking with Vinsreel, who raised the topic.

"However, even for a nation of warriors, you've brought quite a number of seasoned fighters. They appear to be the very finest of Kreisharana's elite…… Vinsreel-dono, what is this about?"

Far too many for mere envoys. Surely they hadn't worried about being attacked by bandits while riding griffins through the sky.

The air shifted at Kokys's words. The warriors lowered their voices, and Vinsreel smiled.

"Being addressed so formally by a warrior as plainly formidable as yourself, Agrand-dono — I confess it feels rather awkward. I'd be fine with your usual manner of speaking."

Kokys glanced at Kreschenta.

"Today is simply a dinner party. If Vinsreel-sama says so, I don't mind either. Matters between warriors are for warriors — I leave it to you."

"You have my thanks."

He bowed deeply from his seated position, then turned his gaze back to Vinsreel.

"Truth be told, this stiff manner of speaking has never been my strong suit. I appreciate it. But if that's how it is, then the same should go both ways. It would be strange for me alone to speak casually to a guest, Vinsreel-dono."

"I should be the one thanking you. ……Unlike Lila, I'm not terribly confident with the lowland language. When it comes to serious discussions like this, I'm always afraid of getting the nuance wrong. That's a relief to hear."

"Glad to hear it…… but truly, I'm impressed by how fluent you are. I can't manage so much as a greeting in another nation's tongue, so I say that with genuine respect."

Kokys spoke with a sigh-like exhale, poured wine into Vinsreel's cup, then into his own. They each took their cups and clashed them together, and Kokys prompted him with a look.

"We've heard from Krische-sama that the kingdom faces an unavoidable invasion from other nations…… What's the actual situation?"

"It's as you've heard. We're being targeted by three major powers of comparable strength — from the east, west, and south."

Kokys spoke and wet his tongue with wine.

"It's likely the three nations will advance in coordination. Even conservatively, the situation is precarious. I don't doubt our victory, though……"

His eyes drifted to Krische as he spoke.

Krische's cheeks were flushed from wine, and she showed no interest in any such talk. She was tugging Bery's hand, trying to get her to sit between herself and Kreschenta.

The beautiful red-haired servant wore a troubled expression as she read the room, whispering admonishments like "that's enough" and "you can't" into her ear, but ultimately she yielded to her beloved mistress and settled into the spot. Krische wound her arm around Bery's, looking thoroughly pleased.

As a figure whose presence was supposed to banish all anxiety, she was rather unconvincing.

Kokys rubbed the bridge of his nose.

"Heh, pay it no mind. I understand Krische-sama's abilities."

"Haah…… we're grateful for that. Still…… it's true that some among the military are getting cold feet. To any normal assessment, the situation is dire enough that one might think there's no hope of winning."

He drained his cup and let out a breath. Seeing this, Vinsreel poured wine into his cup. Kokys thanked him and continued.

"Diplomatically speaking, we expect hostilities to begin in about half a year, but there's a risk they'll start sooner. From the spring thaw onward, we must be prepared for the enemy to move at any time — we're building up our forces with that in mind."

There was, in fact, a high likelihood of exactly that, but he did not state the reason here.

The dragon had visited the capital — that rumor would have spread, and surely it had already reached the three nations. It could be used as a pretext for war in any number of ways.

"I see, so it was the right call to come early after all……"

Vinsreel rested his hand on his chin, narrowing his keen eyes.

"I have a general understanding of the kingdom's situation. However, we cannot simply offer our full cooperation right away. The rift between the kingdom and Kreisharana runs deep — many on our side do not wish for friendship with the kingdom."

He drained his cup and studied the empty vessel.

Though his words were addressed to Kokys, they were really meant for Kreschenta and the others to hear. Kokys was merely playing the role of conversation starter. Both understood this.

"At the very least, the majority opinion among our people is that Kreisharana itself should avoid being drawn into hostility. That we should not be the ones standing in the line of fire."

"Naturally. There's nothing in it for Kreisharana, after all."

Kreschenta replied while having Bery pour her some fruit juice. Those words alone told her most of what she needed to know.

"But you can't simply stand by and do nothing while Onee-sama is in dire straits. ……Shall we call it military exchange and observation?"

Vinsreel's eyes widened, and then he gave a wry smile. Krische tilted her head and looked between her sister and Vinsreel. Kreschenta smiled.

"What you want is a justification for entering the war. You want your opponents to be 'insolent fools who invaded the kingdom without a thought for Kreisharana while it was in the middle of diplomatic exchange' — is that it?"

"Precisely. What we need is a situation that our people cannot help but accept, Your Majesty."

Shedding Kreisharana blood for Alberan's sake — naturally, many would object. By any normal reckoning, standing aside would be the prudent choice. Even for Krische's sake, it would be absurd to risk Kreisharana's very existence by meddling in this war.

But not everything should be weighed by gain and loss alone.

"While there are those who object to lending the kingdom our aid, if Kreisharana itself is shown disrespect, that changes matters. Our coming here is, in a sense, about creating that reason."

"How generous…… but are you certain? It is not my wish to drag Kreisharana into this after you've lived in peace for so long."

She had heard that Kreisharana's population was on the scale of a single town. She certainly needed all the help she could get, but it would be somewhat problematic if Kreisharana ended up destroyed.

The mana crystal mining was one factor, but in the future, she wanted to undertake large-scale griffin breeding within the kingdom. Losing that expertise would be a significant setback. If many of their people were killed in the fighting, Kreisharana would have no choice but to depend on the kingdom, and that would clear away many friction-related obstacles regarding mining — but that wasn't exactly urgent.

That they were willing to enter the war was, given the circumstances, not unwelcome. But in truth, she wouldn't have minded if they simply stood aside, either.

"Peace and tranquility are to be treasured, but before all else, we are warriors. We worship martial prowess and devote our lives to the refinement of our bodies. ……And the sight of Krische-sama, alone before the divine being, demonstrating such martial valor — that image is seared into my eyes still."

Vinsreel spoke and looked toward Krische.

"Facing our interference atop that, she fought the divine being to a standstill and became its covenant-partner. Truly worthy of respect — the very image of the warrior we should aspire to be. To witness such a thing and then merely cower in fear of losing our peace, choosing to stand aside — that would violate our very way of being. To let this pass would mean losing our pride as warriors."

Krische looked at Vinsreel uncertainly and glanced over at Selene. Selene returned a look of resignation, as if to say — just give up.

"Humble though our strength may be, we stake it on this pride. Our numbers are small — I won't claim each is worth a thousand — but each will do the work of ten, or perhaps a hundred. If it would not be a burden, we ask that you permit us to ride to war alongside the kingdom. And let this stand as a testament to lasting friendship — Kreisharana's Chieftain Alkirens also wishes it so."

Vinsreel bowed his head where he sat, and the warriors watched in silence.

Kreschenta regarded him with inward exasperation.

No political maneuvering whatsoever. Straightforward and simple — when a warrior whose head was presumably stuffed with muscle was given the helm, this was what happened to a nation, she supposed.

"Please raise your head, Vinsreel-sama. If anything, we should be the ones asking. To put it mildly, this is a crisis that threatens the kingdom's very survival — for Alberan to receive your aid, I have no words of refusal. ……I swear upon my name that this debt will not be forgotten."

Then again, if they were easy for her alone to manage, that could only be a welcome thing. At the very least they could serve as a deterrent against the west — Elderant — and the ignorant masses who had no idea Kreisharana's national strength amounted to a single town would surely celebrate their support with great enthusiasm.

Though a single nation lending its support was, in practical terms, far less than the name suggested — the mere notion produced an effect beyond its reality. They couldn't be expected to contribute much as a fighting force, but even just diverting the enemy's attention was significant enough, and in terms of easing the populace's general anxiety, there was real value.

Kreschenta had no doubt in her sister's victory, but if even a little could be done to lighten her burden and reduce the post-war strain, it was, risks considered, not a bad proposition.

"Thank you. ……That said, Kreisharana as it stands now can hardly be called a nation. You cannot move in force, and your cooperation will essentially be limited to the kingdom's west — against Elderant alone."

"That is more than enough. Simply drawing Elderant's attention would be a great help."

The mountain range stretching from Albyagel extended far to the west, bordering the northern reaches of Elderant. If Kreisharana entered the war, Elderant would have to consider the possibility of its rear being threatened. Concentrating solely on their main offensive under those conditions would be difficult.

A modest contribution — but given their national strength, it was more than enough.

Vinsreel nodded.

"Our purpose is, as I said, to create a pretext."

He turned his gaze back to Kokys.

"— But, as you suspected, Agrand-dono. The warriors here are the very best of Kreisharana. Their objective is to serve as Krische-sama's personal guard until the war's end — to follow at her side and fight as her blade."

Every Kreisharana warrior present had volunteered. Some revered Krische as a warrior who had stood against the divine being itself; others were drawn by curiosity about lowland warfare. Their reasons varied, but every one of them was, naturally, among Kreisharana's absolute finest.

Their numbers were few, but for that very reason, Vinsreel had confidence.

"Thirty riders may seem trivial against a battlefield of tens of thousands, but every one of us here is a veteran griffin rider. We have no intention of yielding to a thousand. ……I am confident we will prove our worth."

Vinsreel declared it without reservation, and Kokys laughed with pleasure.

"Bold words indeed. But that they don't sound like empty bluster is what makes them so heartening."

"To be praised so by a warrior of your caliber is an honor. If you're willing, I'd like to request a sparring match at a later date, as part of our exchange."

"I'd be more than willing. I was about to suggest it myself."

Kokys spoke while pouring wine into Vinsreel's empty cup. They touched cups together — and Krische, who had been watching while clinging to Bery's arm, spoke up.

"Hmm…… is it really all right? Krische thinks peace is really the best thing, and you don't have to force yourselves to help…… Krische feels a bit bad about dragging everyone into this."

"It is no longer solely for Krische-sama's sake. You could call it a bit of shock therapy, but fighting together is not a bad way to bridge the gap with the kingdom. ……That is what I came to believe over the course of this journey. The others feel the same."

Vinsreel glanced at the visibly tense Argoush and smiled.

The relationship between the two nations was not a good one. But when you looked at individuals, one by one, that wasn't the case.

Small acts of consideration, hospitality like this — the feelings conveyed through each one. If they continued to look at things this way, such ill will would surely fade in time.

"Outward beauty alone does not reach even the divine being. ……Strip away the garment of nationhood, look without preconceptions, and what you find is fellow human beings who can share drinks and laughter like this. As Her Majesty the Queen said, Krische-sama is the catalyst. ……We have simply decided to take up our spears for the future of the kingdom and Kreisharana. There is no need for Krische-sama to feel troubled."

Krische turned to Lila. Lila smiled and nodded, echoing Vinsreel's words.

"It's exactly as my brother says. ……The heart shows in even a single act of hospitality — and we were moved by it, from the bottom of our hearts."

Every dish served was easy to eat for Kreisharana guests unaccustomed to knives and forks; chairs had been deliberately removed and carpeting laid so they could sit directly on the floor.

The truth lay in consideration given as a matter of course, and that held great value.

"This is not something we were dragged into — it is born of our desire for a future alongside the kingdom. We were given the authority to make this decision when we came here. ……Whatever the outcome, the fact that it took this form is simply the result, and there is no reason for Krische-sama to be troubled by it."

"……Is that how it works?"

"Yes, that is how it works."

Krische nodded ambiguously, looking half-convinced and half not, and turned to Bery beside her. Bery, too, nodded without a word and smiled at her warmly.

# Chapter 167: Cross-Cultural Exchange

On snow dusted thinly across the ground, two men fought at the center while hundreds of soldiers looked on.

Both were over six shaku tall and powerfully built. The man in uniform — the larger of the two — wielded his spear with a finesse unexpected from his frame. With the slightest movement he deflected and evaded his opponent's spear, casually delivering strikes that would have been fatal against any ordinary fighter.

At times he deliberately created openings, inviting attacks only to counter. The eight-shaku sparring spear, made of heavy argana wood — its swings carried the sound of a swinging cudgel, and its thrusts punched through the air like arrows. A rough swing could turn even the spear's shaft into a weapon.

Yet behind all of it lay solid theoretical construction, and every movement existed within a framework of calculated technique.

His opponent, however — a tall, strikingly handsome man — was no less skilled. He wielded an eight-shaku spear with equal fluidity, his movements flowing and dreamlike. Like water tracing the crevices between rocks, like wind threading through gaps in trees.

His spear was stripped of all waste, slipping past guards and through openings in stance. He continued evading thrusts that should have been lethal in the blink of an eye, and still had room to return counterattacks.

On this windless day, their space alone was a storm. Powder snow swirled into the air, tracing the trajectories of both spears in silver.

Kokys Agrand, the kingdom's finest warrior.

Vinsreel Sharana, Kreisharana's finest warrior.

Both displayed their superhuman skill without reservation for over a quarter-hour. It was the larger man — Kokys — who withdrew the killing intent from his spear tip first.

"Well now, I'll admit it — somewhere in my mind I was looking down on you, thinking that in the end, yours was a spear that has never seen true battle. But at this rate, a sparring match would never reach a conclusion. We'd end up swinging until we both collapsed."

"So it seems. ……It was only sparring, but a contest is a contest. I came into it meaning to win…… but breaking through your defense is going to require more training on my part."

Vinsreel likewise lowered his stance, shouldered his spear, and spoke with a note of disbelief.

Against anyone other than an anomaly like Krische, he should have been able to win with room to spare. But that confidence had also been a form of arrogance.

Kokys Agrand's spear carried a seasoned mastery reminiscent of his father, Chieftain Alkirens, while simultaneously possessing a raw ferocity. Vinsreel had given his all within the bounds of a sparring match, yet he had no confidence that he had pushed Kokys into a corner.

"You said you'd been looking down on me, but those are my words, Kokys-dono. ……I'd assumed that among the pampered lowlanders, Krische-sama was the sole exception. To think the kingdom had a warrior like you."

Vinsreel called him by name. In Kreisharana, addressing a warrior worthy of respect by their given name rather than family name was proper etiquette.

"If you had been born in Kreisharana, it would not be strange for you to hold the title of warrior-leader. It makes one appreciate the breadth of the world."

"Being praised so directly, even I get a bit embarrassed."

Kokys scratched his head and chuckled ruefully.

"Heh, though I have to say, standing here complimenting each other like this feels rather strange. Let's call it here."

"Agreed. What needed to be said has already been said — without words."

Vinsreel's handsome features broke into a bold smile as he approached. Kokys strode toward him in return and extended his right hand.

"Kokys-dono, your prowess is more than proven. As a comrade to ride to war beside under Krische-sama, I could not ask for better."

"The feeling is mutual. To meet a warrior of your caliber is my greatest pleasure, Vinsreel-dono. I look forward to a long friendship, honing our spearwork together."

Their hands clasped, and cheers erupted from the crowd.

Among the court nobles of the capital, the Kreisharana warriors were poorly received — but in the military, which honored martial prowess and honor, that was not the case. Since ancient times, a warrior's strength was an object of respect that transcended rank and station. When that warrior could be called worth a thousand men, few would be foolish enough to deride them as barbarians.

A warrior who had fought on equal terms with Kokys — the kingdom's finest outside of Krische. Vinsreel was, for many of the soldiers at least, a man deserving of respect.

Kokys placed a hand on his shoulder and bellowed for all to see.

"The strength of Kreisharana's greatest warrior, Vinsreel Sharana, is as you have witnessed — equal to that of myself, Kokys Agrand! To insult this man and his subordinates is to insult me, who crossed spears with him in battle! Know this!"

Then, as was his habit, he turned to the prime spectator's seat where Krische had been sipping tea — rather, to Selene, seated between Krische and Lila.

Seeing Selene nod, Kokys invited Vinsreel over toward those seats while addressing the soldiers.

"From here on, this is a free exchange. Any man confident in his skills, step forward. From what I can see, every Kreisharana warrior beyond Vinsreel-dono is skilled as well. Underestimate them and you'll be shamed — compete with resolve."

At his words, a kingdom soldier with confidence in his abilities stepped forward — a man from Kokys's Second Corps. And from the Kreisharana side, one warrior also stepped out. Watching this scene unfold, Kokys settled into a prepared chair, took the wine poured for him, and clinked cups with Vinsreel.

"Thanks to you, we got to see a fine bout, Vinsreel-dono."

"Thank you, Marshal Christand."

"Aside from one exception, Kokys is the finest warrior in this vast kingdom. And yet in those mountains, a nation of warriors though you may be, someone who can match him——"

Selene spoke with a note of disbelief and spread her hands.

In terms of population, Kreisharana was likely less than a thousandth. Even granting that it was a nation of warriors, the fact that their warrior-leader stood near equal to Kokys — the pinnacle of the kingdom — was astonishing.

It wasn't that Kokys had held back to let him shine, either. The fact was that Vinsreel stood in a domain extremely close to Kokys's own.

Vinsreel laughed and shook his head.

"It was only sparring. In a true duel to the death, it would be a different story."

His gaze went to Kokys's rear — to the massive solid-steel war lance held by the aide standing behind him.

Kokys wore a light uniform, and his spear had been a standard-issue sparring spear, the same used by any soldier. This being a sparring match, he hadn't been in his full battle state.

The small parcel of land they had managed had been voluntarily returned to the kingdom on grounds of inability to administer it, and the debts had been carried as House Argan's own.

Since no damage had been inflicted upon the kingdom, there was no reason for punishment. Her father had likely planned to rebuild from there. He had collapsed partway through, but with the debt cleared, reclaiming their standing would be relatively straightforward.

If, for example, Bogan and her sister Lazura had had another child who inherited the Argan name and achieved some distinction, restoring the house would have been easy. Or Bery could do likewise — marry, or take in a husband. If her sole concern were carrying on her father's will, that was the first thing a noble ought to choose.

But her sister Lazura had likely felt no such obligation either. Of course, if a child had wished to restore House Argan, she would have been delighted and left it to them — but she hadn't wished for a second child for that purpose.

It was a matter of temperament. In a word, both Bery and Lazura were unserious as nobles.

"Well, that's only in the abstract, I suppose. I'm hardly in a position to weigh in on that. What about you, Anne-sama?"

"……I've never given it much thought. I've never met a gentleman who struck me as — well——"

Anne said this and glanced briefly at Krische, her cheeks coloring. When Bery looked at her curiously, Anne shook her head in a fluster.

"No…… it's just, well, I-I do sometimes feel, with someone like Gallen-sama, a certain…… mature charm."

"Ah…… tehe, yes, Gallen-sama is a wonderful person."

Bery chuckled. She knew that Anne was quite attached to Gallen. There was certainly something appealing about him. He had a stern air, but was far gentler than his appearance suggested.

But he was an old man. Anne surely didn't mean it in that sense.

"……I do enjoy love stories myself, but when I try to picture myself in one, it just doesn't feel real. Marrying a gentleman, having children…… as you say, it's the natural thing. And if that's where a woman's happiness lies, then I suppose it must be so."

Anne said that much, then sighed.

"But whether there's a happiness greater than what I have now — I'm not so sure. I'm more than content serving Her Majesty, Selene-sama, and Krische-sama, and learning at Argan-sama's side."

Bery had nothing to say to that. She listened quietly and waited for Anne's words.

"……Many people end up in arranged matches, forced into marriage — knowing that, I think I'm aware of how fortunate I am. ……Since my brothers have both married and have children, there's no pressure on behalf of the family. Father said as much — that I needn't worry about the house."

Anne tilted her head with a troubled look.

"Father says I should still think carefully about how I want to live from now on."

Anne fell into thought, and Bery thought along with her. But there were some things she did know.

"He sounds like a wonderful father. He's clearly thinking of you above all else, Anne-sama."

"……Yes. But I think what he really wants is for me to marry and show him grandchildren. As a good daughter, I suppose that's the right thing to do, but……"

Anne looked up and met Bery's gaze squarely.

"But if I were to marry somewhere, it would become difficult to continue working by the side of Argan-sama — the person I consider my lifelong aspiration."

"I-is that so……"

The words made Bery squirm, but she had a feeling that interrupting would only send them further from the point, so she gave up. The sentiment was appreciated, but Anne was sometimes so direct it was hard to know how to respond.

"And considering Her Majesty's position — with the recent incident, the number of people she can trust is limited — it would be difficult to find a replacement on short notice. ……Given the current circumstances, there's really nothing to think about."

"……I see."

In her own way, she was clearly thinking about the situation facing Christand and Kreschenta. Anne had her shortcomings, certainly, but she was a thoughtful woman. It was only that her thoughtfulness occasionally went too deep and came out the wrong way. Bery herself regarded her as a hard-working, good-natured, and compassionate colleague.

She wasn't so fine a person that she could presume to tell such a woman what to do. But if she were to say something, what would it be?

Bery thought for a moment, then spoke.

"……I think of it as something like a river."

"A river?"

"Yes. We are leaves floating upon it, perhaps. Drifting with the current, carried along, never knowing where we'll end up…… struggling, worrying, but eventually flowing out to sea."

*Fate, destiny* — her sister had used those words. Even if you agonized, you could not return to scenery already passed; everything was irreversible, and the current carried you on.

"In life, the things we can choose are trivial. Whether to give ourselves to a gentle stream, whether to plunge into the rapids, or whether to drift along some forgotten tributary."

In routine. In honor. Lives varied, but what people sought was the same.

"Or…… whether to draw close to a petal floating alongside you, perhaps."

Bery smiled wryly and tickled the nose of the peacefully sleeping Krische. Krische scrunched up her face as if it itched, and Bery's shoulders shook with silent laughter.

"In the end, what matters is whether the scenery you see is beautiful — that's what I believe. Just because everyone flows doesn't mean the current is right…… perhaps I only think that because I'm quite the contrarian."

She laughed softly and continued.

"What Anne-sama should value most is your own happiness before anyone else's. I'm grateful and touched that you think of me and Her Majesty. But it would be a waste to be swept along by duty and station."

"……A waste."

"Yes. Given the freedom you have, it's far better to choose where you flow than to drift along on inertia. Even if you come to regret it later, if it was a path you chose for yourself, at least you'll have that minimum of acceptance."

Nothing about choosing something would dramatically change the world. What changed, if anything, was only how it appeared.

And that was what choosing was for.

"Isn't that what Marquis Giterns meant by telling you to think?"

Bery said, and looked at her.

Black hair tied back, unadorned. But that, too, was surely her charm.

"Whatever you choose, I believe someone like you will find happiness, Anne-sama. You have a way of making the people around you smile naturally — there's that sort of warmth about you."

*Something you were surely born with,* Bery added, lowering her eyes with a smile. It was something Bery herself did not possess.

"The constraints you think you have are often trivial once you change your perspective — they can be dealt with easily enough. Please don't worry. Whatever choice Anne-sama makes, if you believe it's best for yourself, I will support it. As will Ojou-sama and Krische-sama, I'm sure."

She raised her eyes, and—

"……So please, choose whatever you truly wan— …A-Anne-sama……?"

Anne, who had been listening with a grave expression, suddenly began shedding tears.

Bery stared at her in bewilderment. Anne covered her face and shook her head.

"I-I'm sorry. I just…… I was so moved…… for Argan-sama to say such things to me too, I, I just……"

"I-I didn't think I said anything that remarkable……"

"Between Father and now this, when I stop and think about how fortunate I am——"

It was at that moment.

Without knocking, the door opened gracefully, and in swept golden hair gleaming red.

"Your queen has returned, Argan-sama. If you would be so kind as to prepare tea……"

Elvena in tow, Kreschenta entered the room, stopped mid-sentence upon seeing Anne with tears streaming down her bowed face, frowned, and looked at Bery.

"What happened? Has Argan-sama finally reached her limit with Anne-sama and delivered an ultimatum?"

"Th-that's not it. Kreschenta-sama, you mustn't say such things."

"Giving me orders — who do you think you are? But I'm in a good mood today, so I'll let it slide. Elvena-sama, would you prepare the tea?"

"Y-yes, Your Majesty."

Elvena cast a worried glance at the crying Anne, then hurried to pick up the teapot on the table and went off to brew fresh tea.

"Um…… did something good happen?"

"It's Elvena-sama — she's so wonderfully attentive. She doesn't prattle on pointlessly like Argan-sama, either. She reminds me of the comfort I had with Nora. I think Elvena-sama should be my personal attendant from now on."

"K-Kreschenta-sama……"

The timing could not have been worse. Predictably, Anne flinched, and Bery scrambled to smooth things over—

"You can't, Kreschenta. Anne is assigned to Kreschenta's side — that's decided."

The room had grown noisy enough to wake the sleeping Krische. Yawning, she addressed Kreschenta from within Bery's arms, still half-asleep.

"K-Krische-sama……!"

Anne looked up, tears still in her eyes, now brimming with emotion.

"Uuu…… I don't want that, Onee-sama. I want to swap Anne-sama and Elvena-sama."

She froze again at Kreschenta's words.

"Kreschenta, in this world there is such a thing as fate. ……Anne being at Kreschenta's side — that, too, is fate. Kreschenta should be more grateful for this encounter with a servant called Anne. Yaaawn…… Right, Bery?"

"Y-…… yes……"

A rather self-serving use of the concept of fate. Krische wriggled into a new position on Bery's lap and gave her a drowsy good-morning kiss — she was clearly supremely uninterested. Bery could only wear a troubled, ambiguous smile.

"Krische-sama…… thank you……!"

"……?"

Regardless, seeing Anne moved to tears by those words, Bery found herself at a loss for words as well.

Krische, puzzled by the thanks, told Kreschenta while clinging to Bery—

"……Do you understand? Come on, no sulking — come here, come here."

"Hmm……"

She beckoned the displeased Kreschenta over, embraced her, and stroked her head.

"Anne, you're Kreschenta's personal attendant. Krische gave you the day off today, so starting tomorrow, work even harder."

"Yes!"

Anne wiped her eyes at Krische's words, her face alight with renewed determination.

Bery watched her with an indescribable expression, and sighed in resignation.
