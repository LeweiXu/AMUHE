# Chapter 71: Sunlit Night

The surrendered prisoners exceeded twenty thousand.

It would take at least two days to process them all.

"It's been a while, General Velraigh."

"Yes, it has been a while, Krische-sama. You seem well."

Surrounded by a thousand elites, issuing orders from the center of the main camp, was a man in a winged helm.

Nozan Velraigh.

The red-haired young man — handsome enough to be called beautiful — dismounted when Krische appeared, removed his helm with a cheerful smile, and extended his right hand.

In terms of military rank, Nozan stood above Krische, and as a noble who had been granted the title of margrave, such courtesy was not strictly necessary. However, Krische was the adopted daughter of the late Bogan. Deliberately positioning himself as Bogan's subordinate, Nozan chose to treat Krische as his superior.

Krische showed no sign of minding this either way, accepted the gesture readily, exchanged a handshake, and asked:

"What do you plan to do with the prisoners?"

Her very first words were not a greeting but a practical matter.

Nozan let out a wry smile at the girl's unadorned directness, and answered.

"I'd like to absorb as many as we can. These are soldiers the royal prince gathered by throwing around vast sums of gold. If we guarantee their pay, half of them will come over to our side. It'll take time and cause considerable confusion, but in this situation the increase in manpower is well worth those costs."

"Your estimate?"

"……Let's see. Processing will take about two days……Even rushing, another three days on top of that to finish reorganization. Even a rough job would need two days."

"Too many prisoners is itself a problem in this situation."

Krische pursed her lips.

"Haha, well, but looking ahead it's not a bad thing. We have Krische-sama to thank for that. Surprise attack or not, to finish a battle this quickly on this field is something."

"Krische told Varkus Corps Commander the same thing, but……the conditions had been set up perfectly."

After all that groundwork, it would have been harder to fail.

Krische felt a little ticklish being praised like this.

Even so, Nozan and the others meant what they said.

Even with Krische's forces added, their numbers were only even. Not an advantage.

Flanking from the rear and executing a pincer was one thing, but this degree of overwhelming victory was rarely seen.

Focusing the breakthrough on the enemy command and effortlessly cutting down the enemy general's head — that was something only she could do, with her absolute confidence in her own swordsmanship.

She did not consider the possibility that she might fail to break through. She did not consider the possibility that she might be delayed.

At a fundamental level, this overwhelming victory was possible only because of her strength and her confidence.

Even if it had been Nozan and his men who struck from the rear, victory would have been assured in that situation — but it would have taken somewhat longer.

They prepared contingencies for failure. Krische never even considered that she might fail.

That clear difference was the deciding factor.

Despite being a head-on, large-scale clash, casualties on both sides were kept remarkably light as a result.

Nozan, who wanted to preserve his fighting strength, expressed his gratitude straightforwardly.

"But having Krische-sama here is more reassuring than anything. My inclination would be to march on the capital immediately, but……"

"……If we leave now, even in the worst case it would take about a month, if we chose that option."

March directly on the capital.

Not a bad idea. The concern was being completely cut off from the north.

The logistics problem was significant.

If they forcibly requisitioned from each city they could manage, but this was within the kingdom.

It could create all manner of problems afterwards.

That said, even accounting for that it wasn't bad — good enough to agonize over.

If they absorbed the prisoners, their forces would approach forty thousand — if things went well, they would exceed it.

A realistic number for a decisive pitched battle.

If the enemy chose to hole up in the capital, all the better.

In that case, the soldiers only needed to surround it.

In a capital full of cover, there was only one target worth pursuing.

— In that case, Krische thought, she need only sneak in alone and that would suffice.

"But truly, considering the current situation, it's not a bad option."

Seeing Krische narrow her eyes, Nozan shook his head irritably.

"Indeed, even accounting for various issues it's not a bad option. But……one concern. General Hilkintos."

"……The west?"

Nozan nodded.

Aulgorn Hilkintos.

That was the name of the general who guarded the kingdom's west.

"My spy brought word. Last month — upon hearing of the defeat at the Dragon's Maw, General Hilkintos apparently bestirred himself to go wagging his tail at the royal prince. A fair-weather dog."

Making no effort to conceal his hostility, Nozan spoke with evident displeasure.

"It seems his nature favors gold and privileges over a nobleman's pride. I had suspected for some time that he was the sort to back the winning horse……Our rejection of the ceasefire will serve as a convenient justification. He will likely move."

"……That is"

"I sent a messenger to Selene-sama about it just the other day, but it seems he crossed paths with Krische-sama who was heading here."

If they marched directly on the capital now, their rear would be threatened.

Even so, if they took down Gildanstein it would end — but still, that was decidedly inconvenient.

"We can't ignore it. We need to deal with that first."

"Indeed. It's quite precarious."

Krische thought for a moment, then spoke.

"……If they're going to move, it will probably be in this direction. The distance is too great to circle around to the north alone. It might be better to split up and handle the Dragon's Maw and General Hilkintos separately."

"……Hmm."

"In that case, the question is which of us moves."

Elrouga spoke with a furrowed brow.

"Nn……that's right. That is a bit of a dilemma."

Krische traced her rosy lips with her fingertips, speaking as though troubled, and Elrouga gave a wry smile.

Then, watching Krische and Nozan deliberate, he gestured at their surroundings, where the excitement of battle had not yet subsided.

"It's a difficult question, but it wouldn't hurt to think it over while processing the prisoners. You two are being a bit hasty. It's true we don't have much time, but either way we can't move for several days."

"……Indeed, I was getting ahead of myself."

Nozan nodded and sighed.

"Why not gather the corps commanders tomorrow evening to discuss future moves? The details can be settled then."

"True, we should start with what's right in front of us. That's a rebuke I deserve. I thought becoming a general would put an end to Corps Commander Faren's scolding, but, heh, it seems that's not the case."

"Haha, when one grows old one becomes a worrier."

"Being treated like a green youth forever is a tough pill. I'll have to be careful."

Nozan laughed and swept his russet hair back.

After a slow, quiet breath, he spoke with a gentle smile.

"Let us do just that. Resting our subordinates is important too. Your troops must be more fatigued than mine. I've brought plenty of sake. I'll have some sent over later, so let them drink tonight."

"That we should. ……It would be wise to share some with the prisoners as well."

"Ah, indeed. Generosity is important."

Krische looked at the two of them with something like admiration.

Seeing to the soldiers' comfort — Krische didn't pay much attention to such things.

She ought to be more mindful of those matters.

Remembering the trouble Selene went through with such things, she immediately thought about dumping it on someone else.

That sort of consideration was clearly not one of Krische's strengths.

"Thank you. Well then, Krische will head back. The prisoner sorting looks like quite a lot of work."

"I should return as well. With this many people, dealing with deserters is difficult. Shall we let the runners go?"

"Yes, that should be fine. As long as we can crush any major resistance, the rest can be ignored. Forcibly adding soldiers who would flee at a time like this has its own dangers."

They made no effort to restrain the prisoners forcibly.

With this many people, even preparing rope was a chore. Taking their weapons was about the extent of it.

Battalion commander rank and above were individually placed in improvised cells, but even they were not treated roughly — Nozan and his people would interrogate and recruit them later.

Since someone was needed to direct the prisoners as well, centurions were left to command the movement of their own men.

The treatment of battalion commanders and centurions was clearly different.

The distinction lay largely in whether one belonged to the side of command or the side of the rank and file.

Barring anything exceptional, responsibility in war was generally borne by those of battalion commander rank and above, and they followed that convention here.

There were minor skirmishes, but no significant problems arose.

Both sides were citizens of the same kingdom, and they had not been engaged in a prolonged, bitter slaughter.

Though they had been crossing swords moments ago, neither side harbored much ill will toward the other.

Units that had suffered heavier casualties were the exception, but by relieving them early from prisoner-sorting duty, giving them rest, and assigning them to camp construction, those issues were somewhat alleviated.

The vast majority on both sides had become soldiers seeking employment — those who fought for lofty purposes like honor or political conviction were not so numerous.

As long as there was a reasonable cause and someone paying gold, in their hearts they didn't much care which banner flew overhead.

On the whole, the processing of prisoners proceeded without hindrance.

The most troublesome were the scattered minor nobles and the second and third sons of noble houses who could not inherit.

Their treatment required some consideration; handle them carelessly and problems would arise later.

Military rank and noble pedigree.

These were always a nuisance in any army.

Having issued instructions for all these various processes and wrapped up the assorted tasks, Krische settled onto the bed in the tent that had been prepared for her and let out a deep breath.

For Krische, the aftermath was more tedious than the day's battle itself.

A military organization where the thoughts of countless individuals intermingle never runs smoothly.

Convey ten, and along the way it becomes seven, then three, and sometimes thirteen — no matter how meticulously Krische calculated, there were limits.

A reality far removed from ideal.

Krische, who preferred efficiency, found this instantly tiresome.

In the end she trimmed the various opinions down, issued only the necessary instructions, and told them "just do as you please from here," leaving it to each battalion's initiative. She similarly delegated the soldiers' morale-boosting entirely.

Outside the tent, the voices of soldiers drunk on victory wine.

It was extremely annoying, but she could hardly tell them to be quiet.

Krische pursed her lips and silently flicked her fingers, running mana through the air.

A thin layer of mana coated the inside of the tent and inscribed a formula.

The clamor thinned to something like a distant commotion.

A soundproofing spell deliberately made delicate, so that the moment someone set foot inside, it would dissolve.

Her promise to Bery was simply that she shouldn't use magic openly.

Since then, Krische had devised all manner of spells.

She had demonstrated several to Bery — little games between the two of them alone.

A spell to manipulate the soup in a pot like a snake, pouring it into another vessel.

A spell to heat meat all the way through.

A spell to extract only the delicious parts of tea leaves.

Most were cooking-related, but some practical ones existed as well.

This was one of them.

Originally created to insulate the contents of a pot from the outside air so food wouldn't cool, by adapting it she could substantially block sound.

Krische smiled in the now-quiet tent and produced a basket.

From it she drew a wrapped bundle, spread it on the simple desk, and gazed at it.

Inside was a rakura pie that looked ready to melt — yet somehow inorganic.

She and Bery had made it together the day before departure.

They had secretly "taste-tested" a third of it, and she had brought the rest.

"Ehehe……"

On the battlefield, she couldn't eat the kind of delicious things made in a proper kitchen.

This had been a major problem recently, but Krische had prepared a countermeasure.

The rakura pie, which had looked almost like a model, instantly radiated heat the moment Krische flicked her fingertips, and a sweet aroma tickled her nose.

She cut away half with a kitchen knife, then flicked her fingers at the remainder again, weaving a formula for a brief moment.

Pale blue mana overflowed from Krische's fingertips, warped, filled the tent with geometric lines, and converged — whereupon the rakura pie solidified again as if it were a model or some such thing.

What she felt was dizziness and exhaustion.

She could feel a great deal of mana drain away, but a smile remained at the corners of her mouth.

Wrapping the rakura pie neatly again and storing it back in the basket.

Krische drizzled honey over the pleasantly warm pie and brought it to her mouth.

The moist, flaky pastry had been carefully layered, and the heated rakura fruit was so sweet her tongue nearly gave way. Adding honey on top made it the ultimate sweet treat for Krische.

"……It's delicious."

Cheeks relaxing, Krische savored it with small bites.

Texture. Flavor. Aroma.

She ate slowly, savoring each, pecking at it like a small bird.

But the more she ate, the thinner her smile grew, the more her eyes fell downcast.

Sweet, delicious, flaky.

This was surely a truly exquisite pie that anyone would praise.

*'Is it delicious, Krische-sama?'*

*'Yes, very! It might be the most delicious thing ever……!'*

*'Fufu, as I thought, when you're only eating a little, it's better to use plenty of butter. Too much and you'll get heartburn, though.'*

*'Ehehe, Krische will eat it little by little so it's fine!'*

When they had "taste-tested" it together, she had truly thought so, and she had clung to Bery in delight.

A taste and memory that filled her completely.

But here, that was absent.

Having finished eating, she looked around the empty tent.

Then she flopped onto the bed.

The air was somehow cold, and she stared blankly up at the ceiling.

A dim tent, the outside light showing faintly through.

The sweetness lingered in her mouth. She sat up and washed it down with water.

Feeling a slight hunger, but with no desire to eat anything, she lay down on the bed again.

Lying on her side, hugging the blanket, she pressed her face into it in a daze.

— Ah, it was so delicious because she had eaten it with Bery.

The realization came suddenly.

Like the air filling the room, a hollowness filled her chest.

It had only been about a week. She already wanted to go home.

But going home wouldn't change anything.

Bery had headed north with Kreschenta, and Selene was waiting for Krische and the others.

She couldn't go home until everything was finished.

The sensation of tearing flesh on the battlefield.

Screams and resentment.

She had done well. That much was certain.

Being hated, being feared — she was good at those things.

There was no hesitation, and she didn't care if people saw her that way.

*'— Maybe you'd feel refreshed if you killed someone with a kitchen knife. But making food is so much more fun, and it makes you so much happier, doesn't it?'*

But every time she did it, she thought: this is not a happy place.

A blade is a blade; the one wielding it is Krische.

Cutting people or cutting food. A trivial difference, yet so very different.

Her swordsmanship had improved greatly, and lately she had become much better at killing.

The gaps in armor, the fibers of muscle.

Discerning them, avoiding bone, hooking a major blood vessel with the tip of her blade.

Her opponents stood in the way of Krische's objectives. So killing them brought a feeling of relief.

But that was all.

When things went according to plan, it was satisfying.

But that was not exclusive to this particular work.

Having learned so many things that were far more enjoyable, killing now only served to distract from discomfort — nothing more.

No matter how well she killed, Bery would not be pleased.

If anything, Bery disliked that way of using a blade.

Thinking about that made it feel just a little unpleasant.

When Krische used a kitchen knife to cook, Bery was so happy — yet when it came to Krische killing people, she always wore a sad expression.

Krische wanted to become the Krische who would never make Bery wear a sad face.

To feel the same joy, the same sadness, as Bery's values dictated, to nestle perfectly against Bery's happiness and spend time together that way.

If she could do that, she felt she would become so much happier, not just through cooking but through all sorts of things.

But the Krische that Krische wished for — the Krische she was searching for — was not here, at least.

On the contrary, it felt as though she was drifting further and further from the Krische that Bery wished for.

The better she became at killing, the more that thought flickered through her mind.

"Nn……"

She squeezed the blanket tightly.

She had to do it — it was work, a duty.

It couldn't be helped. She had to do her best even if she didn't like it.

So today she had meant to eat as a little reward.

The pie Bery had baked for Krische.

The work ahead would be long, so she would look forward to it, to keep going.

But eating only filled the hollowness in her chest all the more.

"……Krische wants to cook with Bery."

Wishing all of this would end quickly, she murmured.

There was something she couldn't sort out in her head.

It chilled her body, and her thoughts lost their balance.

Compared to before, the swings had grown wider, and she felt herself crooked and unstable.

Something was swirling inside her, round and round.

She rubbed her face into the blanket.

At times like this, sleep was best.

If she could blank everything out, she always felt refreshed.

She relaxed her body, stopped her thoughts, and closed her eyes.

Quietly steadying her breathing —

"Buuunny~ you awake~?"

She sprang upright and drew the curved sword at her side.

"Wha, huh……?"

"K-Kalua, you idiot……!!"

Standing there were Mia and Kalua.

"Ehehe~ the star of a great victory going to sleep? That won't do, come on, come on, come to big sis~"

"S-sorry, Corps Commander, K-Kalua had a bit too much to drink and……"

"Is that so……"

Krische sheathed her curved sword with an displeased look and glared at Kalua.

"……Krische was about to go to sleep."

"Now, now, don't say that. Look, 'It's a good chance to deepen bonds with your subordinates, and sleeping through this is cold' — that's what Mia was saying."

"I didn't say that much!"

Kalua approached without reservation and held out her hand to Krische.

Krische looked at the hand and frowned.

The reasoning about bonding with subordinates was not incomprehensible.

But more than that —

"Kalua, you reek of alcohol. Just so you know, if tomorrow you're hung over or something —"

"Now, now, can't be all stiff and proper all the time~"

"Eh, ah……"

Kalua slipped her hands under Krische's thighs and behind her back, lifting her up.

"The hero of the victory sitting it out while everyone's having fun — they can't enjoy themselves properly, right? Annoying as it is, this kind of socializing is important, Corps Commander. You're already seen as cold and scary, so you need to show your face more."

Laughing merrily, Kalua said this, and Krische hesitated briefly before silently placing her hands around Kalua's neck.

Soft warmth was there.

"See? Told you, bunny's just shy. That's my win."

"Y-you were going to blame me if she got angry……Kalua always does that."

"Hmm, I don't recall anything like that. Did I?"

"Ugh……"

Carrying Krische.

Saying this, they stepped out of the tent.

Ooh — the gathered soldiers looked at Kalua like she was a hero, and Dagra, relieved the two were unscathed, let out a sigh and rubbed his temple.

"Hmhm, I've captured the princess. Those seeking an audience shall go through me."

Shooing away the soldiers nearby with her hands, Kalua sat down right there with Krische on her lap.

"Come on, Mia, what are you doing? Drinks, drinks."

"……Goodness. Corps Commander, would you prefer it mixed with fruit?"

"Um……yes."

Not quite understanding what was happening, Krische found herself invited to the feast.

Countless tents and bonfires.

Those on sentry duty watched enviously as the men drank and laughed.

Some had already fallen asleep where they sat.

Laughter rang out in every direction.

She overlaid it with a scene from the past.

There had been no festivals in the town, but in the village there had been one each year, in the spring when the snow melted.

She was not good at talking, and had no interest in dancing or singing.

With nothing to do, she would go around helping here and there, only to be told to relax for a bit, pulled by the hand, and sat on someone's lap.

A warm sensation.

She remembered that she had often sat on Grace's or Galla's lap just like this, watching.

Under a moonlit night, light dazzling enough to make her eyes blink.

The voices of celebration erasing the quiet of night.

Recalling just a little of that, Krische turned her eyes skyward.

In a world where the brightness had swallowed all the stars, only the moon shone brilliantly.

# Chapter 72: What She Gazes at Beyond the Hilt She Grips

Most of those at the feast were from the black century, and some of the First Battalion's light infantry could be seen as well.

Units that had been on the front lines had been largely exempted from sentry duty and given rest.

Begil was looking over at them, laughing and saying something to Dagra, while Dagra wore an annoyed, exasperated expression as he replied.

Krische watched them from a distance and brought the drink she had received from Mia to her lips.

The wine was quite mild. The freshly squeezed juice was dominant — it was closer to juice than anything.

She could probably drink this normally, she thought, wetting her throat while watching the feast.

Like a cat brought to an unfamiliar house, Krische sat motionless on Kalua's lap, saying nothing.

She had no idea what to talk about.

Unless she had business, Krische still did not initiate conversation with others.

All the more so when they seemed to be enjoying themselves.

Krische sat in silence, her violet eyes watching the merry soldiers, sipping the drink that was little more than fruit juice.

"Hey, there's no plate in front of the Corps Commander."

One man who had been watching spoke up and smacked a young man on the backside.

It was the young light infantryman Krische had been teaching swordsmanship to recently.

The hesitant young man hurriedly piled some snacks from the rough spread onto a plate and set it before Krische.

"C-Corps Commander, please."

"Oh, um……thank you."

There wasn't much of anything special.

Grilled horsemeat and toasted jerky. And some fruit.

But Krische bowed her head politely and looked up at the young man.

Her face faintly flushed from the alcohol.

The young man was transfixed by it, froze — and got flicked on the forehead by Kalua.

"Ow!?"

"How dare you gaze upon the princess's countenance so boldly. Be a little sneakier about it."

"Y-yes……!"

The young man performed an incomprehensible salute and hurried back to his seat.

Laughter erupted from those nearby, and only Krische, at the center of it all, tilted her head in puzzlement.

"Ah……nirkana."

She had been planning to sleep and had been holding back, but now that she would stay up, the mild hunger she had been feeling was becoming hard to bear.

Noticing her favorite nirkana berries on the plate, she plucked one up and popped it in her mouth.

A small fruit, sweet as sugar.

She had eaten them with Bery in Mitskronetia.

They were only fingertip-sized and couldn't fill her stomach, but they were perfect for snacking.

"Tasty?"

"……Yes."

Krische nodded honestly.

Not as delicious as the pie she had eaten earlier. They were merely sweet fruit.

But she did not feel the emptiness she had felt before.

"Mmm, those are too sweet for me."

"Then apples, perhaps."

"Ooh……"

Hearing this, Krische picked up an apple from the plate, took a nearby knife, and cut it into pieces.

She held one of the cut pieces to Kalua's mouth.

Kalua bit into it without hesitation and laughed.

"M-making the Corps Commander cut apples……"

"This is the privilege of an attendant. Heh, delicious~"

"That's good."

Krische smiled, seemingly unbothered.

"C-can I have one too?"

"H-hey……"

A brave man called out and approached.

A man who looked more like a rock than a person.

"……? Of course, go ahead."

But Krische readily obliged this request as well, giving him one of the cut pieces.

"Thank you so much……!!"

Unlike Kalua, he didn't have the nerve to take it with his mouth.

The man received the cut apple with both hands as if it were a treasure.

Then he held it up to show the surrounding soldiers, like a trophy.

The astonished men exchanged glances, stood up, and crowded toward Krische.

— Me too, please. Over here too.

Krische looked slightly surprised but smiled and readily agreed — "Of course, just a moment please."

And so she began peeling fruit.

It reminded her of when she had peeled fruit for the children in the village.

Just as then, a mountain of fruit was brought before Krische, and she neatly cut through it all with a knife.

There were all sorts of fruit, but she did not hesitate.

Her hands were steady and deft, and the men admired her practiced skill.

Naturally, a smile had settled on Krische's lips.

Finding the optimal approach for each shape, cutting out only the edible parts.

Nearly all the ways of peeling the various fruits here, she had learned from Bery.

*This is how you do it, if you peel it like this it comes out well* — one by one.

In the village, the variety hadn't been so great, and in the town she had been overwhelmed by fruits she was seeing for the first time.

So she had learned nearly all of it from Bery, and sometimes they had figured out the method together.

That was how she had come to know them.

Remembering those times, carefully.

The fruit was like a book — touching it brought scenes floating before her eyes.

As if layering those scenes, she carved into the gem-like fruit.

Kalua watched this with faint surprise, and from time to time popped a nirkana — apparently her favorite — into Krische's mouth.

Each time, Krische would say things like "it's delicious" or "thank you" — which was oddly charming.

Through the silver curtain of hair, violet eyes were quietly fixed on the fruit.

Her expression was softer than usual, lacking its usual severity, showing no displeasure or puzzlement at the situation of being made to peel fruit.

The men received their portions one by one, gazing at the world's most beautiful fruit peeler, and when they said their thanks she offered a smile — her gentle manner seemed like a different person from the girl who tore through enemies on the battlefield.

Gradually, together with the sight of her, the atmosphere of the place grew quieter.

Mia glanced at Kalua, silently asking if they should stop, but Kalua hesitated briefly and nodded that it was fine.

Krische seemed to be enjoying herself more than anything, and thinking it was fine as long as Krische was content, Mia said nothing and kept refilling her drink.

Some time passed like that.

Even though it was practically fruit juice, by the third cup Krische's cheeks were conspicuously red.

Her eyes took on a soft, liquid gleam, and a strange allure began to radiate from her.

"……Um, Mia, she's going to drink herself under the table. Switch to juice from here."

"Eh, ah, r-right……"

Mia, who had been unconsciously building anticipation about what a drunk Krische might do, hastily stopped what she was doing.

White skin tinged with red, Krische's dazed appearance.

The soldiers looking inexplicably pleased.

Krische, seemingly unaware of any of it.

Kalua sighed, clapped her hands — "All right, that's enough" — and shooed the soldiers away.

"Come on, your eyes are getting lecherous. How long are you going to make your superior officer peel fruit?"

"……Krische is fine, you know?"

"Everyone except bunny has a problem. Honestly, way too unguarded."

"It was my turn next, though……"

"……First Battalion Commander, sir. Captain Dagra is beckoning."

Begil — who had apparently been queuing quietly — scratched his cheek, slumped his shoulders, and headed back to Dagra.

Dagra was pressing his temple with an exasperated look, giving Begil an earful.

Kalua laughed at the sight and gently stroked Krische's head.

Krische, somewhat dazed, let her do as she pleased.

Silver hair shimmering in firelight and moonlight.

Picking up strands with her fingers, Kalua spoke in an amazed voice.

"Whew, so silky……Out in the field all this time and it's still this nice."

"……Is it?"

"Mine's gone all frizzy. I've been slacking on taking care of it."

"That won't do. You have to take proper care of your hair."

"Huh……"

Mia looked admiringly at the pale pink flower ornament fastening Krische's hair.

"That hair ornament suits you perfectly too……Being that beautiful is really enviable."

"Don't worry. Hair as pretty as bunny's wouldn't suit Mia anyway, so you're just right as you are."

"Why, you……"

"……Come to think of it, Krische forgot to take it off."

She usually removed it before sleeping, but now that it was mentioned, she noticed and took out the hair clip.

Her silver hair cascaded loose, flowing without a hint of curl.

It was a hair ornament shaped like a pale pink flower. Not particularly expensive.

But precious all the same.

Bery had bought it from a merchant who happened to visit the fortress, saying it would suit Krische.

"……It's something Krische got from Bery. She said it suited Krische."

"Ah, that person……"

Krische placed it carefully on her lap and gazed at it with something warm in her eyes.

The faintest curve at the corner of her lips — the firelit profile of the girl looked endlessly beautiful and ephemeral.

"Do you miss Bery-san?"

"Huh?"

"You look like you do."

When Kalua asked, Krische's eyes wavered hesitantly, and she nodded.

"……Maybe. Probably, Krische misses her."

Her head, slightly numbed by alcohol, let honest words slip out.

Kalua tilted her head as Krische brought the juice in her cup to her lips.

"Um……probably?"

"Krische doesn't really understand things like loneliness, or fear. Krische doesn't know for certain whether she's properly lonely."

"Ah, I see……So bunny is serious about that kind of thing too."

Kalua smiled wryly and patted her head gently.

"There's no 'properly' or anything about it, bunny. It's different for everyone."

"Is that so?"

"That's how it is. The Corps Commander is lonely, and that's why she drinks and makes merry with everyone to take her mind off it. ……Or so Mia was saying."

"……Is that right, Mia?"

"Huh? Oh, well, th-there might be something to that, but……"

Caught off guard, Mia nodded for the moment, and Krische considered it for a moment before nodding as well.
