# Chapter 256: The Cage of Sin

"I truly do believe I am the most fortunate person in the world."

She said it the way she always did.

Holding Kreschenta in bed, her voice calm and measured.

"Not only to have Krische-sama's love……but to be so deeply cherished by the great Queen of Alberan herself, a woman whose name is etched deeply into history."

A soft, teasing laugh tickled her ear.

Kreschenta opened her mouth to say something, then immediately stopped.

The inside of her chest kept seizing and spasming with tension, making it hard to breathe, and she did not want to let this servant hear such a pathetic sound.

She pressed her lips together and pushed her face into the servant's chest.

Over her eyelids, over her lips — as though to seal everything away.

The servant tightened her embrace and stroked her head.

"My, you don't deny it. ……For the record, silence is also taken as confirmation, so do keep that in mind."

The teasing voice was gentle, and it was infuriating.

She knew Kreschenta couldn't lift her face, and said it anyway.

"But do rest assured. Such an embarrassing secret of yours shall be kept strictly confidential — locked away in the breast of your servant. A good servant fulfills her master's wishes without being told."

The infuriating servant piled on her worthless words and stroked her head.

Always, always — when it suited her, she was Onee-sama's servant, the estate's servant, Christand's servant.

— And Kreschenta's servant.

She appeared serious but was really careless, and far too clever with words.

Calling herself a servant was just pretense — an individualist who would scoff at social order and class structure.

From the standpoint of the 'ideal kingdom' Kreschenta envisioned, she was the most offensive woman imaginable.

She spoke of everything in terms of emotion, piling on plausible sophistry, bewitching others with her petty delusions, a pest who sought to shape the world to suit herself.

A ship did not need two helmsmen.

And it was Kreschenta who was queen — this woman was, in essence, a political rival.

How much better it would have been if she had killed her sooner, Kreschenta thought.

Then she would never have had to endure such lofty words from above.

*How did it come to this?* she wondered.

Perhaps at some point, she had been taken in by this woman's sophistry without realizing it.

"……Fufu, I keep trying to say things, but I end up thinking too much and can't manage it. Talking about old memories or things to come — you'd be surprised how little you can actually say."

She laughed as if troubled and kept stroking her head.

Her skin was cooler than Kreschenta's, and it felt like soaking in lukewarm water.

Neither scalding nor cold.

Lukewarm water that drained away all strength and made you not want to think about anything.

"I hold no grudge, and I am neither angry nor sad. As an ending to a life, this is good enough, I think."

She said this and rubbed her cheek against the top of Kreschenta's head.

"It is true that it was painful and agonizing. I would have loved to wail and scream about it……in that sense, I truly am grateful."

"……!"

She grabbed the back of the servant's negligee and clenched it tight.

"Lies, all lies. ……No matter how you dress it up with words, you are dying. Being killed……by me. So why should I have to be comforted by the person I am killing?"

"……My, you really are just like Krische-sama, Kreschenta-sama."

*What a beautiful person*, she laughed again.

Happily, joyfully, lovingly.

"Even without being judged by law, you feel that because you did something wrong you deserve to be punished — that very feeling is itself the punishment. ……You understand right and wrong in your heart, Kreschenta-sama, no matter what you say, and anything more would be excessive."

She stroked her head the way she always did and combed through her hair.

Thump, thump — the beat of her heart echoed, and Kreschenta could only sniffle quietly.

"Moreover, I am not a guardian of the law — I am but a humble servant who loves Kreschenta-sama dearly. Comforting you is only natural. And a crying child being comforted is also only natural."

"……I am not crying."

"Oh my, you're right. I did promise to keep it locked away."

She giggled softly and kept stroking.

The strength in her embracing arms was gradually fading.

Understanding that the medicine was taking effect, Kreschenta's spine went rigid as though frozen.

"……Please take care of Krische-sama. She will surely be very saddened."

She laughed, looking a little lonely, and continued vaguely.

"I had promised not to do anything that would make her sad, but……"

Kreschenta lifted her face in a rush and sent mana coursing into her body.

Through her blurred vision, she could see the servant smiling.

Her palm touched Kreschenta's cheek, and her thumb wiped beneath her eyes.

"Ufufu, just like Krische-sama……a crying face really doesn't suit you, Kreschenta-sama."

"……!"

"We mustn't let anyone else know. ……You are my beloved, wonderful, greatest queen in all the world, after all."

The words caught in the back of her throat and wouldn't come out.

She watched the sleepily wavering voice and the trembling eyelids, then grasped both her cheeks.

She rubbed their noses together, drew her face close, and the touch of their lips was simply soft.

For just a moment, the servant opened her trembling eyelids wide, gazed at Kreschenta with her light-brown eyes.

Then slowly narrowed them, and mouthed, without sound — *I know.*

As always, with that teasing smile.

After saying it, she closed her eyes, and let go of consciousness just like that.

"ah……"

Immediately, she wove mana into her.

She thought to stop time, then hurriedly changed the mana into threads.

*Maybe*, she thought. *If it works*, she thought.

She was simply, purely afraid of something.

Her breathing grew ragged, something seemed to crawl inside her chest.

As if fleeing from it, she simply kept weaving formulae.

The mana frayed and split like threads, and formed a cage.

Wrapping around her body, imprisoning everything down to her blood, flesh, bone, and nerves.

*Stop time* — that was the only thought.

With enough time, anything was possible.

Ten years from now, twenty years, even a hundred — someday, it could be done.

All that was lacking was time and room to breathe.

That was all she wanted.

From one end of her to the other, engraving it into every part of her, again and again.

Perfection layered upon perfection, fold upon fold.

As if to prove that nothing was impossible for her, that she had made no mistakes.

— But regardless of all that, results were everything.

Blood seeped through her negligee.

Even the blood that seeped turned instantly into mana and dissipated.

Parts of her body crumbled away like a sandhill blown by the wind.

Mana was poured in, within the cage sealed shut in countless layers.

Inside it, her body turned to blue light — and only the clothing she wore remained.

Unable even to make a sound, she reached out in a daze.

She grasped the negligee the servant had been wearing.

Stared at it.

Held it to her chest.

— How long had she stayed like that?

"Your Majesty! What——"

Before she realized it, someone had called out from the doorway.

The voice seemed to echo from far away.

"Where is Argan-sama……?"

In her arms was only a thin, flat sensation.

Still dazed, she stared at the negligee.

It had belonged to the servant that Kreschenta killed.

She pictured Nora, with the knife driven into her chest.

She pictured the red-haired servant, traced her own lips, and closed her eyes.

Behind her, she heard the sound of someone collapsing to the floor, and she steadied her breathing.

"……Argan-sama has passed away. It happened suddenly."

Then, as always, with perfect composure, Kreschenta layered on her lies.

She had never made a mistake. She was a perfect being, always.

Even if it was a lie, once spoken it became truth.

"Onee-sama and the others will be saddened. ……For now, I suppose we should prepare the funeral."

For just a moment, she pressed the negligee to her face.

As if wiping something away, beneath both eyes.

— To Kreschenta, other people had always been like tools.

As it had been until now, so it would continue to be.

She raised her face, got down from the bed, and looked at the full-length mirror.

Her usual self was there, gazing back at Kreschenta with perfect composure from the other side.

*That's fine*, she thought.

"A shame."

A tool that had been useful had broken down, had come to trouble Kreschenta's own hands, had been beyond saving — so she had simply settled things at the end.

There was nothing that needed thinking about, she told herself, and placed the negligee on the bed.

Glancing at the servant who had collapsed and covered her face, she reached for the door.

"I shall go fetch Elvena-sama. Please rest a moment."

As if looking away from something that clung to her, she turned her eyes to what needed doing.

She stepped outside the room.

What was done was done.

There was nothing to be done about it.

She averted her gaze, looked away.

— Telling herself that eventually, she would stop remembering.

* * *

It was like a cage made of facing mirrors.

The inside of the small mana crystal was carved with precise patterns layered upon themselves, fold upon fold.

In appearance it was small enough to fit in one hand, but it was a multidimensional crystal with space itself bent and folded.

Visually, it seemed as though an almost infinite brilliance unfolded within it from every angle.

Its inner radiance changed each time she looked, and a slight shift in angle revealed a different face.

Within it, five lights burned.

Each time she looked, they caught in the girl's eyes from all manner of directions, from all manner of angles.

She turned the crystal, searching for something, and fixed her gaze on one of the lights.

She thought that someday she wished it would go out, that she wished it would simply vanish — yet every time she looked, that light leapt into her eyes.

Still wearing her white one-piece dress, she rolled onto the bed and let her long hair spread across it.

She looked at it lying on her side, held it up to look, and occasionally enclosed its brilliance within her hand.

She thought that doing so might eventually extinguish its light — yet when she looked again, it was there unchanged.

It was an irksome, hateful light, shining obtrusively.

She brought it close before her eyes again and stared.

Was there no angle from which its light would disappear? She examined it from every possible direction, idly.

— *I hate you.*

Her lips moved as they always did, and the words echoed into the void.

No response came, and the light did not dim.

No matter how many times she repeated it, her brilliance was there, asserting its presence in the girl's eyes.

No angle changed it, and even with her eyes closed, it would not leave the backs of her eyelids.

Overbearing, arrogant, and ill-mannered.

The only thing worth praising was the food.

That thoroughly infuriating servant's mocking little smile, and every single word echoed in her mind, repeating.

She remembered that sensation and traced her own lips.

Something dark and viscous swirled inside her chest, and she closed her eyes at the unpleasantness of it.

When she recalled that face, it overlapped with a face contorted in agony.

Suffering night after night, tears brimming.

The vacant look in her eyes had burned into her mind and would not leave.

As if cowering from pain and death, the figure begging for help was engraved into her memory day after day.

*'……Even if I last, how many years do you intend to wait?'*

*'Of course, as many decades as Krische-sama wishes.'*

And after showing such a sight, she would tell Kreschenta with complete composure.

That she was still happy, as if nothing at all had happened.

She simply conveyed her gratitude.

*Thank you*, she would repeat.

*Thanks to you, another happy day continues* — she would say, gesturing at those days drenched in agony.

Yesterday and the day before, today and tomorrow and the day after, and the day after that.

All Kreschenta was doing was prolonging it.

Making her repeat suffering that even the servant could not hide anymore, suffering with no end in sight.

Every time she received those words, she wondered — gratitude for what?

And so she decided she would end it — she would bring it to a close.

"……I did nothing wrong."

The words rang hollow.

When she opened her eyes, the light was still shining, unchanged from a moment ago.

So hateful she wished she could make it never appear in her sight again, so irksome — yet when she changed the angle, it showed her all manner of colors.

How much better it would be if it were ugly, she thought.

If it were so ugly she couldn't bear to look at it, she'd never have to see it again.

Why had her sister brought such a thing?

Without this, she wouldn't have to remember, wouldn't be imprisoned — but because it was here, it was as if she were locked in a cage.

She tried to escape but couldn't; her own thoughts formed the cage, and the very thing she had meant to imprison was imprisoning her.

— *I love you. I love you.*

Always, always repeating such flimsy words.

They were nothing but lip service and illusion.

Even at the point of being killed, she had kept repeating such words.

She had simply given up.

Because voicing resentment would have been pointless, she had said such things.

Just her usual stubbornness, mouthing pretty words.

No one would be happy about being killed.

No matter how you dressed it up, death was death. There was no way she held no grudge.

So surely, in her true heart——

She enclosed the mana crystal in her hand.

Covered it so its light couldn't be seen.

She didn't care.

— So long as it remained imprisoned forever, those had been her final words.

As long as things stayed this way, she would never speak another word beyond that.

As long as it shone here, the pretty words remained pretty.

No matter how flimsy, they would be etched forever as honest, unfaltering words.

*That's fine*, she thought, and peeked through the gap in her palm once more.

She gazed at the light imprisoned within the mirror-world and stroked it.

That light would remain in Kreschenta's hand for eternity.

* * *

"Kreschenta, Krische is home."

"……Welcome back, Onee-sama."

She slid the mana crystal from her chest, tucking it inside her one-piece dress.

Then she sat up and looked toward the entrance.

Onee-sama was wearing her usual apron dress with a curved sword hung at the back of her waist, and Kreschenta tilted her head.

"What were you doing?"

"Scraggly said he wanted to spar, so Krische was keeping him company. Took Gururun for a walk while Krische was at it."

She unfastened the belt from her waist and set the curved sword on the shelf.

She toddled over, knelt on the bed, stroked Kreschenta's cheek, and kissed her slowly.

They stayed that way for a while, eyes closed.

When she opened them, her sister's purple was peering in.

A gaze that looked slightly troubled, as if wavering over something.

"Scraggly" was, as always, one of the silly pet names her sister had bestowed.

One of the few survivors among the people who knew what her sister desired.

He would probably be the long-lived type, like Felwors.

Bodies of flesh had what could be called a regeneration limit.

Past a certain span, blood and flesh lost their ability to regenerate normally, gradually declining in quality and accelerating in deterioration — regeneration could no longer keep up.

Aging was deterioration.

For those like Onee-sama and Kreschenta, who could achieve complete mana control over their physical bodies, preventing deterioration was simple — but ordinary people could not.

However, among ordinary people, there were those somewhat more adept at it.

Functional decline as aging progressed to a certain degree.

Most people around that time began to prioritize mana over flesh in their bodily control, stabilizing in an aged state — and such people tended to live quite long.

Of course, much depended on the body one was born with, but Aleha appeared reasonably robust physically, and his mana-based bodily control was of quite fine quality.

Since aging had already begun, an ordinary person like Aleha had his limits — but at least for the next few decades, his body seemed like it would hold.

— *If only he would hurry up and die.*

She gazed at her sister's eyes and concluded that he had probably been filling her head with unnecessary ideas.

If time would just wash everything away from around her sister quickly enough, then her sister would no longer have to waver.

One person was enough to be precious to her sister and to be held precious by her — and that was Kreschenta.

"……Kreschenta, um……there's something Krische wants to talk——"

"I'm hungry."

She stared at her sister, cutting off her words.

She extended both hands and hung them around her sister's neck, then reached out with mana to operate the mana crystal in the bathroom and start filling the bath.

Her sister stared at Kreschenta with that troubled look again, then smiled quietly.

"……Then, Krische will cook and we'll eat, and take a bath. ……After that, we'll talk. All right?"

When Kreschenta didn't answer but pressed close, *what a spoiled child*, her sister said, and picked Kreschenta up just like that.

The troubled smile looked very much like that servant's, somehow.

* * *

In the kitchen she made rakura pie, pumpkin soup, and braised lamb.

With just the two of them, they didn't need that much.

She was well-practiced, could make it with her eyes closed.

Standing side by side, the waiting time was longer.

She stirred the soup and gazed at the shelves.

Spices, dishes, cooking tools — their arrangement hadn't changed in a long time.

Originally, it had been optimized for her sister to cook alongside that servant, and after it became just the two of them, there had been room for improvement.

When the people changed, the division of tasks changed too.

It was only natural — and yet whenever her sister tidied up, she always returned things to this arrangement.

From knives to dishes, she polished everything until it gleamed, but never changed where anything went.

— Just as it was when that servant was here.

She didn't take risks the way that servant had.

She made fine adjustments to flavor and used new ingredients when they became available.

But she never said, *Let's try something we haven't done before.*

*'……I'm sorry. Today's experiment was a failure.'*

She remembered that face, smiling ruefully, and touched her lips to the ladle.

The soup was as delicious as always, unchanged from when that servant was here.

With decades of making the same dishes, failure simply didn't exist.

Her sister would deny it, but that servant had many failures.

She was a woman who could produce 'reasonably delicious food,' but took risks that seemed almost deliberate.

Making sauces or desserts from fruits that were obviously going to taste terrible.

She usually failed, wasting ingredients.

And yet watching her, her sister always looked so happy.

On those rare occasions when something worked, she celebrated as if it were the discovery of the century.

*'Kreschenta. This is not a failure — it is simply a challenge that did not go well. There is more to learn from failure than success in this world.'*

Pure parroting, delivered as if it were golden wisdom, finger raised and all.

Onee-sama truly was a fool.

Kreschenta didn't think it was entirely without merit, but it was just food.

One more delicious thing was all it added.

There were plenty of delicious things already; the stability was more than sufficient — so what was the point of reaching for more? She always watched those irrational attempts with exasperation.

She had learned most of the dishes and caught up, but reluctantly went along with such foolishness, got swept up in it.

This kitchen used to be quite chaotic, she thought.

After that servant was gone, it settled into stability, with no more failures — so it would have been more rational to rearrange the tools' placement.

And yet, even now, it remained as it was.

The pungent, hard-to-use spices still sat at the front of the shelf.

Dishes, knives, frying pans, and pots — their positions were all that servant's preference.

— Every time she entered this kitchen, every time she cooked here, she remembered.

The figure of that servant standing here would overlap, and the figure of her sister laughing happily would overlap, and Kreschenta's own figure, complaining but going along with it anyway.

Surely, her sister felt the same.

Repeating the same unchanging, failure-free dishes, yet floating an inexplicably happy smile.

Whether together or alone — that servant lived inside her sister's head.

"……Kreschenta?"

When she furrowed her brows in displeasure, her sister came close and took the ladle.

Then tasted the soup and tilted her head.

She must have thought there was a problem with the seasoning.

"Ehehe, it's delicious."

"……That's good."

She sighed and said so, and her sister pressed her lips to hers with a troubled look.

There was the faintly sweet taste of pumpkin soup.

A taste sickeningly unchanged from back then.

She would go on tasting this for eternity, no doubt.

She noticed then — every dish Kreschenta made had been learned from that servant, and no matter which she tasted, it carried that servant's flavor.

All throughout the estate, too — as people disappeared one by one, that presence slipped into the gaps.

She traced her lips with her fingertip and confirmed the sensation.

Even now, the feeling remained as if it were yesterday.

— *I know.*

The soundlessly formed words echoed in her mind, still unfading.

* * *

They finished their meal, soaked in the bath, changed, and went to the bedroom.

Her sister held Kreschenta on her lap and ran a comb through her hair.

The light of a crescent moon shone in through the window.

Yet the moon never truly changed its shape.

Sunlight from the far side was blocked by the earth, and so the moon merely gained shadows.

The moon was always whole, hanging in the sky.

Only shadows appeared upon it.

"……Kreschenta."

"I don't want to hear it."

Kreschenta had always been a perfect being.

Unchanged from birth, born already complete.

It was the world that was wrong, the world that was twisted.

The moon always hung in the sky, unchanging.

It only appeared to wane because those below could only perceive what they saw as it was.

To insects clinging to the earth, it surely did look like a crescent.

But Kreschenta was not waning.

Nor was her sister — it was everything else that was wrong.

That was the truth, the one certain thing.

And yet, if that were so.

She had changed the world as she wished, obtained everything the world had to offer, become a perfect being acknowledged by all — so why was she still thinking about that servant?

Why was she trapped in thoughts that went round and round?

"……I don't want to hear that kind of talk."

She had decided she didn't need to remember — it was supposed to be a settled triviality.

The only one who could stand beside her was her sister; everything else was accessories her sister happened to like.

They should have been nothing more, and must not be anything more.

They *had* to be nothing more.

— *Because if they weren't, then I……*

She thought this, and again, something unpleasant churned inside her chest.

Her sister pulled her close and pressed her cheek against Kreschenta's back, from behind.

"Kreschenta said that eternity exists here, but……probably, what Krische wants isn't eternity."

Wrapping her in both arms.

As though enclosing her entire body in a gentle cage.

"Just like Krische told Kushenalars-san, eternity is merely a process — what Krische really wants is what lies beyond it……"

And lovingly.

"Rather than a goal, it might be more like Krische's selfishness."

Her sister said it shyly.

"……Probably, in the end, Krische simply wants to see Bery again."

*Ah*, she sighed without a sound.

At the very least, Kreschenta knew that her sister's heart was always there.

"Krische just wants to say, *I'm home*, and hear, *Welcome back*. ……Krische just wants to be hugged the way she always was, and hear, *Good work today*."

Her sister was a child, a little more foolish than Kreschenta.

To meet that servant again required means beyond the ordinary, and to fully enjoy that required a world at peace — and naturally, that had simply become the word *eternity*.

"Krische doesn't understand difficult things like eternal love or any of that……but Krische thinks everyone probably saw right through Krische's silly ideas."

Her sister's ideal world. Her ideal paradise. A childlike utopia.

Her sister had wanted everything she desired, so Kreschenta had let her be.

She knew that her sister's desired eternal paradise could never be accepted, and that only a rare few, peculiar people would accept it.

She knew her sister would be hurt each time she was refused, and that eventually she would stop expecting anything from others — and she had thought that then, perhaps someday, her sister would think only of Kreschenta.

— *Without thinking about anything unnecessary.*

"……What Krische wants is for Krische. What other people want is for other people. When you think about it, it's a really simple matter. Krische is Krische, so Krische just wants to see Bery, and is thinking selfish things like wanting to stay together forever."

*It's all Krische's selfishness*, she said, smiling.

"And on top of that, Selene and everyone said they'd go along with Krische's foolishness."

So utterly happy.

"Krische doesn't know what the future holds. Maybe someday they'll leave Krische's side……but that would surely be because they were satisfied, not because they got tired of Krische."

Kreschenta's flimsy cage of words, like a hollow facade, crumbled easily.

She had known it would come to this immediately.

It felt like it had happened awfully fast, and yet not so fast at all.

"Even if that's not the case, it's fine."

Either way,

"——Krische will choose to see it that way, and interpret it however is most convenient."

Her sister would never forget that servant. She would never let Kreschenta forget, either.

She clenched her fists and lowered her eyes.

Not just her sister — this room too, the kitchen too, the mana crystal hanging from her neck — and in her memories, that servant's scent had soaked into everything.

"……You sound just like Argan-sama, Onee-sama."

She said it, utterly weary.

"Always spouting words that are pleasant to hear. ……That woman was not as admirable a person as you seem to think."

"Whether she was admirable or not is a trivial matter. Krische loves Anne dearly even though she's not admirable at all."

*Krische isn't admirable either*, she smiled.

"Krische is fine no matter how things turn out. Even if, as Kreschenta says, they someday get fed up with Krische……even so, Krische wants to see Bery and the others."

She stroked Kreschenta's cheek, and the words came out as if wrung from her.

With a sincerity that sent a chill down the spine, unadorned.

She spoke exactly as she wished.

"That's Krische's conclusion. ……So——"

"No."

With a single word she cut off her sister's sentence and grasped her arm.

"Why not?"

"……Unlike you, Onee-sama, I have no interest in these people. I'm far happier and more at ease now, with just the two of us."

"Liar. Why do you always pretend to be a bad child, Kreschenta? You feel the same about Bery and the others——"

"I've had quite enough. Stop deciding things on your own."

She shook free, stood, turned to face her, and glared straight at her, sharpening her tone.

"How many times must I say I hate them before you understand, Onee-sama? I only showed them consideration because you held them dear."

The more she thought about it, the more infuriating it became.

Revulsion crawled through her chest, and there was nothing she could do about it.

Each time she recalled it, she remembered the feel of *that scrap of cloth*.

"You really are living in a fantasy, aren't you. Bery, Bery, Bery, like a broken record……I'm truly sick of it."

"……Kreschenta."

"My patience is truly at its limit. ……If you say the name Bery one more time, I will be genuinely angry."

She was astonished at how unstable her own emotions were.

Her body was shaking involuntarily, her breathing shallow, something with nowhere to go swirling inside her.

"If Kreschenta truly says from the bottom of her heart that she wants to be alone with Krische, that she hates Bery and the others and doesn't want to see them, then Krische will consider it."

Her sister looked straight back at her and continued.

"But that's impossible, so even if you get angry, Krische won't stop."

"And how would you know that, Onee-sama?"

"Because Krische is Kreschenta's onee-san. Krische doesn't understand other people's feelings at all, but……"

As always, she smiled gently.

"Krische thinks she understands Kreschenta's feelings better than anyone."

Seeing that, she froze.

"……What could you possibly understand, Onee-sama?"

She squeezed her hands until blood seeped.

— *There was no way her sister could understand.*

She was the only one who had seen.

The things her sister had refused to look at — she alone had watched, right beside her, all that time.

Day and night, repeating every day.

That was why — it had to be that way — otherwise, she would never have done *such a thing*.

"Somehow, this feels just like when Selene and Bery used to bicker."

Her sister said, as if remembering, and stood.

"……More importantly, you said Bery just now, Kreschenta."

She walked over and opened the balcony doors.

"You're always pretending to be angry, always putting on a front. ……If you truly feel that way, why don't you try being genuinely angry for once?"

With the moonlight at her back, her purple eyes gleamed.

— Her sister was serious.

From head to toe, it was as though she were freezing solid.

She swept her right hand — the mana around them erupted in transformation.

Even for her sister, this had gone too far.

"Saying such things……don't blame me if you come to regret it, Onee-sama. Are you aware of the situation? Right now, I can command all the mana in the world."

"Krische knows."

Her sister replied, and extended a thread of mana toward the mana crystal sitting on the desk behind her — and shattered it.

Then, with the tiny amount of mana she gained, she donned blue curves of light and floated into the air.

"But Krische is your onee-san."

"……Do you honestly think I am inferior to you, Onee-sama?"

"Kreschenta is just as clever as Krische. ……But Krische still wins."

*Because Kreschenta isn't serious*, she said.

Those purple eyes narrowed behind silver lashes.

"Do you think I cannot kill you, Onee-sama?"

And Kreschenta likewise narrowed her gold-rimmed crimson eyes.

"I need only create a new body and seal your soul inside it so you cannot defy me."

Cold and dark, that color gleamed.

"……I never imagined you could be this much of a fool, Onee-sama."

Her sister was trying to create distance.

All the mana from Zenith was in Kreschenta's hands.

She couldn't believe her sister didn't understand the disadvantage of giving up this range — the only one where she held superiority.

— She understood, and was giving Kreschenta the advantage anyway.

It was like pouring oil on fire.

# Chapter 260: An Unfinished Story

Kleinmeil's collapse marked the temporary end of magical civilization.

Hatred against the mages who had ruled the world — the century that followed was an age of mage-hunting.

Priceless magical technologies were burned along with precious records, lost — and the World Tree itself was repeatedly targeted for burning.

And given a hundred years, hatred faded.

Just as the World Tree endured, magic itself was not evil — it was the hearts of those who wielded it where evil dwelled.

Monks from the mountains of Albyagel, where the ancient dragons had dwelt, traveled the world preaching this message, and gradually, both the World Tree and mages were accepted once more — and as time passed, stability returned.

Mages were again permitted to conduct research under the banner of 'peaceful development' — though joining a magic academy subjected them to heavy restrictions on unsanctioned independent research and the occasional inspection from the Peace Church.

For ordinary people, mages held tremendous power.

The true reason for allowing them this freedom was likely fear that harsh suppression would provoke rebellion.

Many mages understood that the academies were cages for managing them, but given the history, most accepted the arrangement — and thus things stood to this day.

In the wars at the end of Kleinmeil, millions died, and entire cities were erased along with their inhabitants.

Among the most powerful mages — those designated great sorcerers — some were long-lived enough to be living witnesses of that decades-long war, and their appeal to fellow mages had been significant.

"……You're already that far? You've been skimming too much."

"I mean, there's a lot of filler. Is there a sequel to this?"

"No. It burned in a fire. ……I can't believe they didn't even make a copy of something this important."

The book was almost finished.

There was supposed to be a second volume, but it no longer survived.

It had been a gift to the author's father at the Giterns family estate, buried among a vast library and preserved for years.

When a fire consumed the estate, the then-head of the family had noticed its existence and sold it to a scholar as something of historical value — and that was how this single volume survived.

Karshe smiled wryly at the bristling Mee and turned the page.

After the civil war, the text turned for a while to her rival, Elvena.

Then it spoke of Krische establishing the famous Black Flag Special Service, and how its members began visiting the Christand estate in the royal district.

That had apparently been quite unusual for the time, and the author praised her mistress Alberinea effusively — a person who made no distinction between noble and commoner, including Elvena.

Judging from today, that was likely accurate.

*A pure and kind person*, the author said — and Karshe could nod in agreement.

Elvena apparently had a sister in the predecessor of the Black Flag — the Black Hundred — and the sister and the unit's adjutant visited most often.

That adjutant's name——

"……No way."

Her ears atop her head went stiff, and her tail shot straight.

She felt her wry smile stiffen on her cheeks and rubbed the bridge of her nose.

"What is it? Did you find something?"

"Nah — just tired from reading a book I'm not used to."

"……You know we're pulling an all-nighter, right?"

"Huh? Why?"

"We're writing down everything before I forget. Everything those two said, everything you noticed — all of it. This is a historic event. Once I get famous and have influence, I'm publishing it."

Mee was dead serious; she had already produced ink, a fountain pen, and a notebook.

Karshe's cheek twitched again.

"……Mee-sama, can we do this tomorrow?"

"You forget everything by the next day after sleeping, so I don't trust you. This is your mistress's order."

"Sigh……is it really so hard to just keep it as a memory?"

"Quiet. I'm writing it down so I *don't* forget the memory."

At that, Karshe sighed and returned her gaze to the book.

*'——To quarrel is proof of closeness, so the saying goes. And it fit those two perfectly. Krische-sama would always watch them with a troubled smile, and Elvena-sama would calm them down. Looking exasperated, yet so very, very happy.'*

She closed her eyes for a moment and recalled the girl's purple gaze.

In the museum, as she'd studied the exhibits — the same eyes she turned on Mee and Karshe — they had seemed, somehow, to be looking at something with nostalgia.

She recalled the servant's slightly startled look, and that smile.

A strangely nostalgic smile and gesture.

*She must be Elvena*, Karshe thought, and did not question it.

She didn't believe in reincarnation, and she didn't think that was what this was.

Yet thinking it that way, the whole day's encounter — which could have been pure coincidence — felt just a little bit like destiny, and Karshe quietly let her cheeks relax.

The trivial, amusing daily life described in the book went on without a proper ending — cutting off mid-sentence, leading into a continuation that wasn't there.

The unrecorded days surely went on, unchanged even now — and perhaps they too would look back on today's small memory and smile.

Imagining it, all sorts of scenes filled in the pages beyond the book's end, and Karshe laughed softly to herself.

Only a few pages remained.

Yet beyond them, the story seemed to stretch on infinitely — daunting in its endlessness.

And yet, just as the book described, it also felt strangely cheerful, strangely happy.

Mee, who had begun scribbling the day's events into her notebook, looked up and pouted as if to say *hurry up and help*.

Then her gaze fell on the passage beneath Karshe's finger, and she said with exasperation:

"She's amazing, that person."

"Hm?"

Karshe tilted her head, and Mee continued.

"Calling Alberinea by a pet name — can you imagine?"

Karshe laughed wryly, traced the letters with her finger.

"Really? Fufu……well, unlike you, Mee-sama, I think I understand how she feels."

She remembered — the girl bounding through the snow like a rabbit.

She wondered why she had thought *rabbit* in the first place.

Smiling at the thought, she murmured it like a song.

"I think she's very cute. —— *Bunny.*"
