# Chapter 276: Varkus — Finale

*"Allow me to introduce her — Krische, my adopted daughter. Some of you will know her as the granddaughter of Captain Gallen, the adjutant I hold in the highest regard. It hasn't been decided that she'll join the military, but I've brought her today for a first meeting. ……Krische."*

*"……How do you do. I'm Krische. I look forward to working with you."*

The girl who bowed was unmistakably a child.

Smaller than Selene, and slight. Beautiful silver hair, and a loveliness already half-complete despite her young age. She had the look of some faraway princess about her, and stood out glaringly in a unit made up mostly of rough-hewn men.

Selene, too, was a beautiful girl — but Granmeld felt instinctively that something was different.

The large conference room. Every officer from battalion commander up was present.

Despite those eyes all turning toward her, she showed not the slightest trace of nerves or flinching. Composed, her blank violet eyes tracing quietly over the assembled men, looking back.

*"Something worth noting beforehand — it pains me to use the word, but Krische is what I can only call a genius. She has already put me behind in plain sparring — she dislikes conflict, yet she is beloved of the god of valor."*

A brief stir went through the room.

Bogan's personal ability as a warrior was among the kingdom's finest. The idea of losing to a child was beyond imagining.

So that was what he'd sensed a moment ago.

What Granmeld had felt was the pure vigilance of one warrior before another. Experience and instinct had told him this girl was not what she appeared.

*"In that respect she need not be treated as a child — explain the basics and she will grasp them immediately. In academic matters in particular, she will likely surpass anyone present."*

Selene, standing beside him, pursed her lips slightly. Bogan chuckled and put a hand on her head.

*"Well, beyond that she's still very much a child. She has an oddly earnest streak — jokes go over her head, and conversation isn't her strong suit. Selene will mostly be at her side, so bring your troubles to Selene. ……It seems she's quite pleased to have a little sister, and is very eager."*

*"Father……!"*

*"Heh heh……well, today is introductions only. She'll be coming to watch the training — carry on as normal, just show her what you always do. ……That is all, dismissed."*

The girl had come when the Christand army had grown stable — there was nothing particularly dramatic about it, yet the memory lingered oddly.

As he left the room, Granmeld asked his adjutant.

*"Begil. What's your read?"*

*"……She is already complete, wouldn't you say."*

Begil Sandika answered with a serious face.

His adjutant since making battalion commander — one could also call him the watchman set on the unruly Granmeld. A man who had come up through the ranks from common soldier; a master of Zain-style swordsmanship. In sheer martial ability he was second only to Granmeld in the battalion.

Having won his rank by accumulating merit from the light infantry, he was a sharp man who could command the battalion well even in a melee if you left the rear to him.

*"To think that even now she is already like that — imagining what lies ahead sends a tremor through me."*

*"Oh……?"*

A man trained in orthodox swordcraft, and rich in practical experience besides. His eye was sometimes more trustworthy than Granmeld's own — but,

*"Like a fairy — in two more years, how much more beautiful will she become? ……I had not thought a new mysterious flower would appear in the Christand army, alongside Selene-sama. Another delight to look forward to, Battalion Commander."*

*"……I was a fool to ask you."*

That eye of his was forever clouded.

— Come to think of it, it was around then, after he'd made battalion commander. Up to that point Granmeld had done as he pleased, but troublesome characters began multiplying around him. Nozan had laughed and said he'd grown mellow, but it was more of a nuisance than anything, and it seemed to Granmeld that he'd grown increasingly fed up with things.

His idiot adjutant was the prime example of this. When the man had turned down the rank of corps-commander adjutant and declared he wanted to serve his sword under those beautiful princesses' banner — Granmeld had been so exasperated that, considering the man's genuine competence, he'd felt something almost like relief.

It was a long time ago now that he'd tossed that fool out, along with Fagran, who had apparently taken a liking to the princesses as well.

"Welcome, Krische-sama — you've come a long way."

"It's been some time, Piyo-piyo. I've brought Woof-woof and Rosine with me."

Again Rosine gave a small shudder and glanced at Granmeld.

This was always her reaction whenever she heard the name *Woof-woof*. He found it thoroughly tiresome.

Nozan's estate was not far. Both of them had set up residence near the castle of the former Elsren imperial capital, Renaria. The castle itself was too grand for a single noble and he didn't live in it, using it only for administrative duties and meetings.

"Yes, all preparations are in order. Granmeld — you should stay the night for once. It seems a pity to have Rosine drag you home when you're drunk."

"I'll take you up on that."

*Piyo-piyo. Woof-woof.*

A newly appointed servant, who had evidently not been here long, looked back and forth between them and Nozan in bewilderment, then settled his gaze on Krische. Getting used to being looked at like that — *that* was doubly tiresome.

"Ah, Piyo-piyo — this is a letter from Kreschenta, by the way. Apparently there are some things she'd like you to handle on your end separately from Selene, so it's all written out."

"……Duly noted."

Without any visible reaction to being called Piyo-piyo, the Supreme Commander of the Eastern Army — magnificent mustache, magnificent dress uniform — received it with a reverent bow.

Dropping the titles of queen and marshal without a second thought, calling his subordinates by ridiculous pet names — and not a shred of malice anywhere in it. The man was thoroughly incorrigible.

That she hadn't come in an apron dress was already a point in her favor.

"Enough standing in the doorway. This way, if you please, Krische-sama — I've procured fine ingredients and had my cooks give it their all, to ensure you will be well satisfied. I am confident you will enjoy it."

"Ehehe, yes. ……Piyo-piyo's house always has such delicious food, so I was looking forward to it a little."

— Just as Bogan Christand had called her — something that transcended even Nozan, a prodigy in a class of her own.

Granmeld had never sparred with her himself, but her ability became known quickly. Fagran had been utterly trounced, by all accounts, and within a year even Kolkis, the kingdom's finest spearman, could no longer hold his own in sparring.

Sparring without either side injuring the other — it wasn't the same as actual combat. The conditions were unfavorable for someone like Granmeld, who specialized in fighting in heavy armor with heavy weapons — but even so, when your opponent was a child barely reaching your hip, that was no excuse.

Once you saw her on the actual battlefield, you understood without needing to be told.

The sight of her cutting through the rear of the Elsren army formed up across the river.

Granmeld had found his eyes seized by it once more.

Far removed from the Fourth Corps and Selene's unit alike, cutting into the battle line alone — it was the opposite of anything wild. Her form was almost invisible, yet only the commanding heads were sinking into the battle line one after another.

An efficient slaughter conducted for the sole purpose of shattering morale, with minimum effort. No triumphant cry, no puffed-up pride — an impossible solo assault into the center of the enemy host.

There was no second person in the world who could do such a thing so calmly.

The enemy couldn't tell what was happening as their units collapsed one after another, and by the time Granmeld had crossed the river, the battle line had ceased to be a battle line.

She wasn't enjoying the killing. Nor did she desire it.

She simply carried out her work — persistently, meticulously, without emotion.

With a single curved sword that would barely serve for self-defense.

In a sense, Granmeld — who enjoyed *fighting as combat* — still saw the humans across from him as human.

To this girl, all of that was simply weeding.

Come to think of it, Gallen had that quality too — a pure soldier in the same vein — but she was that, distilled more purely still. That was what she was.

From her very first battle, Krische Christand had been a complete soldier.

And a complete soldier was no longer human.

Compared to her, he himself was a thoroughly ordinary person.

"Ehehe, Woof-woof is just like Gururun. All you've been eating is meat."

"……I take some offense at being compared to a jade tiger."

Passing along the light news from the kingdom's center that Selene had told him, taking occasional sips of something sweet as wine — Krische, cheeks flushed, was contentedly working her way through the abundance of delicacies spread across the table.

Alberinea, the living legend — a peerless hero of an era.

The servants who were witnessing this for the first time showed bewilderment at the sight, so utterly unlike anything that legend suggested — then quickly grew used to it and fussed over her as if she were some distinguished young lady.

Apparently she thanked them punctiliously every time her glass was refilled or her food was served, and they found her amusing.

The atmosphere at the meal was bright and cheerful.

Peaceful.

These servants couldn't imagine that this beautiful and endearing young girl was, in truth, a monster who merely appeared as she did — one who could calmly cut down hundreds of people with the same smile she wore now.

The face that smiled while offering thanks was unchanged from the face that disemboweled enemies.

*The ends justify any means — give everything to the goal.* A great many people uttered such things with grand airs, but a person who could actually *do* it — in this world there was only this girl.

Heedless of others' eyes, wholly and entirely in pursuit of her goal alone — pure.

She would doubtless kill every last person in the world for the sake of what she wanted, and still be smiling.

That was what purity meant. That was what madness was.

Human beings are inherently full of impurities; however much you might yearn for pure simplicity, you cannot strip it away.

By some strange fate this girl's purity had been eroded — but fundamentally insane, that much had not changed.

Compared to that, seeing her eat with apparent enjoyment at a fine dinner was far stranger than seeing himself at one — though most people probably hadn't noticed.

A beast that was freer than anyone, yet chose of its own accord to enter the human cage.

To Granmeld, it felt like nothing but a contradiction.

When the meal ended, Krische and Rosine were called away by Nozan's wives, and the men made their way to his study.

"……What was in the letter?"

"The usual list. Regions with suspicious irregularities in weapons circulation, or where reports may have been falsified. ……Her Majesty the Queen holds the economics of the entire continent in her head."

Periodic reports on the finances of each administered territory were sent to Albernaria each season. From that vast pool of information, Queen Kreschenta scrutinized it to a pathological degree of detail, identified the *unnatural flows*, and sent them to Nozan and the others as lists — dispatched through Krische or her subordinates, the former Black Flag Special Service members.

The reason Krische had come here was not merely what she'd said — to relay Selene's message about military preparations and deliver the Queen's letter. That was literally true, but not the whole of it.

She had come to expose someone — working alongside her subordinates stationed in the east.

"It seems Krische-sama will be working in Bilsinsarne, to the northeast. Bring the persons apprehended to the kingdom's center and process them cleanly without causing a disturbance — that is the instruction. ……I don't know how to thank the two of them."

"That place they said smelled off?"

"Yes. She already had it pinpointed, apparently."

*The Kuro-Fuyo Squad that resolves all manner of things with a pop and a thud* — informally known as *Kuro-Shutan*. (T/N: The unit name is a portmanteau combining くろふよ隊 *Kuro-Fuyo Squad* and ぱぱっしゅたんっ *pop-thud*, making *Kuroshutan*. A deliberately absurd nickname.)

A name that made everyone groan with its utter lack of gravitas — yet the organization itself was an extremely sophisticated special operations unit. They pre-empted revolts across every region, carried out arrests in an instant, and after the war spread out across the continent, alternating between intelligence-gathering and more arrests.

Apparently they carried transmitters with a classified formula that allowed them to communicate across any distance, so that orders could be issued from one end of the continent to the other without any time lag.

It was an invention that, if militarized, would have transformed the battlefield itself — but that was always the way with *Alberinea's inventions*. From the Five-Nations War to the continental unification, the time between opening battle and final peace — taken in total — was less than two years.

Granmeld didn't ask for details, given the sensitive nature of it, but that was only a fragment of the whole. She must have produced any number of other tools for intelligence work.

And on top of that, the Black Flag Special Service was — to put it plainly — a congregation of devotees of Krische Christand: a mana-user unit honed on the battlefield. They were well accustomed to rough work; capturing a noble or two was trivial, and if that girl herself deployed in support, there was not a person alive who could escape.

This was the secret of how the vast territory of the continent — most of it pacified — remained without any notable revolt. Anyone who began to move toward rebellion had their scruff seized before they'd even started. Those on the other side must have been incredulous.

Even the far reaches of the continent, which by rights should have been beyond any king's eye, were watched no differently than the capital.

The common people revered Queen Kreschenta as a compassionate champion of the weak, full of love for her subjects — but the truth of it was an absolutely surveilled society, and none of them seemed to suspect it.

In any case, Granmeld, who had been hoping for some fighting, was left somewhat deflated. The revolts he'd been certain would come never materialized, and the world was rapidly moving toward peace.

"I thought we'd stabilized Elsren to a reasonable degree, but there's still work to be done. Things will get a little busier again. Though it won't require you to move personally."

"A shame. I thought it might kill some time."

"Rosine will be pleased. She's a good wife. Enjoy your daily life to the fullest."

"……What exactly am I supposed to enjoy."

Granmeld said it with an air of resignation.

"I'm not like you, a ladies' man with a houseful of wives. Every day is the same as the last."

Nozan gave a wry laugh.

One wife, three concubines — Nozan was thoroughly aristocratic. His looks matched, and then there was his refinement, his military accomplishments, and the ability to back it all up, so that his standing in social circles was like that of a prince on a white horse. In his bachelor days, women with shining eyes had flocked to him merely by virtue of his presence.

Perhaps it also had political meaning — Bogan, who had married a daughter of impoverished nobility and taken no concubines, had needed a connection between the north and the prominent families of the center.

Even so, Granmeld — who had agonized over whether to take even one wife — couldn't quite fathom it.

"Don't say it like that. If anything, I envy you. ……In the old days they used to call me *Nozan-sama* and press in close, but now I'm the one who feels left out. I'm glad the wives get along, but once the children came along they haven't spared a glance for me."

"That's a fine problem to have."

He said it and then raised the wine to his lips, reading the label on the bottle. Wines from Elsren tended to be better than those from Alberan.

"You should be a bit more honest with yourself. ……Surely it's not unpleasant to have a good woman like Rosine whisper sweet words to you?"

"I agree she's a good wife, but her taste in men is catastrophically poor. I'm uncomfortable every single day."

"Ha ha ha — I've thought so for a while, but honestly, you're a more serious man than your face suggests."

"……What?"

*Too earnest, perhaps*, Nozan laughed, and raised his wine.

"Rather than thinking she came to you on her own, I'd wager you've decided in your own way that you want to make Rosine happy. That's quite kind."

When Granmeld glared at him, his expression softened — *I'm not mocking you*, he said.

"Coarse and rough-edged as you like to appear, you're actually a man who dislikes ingratitude and lives with honesty. And I like that about you. ……And Rosine, too — I suspect she saw that if it were you, you would make her happy."

*Even I was surprised*, he said, and looked toward the wall of the room.

Beyond that decorated wall somewhere, Rosine was also there.

"The sort of woman who can find your virtues — not easily apparent in casual acquaintance — is not the sort you find often in this world. Accept your good fortune honestly, Granmeld. ……The uneventful days you have now——"

And then, with obvious delight, as if teasing:

"……are the *Varkus* you've finally found."

* * *

Sprawled on the sofa in the guest room with a glass of wine, the door opened.

"Oh my — you're back early. Fancy finding you in your room already."

Her cheeks had gone quite red — she'd apparently been rather thoroughly plied with drink.

Her words were clear enough, but her feet were a little unsteady.

He rubbed the back of his head, stood up, walked to the doorway, scooped her up, and deposited her on the sofa. She thanked him with pleased embarrassment and took hold of his arm.

"Heh heh, I'm sorry. The wine was so good, I'm afraid I drank rather too much."

"Sounds like you had a fine time."

"And what's that face for, dear? You look more severe than usual."

She patted at his cheek — a drunk in every sense of the word. *Mind your own business*, he said, and she gave a little laugh and pressed her cheek to Granmeld's thick arm.

"Oh, honestly — Fiere-sama and the others kept asking me all about our life together. *Terrible* — 'if you want to stay quiet, you'll drink a cup first.'"

"And you walked straight into it."

"Yes, but then they seemed to lose interest and moved on to Alberinea. I've thought as much before, but……she really is an adorable lady, isn't she. I know it may be impertinent to say so of someone so far above us — even of Alberinea."

"Only someone who doesn't know her on the battlefield thinks that."

He said it without any attempt to soften it.

"She looks like a child playing with butterflies in a flower meadow — but she kills enemies like stepping on insects and dispatches enemy generals like kicking away a pebble. A monster beyond anything I could match."

"……I wouldn't say she seems like that to me."

"Small mercy. Well — it's unlikely you'll ever see it."

Rosine watched him steadily from below, chestnut hair swaying, and smiled quietly.

"Even so, that alone is not the whole truth. ……I don't think there's anything wrong with seeing that lady as a child who plays with butterflies in a flower meadow — if that is what she appears to be. At least, no one who has met her in everyday life speaks ill of her, do they? She's someone who talks happily about her garden flowers blooming, and what she's been cooking."

Then she put a hand to his cheek.

"The husband I love is not the great-wolf Granmeld Varkus who won such glorious feats on the battlefield either — he's the kind husband who makes a puzzled face when Alberinea calls him *Woof-woof.*"

She turned her wine-moistened eyes on him, full of fondness.

"……Drunk people should go to sleep."

"Heh heh, I beg your pardon."

*Giggle, giggle, giggle* — Rosine laughed on, contentedly.

Usually she nagged at him and it was tiresome; drunk, she was tiresome in a different way.

"I was asked all sorts of prying questions about why I fell in love with you, and that's what came to mind. I gave you such trouble as a child, always making demands, and you'd go along with it even while looking thoroughly fed up — "

"……Your father had looked after me. I don't have any particular fondness for children."

"And when I said I wanted to marry you as a child, and even when I actually asked you to take me as your wife, that same fed-up look — ……but whether you like children or not, the fact is that you're a kind person, dear husband."

"Yes……all right, yes. What good luck to have such a kind husband."

Worn out from dealing with her, he scooped her up again, and Rosine laughed her happy laugh. She put her arms around his neck like a child and was carried meekly to the bed.

He set her down without ceremony, she took off her shoes and shook her shoulders looking up at him.

"Dear — heh heh — are you blushing?"

"……Does it look that way?"

"It does."

"I'm exasperated with you."

Her usually composed face had loosened into something she found, apparently, quite entertaining. Rosine narrowed her eyes.

He roughly tousled her hair, then shoved her bodily into the bed and pressed her head down onto the pillow.

*Go to sleep*, he told her. *Yes*, she answered, and Granmeld sighed and returned to the sofa.

He poured the wine and looked at the bed. Rosine was still watching him steadily, smiling —

"Good night, dear."

"All right, hurry up and sleep."

He said it back and she broke into another laugh, and at last the little drunk closed her eyes.

He watched it, breathed out, drained the wine, and sank into the sofa.

When had he started being swept about by one thing after another like this?

He'd been searching for freedom, and at some point he'd found himself tied down to something.

With every year that passed, the troublesome things multiplied.

*"——It's a word from the old tongue of the north. The meaning is said to be something like 'group,' but the real nuance is more like……family, I'd say. Kin."*

*"……What?"*

*"……Heh heh — for a lone wolf with no weakness, it's rather an apt name, don't you think?"*

— *A fitting name precisely because you know nothing of it.*

What meaning had the petty villain Klaze put into those words?

It was surely something petty-villain-ishly — human — and pathetically simple.

Even Granmeld, who was supposed to have found freedom, had ended up before he knew it inside the cage of those words — a truly indescribable irony.

He stood to find himself something to snack on and stepped out of the room —

"Ah, Woof-woof."

— and met a girl in the corridor, apparently fresh from her bath.

Whether from the drink or from lingering heat, her cheeks were flushed; guided by a servant, she looked drowsy, swaying her eyes slowly up at him.

"Was Rosine all right? She seemed to drink quite a lot."

"……Exactly as you say. She's already put to bed. A considerable drunk."

He said it with the air of someone put-upon, and Alberinea-sama suddenly turned serious. She pointed at him with an authoritative finger and began:

"That's right — Rosine told me all sorts of things, you know. Apparently Woof-woof scatters his laundry all over the place, leaves work half-done to go drinking, and those muddy boots on the carpet——"

She was the second drunk he'd run into.

Like a self-appointed supervisor of some kind — she droned on and on, going through Granmeld's usual transgressions in no particular order. The servant who'd been seeing her to her room was left at a loss, hovering uncertainly.

Granmeld had the headache of headaches.

On top of it all, she was apparently beginning to lose track of what she was saying, and Alberinea-sama's gracious sermon started looping back on itself. He was subjected to nodding along for nearly half a quarter-hour.

"In any case — staying home and relaxing is one thing, but you mustn't give Rosine too much trouble. Woof-woof is a Woof-woof, but from others' perspective you're a very distinguished noble Woof-woof, so you must be a model for other Woof-wooks — no, for the people around you."

"Yes, yes……understood. I'll take it to heart. ……May I go now?"

"……If you understand, then fine. Well — Krische doesn't intend to lecture for too long anyway."

At those words he rubbed the back of his head. So this was finally winding up. He breathed out.

It was plenty long as it was, and *Woof-woof, Woof-woof* repeated endlessly throughout — tiresome hardly covered it.

He thought about pointing out what authority this girl had to lecture anyone about nobility, but kept it to himself.

There was no point arguing with a drunk.

"But ……ehehe — Rosine said Woof-woof is a very kind and wonderful husband and she's very happy. That's a very good thing."

"……Well, thank you."

"Now that there's no more war and we have peace, Woof-woof should take better care of his family and make Rosine happy."

She'd apparently said everything she wanted to say, because she nodded *yes, yes* with satisfied completeness.

*Krische is up early tomorrow so I'm going to sleep*, she said drowsily, and went into her room.

What a thoroughly selfish creature she was.

The greatest monster on any battlefield, in daily life she was nothing but an inconvenient overgrown child. Freer than anyone — yet she chose to walk into the cage of human society, and even concerned herself with someone's wife enough to lecture them about it.

What a strange and unusual monster, he thought — or perhaps, he thought next, he himself wasn't so different.

In the end he too was a fool who'd thrown away the freedom of a stray dog and walked into a cage of his own accord.

His appetite for snacking gone, he went back to the room, took off his shoes, and slid into the bed.

Rosine, still asleep, pressed happily against him and laid her head on his arm.

Listening to that breathing, Granmeld heaved a sigh in response.

The powerful arms that had silenced every opponent on the field of battle — they had come, at this point, to serve as a pillow.

For all that he'd been searching for freedom, he was unfree in every conceivable direction.

If the younger version of himself — the stray dog — could see him now, what would he say?

If Klaze could see him — what would he think?

*"——Starting today, this is your home. And starting today, I'm your big brother."*

He grew sick of thinking about it.

*"……Welcome to the Varkus, Granmeld."*

And then — without meaning to, a laugh escaped him.

# Chapter 277: Water Rabbit

*Narrator: Kalua — Sword Instructor, Usachan School*

Hm? Can you run on water if you try hard enough?

You can, you can. I've seen it myself.

You know who I mean, right — the one in the apron dress who comes by sometimes? Usachan?

It was a while back now……the Dismantling War? The Unification War? Either one, doesn't matter. We were chasing down an important enemy general, but he managed to get onto a river boat and escape.

It was this incredibly, *incredibly* vast — yeah, you could almost see the horizon, that kind of wide river.

Huh? You don't believe me? No, no, it's really true — there really is a river that big, I promise.

I basically don't lie, so.

What's with that look, Mia? I'm playing with the children, go find somewhere else to be. You've got paperwork, right?

Anyway — imagine a place like that.

By the time I and Usachan got there, the boat had already gotten quite far out, so we were talking about what to do — but Usachan said it was fine.

She hopped hop-hop-hop across the river just like a real rabbit.

It's not a lie, I'm serious. Trust your big sister.

Who's that? Who has a complaint for big sister? Bad children who are rude to Teacher Kalua will have their bottoms spanked.

Anyway — the conclusion is that if you try hard enough you can do it.

Next time Usachan comes, ask her about it. I think she'll say the same thing.

If you practice properly like Usachan does, humans can run on water too.

So first things first — stop complaining and swing your sword.

The path of the Usachan school is a steep one.

If you're grumbling at this level, you'll never master the hidden technique *Usachan Jump.*

Right — Usachan Jump is the secret ultimate technique of the Usachan school. The path to it is very, very steep.

Oh, but this is just between us. ……Don't go telling the other children, all right?

Usachan Jump is *so* incredibly difficult that actually — of all of us — the only one who's managed to master the ultimate secret technique, Usachan Jump, is……Mia——

*Narrator: Boz — Former Ferryman*

——You know this, but these parts were back then a piece of a big country called Elsren.

They'd gone to war with the Alberan of today.

The business of the high and mighty — nothing to do with us small folk down below.

We were just hoping not to get swept up in it, hoping the taxes wouldn't go up — that was the conversation you heard, chatting with the folks from the nearby village, while I rowed boats on this great wide Selshe River with my sons.

War was something that happened in some faraway world.

The main bridges were far to the north and south, out-of-the-way parts where outsiders didn't come, so the people who used the crossing were always the same. Listening to news like *oh, so-and-so in that village had a baby* — peaceful, easygoing.

Our ruler was the local lord more than Elsren itself, and that lord was a gentle, unhurried sort of man, so things went along smoothly.

Not many people, mostly small fields and forest foraging. Conscription would have finished the village off, so around here they were satisfied if you just sent them the crops. A good lord who took the common people's feelings to heart.

He even came out in person to our little out-of-the-way village — apologized for the trouble, and asked if we could spare a little of the harvest.

He passed away some time after the war ended, but his eldest son was a good man just like him. Even now, he's governing this territory — ah, no, what do they call it now, a *managed territory* or something? Well, I don't quite understand it, but he's still governing around here.

None of us can thank that man enough.

Ah, sorry. Old people have a bad habit of going off on tangents.

Well — things on our end were that peaceful, but by bad luck the fighting drew rather close to our area.

One day, five people came to me on horseback. All of them in fine armor, probably nobility of some sort.

When they spotted me tending to the boat, they shouted at me to get it out — urgent, with an intensity that put the fear in me.

That was frightening, I can tell you. I didn't just hesitate — if I'd dallied even a moment, I thought I might be killed on the spot. That kind of atmosphere.

What you might call the soldier's air — every one of them had eyes sharp as a beast's, and I felt like my heart was seized in a fist.

I said hurriedly that I couldn't take horses, and they said *we know, be quick about it* — dismounted, looking ready to draw their swords any moment. The eight-man boat — well, the weight wasn't a problem even with armor, so I quickly got the men on board and cast off before any further objections could be raised.

No sooner had we left the bank than several dozen cavalry appeared from the forest path.

These men had been chasing them.

The newcomers hurled abuse — *cowards, running away* — and glared with frustrated expressions.

*"Fear not. No arrows will fly. On the ferryboat, bloodshed is forbidden by convention."*

One man in particularly fine armor said this to me with a laugh, and it calmed me a little. He was clearly a man of great rank — a general, perhaps. The other four visibly eased up.

"……Having kept this sword at Your Majesty's side — never once drawn, never receiving a single scratch. That is my greatest pride. ……I swear by its name — I have not treated it in any manner unworthy of that name."

*Would you allow me*, the old soldier said.

*Wipe, wipe, wipe* — polished with care, going over every detail.

Into the fine decorative details, into the gaps of the carved work.

The drawn blade was lustrous — not a single cloud on the blue blade.

She had been mildly anxious that Anne might have splashed bucket-water on it, but not a trace of rust.

Why Kreschenta needed to be concerned about such a thing she could not say.

It was truly pointless work.

Sealing it away completely with magic — *pop* — entirely protecting it would be simple.

But the red-haired woman had inexplicably declared that maintaining it by hand was best, and Onee-sama, with the face of someone who understood completely, had agreed with her.

The result was that in this house, such pointless work had become a daily occurrence.

This house was nothing but pointlessness.

A world lacking all rationality.

The red-haired women and Onee-sama spending time on pointless work.

Selene repeating her pointless crafting.

Lila who insisted on doting on the lizard, included, nothing but pointlessness.

Kreschenta was the mistress of this world, a god.

What business had she, so very important, carrying a cloth and cleaning?

The question arose constantly, but thinking about it now was beside the point.

That was simply *how things were*. Nothing to be done about it.

*Wipe, wipe, wipe* — caressing the blade, checking the base.

She'd recently taken the whole thing apart and maintained it, so the area around the tang was also fine.

The next time she took it apart to maintain would do.

"My, perfectly polished."

The blade with its newly heightened gleam was being examined when, hands apparently idle, the red-haired woman approached.

The red-haired woman's domain had been unnecessary vases, ornaments, and shelves — but her work was as slovenly as ever. The red-haired woman's work was summarized in one word: *glossing over* — tidying up only the visible parts to look like she'd done the work, then finishing. The red-haired woman was a person who never aspired beyond eighty percent.

A person without ambition.

The whole point was that it was this very red-haired woman who'd said doing it by hand was better — and this woman would only work properly when it came to the cooking and tea she loved.

Wanting to say one or two things was natural.

"Woof!"

This was the natural duty to do, being so well treated.

She held out the sword as a demonstration, and the red-haired woman stroked her head and suppressed a smile.

"Having Kreschenta-sama maintain it so carefully, heh heh — Kirik-sama must be very pleased."

The red-haired woman received the sword and examined it, then slid it carefully into the scabbard.

A dim-witted red-haired woman, and yet another dim-witted utterance.

Kirik had been dead for a very long time now, and was neither pleased, nor capable of unnecessary speech.

And this sword was Kreschenta's property.

Keeping it clean had nothing whatsoever to do with that.

*'I'm afraid that as queen, I cannot accept a reward once given back from a retainer.'*

*'That is……'*

*'So — could you allow me to receive this as……a personal gift to me, from Kirik-sama?'*

The red-haired woman was cheerful, as if Kreschenta were some kind of fellow dim-witted companion.

When she glared in displeasure, the red-haired woman's shoulders shook, and she smiled.

*'If that would suit you — I would place it in the entryway of this house and care for it with great pride. ……As the beloved sword of a warrior — the one I trust most after onee-sama.'*

*'Your Majesty the Queen……'*

*'……This is a sword beyond purchase by a million gold coins — the one and only treasure.'*

It had only happened that way because of how the conversation went — a small reward for all those years of service, and a bit of lip service.

The sword was a sword, a fine piece, worth perhaps fifty gold coins at most.

Since it was being offered for free, accepting it wasn't bad.

That was all it was, nothing more and nothing less.

The red-haired woman handed the sword back to Kreschenta.

She checked it for fingerprints, pursed her lips, and hung it on the stand.

Then — *woof* — she wrapped her arms around that red-haired neck and bounced up and down on her toes.

Taking the mount (physical), she clung on.

"Shall we take a little nap, Kreschenta-sama?"

She rubbed cheeks in assent, and the red-haired woman headed upstairs.

Looking back over the red-haired woman's shoulder, the entryway had unnecessary, inconvenient ornaments displayed in all directions.

This house was nothing but pointlessness.

A world lacking all rationality.

On the stand was Selene's rubbish. A nuisance.

Next to it, more of Selene's rubbish. A nuisance.

Inconvenient ornaments lined up in a row —

*'……How does it look? Kirik-sama.'*

And Kreschenta's sword — unused, taking up space — sat enthroned in the center.

*'Yes. ……There can be no greater honor — than this……where in this world could anything else be so——'*

Polished decorations, gleaming. The ornamental sword that once hung there. Not a scratch, not a cloud.

*'Heh heh — Kirik-sama doesn't know, does he.'*

An old soldier who had always maintained it with care — an old soldier who had presented it with both hands, unable to lift his face, the gleaming helmet floating to mind.

*'……Having such a sword *given to one* is, by far, the greater honor.'*

What had been received, though merely by the flow of things — that too was the same: Kreschenta's nuisance ornament.

——To be a nuisance, forever more.
