# Chapter 121: The Two Faces of the Golden Mean

The royal palace reception room.

Within it sat an elderly man of large build.

His hair, swept back, and his long beard had both gone completely white, and deep wrinkles were carved into his face.

Even so, his narrow eyes held a certain sharpness, and something that could only be called an imposing presence seemed to rise from the old man's body.

Seated directly across from him was a woman with reddish-gold hair — Queen Kreschenta, dressed in a pale blue gown.

Beside her, Krische, having removed her cloak, sat primly in her one-piece dress.

*There is one matter I would like onee-sama to attend before your pleasant trip.*

The day before her departure, Krische had been invited by Kreschenta and found herself in this room.

The one who had requested Krische's presence was the old man before her — Felwors Keesliton.

A hero of the kingdom who had once won great renown — and an opponent they had once crossed blades with.

"You look well, Your Majesty. And you as well, Krische-sama."

"Yes, Duke Keesliton. You look well yourself. What brings you here today?"

— A traitor who had turned his blade against the princess.

However, Kreschenta had determined that he had been coerced into taking the field because Gildanstein had taken his kin hostage, and rather than execution, she had ordered house arrest.

She felt it would be a waste to kill a man who still held influence within the military and enjoyed great renown.

His skill as a general was proven, and he had held the rank of Marshal before his retirement from the military.

With the kingdom having lost many of its commanders, his return was desirable.

A letter had arrived expressing his wish to speak with Kreschenta, with Krische present, and Kreschenta had been uncertain how to handle it. But upon hearing that Krische would be leaving the capital, she agreed.

Part of the reason was her desire to carry out the "Show Onee-sama How Cool I Am as Queen Operation" to raise Krische's opinion of her before the trip, but deliberating any longer would accomplish nothing.

Better to decide how to deal with this man sooner rather than later.

If he would obediently become her pawn, all well and good. If not, that would be the end of it.

She would kill him eventually.

That was all there was to it.

"First, allow me to express my gratitude for the efforts made to spare my life."

"Think nothing of it. A general who has served the kingdom longer than any other — I felt it would be a pity to lose such a life over something like this. You simply stood by your own convictions……it is merely a shame that those convictions were not on my side."

Kreschenta lowered her eyes, wearing the face of a princess.

Krische, for her part, wanted nothing more than to go home and attend to the estate, but sensing this would be a lengthy conversation, she reached for a cookie.

She had faced Felwors as an enemy on the battlefield, but now that it was over, the man was entirely irrelevant to Krische.

Having heard how the battle against Nozan had played out, she considered him a reasonably capable general, but that was all.

If he became hostile, she would kill him. If he became an ally, she simply wanted him to do his job. She had no personal interest in the man whatsoever.

Felwors smiled watching Krische, and Kreschenta spoke.

"But that is all in the past. Let us put the matter to rest for now. I would prefer it if you spoke to me as you did before, without restraint."

From there, for a while, it was something like idle conversation.

Kreschenta raised topics about Felwors's relatives, his health — surface-level pleasantries that did not pry too deeply.

Kreschenta had memorized everything about him — from his family composition and servants to the names of his dogs and the number of his horses.

Creating conversation from that was easy enough, and she repeated questions that were simple to answer, aiming to put him at ease.

She could not have him forget that he had turned his blade against her. But if she was going to make him her vassal from here on, she could not have him feeling too much guilt over it either.

Men like Felwors — warriors — were creatures who placed greater weight on appearances than on personal gain.

If she made him feel too keenly that he was a traitor, there was a risk he would become excessively self-punishing and voluntarily seek punishment, such as returning his noble title.

Naturally, no benefit would come from that. It would only result in losing one capable individual.

There was no point in that.

For now, she would loosen his tension with suitable small talk, ease his sense of guilt, and then move to the main topic — so Kreschenta judged.

That said, what concerned her was Krische beside her, who had taken on the look of a bored cookie-cracking puppet.

Watching that, Kreschenta was seized by the anxiety that this conversation, rather than raising Krische's opinion of her, might actually be lowering it. She cut the chat short at an appropriate point and moved to the main topic.

She ended her questions, allowed a brief silence to form, and subtly prompted Felwors. The old man began by saying, first, the matter of why he had sided with His Highness the Prince.

"……I watched His Highness grow from childhood. I taught him the sword, I taught him tactics……rather than conviction, perhaps it would be better called something like a father's heart."

"A father's heart?"

"Originally, I intended to observe this civil war from the sidelines. I had long since retired after His Majesty, the late King Albaza, whom I served, passed away."

Albaza was Kreschenta's grandfather.

Felwors was a general who had fought alongside the brave King Albaza, and had withdrawn from the front lines some time after the change of reign.

"I am more aware of the circumstances than most. His Highness had his reasons, and Your Majesty has yours — which is right, which holds the greater cause. But such things are trivial……in the end, it is always the victor who becomes righteous. Let me say this first — I have no interest in the truth. ……His Highness was the usurper, and Your Majesty is the just new sovereign who defeated him — that is sufficient."

He was implying, between the lines, that the true usurper was Kreschenta.

But Kreschenta listened to his words without flinching, and Felwors smiled quietly.

"His Highness understood as well, I think. He did not try to move me with just cause. He simply said one thing — that he wanted my aid in a battle that would stake his very existence. That he wished to hold a battle to decide, through pure strength alone, who was fit to be king."

*'Whether this worthless life of mine has a future or not……consider it a gamble of sorts. But I lack the hands to play it. I need you, Felwors. — Come with me.'*

"It was not that I saw justice in His Highness, nor that I held any grievance against Your Majesty……it was simply obligation, and the father's heart of one who had watched over everything until then. Turning my blade was a matter of sentiment, nothing more. And so, I lent His Highness my aid."

Felwors smiled sadly and turned his eyes to Krische.

Krische furrowed her brow and tilted her head, and Felwors asked.

"How did His Highness meet his end?"

"……? Krische cut off his head and killed him. After running from Krische all that time, Krische thought he would surrender at the very end, but he was a strange person."

He was still smiling even after he died, Krische reported, sounding as if it barely mattered to her.

Kreschenta looked at Felwors, her sister's words tinged with revulsion toward Gildanstein, but the old man simply nodded with the same smile.

"Then that is well. He must have found satisfaction in his end."

With those words, silence fell for a time.

Kreschenta pondered in what direction she should make him her pawn, while Krische cursed the misfortune of being dragged into this incomprehensible meeting and pouted.

Watching the two of them — one nibbling cookies, the other silently brooding — Felwors spoke.

"Um, if the discussion is finished, Krische would like to go back to the estate and help Bery."

"O-onee-sama, please wait a moment. Just a little longer……"

As Krische rose to leave, Kreschenta hastily held her back. Felwors, somewhat surprised by Kreschenta's manner, asked.

"Bery?"

"A servant of the Christand household. ……Krische was supposed to do a thorough cleaning of the estate with Bery today, but Kreschenta called Krische here instead."

Krische glared at Felwors with clear dissatisfaction, and the old man laughed.

"Ha ha, the Alberinea herself, second only to the Marshal, doing a thorough cleaning with a servant?"

"As far as Krische is concerned, that other business is the extra. Krische only joined the military so that no one would interrupt the time Krische spends cooking and cleaning with Bery at the estate."

Krische declared it plainly.

Felwors regarded Krische for a while, and something like understanding settled across his face.

"I see, an extra."

"That's right. ……Are you done asking Krische things? Krische heard that Duke Keesliton was the one who requested Krische's presence."

"……Indeed. There was something I wished to ask."

Though it seems I have already half-heard the answer, he said with a wry smile.

"I wished to ask what you fight for. For what purpose you take up the sword and stand on the battlefield — what manner of person you are. That is what I wished to know, and so I included Krische-sama's name in the letter."

"……Krische goes to the battlefield for Selene and Kreschenta. Krische doesn't want to go to the battlefield at all, and if the two of them were fine with it, Krische would like to live quietly in some mountain retreat somewhere, far away from all of that."

Krische folded her arms, visibly displeased.

In Krische's short life, the worst human being she could name was Gildanstein.

She disliked him enough that she did not even want to recall him, and found him unpleasant enough that she wished she had tormented him more before killing him.

She did not care about being on opposing sides as soldiers; she bore no grudge against Felwors on that account. But the way Felwors seemed to mourn Gildanstein was unpleasant.

Gildanstein was, in Krische's mind, someone who deserved to die — someone she had resolved to kill without fail.

For what reason would one keep bringing up such talk in Krische's presence —

"Krische doesn't understand the aesthetics of justice and causes that you people talk about, and Krische has no interest in your relationships or the dead Prince. ……He killed the Master, and made everyone sad. As far as Krische is concerned, he was an eyesore and an annoyance — someone who deserved to die."

Krische turned inorganic violet eyes toward Felwors.

Kreschenta tried to interject in a fluster, but Krische continued.

"That's why Krische killed him. If you don't like that, then say so plainly, and draw your sword. If you do, Krische will kill you the same way, right away."

Felwors met those frozen eyes and gave a wry smile.

Then he sipped his tea and shook his head.

"……If I have given offense, I apologize. Indeed, from Krische-sama's perspective, he was the enemy who took someone dear — my consideration was sorely lacking."

However, Felwors continued with a faint smile.

"An old man like me has no life worth clinging to. A threat is meaningless against one who does not fear it — that is something worth remembering."

"Whether it's meaningless or not is for Krische to decide. ……Making it so you can never speak again would at least make Krische feel better. It's simply a matter of whether it's tolerable or not."

When she said this with obvious displeasure, Felwors laughed, seemingly amused.

"Ha ha, I see. There is that way of thinking as well. Simple and good. Perhaps I am merely overcomplicating simple matters……"

What about this conversation could possibly be enjoyable was incomprehensible to Krische.

Kreschenta, finding herself unable to get a word in edgewise, looked between Krische and Felwors, fidgeting as though she wanted to say something.

Krische sighed at the restless Kreschenta, picked up a cookie, and pressed it into her mouth. Kreschenta chewed reflexively.

"I beg your pardon. I bear no grudge against Krische-sama, nor am I blaming you for killing His Highness. If anything, I am grateful."

"……Grateful?"

"Yes. After exhausting every stratagem, a final duel to settle it — that would be the most honorable end any warrior could wish for. For His Highness, who cursed being born into the royal house, that death was the greatest salvation of all. Of course, from Krische-sama's perspective, he was an object of hatred……that salvation must be unwelcome to you. But even so, as one who watched over His Highness from the moment he was born, I cannot help but feel it was, above all else, good."

Felwors continued.

"Nevertheless, it is true that my consideration was lacking. If you say you cannot forgive me, I would not hesitate to atone with this life."

"……You are a troublesome person. In any case, Krische hates the Prince enough that Krische would want to kill him three……no, four……nn, about five times if Krische could, so please have that sort of talk somewhere Krische isn't present."

"Ha ha, I shall take that to heart."

Krische said it with dissatisfaction written plainly on her face.

"This is tiresome, so Krische will just ask. Kreschenta wishes for your return as a general. The reason she has found pretexts not to punish the officers and soldiers who sided with the Prince is precisely this — to minimize the kingdom's loss of fighting strength. Why you sided with the Prince is irrelevant now. Whether you will be an asset or not — that is all Krische wants to know."

Felwors laughed and stroked his long beard.

It was far too direct — Kreschenta, mid-cookie, looked at her sister as if to say that was hardly appropriate, but her mouth was full of cookie.

She could not physically interject.

"Irrelevant……you harbor no resentment toward me for opposing you?"

"It was simply a matter of being on different sides. The war is over; as long as you don't bring it up, Krische couldn't care less. Fortunately, Selene, General Velraigh, and Gaiko—Vice-Marshal Faren also seem to have no issue with you, so there's no particular problem. ……If you say you are grateful, then Krische thinks you should work your hardest and repay that gratitude."

Felwors studied Krische intently.

Krische, looking displeased, nibbled cookies and fed them to Kreschenta.

Whether this was a serious meeting or not was entirely unclear — the situation was chaotic — but Felwors slowly nodded.

"I suppose one should call you a forthright person. There is a certain precariousness, but nothing of the sort I feared……do you dislike war?"

"Krische dislikes it. ……There are people who enjoy fighting, but if Krische is going to waste time on something like that, Krische would rather be cooking with Bery."

"Ha ha, is that so. An old man like me has grown so accustomed to the battlefield that I felt a touch of nostalgia for it recently. In some respects, it is an alluring place, and yet……one certainly cannot call it a good place. ……Had Krische-sama been the sort of person bewitched by it, I would have had to reconsider."

Felwors smiled.

"It was just as Margrave Faren said. Again, please forgive the presumptuous questions."

"……Skeleton? Ah, you met with the Vice-Marshal?"

"Yes, the other day. ……Do you really call him Skeleton?"

"Yes, it's a pet name."

Felwors nodded with amusement, then stood and saluted.

"Having met you in person like this, I am satisfied. I shall accept that proposal. I am old and my days are numbered — I cannot promise decades of service, but if I can still be of use."

"Nn……yes, well, if you're willing to say that, then that's fine……Kreschenta, do you have anything else to say?"

"Uuu……no, I do not……"

Kreschenta, who had been subjected to an endless stream of cookies, finally swallowed the last of them and looked at Felwors.

Her dignity as queen had already been thoroughly ground to pieces along with the cookies.

Uncertain what expression to wear, she nevertheless addressed him as a queen should.

"O……orders will come from the Marshal in due course. Until then."

"Very well, understood. ……However, you two do seem to be quite close. It is a good relationship. Your face has softened compared to before."

"……I was not aware I had been making a frightening face."

"Emotions seep through — concealing everything is a difficult thing. Those who can see it will see it……but seeing you now, there is nothing to worry about."

*— Surely, the kingdom to come will be a good one.*

Felwors said this as though gazing at something distant.

* * *

"How direct of you."

The place had originally been the estate of Klare Markelus, Central General.

With the owner dead — and in a civil war defeat, at that — House Markelus had "donated" much of its assets to the kingdom and narrowly avoided dissolution, but to cover the shortfall, they had put several of their estates up for sale.

This estate in the first-class district was one of them, and it now served as the Faren household's residence, used by Elrouga.

It was the other day, when Elrouga had been hosting Felwors, who had come to visit.

What Felwors had asked him about was Krische.

"I know a fair amount about the cursed children of the royal house. I witnessed His Majesty the late King Albaza weep as he put his own child to death — an infant that did not cry."

— It was a story from some three hundred years ago.

In an era when the regional lords held power and royal authority was beginning to wane, a single infant was born who did not cry.

Named Reine — meaning "strong light" — the beautiful princess became queen at fifteen, took the powerful Duke Gasre as her consort, and with that strength, swiftly seized control of the royal court.

A few years later, the duke died under suspicious circumstances. Using the absorption of House Gasre as a foothold, she stripped power from each lord — peacefully or by force — and in a mere ten years, she reshaped the kingdom into its current form.

She then launched invasions of the surrounding nations, vastly expanding the kingdom's territory — and if one looked at her achievements alone, she could well be called a great and wise ruler.

But in stark contrast to those brilliant achievements, she carried many dishonorable rumors.

She was said to be licentious, using her beauty to form numerous adulterous liaisons.

She had used her power to mercilessly kill every vassal who defied her. It had been quietly rumored even at the time that she had taken Duke Gasre as her consort solely to seize real power — and that she was the one who killed him. She was called an abnormal person who would do anything without hesitation for her own benefit.

"To varying degrees, infants who do not cry are said to possess exceptional talent and a sensibility that differs from ordinary people. At times they leave behind great achievements; at times they disturb stability and harmony — have you studied the histories?"

"Only to the extent of skimming the texts."

"I see. You know of the split with the Holy Empire?"

"Yes. Only what is written in the books. There are few texts in the kingdom that cover that era."

Reine bore many children, and among them was born another infant who did not cry.

Named Reine as well, after the queen — and with her birth, the queen came to be known as the Great Reine, Grabareine.

The girl born to her was called the Little Reine — Elsreine.

Grabareine greatly doted on Elsreine, the most gifted of all her children, but Elsreine was unlike her vivacious mother — a quiet princess who loved to paint.

Whether for political reasons or personal ones born of her temperament, at some point the relationship between the two soured. Elsreine, along with many vassals who harbored resentment toward her mother's reign of terror, rose in rebellion.

The vast territory of the kingdom was split in two, and Elsreine would go on to create the Republic of Elsren — "the Little Paradise."

The kingdom naturally turned its army against the republic, and the war was expected to end quickly.

The republican system Elsreine had established made a parliament its governing body.

When opinions split, response would naturally be delayed — and Grabareine was a queen rich in decisive action.

There was no way she would fail to exploit that weakness, and with the military disparity as well, the republic was forced into a disadvantage.

However, as it was pressed further, the republican parliament lost its original form, and when real power was once again entrusted to Elsreine, who had led the rebellion, the republic gradually began to resist the kingdom's invasion.

Elsren spread a teaching widely based on the ideals depicted in her paintings, won the people to her side, and came to call itself the Holy Empire with her at its head — from that point, the war reached a stalemate and continued for decades afterward.

The kingdom lost much of the territory it had previously gained, and with the assassination of Queen Grabareine as the trigger, a peace was concluded between the two nations — but what remained was ravaged land and people.

From then on, in the kingdom, infants who did not cry came to be regarded as cursed children, and deemed taboo.

"……The two leaders — one of the kingdom, one of the empire — were said to be infants who did not cry. And from that, the royal house decided to kill such children as cursed. His Majesty the late King Albaza's era — I investigated the matter of the cursed children. Fortunately, I had connections with the Empire, and was able to access the records. For better or worse, it was a bitter decision made for the stability of the kingdom."

"What a load of nonsense."

Elrouga said this and wet his lips with wine.

He loved Krische, and he meant it from the bottom of his heart.

"Whether they cry or not — even if it were possible to identify them that way, where is the person who can judge the good or evil of an infant? Everyone carries both good and evil within them. If there is anyone who can declare themselves to be true goodness, I should very much like to see them."

Everyone has good sides and bad sides.

There are those who strive to be good, but true goodness exists nowhere.

The same goes for evil — a coin is one whole thing, obverse and reverse together. That was what Elrouga believed.

"Krische-sama is certainly a person who differs from others. Some would call her abnormal. But she is at least a person who tries to understand good and evil, who tries to honor what is good. She possesses a far purer heart than those who waste their time on petty scheming."

"……Hmm. You have no reservations?"

"If there is anything in this world that is free from reservations, it would be only the dead. At the very least, I intend to devote the remainder of my life to being of help to Krische-sama. I cannot say it will bring peace of mind, but if there is someone watching over her at her side, that is enough."

Felwors stroked his long beard, looking somewhat surprised at Elrouga's words.

Then he said, *that is unexpected.*

"For someone who dislikes people to go that far."

"Disliking people — that is unfair. I have always been fond of sincere people. This face of mine tends to frighten children, though……"

Elrouga smiled wryly and continued.

"Ha ha, but now Krische-sama calls me Skeleton and such. I used to be bothered by it, but when she calls me that, somehow this face of mine starts to seem not so bad after all. ……A troublesome person, and a mysterious one."

Felwors was at a loss for what to say, but Elrouga laughed.

"In any case, it would be best to meet her yourself. Whether you like or dislike her — personal feelings will differ. But she is an entertaining person. ……At the very least, she is not a bad one."

The words, delivered with that sinister face, carried no trace of a lie.

Hearing this, Felwors nodded deeply.

# Chapter 122: The Fort Belgash Civil War

Past the Dragon's Maw and into the north, it was a silver landscape.

The land was blanketed in white snow, dazzling under the sunlight that peeked through gaps in the clouds.

The snow must have come early this year.

There was a beauty that stole the eye, but it was inconvenient for traveling.

"I'm sorry for the wait."

"It's fine, it can't be helped."

They removed the carriage wheels and replaced them with sleds.

Ideally they would have held out until Fort Belgash, but once the wheels sank into the snow, there was no choice but to switch.

Kirik, Mia, and the others unloaded the luggage from the carriage and worked up a sweat swapping out the fittings.

Krische, bundled in fluffy layers with a blanket draped over her shoulders, was feeding the horses.

One particularly large horse — Bururun — crunched through the carrots he was given, then nuzzled his face against Krische.

Krische reluctantly gave him another carrot.

This horse had taken a strange liking to Krische.

She had ended up bringing him back to the royal territory with no particular plan, but since he was this attached, it was eventually decided to keep him at the Christand estate.

The main reason Selene had decided to keep him was for Krische to overcome her dislike of horses, but as expected, Krische helped care for him alongside Bery yet refused to mount him, leaving him in a state that was more or less a pet.

That said, even Krische thought it was a waste for him to be little more than an ornament, so this time he had been pressed into service pulling the carriage.

The kingdom's horses were originally breeds well-suited to draft work.

Since nobles in heavy armor often rode them, military horses were mostly heavy breeds rather than light ones, and the slender horses used by the southeastern nomads were not particularly favored.

Bururun was no exception — his build was more than sufficient for pulling a carriage, and he drew the carriage carrying Krische without any particular fuss.

"Bururun eats more than the others, doesn't he. He seems energetic, too. Doesn't he get cold?"

"I wonder……he may be more resistant to cold than people, though."

Bery smiled wryly at the bundled-up Krische.

Bery wore her usual apron dress.

In terms of cold-weather gear, she had leather gloves and boots, and aside from that, a single cloak draped over her shoulders.

The others also wore only light winter clothing; Krische was overdressed if anything, but she was apparently still cold.

Bery straightened Krische's slightly skewed scarf and stroked Bururun's neck.

"He really is such a calm boy, though. I heard he was quite unruly with Ojou-sama."

"Nn……Selene tends to be a little rough about things."

"Perhaps it's a matter of compatibility……"

The other day, Selene's own horse had been unwell, so she had mounted Bururun as a substitute, but it seemed Selene had been given a very hard time.

*What is wrong with that horse, I'll have him sold off* — she had been quite angry about something. But from what Bery could see, he did not seem like a troublesome horse at all.

Bogan's horses had changed often during his battlefield days, and having cared for them, Bery felt that horses had distinct personalities.

But from that experience, she would say Bururun, though energetic, was a good boy.

He had also immediately warmed to Bery despite his reputation as an unmanageable horse, and she had wondered whether he would be all right — but now he was completely obedient.

Very docile.

"Be nice to the others too, Bururun. No fighting with the horse next to you, understood?"

Following Bery's example, Krische patted his neck, and Bururun gave a spirited snort.

Watching this, Bery giggled.

Krische, playing the big sister to a horse, was truly childlike and endearing.

Then she looked up, checked the sun's position, and said.

"At this rate, it'll be Fort Belgash for tonight. Trying to reach Gurgain would mean arriving in total darkness……"

"That's true. Ehehe, but the kitchen there was quite impressive, so Krische should be able to do some proper cooking for the first time in a while."

* * *

— Fort Belgash.

With the war over and many soldiers having returned to their hometowns, the fort that had once overflowed with troops was now deserted and quiet.

Those who remained were the minimum garrison to defend the fort, and most of them would serve here as sentries until they completed their terms.

By now, the fort was for them less of a military installation and more of a community — like a village or town. It was home; it was their homeland.

Naturally, they all worked together, striving to make life here as good as possible, looking out for one another and enriching their daily lives.

But then — sudden chaos erupted within that tranquility.

In the kitchen, which had been strict yet genial, civil war had broken out.

"Nn, how is it?"

"Let's see……shall we try adding a little nerip?"

"Yes!"

Silver hair, elegant and bound into a ponytail that swayed like a horse's tail.

Pitter-pattering about the kitchen was the enchanting princess Krische Christand.

Having earned the title of Alberinea — second in the kingdom only to the Marshal — through her great battlefield exploits, she held not a sword but a kitchen knife, happily chopping herbs.

A clean white one-piece dress.

Its long hem fluttered as she moved, and the sight of her occasionally stretching on tiptoe to reach the upper shelves was charming in a word.

If there were such a thing as a fairy of the kitchen, surely it was this girl.

"About this much?"

"Yes, though a slightly rougher chop might be better."

Gazing upon her with a loving smile was a figure like a water sprite luring travelers.

The red-haired servant, Bery Argan.

In contrast to the adorable girl pitter-pattering about, her movements were relaxed and composed — elegant. It was a noble bearing that did no shame to her family name.

She was humble and approachable, yet the gestures she occasionally showed were refined. The swell of her ample chest beneath her petite frame, the cinch of her slender waist — those captivated by them would suddenly come to their senses and feel ashamed, such was her chasteness.

Even the sight of her sampling with a ladle had a certain allure, yet watching her whisper with the fairy carried something that seemed to dissolve reality itself.

Roasting in the oven was a whole chicken.

Its cavity was stuffed with herbs and vegetables, and the outside had been carefully rubbed with salt, pepper, garlic, and more — its skin, basted repeatedly with its own drippings, had a jewel-like sheen.

In a pot, beef simmered slowly with vegetables in a red wine stew, bubbling away.

Watching from a distance were the men of the kitchen.

On a snowy evening, just as they had finished their work and were taking a breather before cleanup — the arrivals at Fort Belgash were two princesses and soldiers in black-lacquered armor.

The snow accumulation having forced them to give up reaching Gurgain that day, they had asked the garrison for the use of the kitchen.

— Visitors more beautiful than the snow.

Head Cook Zalbak, upon receiving this report, immediately ordered the kitchen cleaned. Before they arrived, he had the section they would likely use tidied as thoroughly as possible, polished until one could lick the surfaces.

The crew was now handling the remaining cleanup, but that was merely the pretext — nearly all their attention was directed at the two small cooks.

Their culinary skill was impressive. Even these men, who cooked professionally, could learn much from them.

But more than that, their purpose was the feast for the eyes.

For men stationed at a fort with nothing but open road until the nearest town, watching these two cook was entertainment beyond compare.

"Bery, the meat is also almost……"

"Yes, the sauce is coming along nicely too. Let's carve and plate……oh, we're one plate short——"

Having used this kitchen for a long time previously, Bery also knew where the dishes and utensils were kept.

But where the plates should have been, there were only seventeen.

One plate per person for the meat plating — nine people, nine plates.

Then another plate for portioning the roast chicken and placing the bread — eighteen total.

They were short one plate.

Tension rippled through the kitchen.

— And the one who moved faster than anyone at that voice was the head cook, Zalbak.

In an instant, he seized a freshly washed plate, wiped away the remaining droplets, and with a fluid step advanced toward the two.

Hidden from their sight, he spun the plate at high speed, flinging away even the faintest traces of water — what he held was the supreme plate. Though he had been the fastest to move, he refused to deliver an imperfect dish to them, and with years of experience and technique, he had crafted a perfect plate.

As though it had been prepared from the start, Zalbak's movements were seamless.

"Yeah, my son's. The one I said died in an accident."

Galla gazed at the tag for a while, lovingly tracing the name written on it.

Krische watched Galla intently, then looked up at Bery.

When Bery tilted her head, Krische shook hers and returned her gaze to Galla.

"He was dumb and selfish and quick-tempered……not exactly a model child, but still, there's a loneliness to it. He was my dear only son, after all."

Bery hesitated, unable to find the right words.

Seeing that, Galla gave a wry smile and said she was fine.

"……Well, I'd been thinking it would be about time. It's been years, and I've made my peace. If he's set off on his journey, then all the better — I should see him off with a smile."

Bery nodded and bowed her head to the tag in Galla's hands.

Krische watched intently and asked Galla.

"……Shall we bury it?"

"Yeah, let's. Would you mind helping?"

"Yes, um……Krische will get a shovel."

Krische ran to the house and quickly returned with a shovel.

She cleared the snow from around the base of the tree, drove the shovel into the frozen earth, and after several strikes, dug a small hole, then stepped back without a word.

Galla gazed at the tag for a while, held it to her chest, and wiped her eyes several times.

Then she carefully untied the remnant of cord still on the tree, gathered it with the tag, and placed it slowly, lingeringly, into the hole Krische had dug.

"Next time, live a long life and don't make your parent grieve."

Saying that, she nodded to Krische, and Krische shoveled the earth back, smoothed it lightly, and covered it with snow.

Galla bowed her head again, and Bery followed suit.

Only Krische stood there for a while, watching Galla bow.

* * *

With their departure the next day, Krische and Bery had things to attend to.

They agreed to meet up later and parted from Galla. They went to make arrangements for purchasing provisions for the return trip.

Since the grave visit, Krische had seemed like she wanted to say something, wavering as though undecided.

When asked *is something the matter?*, she would only shake her head hesitantly.

They entered the house Galla had been looking after — Krische's old home — brewed tea, and took a breath.

It was around then.

Krische gazed at her own face reflected in the tea, and quietly spoke.

"……Bery."

"Yes."

Bery smiled as she always did.

Faintly, vaguely.

She had a sense of what Krische was about to discuss.

Had it been only the story she heard from Galla the night before, she might not have thought so.

But given Krische's behavior at the grave, and the way her violet eyes wavered now, tinged with something like fear —

What came to mind was how Krische had looked that time she tried to kill Roland.

She knew Krische had killed bandits while living in the village.

She knew the sight of Krische trying to kill Roland.

When it was necessary, Krische had no resistance to killing.

She had never asked directly, but she had wondered whether Krische might have done such things in the village as well.

The bandit killing — whether that was truly the only killing she had committed in the village, Bery could not say.

"……Oba-san's son — Krische killed him, a long time ago."

What kind of person this girl called Krische was.

And so, taking all of that together — even those words were something Bery had expected.
