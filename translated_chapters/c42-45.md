# Chapter 42: Holy War in the Kitchen

With the onset of war, and with a princess in their care, the estate could no longer be used.

There was no telling when assassins or hostile intruders might break in.

The estate was extremely vulnerable from a defensive standpoint, and leaving Bery and Kreschenta there was out of the question.

There had been a proposal to assign soldiers to guard them, but Krische questioned the reliability of such an arrangement, and Bery — who, though she never said as much, harbored a mild distrust of men — also showed signs of unease.

As a result, everyone would be staying at the fortress for the time being, and——

"When working with ingredients like these, I really ought to be asking Krische-sama to teach me."

"Ehehe, the village never had ingredients like the estate did."

In one corner of the fortress's large kitchen, the two stood side by side, cooking together.

Pumpkin soup and pie.

Herb-roasted chicken.

It could be called simple country cooking, but when Bery tasted the soup, she smiled.

From the perspective of Krische, who had lived in the village, the soup was quite generous with its ingredients.

Even so, Bery — who was accustomed to drawing out flavor by using a wide variety of ingredients in abundance — found herself troubled by the limited selection.

The fewer options she had, the more she found herself lost.

Bery's standards for cooking were extraordinarily high; she always aimed for the very best.

If she felt even slightly short of what a dish needed, she would head straight into town to shop for more.

In that respect, Bery was indeed a noblewoman at heart.

She pursued artistic perfection even in her cooking, and spared neither effort nor expense to achieve it.

For such a Bery, cooking in a fortress where the available provisions were limited was vexing — but Krische, practiced as she was, selected dishes suited to the sparse ingredients with the ease of long habit.

"The peddlers who came by once in a while would bring pumpkins. Krische loved pumpkin pie and soup so much, Krische was always borrowing the oven from the oba-san who lived nearby to make them."

If she had pumpkin, she made pumpkin. If she had potatoes, she made potatoes.

Simple country cooking made from what was available was Krische's specialty, and thanks to the many lessons she had received from Bery, her skill had improved — resulting in dishes that, while simple, brought out the full flavor of the ingredients.

She used every part of a single ingredient, wasting nothing, and made each dish properly.

Krische's approach of examining a single ingredient from every possible angle held many points that even Bery found admirable.

"Oh, is that so? Then I should have made it more often for you, Krische-sama. Scooping out the inside of a pumpkin and turning it into a gratin, for instance. It's fun to look at, and it's delicious."

"Gratin……"

Bery, by contrast, used ingredients extravagantly in pursuit of flavor.

Aristocratic sensibilities ran deep in her; she combined only the finest parts of the finest ingredients.

She would use an ingredient for a subtle hint of flavor or a hidden accent, and if some of it went to waste in the process, she turned a blind eye.

That was the great difference between her and Krische, who used every part of a daikon — from the skin to the leaves.

Having been taught that one must never waste food, Krische had been surprised at first by Bery's lavish approach — but the dishes that extravagance produced were indeed works of art.

Krische had come to admire Bery's philosophy that everything must yield to the pursuit of flavor.

"Fufu, shall we make that tomorrow? You like gratin too, don't you, Krische-sama?"

"……Yes, Krische likes it."

Krische nodded, cheeks faintly flushed with embarrassment, and Bery giggled softly.

"We'll mash the insides and dissolve them into the soup — something a little milkier than today's. I think it will suit your palate, Krische-sama."

Bery said this while stroking her head, and Krische smiled happily.

Bery's culinary philosophy of pursuing the ultimate flavor through combination, and Krische's culinary philosophy of approaching an ingredient from every angle to draw out its charm.

Where there were differences, the methods naturally differed, and so did their points of focus.

For elaborate dishes, Krische could not match Bery, who drew from an endless reservoir of experience to assemble the best possible combination — but for humble cooking like this, even Krische had a fair degree of confidence.

And yet Bery's creativity, which ceaselessly sought artistry, always exceeded Krische's imagination.

Each was the other's cook and teacher, each elevating the other.

Filled with respect and delight, Krische nuzzled up to Bery, and Bery — cheeks reddening, not entirely displeased — gently chided, "The people around us are working, you know."

This large kitchen was currently in the lull after the cooking crew had finished preparing the soldiers' meals and moved on to serving.

The crew was on break, but the men had not returned to the barracks. Instead, they lingered, occupying themselves with work that didn't need doing, watching the two happy women from a distance.

Krische, her long silver hair gathered behind her, swayed it left and right like a horse's tail.

Wearing a white apron, she smiled happily the entire time.

A small, slender frame that stirred protective instincts, and a lovely face centered on large eyes.

The way she sometimes clung to Bery was like that of a small child — utterly charming.

And Bery beside her was no less so.

The red-haired beauty in her apron dress wore a gentle, blooming smile to match Krische's.

Though petite and youthful of face like Krische, her beauty was the kind that made one turn for a second look, and her figure had a distinctly womanly definition.

The apron cinched tightly at her waist accentuated her curves, and the men craned their necks at the view from behind.

That said, she was not so much alluring as she was sensual yet wholesome — her aura of warmth and compassion was thoroughly refined.

The sight of the two was picturesque, and there was something that made one hesitate to break the atmosphere — though had circumstances allowed it, men would certainly have rushed to speak to them.

However, everyone present knew that the charming silver-haired girl beside Bery was the general's daughter and Senior Knight, Krische Christand.

And for Krische, this time spent making delicious food while being spoiled by Bery was the height of bliss — time she did not wish anyone to disturb.

The other day, Krische had warned everyone present with *"This is cooking for Her Highness the Princess, so we don't need any help"* — and thus no one could so much as offer assistance without permission.

Their only chance for conversation was to be given some trivial errand, and so they remained of their own volition, harboring faint hope for such an opportunity, feasting their eyes on the two in the meantime.

But occasionally, this place transformed into a battlefield.

"Well then, let's bring this over before it gets cold."

"Yes!"

——The first to move at those words, with the sharpest reflexes, was the head cook, Zalbak.

A portly frame approaching middle age — yet his movements were faster than anyone's.

There was soup in the pot, herb-roasted chicken, bread, and pie.

These things existed, and they needed to be transported to the dining room used by the princess and the corps commander.

Which meant what was needed was a service cart to carry them.

By around the *"Well then"* of *"Well then, let's bring this over before it gets cold"*, Zalbak had already closed the distance to where the cart was stored.

Long strides, but an unhurried gait.

It was an unspoken rule among the kitchen crew that one must never resort to so crude a measure as jogging.

When things were busy, it couldn't be helped, but an unwritten code had formed in this space: this atmosphere must not be broken.

At times like these, experience was everything——

Zalbak, who sported a magnificent mustache, had been tracking the two women's movements with the eye of a head cook.

Their cooking was precise, elegant, and beautifully coordinated.

But with years of experience, one could roughly gauge the completion time from their skill level and coordination.

When he sensed the end was near, Zalbak had naturally shifted his position closer to the service cart, all while maintaining a casual air.

Zalbak allowed himself a grin — but then, startled by the sound of rolling wheels behind him, he turned around.

Pushing the cart was Kart, the young man who single-handedly managed all meal distribution for this fortress.

Zalbak's eyes darted reflexively to the spot where the service carts were supposed to be — and one had vanished entirely from that place.

And he realized it was in Kart's hands.

——When Kart had returned from overseeing the meal distribution, the two women had just set a pot on the stove.

Kart's exceptional mind — the same one that commanded the distribution of meals to several thousand soldiers — took stock of the cooking implements and laid-out ingredients, and grasped the full picture.

What would it take to get ahead of everyone else?

And what was needed, and what steps must be followed?

Having calculated the answer in an instant, Kart had immediately commandeered a nearby server's cart with an *"I'll put that away for you"*, and lain in wait.

Kart's analysis was that Zalbak would always be one step ahead of him in experience.

Because for Zalbak, this place was practically his home.

Having been entrusted with this fortress's kitchen for a decade, Zalbak was king in this space.

Over these past few days, setting aside minor victories, the score stood roughly at four wins for Zalbak, two for Kart, and two for others.

If one counted the minor victories, the gap would only widen further.

Zalbak always positioned himself where the two flowers would call upon him for some errand.

Kart did not doubt his own excellence, but he squarely accepted the fact that in this confined arena, Zalbak was his superior.

It was frustrating.

But accepting facts as facts meant one could devise countermeasures.

If Zalbak was always one step ahead, then he simply needed to take three steps before that.

Using brains rather than experience.

Had he been born a nobleman, given command on the battlefield, he would certainly have made a fine general. Even without that, had Bogan's planned strategic command come together sooner, his mind would have made him an excellent strategist.

The talented Kart was superb even in the realm of meal distribution——

Zalbak glared at the smirking Kart with the expression of a parent whose child had been murdered, and smiled.

——Not bad, boy.

——Heh, it's naptime at the barracks for old men.

Even as they conversed through their gazes, Zalbak was not done yet.

Their routes were different, and Kart's cart would arrive first.

But Zalbak judged the distance between them to still be just two steps.

Knowing this kitchen inside and out, Zalbak precisely calculated the gap that had opened between them — and resorted to his secret weapon.

"Oops."

——He pretended to stumble over nothing.

A momentary forward lean and a lunge.

In a single move, it closed and reversed what had been a hopeless gap.

What appeared in Kart's eyes was sheer disbelief.

*He'd go that far* — the emotion was plain to see.

But Zalbak paid no mind to that gaze. At the spot where he had stumbled, he laid his hands on a cart that had *just happened to be there*, and pulled it forward.

Kart glared at the smugly grinning Zalbak with the expression of a son whose father had been murdered, and smiled.

——I didn't think you'd go that far, you old raccoon.

——That's the difference experience makes, boy.

Their contest was neck and neck, and the others, unable to intervene, could only watch with bated breath.

A duel between two leaders.

It was nothing less than a holy war.

But the deciding blow was denied them — by the arrival of an even mightier force.

"Krische-sama! Argan-sama! Let me help!"

Clattering in at a jog, shattering the unspoken rules, oblivious to the atmosphere — it was the woman of seven flaws: Anne.

Having developed a fierce admiration for Bery, she had abandoned the coveted position of royal estate servant and arrived at this fortress yesterday as a personal attendant.

*There is no one I should aspire to as a lady other than that woman*, she had declared.

Two days after Krische and the others left the estate, Anne had departed the royal estate, visited her family home — also in the north — had a fight that nearly got her disowned, and then set out for the Christand estate.

There, having been spotted loitering suspiciously around the empty estate, she was questioned by soldiers, tearfully explained her circumstances, and arrived yesterday under escort — and so she was now here, under their protection.

Her only virtues were earnestness and an excess of initiative.

No cunning, no guile — simply the audacity to charge straight through the front. The ambush that had appeared out of nowhere had, in truth, been waiting by the entrance the whole time.

*I mustn't disturb their enjoyment, but when the time comes I must help at once.*

And so Anne had watched for her moment and burst forth in spectacular fashion.

"Oh, excuse me — you went to all the trouble of bringing a cart…… I'll borrow this."

"Ah……"

Anne naturally seized the cart Zalbak had so desperately secured, and rushed over to the two women with a cheerful "Here you are!"

In her mind, the cart had been prepared specifically for her.

She was entirely oblivious to having snatched glory from the side.

Her initiative was one of her few virtues, but Anne was the woman of seven flaws.

Even as she possessed that virtue, she also carried the flaw of being shameless.

Under certain circumstances this could be a strength, but she was additionally incapable of reading the room.

So it remained nothing but a flaw.

"Oh, Anne-sama, thank you."

"Um, that…… y-you won't spill anything, will you?"

"Eh……?"

"Y-you mustn't say that, Krische-sama! If you say something like that, Anne-sama will only become more nervous……!"

Bery spoke in a fluster, and Krische loaded the pot and plates onto the cart with an anxious expression.

Anne said "I-I'll be fine," and the cart rattled under her hands.

Though she possessed the trait of being shameless, she was simultaneously timid on the inside and prone to crumbling under pressure.

Recalling the numerous blunders she had committed in front of Krische, her trembling intensified.

The first person to verify her identity when she arrived, somewhat suspected of being an intruder and escorted by military wagon, had been Krische herself — out conducting recruitment at the time.

That expression of sheer exasperation.

The more she told herself she must not make another blunder in front of Krische, the worse the shaking became.

Krische, for her part, had come to regard Anne as a supremely clumsy woman with a needlessly excessive amount of initiative — a nuisance.

Anne had repeatedly interfered with Krische's desire to be spoiled by Bery in private, and it was still fresh in memory how she had said one unnecessary thing after another at the royal estate, earning a scolding from Selene.

All of her actions were driven entirely by goodwill and affection, which only made her more troublesome.

She was the person who, by continuously committing well-intentioned failures, had first taught Krische the true meaning of the phrase "unwanted kindness."

Naturally, Krische had absolutely no faith in Anne as a servant.

Watching the trembling Anne, Krische took hold of the cart, thought for a moment, and spoke.

"Um…… Krische is in the mood to push the cart today, so would it be alright if Krische does the cart duty? Anne, could you walk a little apart from us and…… ah, yes, Anne is good at opening doors, so Krische would like you to go ahead and open doors and such."

Her poorly concealed distrust was glaringly obvious.

In Krische's mind, she had wrapped this quite neatly — but the transparent attempt at diplomacy only made it more painful, and Anne's heart was wounded.

The never-before-heard compliment that she was *"good at opening doors"* left Anne fighting back tears.

Krische's entire assessment of Anne was encapsulated in those words.

"Y-yes…… th-then, the doors…… I'm g-good, good at it, so……"

There was absolutely no malice, and she was not making fun of her.

In fact, one could tell Krische was trying to be considerate, which only widened the wound — so Bery, recognizing that addressing it would only make things worse, said nothing and ushered both of them onward.

"W-well then, shall we go?"

In truth, Bery felt a touch of the same unease.

Glancing now and then at Anne, she followed behind the two, gave a graceful nod to the frozen kitchen crew while looking mildly puzzled, and Krische followed suit with a polite imitation — and the three departed the kitchen.

The men turned pitying eyes toward Zalbak and Kart, who remained frozen in place.

# Chapter 43: The Troubles of a Young Lady

Bogan had ultimately secured the Dragon's Maw, but Gildanstein had also moved quickly.

They must have acted almost simultaneously.

The back-and-forth of taking and retaking had resulted in considerable losses; Bogan's forces, which had been close to twenty thousand, now stood at fourteen thousand.

Gildanstein, who had possessed thirty thousand troops, still held twenty thousand.

Maintaining control of the Dragon's Maw — the minimum strategic objective — had been accomplished, but advancing on the capital would require even more troops.

In terms of quality, the Christand forces were of an exceptionally high caliber, and many of Gildanstein's soldiers were hastily mobilized.

But Gildanstein was also a skilled tactician.

Even in the narrow Dragon's Maw, flanked by mountains on both sides, he made effective use of his numerical advantage, properly rotating frontline soldiers to reduce fatigue while simultaneously appearing to use the repeated skirmishes as training for his troops.

Had it been a battle on an open plain, they might have been able to crush Gildanstein's thirty thousand and end it there.

But the Dragon's Maw was narrow, making it difficult for either side to deliver a decisive blow.

The combat front was simply too constricted.

It seemed that the main theater had now shifted to the mountains themselves — Mitskronia and Bernaich — and the letter described several close calls.

The capital remained incompletely consolidated under Gildanstein's control, owing to his having moved too swiftly; many fence-sitters remained.

A rapid increase in enemy forces was unlikely, but the letter noted that Gildanstein intended to forge his army into a stronger force through the battlefield training at the pass, raising the quality of his troops.

Here, alongside the recruitment of new soldiers, they were also scraping together reservists and military veterans — men who could be immediately effective.

While such men continued to be sent to Bogan's location, Gildanstein's side was doing the same.

Creating a significant gap in troop numbers would be difficult.

The situation was, for the moment, deadlocked.

Even after reading the letter three times over, its contents did not change.

Selene rubbed the corners of her eyes.

There were many problems requiring attention, and fatigue was beginning to accumulate.

She leaned back in her chair and sighed.

This fortress, positioned deep into the northern territory from the Dragon's Maw, had once been an important strategic stronghold.

Perhaps as a remnant of that era, traces remained of a nobleman having been permanently stationed here and using it in place of a proper estate — including a private room and what passed for an office.

The office was simple but furnished with a proper desk and chair, and the windows were large for natural light.

Since it doubled as a reception room, a low table and facing sofas had been placed before the desk, making it reasonably comfortable — but that very comfort was perhaps the problem.

She often ended up sleeping right there at night, and the fatigue kept building.

Selene, in her military attire, maintained her appearance as befitting a corps commander, but her golden hair had been somewhat neglected and, having grown tired of fussing with it, she had simply tied it back.

Krische was busy.

Bery was not idle either.

When not tending to Kreschenta or preparing meals, Bery helped Selene sort through paperwork.

Bery disliked matters of conflict, but when it came to calculations and management, she was faster than Selene. Selene had her handle the processing of documents relating to provisions and equipment allocations and the like.

Selene was beginning to understand very well why Bogan wanted to create a strategic command staff.

It was not simply a matter of issuing orders; military operations involved an enormous volume of administrative work.

Arranging consumables such as food, clothing, and weapons. Negotiations with the merchants who accompanied the army.

When soldiers were kept away from towns for extended periods, a considerable amount was needed, and the military's own logistics could not cover everything — so it was common practice to contract directly with major merchants to supplement their supply lines.

Outside the fortress, tents were pitched, selling luxury items for the soldiers such as alcohol.

Since the majority of soldiers were men, arranging prostitutes was also necessary.

Merchants gathered prostitutes and provided comfort houses where the men could relieve their frustrations.

Selene did not consider it absurd that she had to concern herself with such things.

Appetite, sexual desire, the need for sleep.

She understood that fulfilling these was what made soldiers disciplined troops who did not disturb order and security.

Her personal views and her views as a corps commander — one who directed soldiers and, on the battlefield, ordered their deaths — were entirely separate things.

And considering the safety of herself, Bery, and Kreschenta, it was better that such anxieties did not exist; precisely because she was not a man, she was even more attentive to these matters than Bogan.

Incidents of soldiers committing rape occurred even in Bogan's well-disciplined army. Such incidents involving female soldiers were also a commonly heard occurrence.

She had grown accustomed to hearing her own name come up in vulgar talk on the street corners — which was precisely why she paid particular attention to these matters.

Being conscientious, thinking seriously about each issue — the more she did so, the heavier the fatigue pressed down on her.

Krische was an unquestionable genius.

Everything Selene ordered was executed beyond perfection.

And she was more diligent than Selene, and purer.

If told, for instance, to work without sleeping, she would do exactly that until she collapsed.

The day after the mountain infiltration, she had come down with a terrible fever and been bedridden for a full day.

Had Selene not noticed and made her rest, Krische would undoubtedly have joined the pursuit operations without batting an eye — and would have continued to do so as long as it was expected of her.

She would always meet expectations. And she considered this natural.

Despite the fact that her physical stamina was not particularly great.

Even the current task of managing the selection process and overseeing training was something only Krische could handle.

She carried it out with a calm face, but there was no doubt that the strain was significant even for her.

Yet Krische never showed the slightest displeasure, displaying only innocent trust toward Selene, and being spoiled by her.

Seeing that made Selene feel she had to try even harder.

Selene was certainly capable, but she was still not even sixteen.

Young, hardworking, with a powerful sense of responsibility — and precisely because of all that, Selene did not know how to let up. The bad habit of being too serious had not yet left her.

As she quietly sighed, a knock sounded at that precise moment.

Only now realizing that the sun was beginning to set, she called out *come in*, and the one who opened the door was Anne.

Following her, Kreschenta entered looking somewhat perplexed, said "Excuse me," and sat on the sofa.

Starting today, meals would include Anne, making five.

They had already been eating together as four.

It had been Bery's suggestion — most likely out of concern for Selene's exhaustion.

She felt it somewhat pathetic that despite insisting she didn't need minding, she was still being looked after like this — but honestly, she was grateful and glad.

It was one of her few moments of respite.

With Kreschenta present, she felt reluctant to relax her demeanor entirely — but even so, compared to eating alone, her state of mind was completely different.

"……Is something the matter, Kreschenta-sama?"

"Oh, no…… onee-sama and the others were walking quite far apart, so I just found it a bit peculiar."

The door was left open.

But there was no sign of anyone entering.

Anne, who had guided Kreschenta to a seat, fidgeted restlessly while peering toward the doorway — and the moment she poked her head out with a "Krische-sama?", there was a clatter of dishes scraping together.

"……Anne, once you open the door, could you wait in the corridor?"

"I-I'm sorry!"

"K-Krische-sama, Anne-sama didn't, um, m-mean any harm by……"

"But, just a little more and the food Krische worked so hard on……"

Such voices could be heard, and Anne bowed again and again, saying "I'm sorry, I'm sorry," as she stepped into the corridor.

In her place, Krische finally appeared pushing the cart, wearing a troubled expression, while Bery hurried after her saying "I-I'll handle the serving," and began ladling soup into dishes.

Krische helped with the task while repeatedly glancing toward Anne standing in the corridor.

Anne was trembling, head bowed, motionless.

"Bery…… what's going on?"

"I-it's nothing…… Anne-sama happened to poke her head out at a bad moment and nearly bumped into us."

"……Anne, Krische thought you were good at opening doors."

When Krische murmured this, Anne's shoulders jumped, and she began repeating "I'm sorry" again.

Krische's brow was faintly furrowed, slightly irritated — likely because the food had nearly been ruined.

It was different from her expression when she was displeased or sulking — this was Krische's face of restrained anger.

Such an expression from Krische was rare, and Selene couldn't help but let a small laugh escape.

"N-now, now, look — we have such lovely food, so let's enjoy it. Here, Krische-sama, please sit down."

"O-onee-sama, Anne-sama is joining us today, isn't she? Then over here."

"Ah…… that's right."

Krische glanced at Anne, then sat beside Kreschenta as directed.

Kreschenta's lips curved with satisfaction, but she pouted slightly as Krische pulled Bery by the hand to sit beside her after the serving was done.

It was clear that Kreschenta was quite fond of Krische.

On the first day, the seating had been Kreschenta on one side and the other three on the other, but Kreschenta had suggested they should sit two and two.

Selene, being considerate about any breach of etiquette, had sat beside her — but Kreschenta's gaze, clearly displeased, kept drifting toward Bery and Krische, and even Selene understood the intent behind the suggestion at that point.

Bery seemed to have noticed, but Krische acted as though the arrangement had been settled once and for all.

Since then, the seating had been Krische and Bery, Selene and Kreschenta.

Kreschenta had made valiant efforts — sitting on the opposite side from usual, or relocating to Krische's side under the pretext that there was dust in her hair — but Krische wanted to sit with Bery.

When Kreschenta moved to the opposite side, Krische moved to face her; when Kreschenta came to sit beside her, Krische stood up and casually helped and hurried Bery along, pulling her to defend the adjacent seat.

The two's back-and-forth was on the same level, which only made it more endearing.

When Anne was introduced to Kreschenta as a familiar servant who had arrived, Kreschenta had volunteered, *"Then starting today, meals will be for five."*

Her lack of resistance to having servants dine alongside royalty — unusual for someone of her station — likely had its reason here as well.

She had wanted an excuse for Krische to sit beside her.

When Selene smiled wryly at the childish side she was showing, Kreschenta glared back, looking slightly displeased.

"Wh-what is it?"

"Nothing. You seem to have taken quite a liking to Krische."

Kreschenta blushed and did not answer.

When Selene sat across from them, Krische frowned at Anne.

"……Anne, the food will get cold, so please sit down quickly."

"B-but…… to share a table with Her Highness the Princess would be too presumptuous……"

"I don't mind. Such lively meals are something one rarely gets at the royal palace…… Please don't concern yourself and have a seat."

"Y-yes……"

Anne sat down beside Selene with great trepidation.

Selene watched Bery soothing the puffed-up Krische and smiled, then said softly, "We give thanks to the blessings of the earth," and offered a small moment of silent prayer.

When Bogan was absent, Selene was the one to declare the start of the meal.

The other four followed her lead, observing a brief moment of silence before beginning to eat.

During meals, speaking with food in one's mouth was poor manners, but conversation itself was common enough.

"How is it?"

"Yes, it's very delicious! You really are a wonderful cook, onee-sama."

"Ehehe……"

It was usually Krische who spoke first, seeking opinions on how the food had turned out.

In the past, meals had been relatively quiet, with Bery taking a bright tone to explain the day's dishes — but now that role had been taken over by Krische.

Back then, Bery had often spoken to Selene — and thinking about it now, that had probably been her being considerate.

Perhaps unsurprisingly — or perhaps as expected — Kreschenta was a hearty eater, and she was quite fond of meals.

She showed no displeasure toward the food, which, while delicious, could not honestly be called lavish — and had seemed perfectly satisfied since moving from the estate to the fortress.

It was partly because Krische had made it, no doubt, but her style of eating quickly while maintaining good manners was remarkably similar to Krische's.

Noble families with mana users as their foundation were generally hearty eaters, but the quantity these two consumed was excessive even by that standard — while Selene ate one piece of bread, the two had already finished two.

Krische reached for her own bread while naturally placing one in front of Kreschenta, who, glancing around self-consciously, blushed with embarrassment but tore off pieces and stuffed them in her cheeks.

Selene had gathered that Kreschenta was putting on an act, and this too was a source of worry.

When she asked Bery, the answer she received was: *"She is very similar to Krische-sama, but she should be fine."*

But it was Selene — who, unlike Bery, knew the Krische of the battlefield — whose anxiety was stronger.

Selene considered it practically a miracle that Krische had grown up to be good-natured.

It was because she had been raised by kind, understanding people — her parents and Bery — and Krische's true nature was a precarious thing.

Unlike Krische, Kreschenta had been raised in the royal palace, and Selene still could not get a clear grasp of what kind of person she was, which made her uneasy.

Selene knew that the royal palace was a den of schemes and intrigue.

Selene loved Krische from the bottom of her heart, but at the same time acknowledged that she was an abnormal person who did not hesitate to kill.

Selene considered herself less virtuous than Bery.

Having been trained to always view things objectively, she clearly recognized that side of Krische as dangerous.

And she believed Krische's true nature lay there.

What allowed Krische to live as she did now — as a girl who was merely a little unusual — was above all Bery's love and goodness.

It was frustrating, but Selene admitted she could not have managed such skillful guidance herself, and she respected Bery more than anyone for it.

But that did not mean she believed Bery's goodness was infallible.

Just because Krische had turned out well did not mean she could unconditionally believe that Kreschenta would too.

She understood, of course, that Bery was aware of this and had still asked to be entrusted with it — but the possibility of failure was deeply frightening.

The sight of Kreschenta happily eating bread and enjoying the food was endearing, the very image of Krische.

But if asked whether she was as good-natured as Krische — Selene could not relax her guard.

"Anne, how is it?"

"D-did you two really make this……?"

"……? Yes."

"Uuu…… My own cooking couldn't even compare. It's so delicious…… When the two of you are such wonderful cooks as well, I wonder what on earth I'm supposed to help with……"

When Anne answered, Krische thought for a moment, then spoke with a small smile.

"Um…… Krische, well, Bery does everything for Krische, and, ah, Krische will help Bery when Krische is around, so Anne doesn't need to do any looking-after."

"Um…… that's, y-yes……"

"K-Krische-sama……"

It was an unmistakable and unhidden rejection.

Anne deflated, and Bery fumbled for words in a fluster.

Kreschenta watched Krische, then turned an observing eye toward Anne.

"Krische has judged you to be capable. Knowledge and experience can be acquired later. But whether someone is clever or not — that's something you're born with, so it's very precious. Whatever Mia thinks, the one who decides is Krische."

"But……"

"*But*? Don't talk back to a superior, fool!"

"Eek——"

Dagra bellowed, and Krische gave him an annoyed look.

"I beg your pardon. ……Corps Commander's Adjutant, if we are to grant command authority, temperament must also be considered. A captain must lead from the front and inspire the troops. I acknowledge this girl's ability, but with her disposition, that would be difficult."

"I see, that's a good point too……"

Krische thought for a moment, then clapped her palms together.

"Then, Dagra — just use her as your adjutant."

"But this is a century……"

In a century, the two captains doubled as adjutants.

Adjutants were typically assigned only from the battalion level up.

"This is a century with a special operational role. There will be times when difficult missions must be assigned to suit the situation, and besides — if we're talking about that, Krische was the adjutant when we were a five-hundred-man unit last time. There's no rule saying a century can't have an adjutant, so wouldn't it be fine?"

"That is true, but……"

"At that time, Krische took the role of leading the way and doing the killing, and Selene directed Dagra and the rest. The person who's good at something should handle that something — you assign roles as the situation demands."

Dagra's face twitched as if recalling something, and he nodded with resignation.

*The role of leading the way and doing the killing* — hearing Krische say it so matter-of-factly, Mia thought in a daze that the rumors must indeed be true.

There was also a desire to flee the reality that she was about to be made an adjutant.

"……This girl's ability is certainly too good to waste as a common soldier. I will take responsibility for training her into something useful."

"Good. Mia is confirmed as a sergeant for now. One of the captains will be Tagel, but…… if Mia won't work, who should the other be?"

Mia had been the last to come for this interview, having been swept along to this point.

Krische had now completed her assessment of all of them.

Tagel was a forthright man, personable, and though he looked young, he was in his mid-thirties. Having previously served in the military, Dagra had no objection to his appointment as captain.

The sergeants were also drawn primarily from those with military experience.

"If you'll permit it, I'd like to recommend someone from outside — a captain who served under me. He can handle mana to some degree, though not exceptionally, and he's an excellent commander with a sharp mind. He accompanied us on the mountain infiltration under Krische-sama."

"Then bring him in. One person should be fine."

There were naturally those already serving in the army who could wield mana, but they tended to hold positions as captains, centurions, battalion commanders, or their adjutants.

Their high ability meant they earned battlefield honors and survived more often.

Pulling such seasoned men promoted from the ranks would weaken the organization as a whole — and Selene had impressed upon Krische that this was generally to be avoided.

"So the framework is in place. What's the training plan going forward?"

"The fundamentals of soldiering have taken rough shape, but I'd like to conduct a light exercise in the nearby forest the day after tomorrow to give the captains and sergeants some command experience. Given this unit's intended role, I feel they should learn to operate in forested terrain. Would that be acceptable? If possible, I'd like to use some of my men who've been sent to other units for instruction……"

"Very well. Your men performed well in the mountains. Krische grants you permission to pull up to ten under the pretext of black training. Submit your selection of personnel today. Krische will process the paperwork so they can report here by noon tomorrow."

"Thank you."

Krische gave thought to what needed thinking, then delegated the rest — in other words, she handed it off entirely.

When someone like Dagra existed — a born *agitator* capable of leading men, and moreover one who respected discipline and was personally excellent — she entrusted everything to them.

She saw herself as occupying the position of stating the objective and policy, with subordinates carrying out the execution — not herself.

In this, her thinking differed greatly from Selene's.

Selene, for instance, carefully observed her soldiers' conditions and issued objectives and policies accordingly.

Krische, who never doubted her own correctness, stated the objective and policy first, then filled in whatever was lacking if it proved infeasible — or else considered replacing personnel with those who could manage it, herself included.

The difference could be described as Selene, who always aimed for the better option, versus Krische, who always demanded the best.

Selene's approach naturally required more effort on her part, but it earned her subordinates' devotion, built morale, and produced stable results.

Krische's approach sometimes demanded the unreasonable, earning her subordinates' fear and dislike — but in exchange, her own effort was distributed and the results approached the theoretical optimum.

Ordinarily Selene's approach was considered the better one, but Krische had the power to produce results.

Thus, the two occupied opposite poles, and neither could simply be called superior.

At the same time, Krische spared no effort in providing what was needed so that the unreasonable became reasonable — in furnishing the means to meet her expectations.

For someone as capable as Dagra, this made her an extremely easy commander to work under, and he genuinely respected and valued that quality in her.

If Dagra had to choose whom he would prefer to serve under, it was Selene.

And should she ever find herself in peril, he felt he would stake his life to protect her without hesitation — so deep was his loyalty.

But when it came to the battlefield, he could think of no one more dependable than Krische.

She was a natural-born killer, and frightening. But she possessed an overwhelming power that seemed to make even the impossible possible, and that power inspired the conviction that she would lead them to victory.

And for such a person to evaluate him and grant him wide discretion — that was the greatest of honors.

The directions differed, but the substance was the same.

He felt genuine respect for both, and Krische, unknowingly, had been steadily building respect and trust among capable men like Dagra.

"For those who have not yet learned physical augmentation, they should also accompany the exercise. Having the team that performed best the previous day serve as instructors would double as a reward and solve the problem. The exercise exemption itself would serve as an incentive. Shall I submit a schedule?"

"No, use your own discretion, Dagra. Krische will inform the logistics section to provide whatever you need. For roughly a hundred people, it shouldn't be a problem no matter how much you use. If there's anything else special you require, report it in advance as much as possible."

Krische tilted her teacup and looked at Mia, who remained frozen in place.

"This unit is a small elite force. They must be able to coordinate to a degree even without forming a battle line. Individual improvement is a given, but reconnaissance ability, judgment, and the concentration to endure demanding missions — Mia, what do you think should be prioritized during forest exercises?"

"Um…… that……"

"Don't mumble, fool! You are to be my adjutant — compose yourself!"

"Y-yesh!"

Mia snapped to attention and saluted.

"S-spreading out and regrouping drills are impor——"

"Say *dispersal and assembly*. This is a military unit, not your backwater village. Always use precise language."

"D-dispersal and assembly drills are, um, something that should be prioritized."

Glared at by the fierce Dagra, made an adjutant before she understood what was happening —

Mia was on the verge of tears.

"Please continue."

"S-…… scouts must not be allowed to lose concentration, so, um, smooth rotation of positions and assignments must be ensured. F-for example, dividing the unit in two by captain and using each half as the other's mock enemy, repeating ambush drills, would improve performance, I think……"

She glanced at Krische and Dagra.

Neither said anything. She understood this to mean *continue*, and, stomach churning, she forced her mind to work.

"During regular sword and combat drills, sentries should also be posted, and one side should be tasked with approaching covertly to stage a surprise attack — maintaining constant tension, with rewards and punishments applied accordingly. The same should apply during major rest periods, with all activity conducted under the assumption of operating in enemy territory, which should naturally heighten awareness. Conditions that invite complacency or force the dispersal of strength should constantly be imposed, with one side tasked to raid the other. This would develop individual judgment and cultivate the ability to counterattack against ambushes and surprise attacks."

"A good proposal. What else?"

"Um, injuries are expected, so someone with medi—— d-no, a medical specialist, several of them. Swords should be wooden, and everyone should be required to wear armor. Live sword training can be done outside the forest — I think it would be better to prioritize practical coordination and movement drills instead. That's…… what I think……"

Krische gazed steadily at Mia, then sipped her tea and looked at Dagra.

Dagra nodded with satisfaction and spoke.

"Not bad at all. Submit a detailed training plan to me first thing tomorrow morning."

"Yes……"

"The instruction cadre will arrive tomorrow at noon. We'll review it then. Tomorrow will be a full day of preparation. Don't expect to rest."

"Y-yes……"

*How did it come to this.*

Just as Mia was about to curse the day she had come here, Dagra smiled at her.

"Don't make that face. In exchange, you're excused from training for the rest of today. Working on the training plan is your job now. When you're done, get some proper rest."

"Really?!"

"Watch your tongue, fool! Dismissed."

"Yes, sir!"

Beaming, Mia saluted and practically skipped out of the room.

Watching her go, Krische felt she carried a faintly Anne-like aura.

"My own daughter is about her age, you know. I can't help but feel she's not a stranger."

"Is that so."

*Mia might be a slightly cleverer Anne*, Krische thought, and mentally filed her in the same Anne Category alongside Anne.
