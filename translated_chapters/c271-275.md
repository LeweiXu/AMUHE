# Chapter 271: Moonlight's Legacy — Finale

The space annexed beside the manor was known as Selene's workshop.

Swords, knives, and cleavers lined the walls, and Selene was wiping sweat from her hands with a cloth as she turned a proud look on Krische.

What Krische held was a slender long-bladed knife — a type of fish-cutting knife used in island nations to the east of the continent.

Single-edged rather than double, apparently used when slicing raw fish.

"Hm-hmm, well? This one's come out rather nicely, I think."

"It… the shape is fairly good."

Krische handed it to Bery, who had also been looking at it.

Bery brought her face close with serious eyes and examined the blade, turning it into the light to look through it.

She narrowed her eyes ever so slightly for a moment, but quickly smiled and nodded.

"Yes, it's certainly serviceable. A sufficient piece of work, I would say."

"……You sound like you have objections."

"Er, no, I…"

"Tell me honestly."

"Objections is perhaps too strong a word……"

Bery exchanged a slightly troubled look with Krische and smiled apologetically.

"It's just… with all due respect, I could not call it a top-quality piece. The surface is a little rough."

"What……?"

Bery showed the spine of the blade and indicated it with a finger.

Now that she mentioned it, the grain of the surface was ever so slightly uneven at a very fine scale.

It must have been where a small warp had been straightened.

Selene noticed and furrowed her brow, fixing her gaze on Bery.

Bery smiled and cradled the knife.

"Of course, it's not a bad knife. For practical use there's really no issue——"

"……Rejected."

"Well……"

"That one's rejected. I'll remake it."

Selene snatched back the knife Bery was cradling and set it on the workbench.

Then she compared it against the reference knife she had bought.

"O-Ojou-sama…… For my part, I think it's more than adequate……"

"Don't say another word. I want to make a perfect knife that no one can find fault with. I don't want you using it just because Ojou-sama made it by hand."

"……"

"We have more than enough time. It's not good enough until a perfect knife is finished. And you — look at it the way you would look at buying your own knife at full price. Understood?"

"……Yes."

Bery smiled in her helpless way, and Krische let out a small *uuu* groan.

Since arriving here, Selene had apparently become absorbed in making things — she had made all manner of items: swords, spears, chests, a shed, fishing gear. A few days ago she had spotted a knife on a trip they took and thought it looked good, but Selene had confiscated it as a reference piece with a "I'll make you one." The knife had been imprisoned in this *Selene's Prison*.

In port towns and island nations there were many dishes that used raw fish, and it had been five years now since they had talked about making some of those dishes themselves.

Without anyone quite noticing, a tacit understanding had formed that dishes with raw fish must be made using a knife forged by Selene, and as a result Krische had been subjected to a mysterious state of withholding for no clear reason.

How many more years would it take to clear Bery's standards?

The thought alone was staggering.

Selene was thorough, but by nature she was careless and clumsy. She could make things that were reasonably good, but Krische simply could not picture Selene ever finishing this particularly delicate knife to perfection.

"U-um…… Selene, just for today, couldn't we try using that knife……"

"No. I cannot let you use something like this."

"K-Krische-sama…… shall we grill that fish? Look, it has such good fat on it — even grilled, it will be very delicious."

"……Yes."

Last night Selene had said *this time it's perfect*, and so today they had gone to the port town to buy a fine fish — and now the worry had proven well-founded.

It was the quality of sixty points becoming sixty-five points after a year's work.

And Bery's 'good knife' standard was ninety points.

How many decades would it be before raw fish was ever eaten in this manor?

Selene Christand was a person who, once she ran out of work, made things.

As if she could only exist within work.

This confirmed once again what Krische had always thought: that Selene was someone who liked troublesome things.

"Bery, I'll be back at the manor by evening."

"Yes, of course. …… Shall we go wake Kreschenta-sama, Krische-sama?"

"……Yes. Sashimi……"

Krische let that out in a small murmur, then suddenly raised her head, brow furrowing.

"…… Krische-sama?"

"Someone's come."

"Someone…… do you mean into this world?"

"Yes. Just wait here a moment."

Krische said this, and then trotted away.

*I'll be right back*, she said, opening the door and stepping outside.

"……Oh, it's you, Elgeinst."

"Ah. How are you holding up?"

"I feel like I'm about to die."

The old man lying in the bed opened his eyes, looked over, and smiled.

When mana-holders lost the ability to wield their mana, their health deteriorated as if rolling downhill.

Waltza had reached the end of his natural life.

One hundred and thirty — like his grandfather before him, he had lived quite long for a mana-holder.

Elgeinst helped him sit up and braced his body, then made him tea.

He had been bedridden ever since he collapsed a few days ago, and while his condition had been worsening day by day, Waltza was the same as ever.

He kept a smile on his face and talked cheerfully, repeating memories as if savoring the old days.

Elgeinst smiled wryly and kept him company in conversation.

With the thought that his time was nearly gone, even the small, trivial, silly stories shone like they were lit from within.

In what had felt like an instant of time, there were more memories than could ever be told, and he came to understand, quietly, that he had grown old.

"But you really don't age, Elgeinst."

"……That's not quite true. I too have begun to age a little. Another few decades and I'll likely die."

"Your face even aged looks the same. You had a corpse-like face to begin with."

"……You."

Waltza laughed — and coughed, and Elgeinst hurried to rub his back.

Then he laid him back down on the bed.

"……I'm sorry, Elgeinst."

"What are you apologizing for?"

"It's a constrained position. …… Even if you were suited to it, I made you do things that weren't like you."

"Don't say things out of character. …… We both just did what was necessary. You and I alike."

*That's right*, Elgeinst continued with a smile.

"But in your heart you were never truly satisfied with it. I said some rather harsh things."

"You worked in proportion to that. …… Besides, this is my wish as well. A wish of mine, entrusted to Alberinea."

"Is that so?"

He laughed and coughed again, and Waltza said:

"You're a hopeless, gloomy shut-in and a stubborn man. There's no reason someone like you would ever have any interest in becoming emperor and governing a nation. …… You like saying things that sound noble, but it's all secondhand in the end."

"What……?"

"…… What is *your* wish, Elgeinst? This peace is something you dedicated to Alberinea, isn't it?"

Elgeinst fell silent.

Then he lowered his eyes.

"You did the finest work. Kleinmeil is stable. …… In a peaceful world, what is it that you want to do, now that you've come this far?"

*It's your life*, Waltza continued.

"I had a reasonably good life, as you can see …… but I find myself wondering. I think about what it will be like when you die someday. …… The one regret I'll die with is the thought of your end — an end where you've suppressed yourself and reigned as emperor over a peaceful world."

And he looked steadily at Elgeinst.

"……Your face has been gloomier than usual lately — even gloomier than gloomy. If you go on like this, you won't be able to die smiling the way I will."

"……I don't mind that——"

"I mind, Elgeinst. Don't leave a stain on my life by making me a man who bought peace at the cost of his friend's."

"……. You really are a selfish man. I'm truly astounded by you."

Even as he said it, he could strangely smile, and Elgeinst closed his eyes.

"Well, it's the last wish of the last friend of your life — even a bit of selfishness should be forgiven."

"That reasoning is cowardly. The one who dies first gets far too good a deal."

"Heh heh. Just live a little more loosely, let your shoulders down a bit, Elgeinst. You've done enough work. It's only fair that you be given at least one reward. …… Even if you did something a little self-indulgent, there isn't a soul anywhere who would blame you for it."

*Is that so*, he said — and Waltza answered, *It is*, with force.

"……What is your wish, Elgeinst?"

Elgeinst closed his eyes for a while, then shook his head slowly.

"……It's a wish that cannot be granted."

"Say it anyway. You've known me since you were a child — what reason is there to be ashamed before a man who'll be dead tomorrow or the day after?"

Waltza said this, and lay looking at the ceiling as though waiting for the words.

Elgeinst looked at his face for a while, thought, and then after a long silence, spoke.

Waltza heard it, and laughed cheerfully: *Are you still a child*.

And then:

"……That's a wish worthy of you."

——*You'll manage it, I'm sure. Elgeinst.*

So he said, with no basis at all for the claim.

Where Alberinea had gone was something Elgeinst had not the faintest idea about.

He used his free time not for research but for painstaking fieldwork.

He went personally, in disguise, to speak with those who had been close to her — nobles such as those of houses Agrand and Velraigh, grandchildren and great-grandchildren of Black Flag Special Service members.

There were some who had met her, and it seemed they had heard talk of her character, but of course no one knew why she had disappeared.

Following those leads, he walked the streets of Albernaria and was referred to a restaurant called 'The Dawn Crescent' by the Black Flag Swordsmanship Academy, where former Black Flag members taught the sword. The shop was closed for the day, but after he pressed a silver coin into the proprietor's hand the man laughed and agreed, and spoke while pouring drinks at the counter.

"My great-grandfather was a long-lived fellow called Koza…… he was an archer squad leader in the Black Flag Special Service. And after the Unification War ended——"

The man began with a light account of the shop's history.

He explained that the shop had been founded by Black Flag Special Service members Koza and Beltz, and that apparently Alberinea had been a regular customer in those days.

Records and documents — he had already researched everything that could be researched.

He naturally knew those names.

Both had been members of Alberinea's Black Flag Special Service — and its predecessor, the Black Century, from the very beginning.

It was surprising that a great noble would visit a single restaurant, but knowing Alberinea, it somehow seemed perfectly natural.

He didn't find it particularly strange.

Apparently the queen had come incognito to eat there as well on one occasion.

"You're a mage, aren't you? You might actually know something — apparently it was quite a commotion back then, and my grandfather and father rushed over to my great-grandfather's place in a panic. Alberinea and the Christand family had both been patrons, so they thought he might know something."

The proprietor pointed upward.

"The sky lit up bright as noon, and a tower that reached nearly to the clouds transformed into a great tree and scattered rainbow-colored petals — quite the uproar, apparently. …… But my great-grandfather wasn't surprised at all; just laughed cheerfully. Said something like, 'I hope she'll be a patron a hundred years from now, too.'"

The proprietor wet his tongue with liquor.

"My great-grandfather was getting on in years, and my grandfather and father thought he'd gone senile …… well, that's where that story ends, but there was one rather odd thing."

"…… An odd thing?"

"Well, it's that kind of shop. We don't get many women customers ordinarily — the occasional couple, but that's about it."

'The Dawn Crescent' was a working-class inn-with-meals in the castle district.

They served liquor, and the clientele were mostly guards and caravan escorts — not a particularly refined sort. The food was good, befitting a shop permitted to display the Christand simplified crest, but it wasn't the kind of place women could easily walk into.

"…… And yet, after the last war settled down, one of the servers told me that a group of women had come in, servants and all. Obviously quality people — normally if a customer like that came in I'd go out myself to greet them, but I was busy and never showed my face, just sent out the food…… later when I remembered to ask, they said they couldn't even remember what those women looked like."

"…… That is——"

"Strange business, I thought …… but just then it happened to remind me of what my grandfather and father had told me, and I thought — maybe that was Alberinea and her party, after all. …… Well, that's all there is to the story, really."

The man picked up the silver coin Elgeinst had given him and looked at it.

"…… Not that we serve anything expensive. Cheap liquor, good ingredients but not luxury goods. Most customers don't pay with silver coins unless it's a large party. …… That day too, there was indeed a silver coin among the day's receipts. Apparently the customer said 'no change needed, as delicious as ever,' and left."

*Suggestive, isn't it*, the man laughed and drank.

"'As ever' — I've never once had any customer like that, as far as I know. So in my own head I've decided that must have been when Alberinea paid a visit…… well, sorry to say it to someone who gave me a silver coin, but that's all I can tell you."

"…… No. Thank you. I heard what I wanted to hear."

That was how it began.

Elgeinst had been thinking that Alberinea might have gone somewhere very far away — but was that truly so?

Alberinea had no need to go anywhere far at all.

Perhaps she had simply withdrawn from society in order to live the peaceful, unhurried daily life that nothing could tie her down to.

"——The divine being is no longer here."

"Not here……?"

"I cannot say why. …… It was quite some time ago — around the year when the absence of the Alberan queen was announced. She disappeared after that."

He had visited Kreisharana himself, combining it with diplomacy, and requested an audience with the dragon — and those were the words he received.

"…… As you say, Yagelnaus-sama was permitted Alberinea's true name, and I understand she was also on close terms with Her Majesty the Alberan Queen. Had an audience been possible, perhaps you could have heard something. But even we have not seen Yagelnaus-sama's form in many long years. You would do well to give up the search."

"…… Is that so."

"I am telling you this not because you are the emperor of Kleinmeil, but because I trust you as a person. As I have already said — please, under no circumstances speak of this to anyone."

Elgeinst nodded, and asked:

"No indication at all of a reason?"

"…… I cannot presume to fathom the divine being's heart. That said—— are you familiar with Kreisharana's holy spirit priestess, Elgeinst-dono?"

Kreisharana's last holy spirit priestess, Lira Sharana.

She had lived alone in the Forbidden Domain until her death in the middle of the Unified Calendar era.

It was said to have been a practice performed in order to make her the last priestess and to serve the divine being forever into the future.

Why had she ended the succession of holy spirit priestesses that had continued for hundreds of years?

And might there have been some reason in the fact that the divine being disappeared several decades after her death? So he said.

The true reason was not recorded anywhere, and all he himself had heard was that she had devoted her body to the divine being — but he also said this:

"When Yagelnaus-sama was still present, a child once wandered deep into the forest…… and the child said that she was helped by a beautiful woman who guided her back to the path. …… Perhaps the divine being, and the priestess, are still watching over us from somewhere — even now, though they cannot be seen."

For something passed down as legend or tradition, it was very recent.

By now, what Elgeinst had was certainty.

Elgeinst knew the formula for spatial transference as well.

It was a fixed installation type — by his standards still an inefficient and incomplete formula, but there were no theoretical errors in it.

The doubt lay, as always, in the formula that had surrounded him on that day.

——Alberinea's inscription, as if rewriting the entire world.

That had clearly been something entirely different from spatial transference.

If that were the case, there would be no reason to inscribe a formula across such a vast area. Something far smaller in scale would have sufficed.

Alberinea had been a person who permitted absolutely no waste in her formula-inscriptions.

Perhaps Alberinea was still in this world even now.

It remained mere conjecture — and yet somehow, it felt to him that this was simply so.

Whatever the principles behind that tower, it had unmistakably been a thing meant to fill the world with mana.

And afterward, it had been prepared for the purpose of *casting a spell upon the world itself*.

It was a preposterous notion.

There were logical leaps.

And yet, knowing with certainty that Alberinea was truly equal to a god, Elgeinst was sure of it.

An eternal childlike purity that never changed.

Forever a young descendant of divinity.

For Alberinea, it would not be strange at all.

To rewrite the world itself for the sake of her own desire.

——*I want to see them soon*, he recalled her face as she thought of her servants and smiled.

Her wish was surely an eternal paradise that continued even after death.

That it had been so shortly after Selene Christand's passing was not coincidence either.

*That's what it is*, he repeated to himself.

When he realized it, his hands and feet were trembling.

Perhaps he had lost his mind.

What Elgeinst had could perhaps be called madness.

To set a destination based on an offhand conjecture.

Even so, Elgeinst resolved to move forward.

He wondered what time was.

It seemed an incomprehensible length of time, or felt short, a creature of some kind that changed its size with every occasion.

Three or four times the span of an ordinary human life.

Twice over even for a mana-holder.

That much time he had lived, and still it struck him as strange.

Some looked at Elgeinst with puzzlement, this man who became emperor without any luxury, shut himself away, and continued his research with fervent dedication for decades on end.

Others spoke of him with respect, calling him a mage among mages who had never lost his curiosity.

They might have been living through time even longer than Elgeinst's.

For Elgeinst, the decades since becoming emperor — since he had begun seeking Alberinea — had passed in the blink of an eye.

Strange, to think they were living through the same time.

Before he knew it Elgeinst had aged, and even Waltza's great-grandchildren had grandchildren of their own.

The time he had spent with Alberinea was surely only a tiny fraction of a life spanning nearly two hundred years.

Once every few months, for only a brief interval.

The total time he had spent face to face with her would not even amount to a single full day.

Even if he counted the thirty years during which she had been present, that was still only a sliver of two hundred.

And yet, that sliver of time felt like an incomprehensible span — felt as though it had been more than half his life.

Why did he cling to her so?

Every time he asked himself that question, this is what he thought.

It was not physical length but felt time that was the truth.

In Elgeinst's life, Alberinea had indeed been at its center.

He studied space, and attempted to encompass everything that composed the world.

He sought to know all that constituted matter and space, and the only things he came to understand were a succession of answers called *this is not it*.

The distance between himself and her was surely not a physical one.

His body aged daily, and yet the one thing that remained unknown could still not be resolved.

For Elgeinst, it had been an instant.

Even his ability to wield mana had begun to dim, and with a measure of resignation he murmured, *this is not it*.

Something close by, yet not close by.

Said to exist in people, yet never to be seen.

It was probably something like that, Elgeinst thought.

That she was in an eternal paradise that continued even after death.

Like a blind, unknowing crowd. Like those who offer prayers to the formless.

Elgeinst, who had sought to illuminate the unknown through accumulated theory, had in the end decided to entrust himself at the last to mystery.

He gave away his private writings, the countless books he had accumulated — or burned them.

Everything Elgeinst had built vanished in an instant, and what came to mind was something that existed after death, after the body had been cast aside.

Resolved to stake everything on that alone, he clothed himself in nothing but his robe and a single mana crystal 'frying pan.'

She was a perfect mage, and a great teacher.

She did not make things that could be found through the accumulation of reasoning, that others could illuminate.

That was the realization brought to him by that mana crystal given to him as a study tool.

The art of changing one's vantage point to find things — that was what she had given him.

So he would discard all reasoning.

He called to mind her inscription carved into the world, and closed his eyes.

*——You'll manage it, I'm sure. Elgeinst.*

The mana crystals arrayed around him blazed with light, and began to decompose his body.

What he felt was a moment of pain.

He held only his sense of self in mind, seared the pain into himself by force of will, held it there, gritted his teeth.

And then——

A pleasant wind touched his body.

When he opened his eyes, the first thing that entered them was a sky blazing with light.

He tried to sit up but found no strength in him, the feeling in his limbs uncertain.

Even the robe he wore seemed blurred, rippling.

——*It was simply, utterly comfortable.*

The scent of grass and flowers tickling his nose.

Warm sunlight, and a cool breeze.

A sensation as if everything in his body were melting, as if something were drawing him toward it — but there was neither pain nor fear in it.

Not the slightest unease about surrendering to it, and he could even think that this was fine.

The only reason he did not was because he saw a figure against the sky.

Something winged, drifting gently, descending on the wind.

A black-and-white apron dress, silver hair glimmering in the sun.

——Violet eyes, like gemstones.

The girl landed by his side, peered at him with a puzzled look, and waved a hand in front of Elgeinst's face.

"Hm…… are you alive there?"

Did she not notice his eyes were open?

A fingertip poked him lightly in the cheek, and suddenly something welled up toward his eyes — he made an effort and put on a smile.

"Yes…… Alberinea."

At the sound of his voice, Alberinea tilted her head slightly, thought, and peered closely at Elgeinst.

For a while she looked at his face.

Then her gaze fell to his chest, and she seemed to notice the mana crystal 'frying pan.'

"Oh……"

She clapped her hands together with a *pon*.

"I wondered who it could be. It has been a while, hasn't it, Nemunemu."

She smiled and said this to Elgeinst.

How was he to put it.

Inside Elgeinst, something beyond expression was overflowing.

*A while* — measured in time, that was true. A hundred and fifty years had passed.

And yet Alberinea was exactly as he remembered her — as if they had just seen each other the other day.

"……You…… remembered me."

"Ehehe, of course I remember. …… But never mind that — are you all right? You look like you're about to die."

The question brought his body back to mind, and he glanced down at it.

The sensation of his body dissolving — uncomfortable and yet inexplicably pleasant — had not gone.

He was probably, right now, in the boundary between life and death.

And that was precisely why he was here.

"Just a moment, let me fix your body up a bit for now——"

She reached a hand out toward him.

He touched it.

And then, *it's all right*, Elgeinst said with a smile.

"……I only wanted to see you one last time."

Anything more than that would have been too much.

He had not deciphered the magic Alberinea had cast upon the world.

This was time granted precisely because he had entrusted everything to something that could be called heaven.

To ask more of something granted by prayer — that kind of faith — would be greed.

It was because he had accepted the unknown as unknown that he was here now.

Alberinea thought for a moment, traced her finger across her lips, and murmured a low *hmm*.

After a little while, in that case, she seemed to decide, and settled herself beside him.

"What a beautiful view. …… Have you been here all this time since then?"

Elgeinst smiled, and with eyes that looked as if they were sleeping, looked up at the sky and around at the trees and said:

Perhaps the reason everything looked as if it were glowing with light was that this world was filled with abundant mana.

Whatever the explanation, the scholarly question disappeared quickly.

He simply gazed at his own heart thinking, *it's beautiful*, and let the warmth spread across his face.

——*This mystery-laden world was surely here for her sake.*

"Yes. Everyone moved here together. Apparently Nemunemu built a country called Kleinmeil and worked as its emperor …… ehehe, I heard someone say you were the second Alberinea."

Alberinea said this as a matter of course, and Elgeinst felt his cheeks grow warm and shook his head.

She had known the path he had walked.

He had thought that perhaps she had no interest in him at all.

That she might not even remember him.

He had thought that, and then felt ashamed of having thought it.

"……An embarrassing story. Please forgive me."

"Krische doesn't really care about names like that, so you don't need to worry. That name was something Kreschenta made up on the spot anyway…… I only used it because it was convenient."

*I don't use it now*, Alberinea said with complete composure.

*You haven't changed*, Elgeinst said and smiled.

"…… Well, Krische hasn't grown taller or anything, and there's no aging…… but——"

"That's not what I mean…… heh, that's exactly the thing."

"……? Um…… ?"

Alberinea tilted her head in apparent incomprehension, and Elgeinst shook with laughter at the sight.

She was exactly as he had known her — unchanged from his memories in every detail.

It had been nearly two hundred years since they first met, and she was still the same as she had been then.

The Alberinea he had always held in his mind was indeed Alberinea.

The symbol of unknowable mystery.

And yet, Elgeinst knew her well.

*Well, never mind*, said Alberinea in a tone of resignation, and she took hold of Elgeinst's beard with her fingers.

"Nemunemu has changed quite a bit though. Not just your hair — growing out your beard so long like this. …… Is there some rule that men must grow beards?"

"Nothing like that…… Something like a vow, I suppose."

"……You sound like Bery."

*You were only this small when we met*, she showed with her hand the height Elgeinst had been at their first meeting.

*When is she talking about*, he thought, amused, and laughed again.

Alberinea, who spoke of it as if it were yesterday, was, as ever, Alberinea.

"But I was a little surprised. Normally even people who have died can't come in here……"

"……Because I wished to see you. For a very long time."

"Krische?"

"Yes."

Krische tilted her head, puzzled.

"Hmm…… could it be that a strong intention is what preserves a soul?"

"A soul……"

"Nemunemu is in almost entirely a soul state right now."

And then she raised a finger, as in the old days.

Despite being a great noble, despite being the king's elder sister, she was like a village girl.

Like a neighbor's older sister — and yet not in the least ashamed of it, naturally, unabashedly.

It was surely because of that very quality that so many people had been drawn to her.

"…… Normally, something in that state would journey to what is called the source — an extraordinarily large mass of mana — and dissolve into it, and be reborn……"

She got that far, then said, *but Krische doesn't really understand it either.*

"Even Alberinea doesn't know?"

"Hmm…… actually, I haven't investigated it. I once thought I might, but Bery said that not knowing leaves room for all sorts of imaginings, and that's enjoyable — so."

Elgeinst's narrow eyes opened very slightly.

Then he smiled and closed them.

"……What a lovely way of thinking. It's precisely this — not all joy lies in knowing."

"Ehehe, you're saying the same thing as Bery."

Elgeinst smiled wryly.

"I only arrived at that understanding just now, actually……"

He felt he had taken a very roundabout path.

And yet that roundabout path had led Elgeinst here, and given him the answer.

Alberinea wore the face he remembered, and the smile he knew.

In her there was not one unknown — or else everything was unknown.

She was an existence like the moon in the night sky.

With nothing but void around, one could only be lost.

But it was because the earth was there, because the trees were there, because the mountains were there, because the clouds were there, that one could first know one's own place — could first know the height of the sky — and she was a guiding light.

Something that shone plainly visible, and yet in a place forever out of reach — always there, always illuminating you.

There was no meaning in trying to decipher it.

He had no desire to decipher it at all.

"……People are creatures who can be happy while remaining foolish."

Everyone praised Elgeinst as Alberinea's successor.

They called him the greatest of mages, a ruler who could match even the queen.

But Elgeinst, from those days before he was ten, had not changed by a single thing.

And Alberinea had not changed by a single thing either, and called him Nemunemu.

Even now, with a smile.

Great intellect was not where happiness lay.

In the first place, he had not been seeking to illuminate the unknown.

He had simply wanted, knowing it would never be understood, to watch from a little closer by.

The figure of mystery in the apron dress — more beautiful than anything.

The old man with his long hair and beard smiled with satisfaction once more.

He watched the silver hair move in the breeze, and then looked at the violet eyes.

The old man looked on with simply gentle eyes, a fulfilled expression on his face.

The pleasant sensation of his body seeming to melt away.

The strange force pulling at him had grown stronger, and the will to resist had left him entirely.

Alberinea watched this and asked:

"……Why did Nemunemu come here? You said you'd wanted to see Krische for a long time, but……"

"That is all. ——Ah…… no, that's not quite right."

Elgeinst looked up at the sky.

"I solved your mana crystal and heard your message."

He thought of Waltza laughing at him, *are you still a child*.

"…… If Alberinea was still nearby, then the one who solved it would be praised, or so you said."

Truly, he supposed, it was childish.

He thought so without a trace of shame, and felt that this was as it should be.

He knew that she would never laugh at him for something like that.

"Oh, right, I had put that in there."

Alberinea clapped her hands with a *pon* and nodded a few times as if remembering.

For a soldier or warrior it was also a virtue, though it could become a flaw.

Viewed even more broadly: it would have been better for results, and fewer people would have died overall, if some battalion commander had been let die and Gahren had taken over.

*I understand your logic. But I could not leave someone I was capable of saving to die and rise on that. …… Whoever else might forgive it, I would never forgive myself for using base means for the rest of my life.*

*But you——*

*I feel bad for putting you all through hardship. But I am also someone's husband and father. …… Unfitting though it may seem — I want to be a self that my wife and daughter can be proud of.*

In a world full of beasts, Gahren was a cold military man while also clinging to something worthless.

A hunter from the countryside somewhere — the same as Klaze, an entirely ordinary person.

Granmeld too, faced with those words, could only be exasperated, and give up — and as a result Gahren too, because of that softness, ended up leaving the military.

It was probably just a bit of petty harassment from the helpless battalion commander.

The order given to the upright man was a demonstration against a village that had sheltered fleeing Elsren soldiers.

Refuse, and he'd be charged with insubordination — so the man consumed by jealousy told him.

However famous a soldier, a superior's word was absolute.

At least the army as an organization was built on top of those kinds of insane rules.

The enemy harboring soldiers was a serious crime — whatever the case, there was some justification in the order to burn the village, and once there was, he couldn't refuse.

Whatever harsh situations the centurion had passed through without batting an eye — that order was harder than any mission.

A hunter's son who had grown up in the same kind of village.

Perhaps he'd been thinking of home as he gave the order to burn a village.

Gahren retired from the military on that last note.

The soldiers pleaded with him, but learning that his wife had died of illness during the long war with Elsren was salt in the wound.

——So disappeared the centurion who had amassed glittering achievements.

The unit was taken over by Bogan, and the next battle saw that battalion commander also killed.

He didn't know the actual details.

That battalion commander had driven out the soldiers' hero by abusing his authority. The soldiers' eyes on that man were cold.

Unable to bear the surrounding looks, the battalion commander had apparently been desperate to achieve results, and ended up isolated and dead in the end — though it was possible he'd been deliberately isolated.

Bogan, who continued piling up achievements just like Gahren, rose with a rush now that the obstacle was gone — but everyone murmured that the only regret was that Gahren wasn't here.

That said, there had been a real vulnerability.

As when Klaze died, Gahren had been driven to that situation by himself.

*He was too much of a proper human being*, was Granmeld's conclusion.

People were better off having no weaknesses.

Looking over the whole sequence of events, Granmeld's conclusion remained that.

"It's a rational way of thinking, certainly — but it's not a matter of reason. What soldiers aspire to is the heroic epic's heroes."

"……Heroes?"

"Yes — of the highest integrity, laughing in the face of death, and winning all the while. Merely insane doesn't do it; merely rational doesn't do it either."

Telling Norzan this one day, those were the words he got back.

A room typical of Norzan, cleanliness and refinement arm in arm.

A room Granmeld found uncomfortable, swirling wine while thinking back.

"Captain Gahren was, by your acknowledgment too, a noble soldier. A soldier that noble putting up such great achievements — that's why everyone admired and respected him. The same holds for Lord Bogan. …… Which is why the soldiers say, for this commander, I'll entrust my one life."

*Mere roughnecks won't produce that*, Norzan smiled.

"Most people aren't able to throw away their lives the way you are. Mostly they're cowards — and for those men to feel they are themselves characters in a heroic epic, for them to dream that dream, is what makes a unit formidable. You stand above others now — understand that well going forward. …… Unless you're saying you personally can kill ten thousand people."

"Well, that's quite a thoughtful thing to tell me. I'll keep it close to my heart."

"Your attitude never improves. Well, that aside — I called you here for a reason."

Norzan took out a sheet of parchment and placed it on the desk.

"You have the Nea investiture in abbreviated form. Given your achievements, Rinea might be a possibility — but for now never mind that. With the war over, when formally submitting it to the state, you'll be carrying a noble surname from here on. That's the important part — if you have a preference, tell me."

"Surname?"

"If your parents carried a surname, using it is common."

Norzan said with a smile, and Granmeld glared.

Norzan knew well enough what kind of upbringing Granmeld had.

"How kind, Vice-Adjutant. Pleased to have such common sense explained to me. Do you think I have one?"

"You don't seem to."

Norzan was a fundamentally terrible man.

He'd lost count of how many times he'd thought of killing him.

"Otherwise you can decide for yourself, or it's common to use the name of the village you were born in. Best to avoid a city name. No hard rule against it, but the noble managing the place would be unhappy."

He hadn't even decided his own name himself.

A surname was obviously not something that came to him instantly.

That it came to mind was inevitable, one could say.

"……Varkus. Granmeld Varkus — make it that."

Norzan heard this and for some reason opened his eyes wide in apparent surprise.

Then he said *good name*, with a face like he was suppressing a laugh, and wrote it out on the parchment.

Granmeld furrowed his brow.

"……Hey. What's funny?"

"Well…… saying weaknesses are bad, and yet choosing quite an amusing name, I thought."

"……What do you mean?"

Norzan turned his face away, shoulders shaking as he left the room.

Listening carefully, laughter could be heard beyond the door, and then a little later, face straight as ever, he came back with a *pardon me.*

It seemed he somehow felt it a matter of pride not to let others see him laughing, but he was completely failing to conceal it.

Feeling the blood vessel in his forehead pulse and twitch, he called out again: hey.

"Varkus — why that?"

"……The name of a small criminal gang I was in once."

"I see. Did you even ask the origin?"

"Wasn't interested."

*Is that so*, again with a face like he was suppressing a laugh, Norzan looked at Granmeld.

"It's a word from the old tongue of the north. The meaning is said to be 'group' or 'collective,' but the actual nuance is——"

And when Granmeld heard it, his mouth fell open like a fool's.

"……Heh heh — quite a fitting name for a lone wolf who wants no weaknesses, don't you think?"

"——*Woof-woof.*"

"……Oh my."

With Rosine wearying him by asking for the story, a young voice rang up from the garden.

Rosine got off his knee and went to the balcony railing, and Granmeld followed.

Seated on a jade tiger, guards at her side, looking up at them was silver hair.

"……It has been a while, Alberinea."

"Ehehe, since the wedding, hasn't it, Rosine. You look well."

"Yes, thanks to you……"

Rosine bowed deeply, then murmured *woof-woof* in a small voice and shook with laughter.

*Of all things to appear when I was already weary* — Granmeld sighed.

Outside of the battlefield, she was not someone he particularly wanted to see.

"It's been a while, Krische-sama. …… What brings you today?"

"I came on an errand from Selene to Piyo-piyo's place. It's on the way, so I thought I'd call on Woof-woof too."

She hopped and lightly vaulted to the balcony.

Same as ever — seemingly polite yet somehow graceless.

A balcony was apparently to be treated as outside rather than inside someone else's manor.

She usually appeared without any connection to Norzan's affairs — and today was the same.

Granmeld didn't mind, but even if he minded, there wasn't a person who could say anything to this girl.

High-handed in everyday life and in extraordinary circumstances alike, someone more willful than Granmeld was hard to come by, yet even for Granmeld, Krische Christand was beyond him.

Behind the beautiful, adorable, utterly harmless appearance was an existence that was unreason made flesh.

"Would Woof-woof like to come to Piyo-piyo's place too? You look bored."

"……You always do this out of nowhere."

Seemingly thoughtful but actually short-sighted.

Fundamentally thinking of nothing, acting on animal instinct.

Acting on whim and fancy, deciding things unilaterally and stating demands — probably the freest creature in the world.

High-born and still a high-born princess in name, yet the sensibility of a village girl.

She called on aristocratic etiquette and form no more than the next person would stop to call on a neighbor for tea.

"Ehehe, Rosine too."

"I…… well, it would trouble the Margrave if I imposed……"

"'It would be nice to bring Rosine along too,' Piyo-piyo said the other day, so it's probably fine."

"W-well……"

*Right, Woof-woof*, Krische smiled — and Rosine looked up at Granmeld with a troubled face.

He had indeed said so, but *probably fine* hardly left room for *yes, understood*, and there weren't many women bold enough to show up at their husband's superior's house on that basis.

"Ah, um — have you sent word to His Excellency?"

"Probably Selene sent a message. She said to come today."

"……, Ah, is that so."

Not the most reliable of words.

The great continental hero Alberinea's visit.

Incognito or not, there was such a thing as appearances, and they'd presumably make certain preparations.

Two more guests made no practical difference, but some consideration was warranted.

"You must be tired first. The sun's still high — if we go, better to rest here a little first."

"……? Krische isn't particularly tired——"

"……Rosine, write a letter saying we'll come too, and have someone run it."

"Heh. …… Yes, Sir."

Why on earth does this fall to me.

He exhaled, scratched his head.
