# Chapter 301: Reine — II

――She performed the obvious with such ease that people found it unsettling.

The world was full of people who could not even manage the obvious, and she understood quickly that she was the exception.

No one could understand her; they kept their distance.

*"Father, I've been thinking about the trade arrangement with Nilse, and I really don't think it serves our interests in the long run. Going forward, even the iron ore——"*

*"Quiet! Since when do you presume to advise the head of household?! Rather than occupying yourself with such matters, you would do well to learn to close that grating mouth of yours so that other nobles will find you agreeable! I am telling you, Reine — do not breathe a word of this sort outside these walls. Whatever embarrassment a precocious little girl's prattle brings down, it is I who bear it."*

*"……Yes. I'm sorry, Father."*

In her early years she had been foolish enough to try to close the distance between them, but it was useless.

Her father had been born to be a king — a man whose only asset was his pride.

He had neither intelligence nor wisdom, nor any ability to speak of.

He clung to the throne alone, and nothing else.

Alberan at the time was a kingdom in decline.

Even the prestige the founding king Bazarishe had built had lost its luster, and the balance of power between the royal house and the domain lords had collapsed.

The feudal lordship system Bazarishe had established was not in itself a bad thing — as a means of expanding territory, which had been the urgent need of the time, rewarding retainers with land was not a poor idea — but that it would eventually come to this end must have been foreseen by anyone thinking about stability.

Improvements here and there, like drops of water on a hot stone, could be observed from time to time, but they had their limits.

In twenty years at best, in fewer than ten at worst, Alberan would fracture.

In such a situation, where a strong king was demanded, her father was far too incompetent.

And that incompetence endangered even her own life.

She had begun to perceive this dimly at the age of six.

She had made her decision at eight.

Her father, in the interest of self-preservation, wished for Reine to take a husband from among the powerful nobility.

However much the royal house had lost its luster, it was still the royal house.

To the great nobility who had nothing but power, the title of royal blood was still reasonably valuable, and to obtain it was to hold both name and substance over Alberan.

If the heir to the throne were a princess rather than a prince, a marriage could be arranged, and for a man born of a ducal house already mixed with royal blood, it was not difficult to become king by bypassing the order of succession.

A royal house with no prince was enormously attractive to them.

Her father had greatly rejoiced that a princess had been born and not a prince.

Had it been a prince, the prince would have been the foremost heir to the throne, and custom would have compelled her father to name the prince as successor — which, naturally, the other nobles would not have welcomed.

Most would likely conclude that dissolving the nominal royal house and establishing a new dynasty was the simpler path.

In that case, there would naturally be no reason to keep her father alive — but if a powerful noble entered the royal house as a princess's consort, the matter was different.

Inevitably, her father's authority as king of Alberan would be protected by that noble.

Hence, his daughter's name was to be something radiant — the name Reine was her father's light.

He could also have chosen to remain childless and designate an heir, but he had disliked the prospect of civil war.

The current first in line to the throne was the king's younger brother — but in terms of actual power, he ranked fifth from the top.

To designate anyone from second place downward, the most powerful noble at present was the Duke of Gasrel, who stood seventh.

Finding a justification for naming him would be difficult.

What her father wanted most was for the duke's eldest son to enter the royal house as Reine's husband.

Appearances were what mattered.

In essence there was no difference between the two, but there was a great difference between Reine and the heir arriving at a genuine bond and entering into marriage, and designating the heir directly as successor.

Nobles valued their standing.

Of course they would voice dissatisfaction at a designation that bypassed the order of succession — but if it was a marriage to a noble the princess herself had chosen, it had to be celebrated in form, at least.

Even if they harbored grievances, they would lack the justification to raise a banner of revolt.

Her father's conclusion, at least, was not greatly mistaken.

From that point on, Reine resolved to learn the art of camouflage, in order to win favor with the Gasrel house.

Many men disliked a clever woman.

It was better, on the contrary, to appear appropriately foolish; she ceased voicing her own thoughts, and observed what kinds of women were considered attractive.

Fortunately her appearance was fine, and the rest was manner and comportment.

Sweet, pure, gentle at heart — she remade herself from the ground up to appear so.

It must have seemed strange to her father, her mother, and the servants, but she told them she had reformed her character, engaged with them accordingly, and gave them appropriate satisfaction, and in time they came to accept this new Reine.

It was a remarkably simple thing.

She would smile at times, wear a sad expression at times, and show anger at times.

For things she found neither amusing nor sad nor infuriating, performing these in just this way led the people around her to mistake Reine for one of their own kind.

People fear what they cannot understand. They direct hostility toward it, and feel disgust for it.

If she performed within the range they could understand, in a way they could easily understand, most problems resolved themselves.

She moved in earnest at thirteen.

The reason was that she had come to feel — directly — that the people around her perceived her as sufficiently attractive.

*"It has been some time, Duke Gasrel."*

*"Quite so, Your Highness."*

*"I hear word even of Alberl-sama's fine performance the other day. I understand he distinguished himself considerably."*

*"Ha. A chance distinction…… I still cannot match Father."*

*"How admirable. He takes after his father in that. …… I had been worried when I heard that the Duke of Gasrel himself received an arrow wound to the arm. How is your body faring?"*

Reaching out naturally as she inquired to touch Dreyrl's arm, she caught the slight twitch in his reaction.

Alberl was still young, fresh from his first battle — a straightforwardly earnest and handsome young man, one might say, with nothing particularly notable about him.

What mattered was his father, Dreyrl Gasrel.

Known for his valor, he was also a man enslaved to his appetites.

She had known he showed an interest in Reine every time they met.

*"Ha ha, what, an arrow wound or two hardly counts. Given the hardy body forged on the battlefield, I had entirely forgotten I'd even taken an arrow until Your Highness mentioned it."*

*"My, my. But please do take care of yourself. A body like the Duke of Gasrel's cannot be purchased with any amount of gold……ah, I do beg your pardon."*

She withdrew her hand, letting a faint flush color her cheeks as though embarrassed.

Not flattery — a pure, innocent girl genuinely concerned for his well-being had touched him.

That appearance — sweet and pure — is what many men found gratifying.

She sensed Alberl was entranced by the sight of her, and caught the flicker of appetite that crossed Dreyrl's face for just an instant.

*"No, no pardon needed. Though if Your Highness were to attend to me in such a fashion, one might almost wish to take an arrow wound with each campaign."*

*"Aha, thank you for your consideration. …… Actually, Father instructed me to take particular care to help the Duke of Gasrel, newly returned from such a grueling campaign, find some rest and respite…… so I could not stop thinking about the wound. Father does not often say such things to me, and I began to wonder whether the arrow wound might in fact have been a very grave injury."*

*"Oh? His Majesty did?"*

Dreyrl glanced toward her father.

Her father, speaking with another noble a short distance away, caught the glance and offered a light lift of his hand in acknowledgment, smiling.

Her father had not actually said any such thing — but phrasing it this way would lead Dreyrl to infer on his own that this was her father's intent.

It was, at minimum, not an unwelcome development for her father.

*"I see. Then I should like to pay my respects to His Majesty in private after the banquet."*

Reine smiled as though she understood none of this.

What Dreyrl would infer as her father's intent was, in essence, that Reine was an offering to Dreyrl.

If he took the bait, all that remained was to bring Dreyrl to heel.

Reine did not doubt her own ability.

Was it preferable or not — the latter — but the return was more than sufficient.

It was worth it more than enough.

*"Yes, Father will be pleased if you say so."*

As Reine said this, a voice came from her attendant — *My Lady*.

There was nothing more to discuss here.

She gave her farewells to both men with a brief *do excuse me*, and allowed herself to be led away by her attendant's hand.

Alberl had been entranced by Reine until the very end.

Father and son alike appeared to lack good sense — which was remarkably convenient.

*"Is something the matter, Benuure?"*

*"……No, I'm sorry, I was just lost in thought."*

The older attendant — Benuure — looked at Reine with eyes that seemed to worry.

Even the servant who had spent more time at her side than anyone no longer doubted Reine's *guilelessness*.

She must be thinking something like *the poor princess*, Reine thought, and kept up her smile as though she had not noticed.

That very evening, Dreyrl came to Reine's room on a pretext of private business.

He explained at length the state of the royal house, laid out the precariousness of Reine's position, and stated that he had obtained her father's permission before making his demand.

When she nodded with a feigned expression of fear, Dreyrl pressed her down like a beast.

――In name, she became Alberl Gasrel's betrothed.

In practice, she became Dreyrl Gasrel's mistress.

Before the servants she wore the expression of a woman putting on a brave face, while letting Alberl believe she harbored genuine affection for him.

For some time already she had been secretly giving her father a slow-acting drug to weaken him, but he was now no longer of use.

She adjusted the drugs accordingly, and put him to bed with an illness of unknown cause.

Whether weakness of body begat weakness of mind, the father who had been so overbearing called for Reine nearly every day since taking to his bed, repenting and apologizing for what he had done to her.

On those occasions, she wore the face of a daughter who loved her father.

*It could not be helped*, she whispered to him, *I love you*, and her father smiled in relief.

Directing him toward passing the throne to her was simple enough.

Dreyrl too was driven by appetite, but at heart he was cautious and cowardly.

If she whispered the right words in bed, his feelings would quickly flow toward the option of securing stability rather than seizing the throne.

Dreyrl was satisfied with Reine's body; he had a mild taste for cruelty, but by that point Reine had grown accustomed to the acts, and performing compliance gave her no trouble.

If she made it plain in word and manner that she could not defy Dreyrl, Dreyrl, like her father before him, without thinking overmuch about it, gave Reine leave to take the queen's seat.

He had the illusion that granting her real power would change nothing, that she would move as he directed — and Reine put on the appearance of his puppet.

Without knowing he was being guided by her, he poured the enormous assets of the Gasrel family into the royal house, believing himself to be reviving a rusted throne he had claimed for his own.

By then he no doubt thought himself king already.

Father and son, both fools.

She managed Dreyrl by making him believe Reine was his woman, and managed Alberl by making him believe Reine was a wretched wife being toyed with by Dreyrl.

Playing the tragic princess of a fallen royal house, showing tears and begging forgiveness for the infidelity, saying that he alone was the one she loved — Alberl believed it readily, swore on his name that he would do anything to help Reine, and whispered his love.

His head was filled with the heroic tales of fantasy stories — father and son alike, neither had a brain.

She had found it inwardly hilarious.

Dreyrl poured great sums into the royal house, and Alberl likewise enriched it from below the surface, taking care not to be found out by Dreyrl.

When Reine had gathered sufficient power, she had Dreyrl assassinated, and once the absorption of the Gasrel house was complete, she disposed of the now equally useless Alberl.

By that point there were naturally those who had grown suspicious of Reine, but the preparations were finished.

No one could defeat Reine, who now held the title of queen and the power befitting it.

There was no shortage of those who would wag their tails for the price of being taken to bed, and no domain lord could defeat the army Reine commanded.

Uprisings crushed by force, authority concentrated in her own hands — the royal house was unassailable.

There was no longer anyone in the world who could threaten Reine.

――She happened to notice it, at the very moment she noticed it.

What exactly did she want, in a world like this?

What was she after — what was she alive for, in a world like this?

"Kaa-sama, I've finished these documents."

"Have you. Well done, Reine."

"Ehehe……"

She smiled at Reine, who had come to sit on her lap and seek affection while she occupied the chair of the study, and leafed through the documents her daughter had brought.

Her daughter did not make mistakes. Just as Reine trusted her own abilities without doubt, she did not doubt her daughter's excellence.

It was simply a task for memorization.

When she had finished looking them over, she lifted her daughter, held her close, and stroked her back.

*Radiant. Light.*

The daughter she had named Reine, the same name as herself, possessed the same high intelligence — and in some respects, she seemed to surpass Reine herself.

And yet, in one sense, something of a disappointment.

She was fourteen now, and she was still inflexible, her thinking still childlike.

*Pure*, if one put it charitably, but she was a little — *simple*.

Perhaps like a person keeping a pet, Reine thought.

It probably was something close to that, yet at least the sight of her daughter seeking affection struck her as sweet, and holding her like this settled something inside her.

In some respects her daughter was decisively inferior to Reine, yet she did not dislike those very failings — she found them endearing.

It might take more time yet before her daughter came to understand her.

Perhaps it was partly that she had grown up in a sheltered environment, unlike Reine.

But that did not mean she wished to forcibly change her daughter somehow.

*Strange*, Reine thought.

She who hated others' incompetence more than anyone felt no displeasure at that very quality in her daughter, and only in her daughter.

Intelligence her only asset, no wisdom, foolish and childlike.

*Kaa-sama, kaa-sama* — she rubbed up against her, showing affection with utter lack of guard.

Carried away by each momentary desire, impulsive and artless and guileless — but the time she spent with that daughter was the only time Reine could think of nothing, and simply cherish what her daughter was.

A light broke through, like a sudden clearing——into the dark, gloomy emotions that swirled in her head.

A knock sounded at the door and she permitted entry.

The one who appeared was the senior head of servants, Benuure.

"Forgive the interruption, Your Majesty."

Benuure, who by now ought properly to be called an old woman, looked at the daughter with a slight concern.

Reine felt a touch of displeasure, and stroked her daughter, telling her the work was done for the day.

"Alright, then Reine will go see Fiiri!"

"……Yes."

Fiiri Neeras. Born of a cadet branch of the Gasrel family.

Her mother had died of illness; her father had been killed in battle during the civil war; she had no siblings.

She had fine looks, and had been intended as a gift for a noble, but when Benuure told her that it seemed a waste of the girl's talent, Reine agreed to put her to work as a servant — and that was twenty years ago.

She made a light inspection of the backgrounds of servants herself, but if they came back clean she allowed Benuure a degree of latitude, and anyone would do as long as minimum trust could be placed in them.

As one might expect of someone Benuure had specifically requested, the work was not poor.

She had Benuure's trust as well, and was effectively the number two among the servants.

When she had been deciding whom to appoint as her daughter's attendant, those had been her reasons for choosing her, and she had rated the girl accordingly — though now she greatly regretted it.

A meddlesome, insufferable woman.

She had separated them, citing the bad influence on her daughter, but her daughter still favored the woman.

She had been teaching her daughter nothing but worthless things.

*"Please do not put foolish ideas into that child's head. …… I will be changing her attendant starting tomorrow. You should be grateful you are not being turned out of the palace."*

*"……I am grateful for your generosity. As Your Majesty commands."*

What she could not stand, above all else, were her eyes.

She prostrated herself in feigned submission, while looking at Reine with eyes that somehow seemed to pity her.

*"What is the meaning of that look?"*

*"……Nothing."*

*"Speak. Before I change my mind about wanting to see your face."*

At that she showed not fear but a troubled expression.

A reaction that made one wonder whether she understood who was standing before her.

*"Then……forgive the presumption, but may I ask a question. …… What is it, exactly, that Your Majesty wishes to become of Elsreine-sama?"*

*"Wishes to become……?"*

*"Elsreine-sama is wise, yet dangerously pure. Each day she takes interest in many things, and troubles herself over a great many of them. If what Fiiri thought was best to offer her does not accord with Your Majesty's wishes, of course the fault is Fiiri's."*

She did not know her own place.

Standing before someone who could have her head with a single word.

*"But in that case, if Your Majesty would be so good as to tell Fiiri what you intend…… I would ask that you tell me. I, Fiiri, swear on my name that I wish for Reine-sama's happiness above all things, and am prepared to stake my life for it…… for a servant of no standing to presume to ask about Her Majesty's heart is the height of impertinence. And yet, if it serves Reine-sama's happiness, I most certainly intend to be of use in that way, and to show my loyalty through it."*

The head now bent low — what expression was on that face?

*"My command to you is: put no more ideas into that child's head. …… Go."*

A meddlesome woman. A vexing woman.

Simply, infuriating. The very servants who had once been afraid of her daughter had somehow begun treating her like a perfectly ordinary child, before she had noticed.

Casually, as though they were her own *kind*.

Her daughter also labored under the same illusion.

The work of that woman, no doubt.

Her daughter had none of the dexterity to build human relationships with calculation.

The one pulling the strings from behind was that woman.

If she had wanted power, it would almost have been endearing.

But she did not, which was what made her so troublesome.

"……What is it, Benuure?"

She stopped her thinking and spoke.

Every time she thought about that woman, nothing came of it but mounting irritation.

"……Ha. Seil-sama requests an audience with Your Majesty."

"His purpose?"

"He wishes to speak with Your Majesty directly, he says."

"I believe I made myself clear. I have no interest. Listen to what he has to say and keep me adequately informed. Tell him I may grant a modest request or two."

"……Your Majesty."

Seil was the first prince in name.

He was ostensibly a son of hers, but the correct way to put it was probably *the first of her failures*.

She never met her son or daughters by blood except at ceremonial occasions, never went to call on them, and even kept separate lodgings.

She had no interest, as she said, and while they were given lives befitting a prince or princess, they were bothersome presences.

She had thought some time ago of engineering a revolt and disposing of them, and had even given them appropriate lands for that purpose, but they had been slow to move.

"……Might you not give even just a little of the affection you direct toward Elsreine-sama to Seil-sama and the others? To Benuure it seems……too pitiable. Seil-sama and the others love Your Majesty——"

"I've grown quite weary of your sermons, Benuure. …… And as for love——"

Reine shook with quiet laughter and looked at Benuure.

"Is it the same reason a fledgling cries out? Those children are simply selling their flattery — doing so that I will give them more feed. I give them adequate feed, and adequate shelter. If I were to give them still more simply because they cry out for it, they would merely become more and more presumptuous."

Benuure let her now-white golden hair sway and cast her gaze down.

"Creatures without Reine's abilities, who do nothing but seek feed. I am already giving them considerable care, extending this charity to them…… and that sort of charity is what you and the others call love, is it not?"

"……You are mistaken, My Lady."

Benuure gripped her hand into a fist.

There was a faint sheen of tears in her eyes.

"I have served at My Lady's side since she was small. …… Benuure knows better than anyone how arduous the path My Lady has walked, and how much she was forced to kill inside herself, and how much she was compelled to abandon along the way."

"Aha, you only believe you know. You and the others think about things too conveniently."

Reine pointed at her own temple in a mocking gesture; Benuure shook her head.

"At least as a young child, what My Lady directed toward the late king was no illusion — it was a certain truth. …… Knowing the My Lady of that time, I pledged my life to My Lady's service, swore on my name, and have been at your side thus."

"You were merely begging for feed as well, like the others. How foolish."

"That is not so. Never, and it was not foolish either. …… What My Lady sought was not something born of calculation — it was undeniably love from the late king."

She laughed in amusement, and Benuure asked why she was laughing.

"Would a man like the late king, who thought of nothing but his own self-preservation, have given My Lady something in return for her crying out? My Lady is a perceptive person — surely she would have understood right away that it was futile. And yet, she continued. Was it not because she hoped? That the late king might come to love My Lady?"

"Benuure."

Reine sighed, and glared at Benuure.

"You grate on my ears. …… You are a servant, and I am the queen. Whatever you may say, I will not do anything more for those children, and that opinion will not change. Saying *I have no interest* was also too roundabout. …… To be direct: they are an unpleasantness."

"……An unpleasantness?"

"Yes. The idea that such defective things were born of me — it is a stain on my life. …… What I wanted was only Reine. …… In a world full of fools like you, the only one who will surely understand me — that child alone."

Yes, that child at least would surely understand her.

My dearest Reine. My darling.

The only treasure in the world.

With that alone, everything else meant nothing.

"You had best leave before you displease me any further. …… If you intend to say another worthless word like this again, consider that you have no place here."

She told her, and returned her gaze to the documents.

Several tasks remained to be processed.

Fireseeds were scattered here and there, the palace included.

She had wanted to let them flare up once before the coming eastern advance, to sweep out the disruptive elements in one go.

To that end she had deliberately squeezed the populace, and had made an example by burning several villages that could not pay their taxes.

No matter how much she ground the common people down, Reine was the king, and the only thing required of her subjects was whether they obeyed her commands or did not — there were only those two choices.

A great many fools who raised their swords against her under the petty pretexts of justice and righteousness would also be culled in this way.

Seil's matter was connected to all this.

That the other princes were certain to form the core of the uprising was also part of the reason.

Since there was no possibility of inheriting the throne, and no basis for rivalry over succession, the princes got along well — and she could see that Seil was moving to prevent the uprising before it occurred.

He evidently failed to understand that resolving the problem that way would only drag things out.

What she needed was the justification to kill the troublesome princes — if no uprising occurred, it only drew things out.

He was, rather, prolonging the problem, which was nothing but a nuisance.

"Benuure, you are distracting me. …… I have been made irritable by foolish talk."

"……, Yes."

Benuure, understanding that Reine no longer wished to speak, left the room after a moment.

Reine did not so much as glance in that direction.

# Chapter 302: Reine — III

Fiiri's room had become the two of them's atelier.

Not only painting but gathering various pigments, exploring and studying new techniques.

From the softness of watercolors to the beauty of contrasting light and shadow in oils — a variety of paintings now covered the walls, and a whole additional room had been converted into storage.

How to paint, how to use color.

Nearly every day they would sit and talk at length — this part here is nice, that part there is beautiful in just this way.

"Aha…… what a truly lovely painting."

"Ehehe, thank you so much. But…… should I perhaps try layering in a few more different colors……?"

Facing the two of them:

The oil painting Reine had done showed Fiiri and the servants, along with two smiling Reines.

In a forest clearing, a table and chairs; through gaps in the trees, dappled sunlight filtering down.

It was something Reine had painted, taking as reference a watercolor Fiiri had once made.

"Hmm…… at this point, one might say it comes down to preference, really. It would be perfectly fine to finish it as it is."

The painting looked nothing like a fictitious scene.

It was painted in vivid, precise colors, as though a real moment had been excised from life.

Complete as finished work — no one else could reproduce it.

Such had always been the case with her paintings.

"And yet, trying to add more color could also be interesting."

Reine could paint nothing but perfect things.

But what she sought lay in a slightly different place.

"……Color."

"For instance…… light. Something like emphasizing the expression of light."

Reine favored vivid oil paints and worked with precise, meticulous detail.

But Fiiri, if anything, preferred watercolors with a soft, ambiguous quality that left something blurred.

Reine was captivated by the ambiguity in Fiiri's paintings; Fiiri was captivated by the vividness of Reine's paintings that seemed to cut out the world itself.

By now Reine was clearly more technically accomplished, but the nature of the two of them remained unchanged.

Sitting beside her in a chair, tapping the back of the brush against her lip in thought, Fiiri pondered —

and Reine did the same, tapping the back of her brush against her lip as she tilted her head.

"Light, do you think?"

"Yes, it might be lovely to try bringing out the expression of light a little more."

Fiiri gazed at the painting and smiled.

"Reine means *radiant thing*. …… Surely Her Majesty named Elsreine-sama with many thoughts and feelings, choosing her own name, Reine. If something of that sort could be expressed in this painting of Elsreine-sama's — I think it would become something even lovelier, and so I thought……"

"Many thoughts and feelings……?"

"Yes, of course emotions are difficult to express in words."

Fiiri ran her brush through empty air.

Slowly, gently.

"But I think that sometimes, in a small touch of the brush or something of that kind, such things can be expressed. …… What draws me to painting is precisely that I want to express what cannot be put into words…… if such things could be painted, I think a single painting can speak far more eloquently than any exchange of words, and express so many different things."

Saying this, she gave a wry smile at the sight of Reine making a face and thinking hard.

"Aha, I'm sorry. I'm saying something rather hard to follow, am I not."

"……No, Reine is simply being a little slow."

"My, that isn't so at all."

As she spoke, Fiiri tickled Reine's cheek with the clean tip of her brush.

*That tickles*, Reine laughed, leaning back — then took hold of Fiiri's arm and swung herself sideways into Fiiri's lap.

Then she gazed at her own painting and thought, and nodded.

"Hmm, Reine will think about it a little more."

"Do. But…… truly, the matter of preference — as far as I'm concerned, it's already as lovely a painting as could be, so please don't trouble yourself over it too much. These days Elsreine-sama is the teacher and I am the student…… aha, this is just the pupil speaking out of turn."

"Fiiri's paintings are incredibly beautiful. Reine still cannot paint anything like them at all."

"You are too kind…… but I have so little technique, I'm really just fudging various things."

"Reine still doesn't understand those fudges at all, so Fiiri is really still Reine's teacher."

At Reine's petulant expression, Fiiri gave a wry smile, poked her puffed cheek, and shook with laughter.

It was true that Fiiri could not render things as precisely as Reine, yet Fiiri's paintings had a strange charm.

The other servants, upon seeing Reine's paintings, would look astonished and offer praise — but when they saw Fiiri's paintings, they showed a different reaction from when they looked at Reine's.

There were those who, looking at a single landscape painting, would well up with tears saying it had reminded them of home — and surely Reine was not the only one who felt that *that* was something remarkable.

More eloquent than any exchange of words.

Something that could not be put into words was certainly there, in Fiiri's choice of colors, in her brushwork, in the paintings she completed.

Compared to that, Reine's paintings were much simpler.

She was just pulling images from memory and combining them like pieces of a puzzle.

What Fiiri was trying to paint was surely in a far deeper place than what Reine reached.

"In any case…… I'm quite sure Her Majesty will be pleased. This painting feels as though Elsreine-sama's many feelings are dwelling in it."

"……Does it?"

"Yes. Honestly, I was surprised when Elsreine-sama said she wanted to give the painting to Her Majesty. Until now you had always been painting things just to please me."

Reine's cheeks reddened, and Fiiri smiled quietly.

"Her Majesty has no great interest in paintings. But Elsreine-sama knows that full well, and yet even so, she wishes to please Her Majesty — she is trying to paint a painting to give to Her Majesty. That is something very difficult…… and it is precisely because of that, I think, that it is something wonderful."

"……Wonderful."

"Yes, very. I'm sure the feelings Elsreine-sama has put into this painting will reach Her Majesty."

When Reine nodded, Fiiri stroked her gently.

*I hope they reach her*, Reine thought.

Surely it was just a misaligned button.

Her mother was still admirable, and excellent, and she respected and loved her.

But regrettably, her mother disliked everyone except Reine.

She had said that everyone other than her mother and Reine was foolish, and that they were different creatures.

It wasn't that Reine could not understand her mother's logic at all.

The world did contain bad people and terrible people — Reine knew that now.

People who raised taxes on their own authority and lined their own pockets; people who fabricated crimes to push others down and trip them up; people who took pleasure in tormenting others.

As Reine came to help her mother with her work and to know more of the outside world, she learned that there were all kinds of people — but not only those kinds.

Fiiri was not like that, and neither were the servants here.

And surely there were many such people all over the world.

Her mother was simply mistaken, Reine thought.

# Chapter 303: Reine — IV

Sacred. Demonic.

Less *attractive* than as though placed under a spell.

Alberan — the nation governed by a child of the gods.

# Chapter 304: Reine — V

The revolt came a month later.

Where a degree of failure and mishandling in governance might be overlooked, military personnel could not be afforded that latitude.

That was the rule of the military, of the battlefield, and that leniency would directly cause losses among the soldiers, and losses of military strength on the battlefield were irreparable.

Her mother's explanation was clear.

When wielding a sword, even Reine could not win if her hands and feet moved however they pleased.

She had hoped to do things amicably if possible, but a battlefield was a place of killing.

She absolutely could not lose, so efficiency had to be pursued.

Casualties were inevitable.

Even if she considered the rights and wrongs of executing a hundred people, if those hundred people would cause the deaths of thousands, tens of thousands, then of course they should be executed — and the military had its regulations built on that premise.

It was not an enjoyable place, nor was it comfortable, but she had no particular doubts about her mother's words.

It was arithmetic with people as the variables.

She would calculate an estimate of the ability of the corps, the battalion, the century, the individual soldier, derive their value, and eliminate those where the disadvantage did not balance the cost.

Battle too was the same as a board game — a simple matter of adding and subtracting from estimates of one's own losses and the opponent's.

They claimed to fight for the people, and yet they let people die in pointless battles.

Broken swords and broken spears, armored corpses scattered here and there.

# Chapter 305: Reine — VI

The difference in mass between them was clearly etching superiority and inferiority between them.

"……This is my victory, brother. I may have been losing overall including our practice bouts, but……"

His right arm — likely broken, or at least cracked.

Internal organs taking the impact too — he could rise, but he could no longer fight.

Seil's defeat — as a warrior, and as a general as well.

Both armies' fighting had been evenly matched.

At least Seil had worked out his tactics on the premise of winning, and at least were this a battle on a board, Seil's victory would have been certain without doubt.

The soldiers' morale — that was the clear difference.

The common people suffering under oppressive rule.

Rougran, who had risen to save them, had the moral high ground, and the soldiers fought with all they had, to the death.

Against them, the royal army — even Seil, did not know what he was fighting for.

Seil at least had *for his mother* as his reason.

But all that was in his soldiers was greed for gold, or fear of his mother.

From anyone's eyes, it was obvious on whose side righteousness stood.

The one step he should have pushed forward he could not push, the one step he should not have been pushed back he was — and so Rougran had driven into the main camp and was standing there.

If this were a story, Rougran would have cut down his brother and become a new king.

Surely he would have reigned as a good king.

But this was not a heroic tale — it was cruel reality.

From behind, in the enemy's line — the soldiers parted, and what appeared was golden hair tied up high like a horse's tail.

A longsword at the hip, a military spear in hand — bearing no helmet, in silver armor with the royal crest.

A small-framed girl in armor, accompanied by soldiers, appeared.

Both Seil and Rougran had noticed, shortly after this day's battle began, that her army had appeared from the rear.

This battle had no meaning.

Seil's and Rougran's fight had been near-equal — and into that situation, a whole army had appeared, led by Elsreine.

Rougran had no longer any hope of victory.

In this situation, there was no choice but to withdraw the army — and yet Rougran had pressed forward, and Seil too had not played for time, but faced him head-on.

If only his nobler-than-noble younger brother's death could come by his own hand.

But that wish went unanswered, and time ran out.

"It has been a while, Rougran-niisama."

"……Aye, it has, Elsreine."

"Given the situation, Rougran-niisama has no chance of winning. Fighting would only be pointless — it would only produce needless deaths. Why not surrender?"

Elsreine said it without agitation, entirely composed.

"……Are all three dead?"

"Yes, only Rougran-niisama remains now."

"……I see."

Rougran narrowed his eyes, and a smile came over his manly face.

"Rougran-niisama was the ringleader of the revolt — ordinarily he would be quartered, and his wife and children would be hanged. But I will try asking kaa-sama to spare the family, and to have Rougran-niisama end with hanging as well. What do you say?"

"……A welcome proposal."

"Well, Rougran-niisama will still die regardless of everything…… if you prefer, you could also die in the form of a duel. Honestly, for Reine, as long as unnecessary deaths among the soldiers don't occur, either is fine."

Rougran gave a wry smile and turned his back, putting distance between them.

Then he turned back to face Elsreine and assumed his stance.

"……Surprising, that you would show such consideration. But…… Mother is not lenient. Rasria and my son understand — if they hear that I lost, they will take their own lives. I made my resolution when I chose to stand here."

"……Is that so."

"I accept the duel. ——Second Prince, Rougran Age Vel Nilkrinea Alberan, declares! All soldiers present on this field — stay your blades!! By this name and the royal blood within it, I swear a fair and proper duel with the fourth princess, Elsreine Alberan!!"

A great voice like a roar to the heavens.

The air seemed to shudder in response, and the sounds of battle that had still been ringing out began to fade under that voice.

"Sacred royal duel, by the royal blood that runs in our veins — let this be the final bloodshed of this battlefield, and let none defile it. Let the sole survivor command the allegiance of all!! Those who defile this shall know the divine punishment of the god of valor, Koreis!!"

He sang out the oath aloud, and once more Rougran turned to Elsreine.

"I also wanted to settle things with brother — and once with you, too, I had wished to fight. You, the sister our mother uniquely loved, Elsreine."

"……Reine doesn't really enjoy this much…… I'll try to make it as painless as possible."

"Magnificent confidence. But don't be careless——!!"

Faster than a charging bull.

A charge that seemed impossible for someone wearing heavy armor.

With the great shield facing forward, it was as though a mass of steel had been launched.

The swordsmanship called the Rolka style was in origin a defensive style.

It deflects the opponent's advance with the left and takes the initiative after the counter to cut them down.

But Rougran, who had trained his warrior's body as far as it could go and wrapped it in heavy armor, was now unmatched even in a full frontal charge — even against that of a magical beast.

Whatever counterattack came, the great shield would deflect it, and if he retreated the shield would crush him, and if he evaded the greatsword would swing from that powerful arm.

There was no room to target the slightest gap.

With the great shield blocking the line of sight, Rougran cleverly used his armor's steel to seal off his opponent's blade.

Rougran was a great beast of steel.

He was already beyond the realm of humans, not a warrior that any one technique could overcome.

No one watching could see a future where the small-framed Elsreine defeated him.

At minimum, Elsreine's path to victory was a war of movement — using her small frame to her advantage, tenaciously drawing out an opening from the gapless Rougran to take his back.

But Elsreine faced him head-on, taking her stance with the spear right there.

Even Seil, who knew Elsreine's ability, felt she was being careless.

Against Rougran, a test of strength was madness.

But — Elsreine made a gentle forward lean.

And over that came, in the space of a single thought, a layered tone of steel.

By the time anyone noticed, she had slipped through the steel colossus, and Elsreine was standing behind Rougran, empty-handed.

"……ah……"

The massive body dropped to one knee — the military spear had passed through the great shield of steel as though it were cloth, pierced the armor at the chest, and the spear's head had broken through all the way to Rougran's back.

It was no special spear.

A sturdy though otherwise ordinary mass-produced eight-shaku military spear no different from what the soldiers used.

And it had skewered the great steel shield — the one that had been repelling Seil's blade — and the armor, with stunning ease.

Everyone was lost for words.

Rougran stared blankly at the spear buried in his own chest — and then for one instant, he turned his gaze to Seil.

With a faint smile at the corner of his lips, he fell forward with the spear still through him.

As Elsreine's adjutant called out her victory aloud, Seil merely watched his brother's figure, and watched the figure of Elsreine who stood quietly.

She wore an expressionless face that showed neither the joy of victory nor the grief of killing her brother.

She directed the cold gaze so like her mother's at the people around her.

Had he asked her how she viewed the current situation, she had been vague in her answer.

When told that Elsreine would end up killing her brothers, she had said *so she would*, sorrowfully.

She had been trusted by Benuure more than anyone.

Sharp-minded, closer to her mother than Seil.

She surely knew his mother more deeply than he did.

But in her, there was no sign of any intention to act.

She sat with perfect posture in her chair, and incongruously with her careful demeanor, she was like water.

Press hard and she would yield however far; cup her in your hands and she would rest in your palms.

The words she gave him in answer were all uncertain and vague.

*"……Have you no wish to change the current state of things?"*

*"……With respect…… Your Highness has been saying strange things for some time now. Fiiri is a servant…… there is nothing she wishes for other than her mistress's happiness."*

*"Even if Mother is…… mad?"*

If he asked, she would once more smile vaguely.

It was the look of someone watching a troubled child.

*"Why do you say Her Majesty is mad?"*

*"……Because if she were not, she would not deliberately incite her own sons to revolt and dispose of them. I cannot think that she has human feeling."*

*"Is that so? Her Majesty is merely…… a pure person. If anything, it might be the rest of us who are mad."*

Fiiri narrowed her eyes and traced the rim of the teacup.

*"That she invited Your Highness, whom she had not seen for so long, into her room is likely because she heard out Benuure-sama's wish, is it not? Had Her Majesty truly been a person without feeling, she would not have done such a thing."*

*"That is……"*

*"Would a person without feeling dote so on Elsreine-sama? To say that a person who has closed their heart is mad — then I and Your Highness and others are the same. There is no difference in having closed one's heart with reasoning."*

*It is only a difference of degree*, she continued.

*"Surely she was someone like Elsreine-sama once. Opening her heart to all she saw, without the armor of reasoning, defenselessly pure. …… But this world is a painful place for someone that pure to live. The reason people close their hearts is because they know the pain of being hurt…… to call that madness is rather a rough way of putting it."*

*"……But even so——"*

*"Your Highness and I are in different positions. I cannot claim to understand the suffering of not receiving Her Majesty's love. …… The frustration with the current state of things, the feeling of injustice — those are Your Highness's own. The breadth of the world we see, the place we stand in, everything is different. Besides……"*

*I am a servant*, she told him.

With clear blue eyes.

*"Of course, I hope for the day when the heart Her Majesty has closed will once again open. And also that someone like Elsreine-sama will not be hurt in the time ahead. …… But all I wish for is such happiness as lies close at hand. Watching over the two of them, believing in them — that alone is my role……"*

Seil held back the words he had been about to say, and looked downward.

Untouched tea was reflecting his own face.

*"……No matter how badly Her Majesty may be spoken of and looked down upon — I will only believe and wish, ten years, twenty years…… or far beyond that, that the world she longs for will come to be seen. If such a day comes, for that end…… I will accept anything Her Majesty may do. At least I…… believe that world will be a more beautiful place than anything."*

He looked at her smile.

Red-glimmering golden hair — the eyes that peered through it were like gemstones.

*"……Why can you say that with such certainty?"*

*"Because I do not doubt the love Her Majesty directs toward Elsreine-sama. …… If Her Majesty loves Elsreine-sama, and if she wishes for Elsreine-sama's happiness, then the world created through that will surely be a gentle, wonderful paradise."*

That transcendent bearing made him think of his mother.

At minimum, what she saw was something different from what he could see.

She was not afraid of that mother — the one before whom everyone trembled and prostrated themselves.

And she coldly, mercilessly, had cut away all else.

Surely she was not ignorant of the people's suffering.

The people of conscience slain at his mother's order; Benuure who had given her life for his mother — she covered them all with the single word *sacrifice* and calmly wished for his mother's and Elsreine's happiness.

She was not on Seil's side.

*"What is it that Your Highness desires?"*

*"……I"*

*"Is it to save the suffering common people? Is it to save Rougran-dono and the others? Or Her Majesty…… or perhaps Your Highness yourself?"*

Those graceful lips pressed to the teacup — she gazed downward quietly.

A glimpse of the glossy tongue dancing at the rim, and his gaze was drawn astray.

Words wound around his body like tangled thread.

*"……People's reach has its limits. Even Her Majesty's is the same. And things you want are not always obtained even by throwing yourself at them. …… Your Highness should think once more — what it is you truly desire…… I think you ought to consider that."*

*"……, Cut them away, you mean?"*

If that was what he wanted, she said with a wry smile.

*"The world is unfair, unreasonable, and cruel. …… Within it, what everyone has equally is only the right to wish for happiness. Your Highness has his, I have mine…… the one who should answer that question is not me."*

Then she turned her gaze to the window where the sun had begun to lean, and rose.

*"……I only pray that the path ahead leads to the same place."*

――*That is all I pray for.*

She continued with those words, and what was in her eyes as she did — what she saw.

At least Seil did not know then, and was still at a loss now.

"……Come in?"

Seil, standing still at the entrance to the tent, saw Elsreine set down her brush and tilt her head.

*It's nothing*, he shook his head, and went to sit in the chair at the center, looking around at the beautiful paintings placed here and there.

Paintings no human hand seemed capable of making — paintings as though crafted by a god.

That she painted had surprised him, but the finished paintings themselves held no surprise.

Elsreine embodied with ease the ideals of ordinary people, with her talent.

The clearing-up after the battle was complete, for the moment.

The burial of the dead and the processing of prisoners — originally it had been almost entirely their work.

Among the twenty thousand Elsreine commanded, there were no casualties. In the battles before coming here, two armies, three brothers had been killed, and the loss of soldiers was a mere three thousand.

The reinforcement army she had brought to this battlefield had arrived in full strength and fully supplied, and against Rougran's army there had been no clash — the duel had ended things.

The reason she was here when he had expected her to go home immediately was apparently the prompting of her adjutant.

Seil's army was exhausted from the battle with Rougran.

The battle was over, but this land far to the east of Wulfenite was an unstable region to begin with.

Most of it had risen together with Rougran, and it could not yet be called settled.

If urged that it was appropriate for both armies to cooperate in dealing with the aftermath to guard against any contingency, Seil — having lost much of his strength — could not say no.

Her adjutant, Gorzarius Rukazan, was known to be bold, but apparently more cautious than expected.

At least the sight of Elsreine placing him in a valued adjutant role had been apparent over these past few days — but somehow, he still could not fully trust him.

Not that there was anything specific — perhaps a kind of aversion or distaste.

Most of the nobles with genuine conscience had remonstrated with his mother and been killed.

Most of the nobles remaining were fence-sitting opportunists — at least those who had silently watched that.

Even so, to say of someone you barely knew that he too was an opportunist was unreasonable — yet at minimum, he could not form a favorable impression.

A crude soldier who kept his distance from politics — that rang a little too powerful a note for his standing.

For someone born of a baronial house, he had risen well.

――No, this was no time to be thinking about such things.

He sighed and began:

"……Let me apologize again for the other day. You were right, that was taking out my frustrations…… I was a little on edge about Mother."

"Reine isn't really…… it was just that I thought it was too hard on Fiiri, that was all. Reine doesn't want to quarrel with Seil-niisama either."

"……Same here. Even knowing the logic, I fall into that sometimes. I'll try to see to it that it doesn't happen again."

Elsreine nodded while preparing tea.

Still somewhat displeased, but tolerating it — seen like this, she was entirely a child.

At least he knew she was an artless, kind girl.

If she could be called mad at all, it was only that a girl who had killed four brothers could still live as a child just as artlessly — that much, at most.

She had taken Rougran's life without emotion, and showed no sign of carrying that.

Her fundamental values were different from Seil's.

Was it madness he felt for it? Perhaps that came from his own confused feelings.

She had simply cut down an enemy general on the battlefield.

What she had done was only that, and in essence it was right, and the result of a duel — it was something to be praised, and to blame her for not grieving was unreasonable.

He could understand it logically, and yet his feelings would not yield — that was how he felt about her and about his mother.

A mother who spent her son's life with the single phrase *for future stability*.

And yet, Rougran and the others, who turned their blades on the kingdom in the end, were in the wrong.

After that, if the kingdom's stability followed, they were nothing but rebels who defied a rare fine ruler.

At minimum, the people's suffering was not going to continue forever.

Even if every person had shown blind compliance to his mother without raising a banner of revolt, simply by reason of gain and loss, his mother would certainly have stopped it before the country was exhausted.

His mother was truly a statesman worthy of the name Alberan, and the finest leader in the world.

Everything had been arranged so that in the end it would all come out well — his mother would surely build a perfect kingdom.

He had that conviction in him, and yet only his feelings would not allow acceptance.

The suffering was simply there.

"……I don't understand you or Mother."

"……?"

"I do understand in part. The reasons for inciting this civil war, and everything else — the logic of it I can follow to a degree. Only…… my feelings won't accept it."

Pouring tea into a teacup, Elsreine looked his way with a puzzled air.

Through the gaps in the golden hair, violet eyes shone without cloudiness.

"……I am proud to have been born his mother's son. She is a fine ruler worthy of that, and surely she will make this country richer and more radiant than ever before. I have no intention of doubting that, and you — there is no one more suitable to stand at such a mother's side. You have the ability. I had thought I knew that, long ago."

Seil had been blessed with more talent than most.

And he had, without being arrogant about it, accumulated practice that was blood-soaked.

And yet, all of that, the girl before him transcended with as little effort as drawing breath.

Elsreine was truly one among the lineage of gods — she was his mother's child.

No matter how much Seil wished or hoped, what she had been born with from the start, he did not have.

"The truth was, I wanted to stand at Mother's side the way you do. …… So I was jealous of you for understanding Mother, and envious. That is my honest feeling."

"……You're jealous of Reine?"

"Yes. …… At least, it's not that I dislike you. It must have felt cold, the way we and the others have treated you. I can't say for certain about the others, but…… at least what lay at the root was a feeling similar to this."

*I'm sorry*, he said in a single word.

He had not been in the wrong, in the end.

She had simply been born with it — what his mother sought.

"It isn't just the other day — saying *forgive me for everything up to now as well* would be a convenient thing, so I won't say it. But…… for what Mother needs…… I want to do what I can, to the best of my ability. …… For Rougran and the others who were sacrificed as well."

Elsreine watched Seil and seemed to think for a moment.

Stirring the tea, into which plenty of milk and honey had been poured, with a teaspoon, she dropped her gaze.

"Saying that…… all Reine can say is, *is that so*. Why say it only now? If Seil-niisama thought that, he should have shown it in his manner from the start. If he had, Benuure might not have had to die."

"……Yes."

"Reine heard from kaa-sama that Benuure died to grant Seil-niisama's wish for a meeting, no matter the cost. What was it Seil-niisama wanted to do?"

Elsreine glared at Seil.

"Even if kaa-sama had listened to what Seil-niisama said and lowered the taxes, and even if Rougran-niisama and the others had not revolted — then everything done toward that end would all go to waste. The many common people sacrificed in this round of oppression were sacrificed so that no more people would be sacrificed going forward…… was it the result you wanted to see them die in vain?"

What had he wanted to do?

As she said.

*"——Why did you not join forces with Rougran and the others?"*

He recalled his mother's contemptuous face.

What Seil had done was only stir things up without meaning.

If there had been something of worth, it was only self-satisfaction.

In his heart he had known his mother had no intention of hearing his wish.

If he truly thought of the people, he should either have led Rougran and the others and risen — or shown compliance to his mother from the start and made their deaths mean as much as possible.

Those had been the only two options.

"……I know Rougran-niisama and you were close. And Reine doesn't think Rougran-niisama was a bad person, and doesn't want to think Seil-niisama, who Benuure was willing to sacrifice herself for, is a bad person either. …… But Seil-niisama's words and his actions are upside down."

Holding Rougran back from rising had prolonged the common people's suffering.

And for Seil's sake, Benuure had been made to die for nothing.

"At minimum, Benuure, who need not have died, died for Seil-niisama because of his pointless action. …… Honestly, Reine can't come to like Seil-niisama very much."

"……There is no excuse. As you say…… that Benuure was sacrificed is the result of my foolishness."

He had been dithering — cowardly and foolish.

If he had wanted to do something, he should have done so from the start, with the resolve to die, as so many loyal retainers who had died had done.

Had he publicly joined forces with Rougran and the others in open forum proposing it by name, weighing gain and loss, at best it might have been heard.

Not only the remonstrating retainers but all the princes would find no rational reason to execute at once.

Seil had only been dancing, at a loss, doing nothing.

Between his mother and his brothers.

Nothing was left in his chest but regret.

"……, The other day, after that, I spoke with Fiiri."

"With Fiiri……?"

Elsreine furrowed her brow, and Seil nodded.

"I decided to have a proper talk like this because of that. I——"

As Seil was about to say it, the sound of a horse galloping.

*Your Highness*, a voice called from outside the tent, and both of them rose.

"……Rukazan? Right now Reine is in the middle of talking with Seil-niisama……"

"Urgent business warrants."

"Urgent…… come in, please."

Elsreine moved to the entrance, and the large man entering was a man with a burn-scarred face.

Gorzarius Rukazan dropped to one knee and said gravely:

"A disturbance in the royal palace, and word has come from a subordinate I had left to report."

"A disturbance……?"

"……Surely not, a coup? Is Her Majesty safe, Rukazan?"

"Ha. Her Majesty is unharmed. But, from what I heard, the one who was killed was…… a servant of the palace."

Gorzarius raised his face and looked at Elsreine.

"A servant by the name of Fiiri, it is said."

"……Eh?"

――The day after Elsreine left the royal palace.

Elegant and yet cold — the queen's bedchamber.

The one who entered with a bow was a red-glimmering golden head of hair.

Wrapped in an apron dress, a beautiful servant.

The woman reclining and sipping tea alone — the woman in a white dress, Queen Grabareine — noticed her figure and offered a greeting: *Welcome*.

Her golden hair, fine as threads of gold, swayed with her movement.

"I have come at your summons, Your Majesty."

"Yes. Why do you suppose you were called, then?"

"……To keep you company for tea, perhaps?"

The servant also smiled, and then asked, *or perhaps*:

"Or perhaps, at last Fiiri has become an eyesore, and you intend to be rid of her?"

The queen narrowed her eyes and fixed her cold violet gaze on her.

"My, you came knowing that. Have you turned reckless? Or do you think you won't be killed because you're Reine's favorite?"

*What a strange thing to say*, the servant returned.

"Fiiri is a subject of Her Majesty, and this body and life have been offered to Her Majesty. When called, she will come running, and offer everything to serve in loyalty — that is her duty. …… Should Her Majesty command it, she will part this neck before Your Majesty's eyes this very moment."

*What would you have of me*, the servant smiled once more.

In contrast, the queen, wearing displeasure, indicated the chair across from her with her gaze.
