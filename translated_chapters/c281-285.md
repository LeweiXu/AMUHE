# Chapter 281: Once Upon a Throne of Gods

The hollow was threaded through with the roots of the World Tree, its sky hidden beneath the World Tree's spreading boughs.

The mana that filled it set the air aglow, and the petals drifting without end scattered rainbow light across the sunbeams—raining down, and dissolving into blue particles before they ever reached the ground.

The season in which the World Tree flowered was spring.

The world was dyed in fantastical hues. Wind sprites played among the petals as though rejoicing, and even in the earth where the World Tree spread its roots, the earth sprites darted busily between the gaps, tending to the small flowers there. As though gathering the vast quantities of water the World Tree demanded, the water sprites brought a waterfall to the hollow—yet with no lake, no river—and still, a mysterious abundance of water poured down from above the hollow, and the World Tree shook its branches as though drinking it all in. The fire sprites gathered and danced to undo the water sprites' labor, and from the plunge pool, steam rose up.

The air had a faint mist to it, rendering the fantastical colors still more uncertain, more layered.

And beside the trunk, four mounds of blue-grey.

The beasts that all living things worship as gods—dragons—had curled their great bodies there, and before them stood a single girl.

Her long black hair loosely bound, wearing only a wrap and a hip-cloth, she too seemed to dissolve into the scene, seated upon a root.

She smiled wryly at the sprites dancing without a word, and gazed at the hundreds of mana spheres floating above her head.

What appeared an irregular arrangement was, in fact, a regular one.

Judging from the sun's angle that it must be nearly time, she looked back, and turned her eyes to the peculiar door that stood in the corner of the hollow—no building around it, simply there.

——A moment later the door opened, and the face that peeked through belonged to a beautiful servant with red hair.

As she entered, a mischievous wind sprite flew toward her; in response, she let a mature smile play across her somehow childlike features and gave a small wave of her finger.

The moisture in the air gathered, and taking shape in midair was a water-cat as large as a cottage.

Two, four, eight, sixteen—the water-cat split apart each time it sprang, and in an instant more than a hundred small water-cats were racing through the hollow up into the sky.

The winged wind sprites rang out their voices merrily and went chasing after the water-cats.

The servant too gave a small skip, pushed forward by the wind, and landed gracefully before the four—beside the spirit-shrine maiden Lila—with a gentle bow.

Then she pointed the tip of her finger at one of the floating mana spheres, moved it ever so slightly, and that was her work done.

She turned her gaze to Lila and stepped closer.

From the servant drifted a sweet, bewitching fragrance—Lila's eyes swam slightly.

"Lila-sama, today, with Elvena-sama's wishes in mind, I tried making tarts using a variety of fruits."

"I—is that so……"

"Yes. From the taste test, I would say they turned out splendidly."

A soft smile on those rose-colored lips.

Thin in color and in substance, not insisting upon their presence—

And yet they were lips of uncommonly sensual appeal.

That the lips seemed so alluring was surely because she had been sampling the sweet tarts—which had, in all likelihood, been generously drizzled with honey.

——This was a garden of temptation.

The seemingly decadent life of the manor knew no limits.

It was a world in which one could fall to the very depths of the earth, should one wish to.

A paradise where, without self-restraint, one would roll endlessly downward—a prison of desire.

To maintain one's reason as a Kreisharana shrine maiden within it was no ordinary feat.

She had resolved to sleep in a cottage rather than the manor, to eat only the forest's fruits—and had most recently maintained this for five years—yet that too had been broken a few days ago.

——*Just a small extra touch.*

That had been the reason: she had listened to that invitation.

Food made using only the forest's ingredients. Not extravagant. With one small extra touch, it becomes very delicious.

Lured by such words, she had tasted it—and before she knew it, she had, at Krische's urging that it came from the forest, even reached for meat.

Meat after five years was so delicious it nearly had her drooling without dignity.

At this rate, before long the words abstinence and temperance would vanish from her head entirely; of that there was no doubt.

And she would spend another round of days in dissolution, more times than she could count.

——*This must be stopped while there is still time.*

Lila had resolved herself to that—but before she could say anything, the servant's hand stroked her cheek.

And then a soft laugh, a bewitching smile.

"……A little further ahead, there is a banquet planned. Krische-sama would very much like to hold it as a celebration of Lira-sama completing her training as well……"

"A, a celebration……"

"Fufufu, Lira-sama always looks so delighted when you eat the food, so as the one making it, it pleases me as well. ……Of course, I do feel a little sorry for Lira-sama, who holds temperance dear……"

Mischievous, gentle, troubled—all at once.

Her smile held many things mingled together, and that was what drew the eye so.

It was different from the brilliance of Kreschenta or Selene—

"……If you are willing, might you stay with us a little longer? Krische-sama is quite determined."

Faced with a smile like that making such a request, no words of refusal came to mind.

In truth, even when she tried to refuse, she always ended up going along before she knew it. That was always how it was.

Lila groaned quietly and nodded.

"U-uu…… j-just a little longer…… if it is only that……"

This servant was always a kind person who looked after Lira—but she was a servant of servants, one who without fail answered her master's wishes.

Whether Lira wished it or not—from the moment her master said she wished to invite her, there was no longer any escape.

"Fufufu. Yes. ……Just a little more."

She laughed mischievously, her shoulders shaking, then looked over Lira's figure.

The wrap and hip-cloth—Bery studied the sparse, minimal cloth thoughtfully and traced the tip of her finger across her lips.

"……Excuse me, is something the matter?"

"No, I was just thinking it might be a little cold."

"Cold……. Where is the banquet to be held?"

Red hair swaying, she narrowed her eyes.

And then, Bery Argan smiled.

"This time——"

* * *

——An endless snowfield as far as the eye could see.

He pressed forward, compacting the snow—which would have swallowed him to the waist had he simply stepped into it—using his knees.

The blizzard had stopped, but still the snow fell endlessly.

Spreading in all directions was a world wrapped entirely in white and deep blue.

Into this sacred, untouched ground, only he carved his footprints.

Ground that no one had touched. Virgin territory.

This was the most sacred place in the world.

Greybyalbe—the highest mountain in the world.

A divine domain that no one had ever set foot in.

And the first to carve their footprints into it was himself.

"……The snow is firming up."

The southern face of Greybyalbe.

Relieved at last to feel his footing grow sure, Borok Andorzea stroked his frost-crusted beard and let out a breath.

Head to toe wrapped in furs and insulating cloth, thick gloves, steel crampons on his boots.

Ten mana-crystal rings on his fingers, and mana crystals in his boots as well.

Mana crystals worn about his body in many places—the formulae inscribed upon them maintained his body temperature, but what blocked the way to this mountain's heights was not that alone.

A vast gentle snowfield stretching beyond ten thousand shaku.

Taking twenty paces was enough to leave him breathless; he would take deep breaths repeatedly to steady himself, then press on.

The air was thin—but this was still only the beginning.

He had climbed mountains exceeding twenty thousand shaku in height before.

And here was the divine domain that would exceed thirty thousand shaku.

The heights that no living creature should be able to survive—that was what this mountain pierced through.

Borok could readily understand that trials unlike any he had faced in his life awaited him ahead.

To cross the mountains in the far northeastern corner of the continent had been a half-month's journey one way from the town at their foot.

He had made that journey back and forth many times carrying supplies, and preparations had taken years.

In financial terms too, failure here meant no second chance.

He never thought of turning back partway.

Reach the summit and return—or not return at all.

That was what this challenge was.

He simply put one foot forward across the great snowfield.

He tossed snow into a heated flask, warmed it and melted it, and drank.

With snow all about—with moisture everywhere—yet on the mountain, water was taken from the body at a startling rate.

Perhaps it was because even the moisture in the air froze.

Even when his throat felt moistened, he drank water.

Again and again—no matter how much he drank, the thirst at the deepest core would not stop.

And so he walked for a full day, and came out before a great ice wall.

Approached, it was an overwhelming wall.

The upper part of the wall, which combined rock and ice, overhung sharply outward, forming a kind of roof.

This was the first rest camp.

The inverted-slope roof of the great ice wall guarded against falling ice blocks from above and against avalanches.

A large tent could be pitched here without difficulty, and if one wished, one could even sleep spread-eagled.

Much of the food was stored here, along with all manner of equipment; one could spend an entire season here waiting for favorable weather—yet this was also the last place where one was permitted such rest.

He entered the tent he had pitched in advance—its cloth exterior packed thickly with compacted snow—confirmed the air hole was open, and lit a fire.

Firelight filled the tent and gave warmth.

No matter how well one insulated, the cold remained cold.

The most one could do was prevent the heat the body produced from escaping.

But even so, Borok's cheeks softened.

He brought the food stored in the snow outside in, packed the pot with snow, and set it over the fire.

After breakfast he had been going on trail rations.

Dried nilkana fruit, high in nourishment—it converted to energy, but it did not cure the hunger itself.

But here at least, he could fill his belly to the brim.

Meat and vegetables into the pot, and rock salt.

He split the frozen bread as well and threw it in.

Borok waited for the pot to come to a boil, and went outside.

He lay spread-eagled on the snow and looked up at the sky.

In the dark sea of deep blue, a pale blue river made by the stars.

And the glowing blue—the flow of mana drifting through the sky was clearly visible, writhing.

That was said to be the result of a great magic worked by a great queen in ages past.

Passed down as the Great Rite of Heavenly Change, its result had filled the world with mana—and it was for that reason, they said, that the sky even now was enveloped in a vast quantity of mana.

By climbing a high mountain and looking up at the night sky, one could perceive it clearly with the naked eye.

The first time he had sensed its presence had been at the age of eighteen.

When he had climbed the World Tree.

The night sky he had looked up at then—the great flow of mana streaming through it.

Borok had been simply overwhelmed, and had come to know the smallness of himself, who had been proud of conquering the World Tree.

A great queen who, fifteen hundred years past, had cast magic upon the world itself.

And himself, who took pride in a single small summit.

——From then on, Borok traveled the world.

He stood atop mountain summits, and visited World Trees in various lands.

In some regions it had led to complications and he had abandoned the attempt, but in many places he had been celebrated as an adventurer who had challenged great things.

But fame was, in the end, only a tool and stepping stone to the next height.

He set down in writing the journey he had made thus far, sold his name and gathered funds, conquered many mountains.

All of it was for the sake of leaving his footprints upon this mountain's summit.

This was the culmination of Borok's life; all the footprints he had carved until now could be said to have been part of the ascent toward this climb.

Greybyalbe was the corner of the earth.

An isolated peak jutting up sharply away from the surrounding chain of mountains.

He had done no more than climb the gradual great snowfield, and yet even from here—the entrance—the surrounding mountains were already below him.

And from here, Greybyalbe rose still further, thrusting upward like a horn, walls of ice and rock toward the sky.

Many adventurers, many ascetics, had challenged it and never returned.

Avalanches and storm-force gales, ice and air that permitted no human survival.

Even for those with mana, it was not something one could simply dash up.

If one's body were pulled away from the cliff face, a single gust of wind would hurl a human body far below.

That clear imagining, drawn from all his experience, carved the mountain's difficulty into his body in the form of trembling and fear.

And unless one could face that fear head-on and swallow it down, one would never be permitted to stand atop this mountain's summit.

Borok looked up at the sky and stretched his hand toward the floating moon.

Then he clenched it tight and closed his eyes, shook the snow from himself, and stepped back into the tent.

In the boiling pot, the fat from the melted meat and the vegetables made a rich fragrance.

Nourishment and flavor had dissolved into the murky soup.

Just meat and vegetables—and already the soup was good.

He ladled it into a bowl and slurped the hot soup—a scorching savory richness down his throat and into his stomach.

He had thrown in some garlic, and that was good.

He felt his chilled body warming from the inside.

Savoring the taste of the meat, he spread out the gear he would take.

Rope of mana-thread—elastic, heavy, but under no great load it would extend a thousand shaku.

The axe was a synthetic Kiuiris steel whose affinity with mana had been enhanced. The crampons were likewise.

Both had inscriptions for controlling heat; drive them into an ice wall and run mana through them, and they would melt and immediately resolidify, making it easy to secure anchor points.

For conquering the ice and rock walls ahead, no better tools existed.

The tools he had spent decades refining were the work of Borok's life itself.

In words, what he had to do was simple.

Keep the foot-spikes and axe driven constantly into the wall, crawl up without letting the gale tear him from the rock, and haul supplies up on the rope after securing a safe position.

If not for the problems of weather and avalanches, he had the confidence to accomplish it for certain.

He had accumulated that much experience, and had the ability to match.

Looking back through history, he could say without hesitation that in mountaineering, no one outstripped Borok.

But even so, it was not certain.

Nature was, for the most part, not made to be something humans could prevail against.

Borok, who had trained himself rigorously, had the confidence to survive even against magical beasts—but that said, it was impossible to emerge victorious from an avalanche.

Even setting that aside, a direct hit from an ice block the size of a manor house would kill him instantly.

Whether or not he could stand on the summit was, in the end, a matter of fortune.

Do one's utmost, eliminate every factor for failure with near obsessive care, train oneself to the limit—and then entrust oneself to luck.

That was what it meant to challenge a mountain.

He understood it was madness, and still pressed forward—perhaps that was human nature.

The soup was good.

But food eaten in the lowlands was far better.

There was no gold or silver at the summit.

There was no paradise ahead.

*Why,* he thought, *do I wish to climb this mountain?*

He had sometimes offered convenient answers when asked why he climbed mountains, but no answer was ever the truth.

Borok climbed in order to end his own challenge.

He climbed in order to make himself understand that there was nothing to be found by climbing to a high place.

He climbed in order to be freed from what had taken possession of him on that day.

——*Someday, I want to go to the highest place in the world.*

He had thought that, vaguely, at the time.

He loaded the pot with soup without leaving a drop, devoured some fruit, and brewed tea.

He mixed in generous honey and converted it to energy.

The view he had seen from atop the World Tree had been both blessing and curse.

A wonder great enough to determine the course of one's life was, after all, something like that.

He waited for the weather to stabilize, and set out from the tent five days later.

Clinging to the rock face, climbing higher.

When climbing, one thought only of climbing.

From the rock's shape—where along the way was one most likely to encounter falling rock.

If an avalanche occurred, which position was the safest.

One always thought in terms of risk and return.

Even if dangerous, if one could pass through it in an instant—even if safe, if it would take too long—so he managed the level of danger and chose his route.

There was nowhere on a mountain that was absolutely safe.

In any location, the chance of ice or rock from above crushing one's skull was never zero.

In the end, ascending quickly was the lighter total risk.

He had drilled the route he should take into his head beforehand.

Up a wall that felt nearly vertical to the body—each step, where precisely to plant his foot, where to step next—all of it clear in his mind.

All that remained was to trace the image.

Three days to conquer another ten thousand shaku of wall, and he arrived at the Great Jaw of Greybyalbe.

Where countless rock pinnacles jutted up toward the sky, and beyond—once more a gently sloping great snowfield.

Beyond this was unknown territory—because of the clouds and rock peaks, this area was indistinct even when observed from the surrounding mountains.

What was known was that it was the breeding ground of avalanches.

In the distance ahead, the shape of a horn-like summit could be made out, and before it spread the great snowfield.

Beneath the snow, the tips of countless rock pinnacles jutted through now and then.

It would have rough, uneven bare rock in its true nature.

The snow was merely making it appear as a gentle flat surface.

Step in the wrong place, and one would be imprisoned forever in the prison of a crevasse.

The pleats of snow surface visible here and there.

Like the graceful skirt of a noble lady—they were natural pitfalls, formed by snow layering over crevasses.

He thought briefly about whether to rest ahead.

It was too early to call it dusk.

The rocks visible a little further ahead were solid and well-suited to pitching the tent, and he should be able to reach them before dark.

Better to gain a little more distance here.

So he thought—but could not put his foot forward.

He wavered, wondering why he didn't move, and looked at the rocks ahead.

"——!?"

——In that instant, the great snowfield had split.

A crack opened in the very spot he had been about to step, and—slid.

Without thinking, Borok clung to the rock beside him.

Closing his eyes without thinking, what he heard was a sound he would never hear in everyday life.

The tremendous roar of snow enough to swallow a small town whole sliding down from cloud-height.

The body, flushed from climbing the rock face, seemed to freeze to its core.

The sound of collapse kept on for some time, from far below.

Sensing the echoes had died down, he opened his eyes and steadied his shallow breathing.

It was presumably only the snow that had been piling above sliding down.

But the scenery had been utterly transformed.

The snow to the left had been largely stripped away, and on the boundary between it and the great snowfield on the right, a snow wall some fifty shaku high had been born.

The snowfield Borok had been about to step onto no longer existed, and had he taken one step, he would certainly have been caught in it.

Which was to say he would have been dead.

He crouched down right there, and consciously, slowly, breathed in and out.

Thin air—excitement accumulated serious damage in the body.

He decided to pitch the tent there that day.

The sky that had seemed stable began to send out an unpleasant sound of wind.

There was something strange about the moment when a seemingly smooth progress shifted—somehow, he understood it intuitively.

That was what those who had carried something no different from a beast's instincts since ancient times called primal sense.

That moment had been exactly that.

He secured the small tent firmly, drove multiple anchors into the ice and rock.

He packed the outside of the tent with compacted snow as a windbreak—and in a complete reversal from the smooth progress he had been making:

In the end, Borok would spend two weeks inside it.

Inside there was no light except the faint glow of the mana crystals used as heat sources.

From that point the sky had grown wild, and outside a blizzard had begun.

A storm of snow that had rained up, as though rushing up the mountain.

The tent entrance was already buried in snow, and the air hole was extended like a chimney.

Peering through it, the sky was dim, informing Borok that the blizzard was still ongoing.

The food he had brought was two weeks' worth in total.

Even cutting back, after standing on the summit and returning, none would remain.

——It had been a failure.

And yet—would descending the mountain the next time a clear spell appeared allow him to be saved?

Thinking so, and yet, once the storm let up, Borok headed upward without hesitation.

Using the faintly visible rock-face ridgeline exposed by the avalanche, clambering up and down it, crawling so as not to slip.

This was a once-in-a-lifetime opportunity, and it was the whole of Borok's life.

The great snowfield—crossing the Jaw of Greybyalbe took a full day, and it was night when he reached the horn.

He pitched the small tent, put dried meat and dried nilkana in his mouth, rested his body, and the next morning began driving his axe into that ice wall.

The wind was ever a storm.

Against the gale that would, if he lost his attention for a moment, blow his body away—pressing himself against the rock face.

Right, left, right, in regular succession—sometimes raising his leg far enough to nearly split at the groin.

Or catching the momentum of the wind pushing upward from below, leaping and gaining still more height.

Each leap a gamble with his life as the wager.

But he knew that if he did not do that, his physical strength would not hold out.

He no longer had the food to climb with composure.

At times like this, he was grateful to his parents for being born as a mana-bearer.

Impossible for an ordinary person—but for a mana-bearer, one could push through unreasonable obstacles. He could drive his axe into rock rather than ice by force, and could gain considerable altitude with a single leap.

That he could move at all in this extreme cold was because he was a mana-bearer.

Strangely, what welled up near death was gratitude for having been born.

Thoughts of his father and mother.

It had been a long time since he had seen his parents. He had never felt a wish to see them.

They probably had no desire to see him either, the son who had fled the household.

As an adventurer his name must have reached their ears, but not a single letter had come, and Borok had not sent one either.

A strange thing.

He had resented them in his younger days, weary of their strict education and the responsibilities demanded of him.

That was why he had fled the household.

And yet here in such extremity, what always came to mind was invariably his parents.

A prestigious old family—in name only.

His father, watching over the household as lord of a small domain.

His mother, supporting such a father.

Two people upholding musty old customs.

There was no freedom in it, it was oppressive, suffocating—and yet his parents spent their unchanging days in order to protect what had been passed down from their ancestors.

When he thought of the two of them, he thought about why he climbed mountains.

In truth, there was no lofty reason whatsoever.

Had he perhaps simply wanted to be acknowledged by those parents?

Their thinking had simply been different.

But even so—*I am your son*—had he perhaps wanted them to feel *he is the son we can be proud of, before others, before our ancestors*?

He thought of his parents, who had been strict with both themselves and others.

Striving for temperance, permitting no waste—they had seemed like will-less magical constructs.

Their way of life had not appeared happy in the slightest—and yet the way they sometimes let a smile slip in quiet everyday moments had seemed happy, and they had been deeply loved by their people.

In times of famine, they had without hesitation disgorged the private wealth they had accumulated to protect them, and when younger he had always wondered why they could not direct such love toward him.

*"We are administrators. The things they worked and sweated to obtain have been entrusted to us, and we return them as needed. Never forget that this manor, this food, these clothes—they have all been given to us by them. ……The role of a lord is the one that requires the most self-restraint, above all others."*

The children who lived in the towns and villages had looked happier to him.

From a young age, more than enough had been demanded of him; he had cut into sleeping time to train and study.

He had envied seeing children of the same age be indulged by their parents, worked hard only to be acknowledged, only to be praised—

And still, all he was ever given was a single: *well done*.

His parents were always busy with the people.

He clung to the rock, steadied his breathing, swung his right arm.

Unless something truly exceptional arose, he always kept three of the four—both hands and both feet—driven into the rock and ice.

Without that, the gale would instantly blow his body away.

Right hand, left hand, right foot, left foot.

Right hand, left foot, left hand, right foot.

Leaving claw-marks in the rock and ice. As though to tell it: *I am here.*

*I am doing something no one has ever done,* he thought, moving with the pace of a tortoise.

Knowledge, wisdom—his parents had given them to him.

The use of mana—his parents had given it to him.

The way to strive—everything, everything—his parents had given it to him.

The reason he could be here was because they had been there.

No matter how far he had traveled, he was the two of them's son.

His breathing grew shallow. He stopped and breathed in and out.

The thin air gave no satisfaction no matter how much he drew in, and he looked up at the sky.

The summit was no great distance away now.

Feeling his head begin to grow hazy, he simply moved upward.

Climbing was suffering.

Working every part of the body to exhaustion, to stand atop the summit at the end of suffering.

The view from the heights looked at through a hazy mind sometimes left no memory at all.

What awaited at the summit? In the end—there was nothing. He had no expectations.

There was only the result of having climbed.

*Even so,* he thought, *I will climb.*

Higher, higher.

To be the one to visit where no one had ever reached, to carve his name there.

To have that name resound and be carried forth.

Just a little more, just a little more.

His head was hazy.

His thoughts would not cohere.

And still, the enormous experience carved into his body guided Borok upward.

Drive in the axe, plant the spikes, crawl upward.

The moment he swung the final blow, a numbing sensation ran through his body.

——The feeling of the summit.

The realization that he had conquered this Greybyalbe.

He gathered the last of his strength and let mana surge through him, pulling his body up——

"Excuse me…… what are you doing……?"

What was before his eyes was a silver-haired girl, gnawing at a skewer of meat.

Confusion crossed her out-of-place features as the goddess tilted her head.

Borok's foot slipped.

He let out an involuntary cry, slid down the steep slope, and what suddenly enveloped his body was a sense of weightlessness.

His body floated upward, wrapped in a sphere of mana.

Defying gravity as though lightly, his body was carried back to the summit.

He was once more looking into the violet eyes that gazed at him, skewer in hand.

No summit he had ever reached had given him such a shock as this.

The temperature had risen at once; the air that had been extreme cold now carried warmth, and there was no wind at all.

And there, before him, were beautiful girls.

Was this some kind of banquet?

Not a single one of them wore anything resembling the cold-weather gear Borok had on. They held skewers of meat dripping with fat, grilling them.

In fact most of them wore apron dresses, and the only one wearing trousers was a single golden-haired girl.

One in furs wore nothing but fur, cloth wrapped around her chest and hips, her navel exposed.

A grill was set over a campfire, sizzling away; everyone else stood silently, looking at Borok with troubled expressions; and the silver-haired goddess (she too wore an apron dress) who had called out to him was studying him with a puzzled look.

Partly from the cold, he could not even speak, and simply stared at them blankly.

Beside them was a large tent—and yet one that would easily be blown away by a gust of wind.

For some reason, countless small snowmen had been arranged all around.

At the highest place in the world—like a winter street stall, or some such thing.

What should have been an ever-stormy world was, only here, unnatural in its calm; it seemed as though he had died.

But the sphere encasing him was surely the work of magic.

He knew that great mages could levitate their own bodies in the air.

He had also been acquainted with great mages of that caliber, but the existence before him was something that far transcended even the realm of great mage.

While gnawing on a skewer, with one hand free.

The silver-haired girl who pointed the tip of one finger at the falling Borok—it had been an instant.

The precision and transcendent speed of the formulae inscribed and drawn around him in the moment before he had even registered that magic had been used—it was nothing short of divine.

The silver-haired girl gave a little clap, skewer still in hand, then came trotting over from the grill with another skewer and held it out to Borok.

At the same time, Borok's feet that had been floating touched snow.

"Umm, would you like some?"

"……N, no……"

He was in a state of peak confusion, but for the time being sank to his knees.

An entirely abnormal situation—even with his muddled thinking, he understood enough to grasp what was happening here.

A divine banquet.

He had, in all likelihood, stumbled into it at this mountain's summit.

"……F, forgive me. To profane such a…… sacred banquet……"

"Sacred……?"

The beautiful girl tilted her head and thought this over.

The more he observed her, the more her beauty had something divine to it.

A beauty built like a sculpture—a girl of complete, constructed beauty.

Silver hair shining in the moonlight and firelight.

The large eyes framed by long lashes were like amethyst, and her figure was, unmistakably, a being created as beauty itself.

*Right now, I am in the presence of the gods.*

So he thought, and then consciousness gave out——

"……?"

——When he suddenly awoke, he was inside the tent.

He looked at his glove-covered hands, confirmed them, confirmed that he was alive.

He sat up—his body was, strangely, in fine condition.

"Why inside the tent……"

Had he pitched the tent unconsciously?

In that dazed state, having had such a dream——

Frowning, he pulled on the tent and went outside, then froze.

This was the summit.

And yet there was no wind, and the air was thick.

He looked at the clouds and white mountain ranges below him, and looked out at the strangely windless world.

The small tent outside—not even packed with snow for a windbreak—had somehow survived the night intact.

From the eastern sky the sun shone, lighting the summit where Borok stood.

He simply looked at it, and suddenly, tears fell.

"What's probably necessary is only one's own satisfaction. ……I think love is probably not something so complex and difficult as to brood over—but something very simple."

She closed her eyes at the feeling of being stroked, and listened to those comfortable words.

There was probably nothing more blissful than being affirmed by someone.

"……Perhaps so."

Having been invited into the manor as a stranger, Elvena had before she knew it been accepted like family, trusted, worried about, comforted.

What she had been uneasy about.

A thin understanding came, and she half-opened her eyes.

In the deep dark of night, three lights—ahead was a dim corridor.

The body she was held against had warmth, and gentle fingertips tickled her hair.

The days when she had slipped into her sister's bed and looked at the constant-magic lantern came to mind.

She had probably always been afraid that it would be lost someday.

Accumulating experiences, various emotions going far away.

But even now, the Elvena who was still a child was here.

"I love my sister."

*Yes,* she nodded, adding just a little strength.

Elvena let her muscles relax, entrusting herself.

"So…… I feel a little spiteful, a little lonely, that Mia-san has taken her from me."

Now that it was in words, it came naturally, and she could accept it as that.

She wished for her happiness, wanted to give her blessings, the feeling of joy was genuine—and yet she thought that too.

No matter how much she traced the outline, tried to sort it—that wouldn't make it disappear.

"……May I stay like this just a little longer?"

Without borders, chaotic—she scooped up what she would stir at random—and tried giving the uncertain, vague doze the name *love.*

Not *probably,* not *perhaps.*

Just deciding that was how it was, making no distinctions, simply, loosely into the box.

It seemed like it might not be a perfect fit—and yet strangely it settled right.

"……Yes, of course."

At least, it had fit in the box.
